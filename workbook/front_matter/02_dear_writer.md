# Dear Writer,

You bought this workbook because somewhere in your head lives a story. Maybe it's a dragon bond that snapped into place halfway through *Fourth Wing* and left you desperate to write your own. Maybe it's a fae court that's been haunting you since you closed *A Court of Mist and Fury*. Maybe it's nothing that articulate yet, just a mood, a pairing, a line of dialogue, a kingdom with no name.

Whatever it is, you want to build the world it lives in. And you've probably tried.

You opened a blank document, typed "Chapter One," and realised you didn't know where the castle was, what the magic cost, how the lovers met, or whether anyone in this world even wore trousers. So you went looking for a worldbuilding guide. And you got buried in questions about trade routes, crop yields, and the astronomy of the third moon. Questions that had nothing to do with the story you actually wanted to write, which was, honestly, two people falling terribly in love.

This workbook is for that.

## Romantasy Worldbuilding Is Its Own Discipline

It isn't generic fantasy worldbuilding with a kiss dropped in at the end. The world has to work *for* the romance. Your world pushes your lovers apart, forces them back together, raises the stakes of their first kiss, keeps them missing each other, and makes their ending feel earned.

A mountain range isn't just a mountain range. It's the wall between your heroine and her mate.

A magic system isn't just a magic system. It's the reason they can never safely touch.

A coronation isn't just a coronation. It's the political bomb that goes off on their wedding night.

Every section of this workbook ties back to that principle. You'll build a map, and then you'll look at the map and ask: *where, on this map, does my couple lose each other?* You'll design a magic system, then audit whether it's solving your lovers' problems too easily. You'll sort out courts, crowns, taxes, holidays, and fashion. And every time you do, a **Romance Leverage** prompt will ask how the thing you just built is going to serve your romance.

## A Few Things This Workbook Isn't

It isn't a novel-writing course. It won't teach you how to write a sex scene or pace a chapter. It won't diagnose your first draft. It assumes you'll learn sentence craft somewhere else and that you're here because your problem is world-shaped, not prose-shaped.

It isn't a trope glossary either. You already know what enemies-to-lovers means. You don't need *fated mates* explained in a footnote.

And it isn't a homework assignment. You don't have to answer every question. You won't fail. There's no grade. Some prompts will spark entire plot arcs for you, and others will feel irrelevant to your story, and both responses are correct. Skip what doesn't serve you. That isn't laziness, that's craft.

## How the Prompts Are Tiered

The workbook tags each prompt with one of three levels.

**[L1]** prompts are foundational. Answer these and your world will stand up.

**[L2]** prompts deepen. Answer them where the topic matters to your plot.

**[L3]** prompts are for the romantasy specialist who wants to stress-test every decision.

A beginner can follow L1s through the whole book and finish with a complete world. A more experienced writer can skim for L3s where they want to push further. Nobody is doing it wrong.

## The Two Examples You'll Keep Meeting

Two worlds recur throughout the pages. You'll know them both already.

***Fourth Wing***, by Rebecca Yarros, is your anchor for dragon-bonded military academies, signet-style magic, kingdom politics, and a protagonist who has to plan around her own body without it being treated as a flaw.

***A Court of Thorns and Roses***, by Sarah J. Maas, is your anchor for fae courts, mate bonds, glamour, bargains, and love across a wall between realms.

When the text says *"in* Fourth Wing*…"* or *"in ACOTAR…"*, it's pointing at what Yarros or Maas did.

## One Last Thing

You aren't too late, too new, too unqualified, or too derivative. Every romantasy writer who ever sold a book started where you are. Your job isn't to be original. It's to be honest about what you love, and to build a world that actually holds it up.

Start wherever you like. Page one is a fine place. So is page 147.

Welcome to the work.

PlotBrew
