# The Romantasy Promise

A promise has two sides. There's what this workbook promises you, and there's what you might, in return, consider promising yourself.

## What the Workbook Promises

By the time you reach the last page, you'll have:

- **A world that holds up.** Geography, magic, species, courts, culture, all sketched clearly enough to write from, and not so exhaustively that you never write at all.

- **A couple wired into the world.** Your heroine and her love interest will have obstacles that come from your worldbuilding, not obstacles stapled on top of it. Every major decision you make about the world will have been stress-tested against the romance.

- **A clear answer to the question "what subgenre am I writing?"** Along with the conventions that genre's readers expect, and a sense of which of those conventions you want to honour, which you want to subvert, and which you want to cheerfully ignore.

- **A bond or magical intimacy system that's yours.** Not copied from ACOTAR, not borrowed from Fourth Wing, not lifted from the Psy-Changeling mating dance or the IAD Lykae Instinct. Yours, built from a toolkit of known mechanics with at least one choice nobody else is making.

- **A clear position on consent.** Whether your book is sweet or explicit, light or dark, your world will have a considered take on glamour, heat, bargains, coercion, and power. You'll know what your book is doing and why.

- **Enough material to start drafting.** Not a perfect world, and not every question answered, but enough to begin.

## What the Workbook Doesn't Promise

It won't write the book for you. It won't plot the book for you past a light final nudge. It won't teach you line-level prose craft, pacing, POV mechanics, or dialogue tagging. Those are other skills, and there are excellent books and courses for each.

It also won't tell you whether your idea is any good. That isn't a question a workbook can answer, and anyone who says otherwise is selling something.

## What You Might Promise Yourself

The workbook only works if you meet it halfway. A few things that help.

**Honesty about what you love.** Not what you think you should be writing, not what's trending on BookTok this quarter, not what an agent once told your friend is selling. What *you* stay up reading. What you re-read. What you have an opinion about at 2am. That's your material. Start from there.

**Finishing the sections that serve you, and stopping the ones that don't.** Some of these pages will unlock your book. Some will feel like a waste of time on a Wednesday in March. Both are correct. You aren't being graded. There are no completion metrics. Nobody is counting.

**Writing things down even when you feel silly writing them down.** Especially then. The prompts that feel twee are often the ones doing the real work. If you find yourself embarrassed by an answer, that's usually a sign you've written something true.

**Permission to change your mind.** Your answer on page 40 is allowed to contradict your answer on page 140. This happens to every writer who drafts honestly. Cross out, rewrite, and move on.

## The Thesis, One More Time

Worldbuilding in romantasy isn't decoration. It's the thing that keeps your lovers apart, pulls them back together, and makes the stakes of their story real.

Build the world so it forces your couple to fight for each other. Everything else is trim.
