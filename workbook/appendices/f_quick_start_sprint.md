# Appendix F. The Quick-Start Sprint

The one-hour worldbuilding pass for the writer who needs to start now.

You don't have weeks. You have an hour. The sprint's designed to get you from blank page to a draftable world with enough spine to begin.

You'll still refine later. The workbook's the long game. The sprint's the first move.

---

## Before You Begin

- A timer. Your phone's fine.
- A blank document or notebook. Whatever you draft fastest in.
- No internet. No research tab. You're going to make things up.
- A drink. Water, tea, whatever keeps you going.

The rule: write through the full block. No pausing. No editing. When the timer dings, move on.

---

## Block 1. The Premise (5 Minutes)

One sentence.

Fill this template and don't second-guess:

> In a world where [one fantastical rule], a [heroine type] must [goal] with [love interest type] before [stakes].

Write three versions. Pick the one that interests you most. Circle it.

That's your premise.

## Block 3. The World's One Big Rule (5 Minutes)

Every romantasy world has one rule that defines it. The bond that seals. The magic that costs. The law that binds. The threshold that closes.

Write yours in one sentence.

Examples (not yours; yours should be specific):

- Fae can't lie, but their truth twists.
- Dragons bond for life; rider death kills the dragon and vice versa.
- A mark appears when the mate's touched; it never fades.
- The kingdom's magic's tied to the crown; the crown's tied to the blood.

Write one. Make it weird. Make it yours.

---

## Block 4. The Setting Sketch (10 Minutes)

Five questions. Answer in one or two sentences each.

- **Where's the book set?** A named region, city, academy, court.
- **What's the geography?** Mountain, sea, forest, plain, urban.
- **What's the political structure?** Court, academy, kingdom, pack, federation.
- **Who rules?** A named person or body; one sentence.
- **What's the rival, outside force, or threat?** The thing pressing in from outside.

Don't polish. Draft.

## Block 7. The Obstacles (10 Minutes)

Four obstacles. One line each.

- **Physical (terrain, distance, barrier):**
- **Political (faction, law, ruler):**
- **Magical (curse, rule, limit):**
- **Personal (wound, oath, fear):**

Name the one that hits first. Name the one that falls at the midpoint. Name the one that's still standing at the climax.

---

## Block 8. The Ending (5 Minutes)

The HEA, or HFN. One sentence.

Then:

- **The climax scene.** One sentence. Where are they? Who's there? What breaks? What holds?
- **The last image of the book.** Where she stands, what she's doing, what she sees, in the final moment.

You're not locked in. The ending changes in drafting. But naming it now gives the sprint somewhere to aim.

---

## Block 9. Look Back (Whenever The Timer's Done)

Read what you wrote. Start to finish.

- What surprised you?
- What interests you most?
- What feels weakest?
- What would you draft first, from this sketch?

Circle the answer to the last question. That's your opening chapter.

---

## What You Have Now

In one hour, you've built:

- A premise.
- A subgenre and comps.
- A world with one anchoring rule.
- A setting sketch.
- Two leads with Wound and Want.
- Four obstacles.
- A shape for the ending.
- A sense of where to start.

That's not a finished worldbuilding document. It's the bones.

---

## What Comes Next

- **Return to the full workbook.** Start with Part 1 and 2 (foundations); the sprint gives you something to revise against, not a final draft.
- **Pick one part per week.** The workbook's built for long engagement; the sprint's your on-ramp.
- **Start drafting chapter one.** You can draft in parallel with the worldbuilding.
- **Re-run the sprint in three weeks.** See what's changed. See what's clarified. The sprint takes snapshots; compare them.

---

## The Sprint, Condensed

If you can't spare an hour, here's the fifteen-minute version.

- **3 minutes.** Premise sentence.
- **3 minutes.** Subgenre and three comps.
- **3 minutes.** One big world rule.
- **3 minutes.** Heroine and love interest, one line each.
- **3 minutes.** Climax, one sentence.

Fifteen minutes; a book's skeleton. Build from there.

---

## The Sprint's Value

It's not the answers you write. It's the speed at which you write them. Speed bypasses perfectionism. You'll produce bad sentences and weird ideas and half-baked characters, and inside that mess will be the seed of your book.

Come back tomorrow. Polish one thing. Then another. The book grows from the sprint, not from the polish.
