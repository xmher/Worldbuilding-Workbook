# Appendix E. Recommended Reading

The books that teach you how to write the book you're trying to write. Some are novels; some are craft books. All have something specific to teach.

None of these are required. Treat the list as a menu, not an assignment.

---

## The Romantasy Core

The books most often named as shaping the current market. Read at least three. Take notes on what they do that works.

- ***A Court Of Thorns And Roses*** (Maas). The fae-court model for the current wave. Study the court structure, the bond, the consent scaffolding.
- ***Fourth Wing*** (Yarros). The dragon-rider academy, modernised. Study pace, stakes, bond, the competition frame.
- ***From Blood And Ash*** (Armentrout). Kingdom and royal secrets. Study reveal-and-pivot structure.
- ***The Cruel Prince*** (Black). YA-to-crossover fae. Study voice, political cunning, enemies-to-lovers.
- ***Crescent City*** (Maas). Urban-fantasy-adjacent romantasy. Study ensemble, pace, and world density.
- ***Haunting Adeline*** (Carlton). The indie dark-romance titan. Study consent scaffolding, content warnings, and the earned darkness.
- ***The Plated Prisoner*** (Kennedy). Dark romantasy with captivity frame. Study the heroine's agency arc.

## Foundational Fae And Courts

If your book sits in the fae-court subgenre, read the lineage.

- ***Lords And Ladies*** (Pratchett). The old fae. Study the threat.
- ***Tithe*** (Black). Early Black; study her eye for the unsettling.
- ***An Enchantment Of Ravens*** (Rogerson). Short, taut, mortal-fae bargain.
- ***Wicked Lovely*** (Marr). The young-adult fae wave that preceded the current one.

## Dragon Heritage

If you're writing dragons, the lineage matters.

- ***Pern*** series (McCaffrey). Foundational. Some dated content; the bond mechanic's durable.
- ***Eragon*** (Paolini). YA dragon rider. Study the academy-adjacent training structure.
- ***Temeraire*** (Novik). Napoleonic-era dragon corps. Study companion-bond without romance pressure.
- ***His Majesty's Dragon*** (Novik; same series, book one). If you read one Novik, this one.

## Magic System Teachers

Books that teach by doing magic systems beautifully.

- ***Mistborn*** (Sanderson). The hard-magic teacher. Cost, limit, rule, consequence.
- ***The Name Of The Wind*** (Rothfuss). Named magic, sympathetic magic, specific cost.
- ***A Wizard Of Earthsea*** (Le Guin). True names. Read this if you're writing fae or any name-magic.
- ***Uprooted*** (Novik). Magic rooted in place, in language, in blood.

## Craft Books For Worldbuilding

- ***Wonderbook*** (VanderMeer). A visual, expansive guide to building imaginative worlds.
- ***The Art Of Language Invention*** (Peterson). If you're inventing even a few words, read this before you do.
- ***Ursula K. Le Guin's *Steering The Craft***. Not worldbuilding-specific, but the voice chapters will change how you write anything fantastical.

## Craft Books For Romance

- ***Romancing The Beat*** (Kennedy). The beat sheet. Read it; then decide how much of it applies to your book.
- ***The Romance Writer's Phrase Book*** (Kent and Shelton). A phrasebook, not a craft book. Useful for unblocking the feelings paragraph.

## Craft Books For Story

- ***Save The Cat! Writes A Novel*** (Brody). Structural. Useful for plotters.
- ***Story Genius*** (Cron). Character-first structure.
- ***Bird By Bird*** (Lamott). For the mindset of drafting.
- ***On Writing*** (King). You've read it. Read it again.

## Craft Books For Sex Scenes

- ***The Erotic Writer's Market Guide*** (older, but the craft content holds).
- ***Talk Filthy To Me*** (Bornstein). Practical, opinionated, funny.
- Close read of any Maas open-door scene, any Yarros open-door scene, any dark-romance scene you admire. Study what they name, when they name it, what they skip.

## Craft Books For Voice

- ***Several Short Sentences About Writing*** (Klinkenborg). The sentence as unit of craft.
- ***Writing Down The Bones*** (Goldberg). For the writing practice itself.
- ***The Sound Of A Wild Snail Eating*** (Bailey). Not a craft book. Voice, in its distilled form. Read to remember what prose can do.

## Diverse Voices In Romantasy

The genre's wider than the bestseller shelf. Names to seek out.

- **Nalini Singh.** Prolific across paranormal, shifter, and psy romance.
- **Ilona Andrews** (husband-and-wife duo). Urban fantasy romance with magical-creature politics.
- **Rebecca Yarros** (Fourth Wing). Contemporary bestseller; read for pace.
- **Tracy Deonn** (Legendborn). YA fantasy with Arthurian retelling and Black heroine.
- **Roshani Chokshi.** Retellings and magical realism.
- **Tasha Suri.** Court intrigue and fantasy rooted in South Asian tradition.
- **Shelley Parker-Chan.** Literary fantasy. *She Who Became The Sun* and sequel.
- **Zoraida Córdova.** Witch-and-fantasy romance.
- **Namina Forna.** Epic fantasy with a West African setting.
- **C.L. Clark.** Queer fantasy romance.

## The Mythology Pile

If you're writing mythology-based, read the myths first. Not retellings; the sources.

- ***The Odyssey*** and ***The Iliad***.
- Any Norse saga, any Irish mythology cycle.
- ***The Mahabharata*** (abridged if necessary).
- Any West African or East Asian myth cycle relevant to your retelling.
- The one from your own heritage.

Then read the retellings:

- ***Song Of Achilles*** (Miller).
- ***Circe*** (Miller).
- ***The Penelopiad*** (Atwood).
- ***A Touch Of Darkness*** (St. Clair).

## The Dark Side

If you're writing dark romantasy, study the craft of darkness done well.

- ***Haunting Adeline*** (Carlton).
- ***The Plated Prisoner*** (Kennedy).
- ***Kingdom Of The Wicked*** (Maniscalco).
- ***Corrupt*** (Douglass). Contemporary dark, but the craft transfers.

Read *with* warnings. The books are hard. Reading them while you draft your own is harder. Pace yourself.

## The Paranormal Lineage

If urban fantasy or paranormal's your shelf.

- ***Mercy Thompson*** (Briggs).
- ***Kate Daniels*** (Andrews).
- ***Jane Yellowrock*** (Hunter).
- ***Anita Blake*** (Hamilton; early books; the later ones are a different beast).
- ***Guild Hunter*** (Singh).

## The Monster Shelf

If monster romance is where you live.

- ***Ice Planet Barbarians*** (Dixon). Foundational to the current wave.
- ***Morning Glory Milking Farm*** (Nascosta). Study the blend of humour and heart.
- ***A Monster Of A Man*** (James).

## For The Long View

Writers worth reading because they'll teach you something no single book can.

- **Ursula K. Le Guin.** Anything.
- **Terry Pratchett.** Anything, but especially *Witches Abroad* and *Wyrd Sisters* for fairy-tale structure.
- **Diana Wynne Jones.** Anything, but *Howl's Moving Castle* in particular.
- **Patricia McKillip.** Lyrical, dreamlike, short, dense.
- **Robin McKinley.** The craft of retelling. *Beauty* and *Deerskin*.

## Beyond Novels

- **Podcasts.** Writing Excuses, The Bestseller Experiment, Romancing The Niche.
- **Newsletters.** Jane Friedman's newsletter, Romancing The Trades, The Hotsheet.
- **Twitter (if you use it).** Follow indie romantasy authors; they share craft in real time.

## How To Use This Appendix

- **Don't read it all.** Pick three books a quarter.
- **Read in your subgenre while drafting.** Read outside it while revising.
- **Take notes as a writer, not a fan.** What does this book do that works? What's the craft move?
- **Come back.** The list evolves. So do you.
