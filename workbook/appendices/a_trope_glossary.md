# Appendix A. Trope Glossary

A quick reference for the tropes this workbook names. Use it as a dictionary when a term's new, as a checklist when you're identifying your book's tropes, or as a prompt list when you're looking for a trope you haven't tried yet.

Each entry: the trope's name, a short definition, and one line on how your worldbuilding carries it.

## Romance Structure Tropes

**Enemies to lovers.** Two characters begin as adversaries and become partners. Your worldbuilding decides the enmity's depth (faction, family, oath, prophecy) and the cost of reconciliation.

**Rivals to lovers.** Lighter than enemies. Competitors in the same field or faction. Worldbuilding sets the competition (academy ranking, court position, shared title).

**Friends to lovers.** Long-term connection turning romantic. Worldbuilding decides how they know each other (childhood, shared war, shared order) and what changes.

**Second chance.** A couple reunites after prior separation. Worldbuilding decides what happened and why reunion's possible now.

**Fake dating.** Pretend-relationship becoming real. Romantasy version: fake betrothal, fake mate, fake court consort.

**Marriage of convenience.** Political or practical marriage that becomes love. Worldbuilding decides the treaty, debt, alliance, or threat that forces the marriage.

**Forbidden love.** Love prohibited by culture, religion, law, magic, or species. Worldbuilding determines what forbids and what penalises.

**Slow burn.** Romance developing over long page count. Worldbuilding creates the obstacles that slow it.

**Insta-love.** Rapid, often fated, connection. Worldbuilding usually provides the mechanism (mate bond, prophecy, magical recognition).

**Why-choose / reverse harem / polyamorous.** Three or more central leads in one romance. Worldbuilding decides the culture's shape around polyamory (bond-sharing, sanctioned multi-marriage, or countercultural).

## Bond And Fate Tropes

**Fated mates.** A cosmic or magical pairing. Worldbuilding decides recognition rules, resistance costs, acceptance benefits.

**Soulmates.** Similar to fated mates; often more mystical. May include shared feelings, shared dreams, reincarnation.

**Bond severance.** A broken or denied bond's central conflict. Worldbuilding determines what severs and what, if anything, reforms.

**One of a kind.** The love interest's uniquely suited, irreplaceable. Worldbuilding establishes the uniqueness (last of a species, chosen by the gods, the only one her magic responds to).

**Reincarnation romance.** Lovers finding each other across lifetimes. Worldbuilding handles the cosmology.

## Power Dynamic Tropes

**Grumpy / sunshine.** Dark-tempered with bright-tempered. Worldbuilding doesn't strictly carry this, but the subgenre often determines which role the heroine takes.

**Morally grey hero.** The love interest acts wrong for right reasons, or right for his own reasons. Worldbuilding creates the context (faction, war, court, curse) that shapes his moral compass.

**Cinnamon roll hero.** Gentle, devoted, kind hero. Worldbuilding creates the space in which his softness isn't weakness.

**Protective love interest.** Defends the heroine physically or emotionally. Worldbuilding determines what he's defending her from.

**Possessive love interest.** Claims the heroine as his. Worldbuilding provides the mechanism (mate bond, alpha instinct, cultural convention) that makes possession legible.

**Stalker love interest.** Watches, follows, monitors. Common in dark romance. Worldbuilding provides the reason (bodyguard role, assassination assignment, obsession rooted in a specific Wound).

**Warrior heroine / soft love interest.** Inverted-gender trope. Worldbuilding establishes a culture in which her warrior role's legible.

**Warrior love interest / soft heroine.** The default. Worldbuilding establishes his war and her sanctuary.

**Age-gap.** Significant age difference. Immortal / mortal's an extreme version. Worldbuilding carries the weight.

## Proximity Tropes

**Forced proximity.** Circumstances keep the couple together. Worldbuilding supplies the force (mission, bond, captivity, marriage, shared quarters).

**Only one bed.** A classic forced-proximity scene. Worldbuilding creates the travel, inn, wilderness context that makes the bed situation real.

**Captivity.** One holds the other. Dark-romance heavy. Worldbuilding establishes the captive role, the rules, the arc toward agency.

**Journey romance.** Couple travels together through the book. Worldbuilding's your map, your weather, your danger.

**Academy / school romance.** Shared institution. Worldbuilding's the academy (section 51).

**Neighbour romance.** Shared territory. Rarer in romantasy; more common in contemporary.

## Identity And Secret Tropes

**Hidden identity.** One character's hiding their identity. Worldbuilding provides what's hidden (lineage, magic, role) and why it matters.

**Mistaken identity.** One's misread. Worldbuilding provides the conditions for the misread.

**Amnesia.** One's forgotten. Worldbuilding provides the cause (curse, magic, trauma) and the possibility of return.

**Royal in disguise.** Classic trope; the prince pretending to be a commoner. Worldbuilding's your court structure.

**Chosen one.** Prophecy names a character. Worldbuilding's your prophecy and cosmology.

**Secret heir.** One's the rightful ruler, unknown. Worldbuilding's your succession rule and politics.

## Content-Heavy Tropes

**Virgin heroine / experienced hero.** Classic convention; examined or unexamined. Worldbuilding can engage with virginity as cultural fact or set it aside.

**Open-door.** Explicit sex scenes on the page. Worldbuilding supports through consent culture and sexuality norms (sections 29 and 48).

**Closed-door / fade to black.** Sex implied or skipped. Worldbuilding can still carry the beat through bond-completion, marking, or ceremony.

**Dubcon.** Dubious consent situations. Worldbuilding has to engage with consent scaffolding directly (section 48).

**Noncon.** Non-consent. Warning territory. Worldbuilding and content warnings must support.

**Heat / rut.** Biological drive cycles. Shifter and omegaverse conventions. Worldbuilding sets frequency, symptoms, scaffolding.

**Knotting.** Specific shifter / omegaverse sexual biology. Worldbuilding establishes the mechanic.

**Mating bite / bonding bite.** Physical claiming. Worldbuilding sets visibility, permanence, consequence.

## Subgenre Tropes

**Shadow daddy.** Dark-magic hero, common in fae romantasy. Worldbuilding's your darkness-wielding magic system.

**Fae bargain.** Deal struck with a fae, binding by magic. Worldbuilding's your bargain rule.

**True name power.** A fae's true name held over them. Worldbuilding's your true name rule.

**Dragon bond.** Rider and dragon linked. Worldbuilding's your bond mechanic (section 50).

**Signet / unique magic.** Each character has a signature magical ability. Worldbuilding's your magic system.

**Vampire feeding.** Blood as food and intimacy. Worldbuilding's your vampire rules (section 53).

**Pack dynamics.** Shifter social structure. Worldbuilding's your pack (section 54).

**Alpha / omega.** Omegaverse biology and hierarchy. Worldbuilding's your dynamics (section 54).

**Monster compatibility.** Non-human love interest's body design. Worldbuilding's your specifics (section 55).

**Court politics.** Factions, rivalry, succession. Worldbuilding's your court (section 52).

## Emotional-Beat Tropes

**Touch her and die.** Possessive hero defends his partner violently. Worldbuilding provides the credible threat he's defending against.

**Only she sees him clearly.** The heroine understands the hero where others don't. Worldbuilding provides what others misread.

**He's soft only for her.** Hard hero's gentleness reserved. Worldbuilding provides the pressure that's kept him hard.

**She risks everything for him.** Heroine's grand gesture at the climax. Worldbuilding's what she's giving up.

**Redemption arc.** Morally compromised character's journey to become worthy. Worldbuilding provides the stakes of the redemption.

**Found family.** A non-biological group bonds together. Worldbuilding's your inner circle (section 63).

**Mentor death.** Older guide figure dies, often mid-book. Worldbuilding provides the weight.

**Beta couple.** Secondary pair whose romance runs alongside the main. Worldbuilding's your inner circle.

## How To Use This Glossary

- **When you spot a trope in your book you hadn't named,** use the glossary to confirm the term and consider the trope's expected shape.
- **When you're trying to pitch your book,** use the trope list to pick two or three that capture it fastest.
- **When you're comping,** use the trope list to compare your book's trope stack against a published book's.
- **When you're stuck,** scan the list for a trope you haven't tried. Sometimes the unlock's a fresh angle.

Tropes aren't formulas. They're shorthand. Your craft's in what you do with them specifically. The glossary names them; your book makes them yours.
