# Appendix B. Subgenre Cheat Sheets

One page per subgenre. Use these when pitching, comping, or deciding which subgenre your book sits in. Each sheet lists the conventions your reader expects, the most common comp titles, and the worldbuilding load the subgenre carries.

For the deeper dives on each, see Part 7.

---

## Fae / Faerie Court

**Expected conventions.** Fae who don't lie (sometimes), bargains that bind, true names that power, glamour, iron vulnerability, mortal-fae romance, court structure, High Lord or equivalent title.

**Common comps.** *A Court Of Thorns And Roses* (Maas). *The Cruel Prince* (Black). *An Enchantment Of Ravens* (Rogerson). *A Deal With The Elf King* (Kova).

**Worldbuilding load.** Heavy court politics (often several courts), a clear magic system tied to fae power, specific rules on fae behaviour (lying, bargains, names), a threshold between mortal and fae realms.

**Reader expects.** Beauty and danger together. Court intrigue. A bond or bargain between leads. A threshold crossing. Seasonal or elemental variation across courts.

**Heat level range.** Typically open-door. Some fade-to-black. Closed-door rare at the market's core.

---

## Dragon Rider

**Expected conventions.** Bond between rider and dragon (mind-link, shared pain, sometimes shared death), training order or academy, war or conflict backdrop, dragon species with distinct traits, battle set-pieces.

**Common comps.** *Fourth Wing* and *Iron Flame* (Yarros). *Eragon* (Paolini, YA). *Pern* (McCaffrey, older but foundational). *Scales And Sensibility* (Haskell).

**Worldbuilding load.** Dragon biology, bond mechanics, rider society, war or political conflict, sometimes academy structure.

**Reader expects.** A dragon with personality. A high-stakes bond. Combat scenes with dragons. A rider-love-interest dynamic (often fellow rider). Military or institutional texture.

**Heat level range.** Open-door common. Fade-to-black acceptable. Closed-door unusual.

---

## Academy / Magic School

**Expected conventions.** Institutional setting, cohort dynamics, rivalry within class, instructors both mentor and antagonist, trials or tournaments, year-based structure.

**Common comps.** *Fourth Wing* (Yarros; overlaps with dragon). *Zodiac Academy* (Peckham and Valenti). *A Deal With The Elf King* (Kova; academy-adjacent). *These Hollow Vows* (Dennis).

**Worldbuilding load.** Academy structure, faculty, curriculum, the rules the students live by, the forbidden thing, the failure stake.

**Reader expects.** A cohort (four to six classmates). An instructor mentor and antagonist. A rival who becomes something else. A trial or tournament. A midwinter or final-exam set-piece.

**Heat level range.** Wide. Indie academy runs open-door; traditional publishing academy sometimes runs closer to fade-to-black.

---

## Kingdom / Court / Royalty

**Expected conventions.** Throne, succession, political marriage, court factions, rival kingdom, inheritance conflict, prophecy sometimes, coronation or wedding climax.

**Common comps.** *The Bridge Kingdom* (Jensen). *From Blood And Ash* (Armentrout). *Serpent And Dove* (Mahurin). *These Violent Delights* (Gong).

**Worldbuilding load.** Political structure, ruling house, treaty history, faction dynamics, court etiquette, succession rule, sometimes cross-border cultures.

**Reader expects.** Court politics. A named ruling house with internal rivalry. A political marriage or betrothal. A faction conflict. A throne room scene. A climactic coronation or wedding.

**Heat level range.** Open-door common in adult romantasy. Fade-to-black in YA-adjacent titles.

---

## Vampire

**Expected conventions.** Blood feeding, bite scenes, sun rule, turning possibility, a vampire society, age-gap romance, gothic register.

**Common comps.** *A Discovery Of Witches* (Harkness). *Black Dagger Brotherhood* (Ward). *Crescent City* (Maas; vampires among other species). *Interview With The Vampire* (Rice; foundational).

**Worldbuilding load.** Vampire biology, feeding rules, sun tolerance, turning mechanic if used, vampire politics or society, old rules (threshold, iron, running water) if used.

**Reader expects.** The bite as a loaded scene. The vampire love interest with centuries of history. A feeding rule that matters. Some old-rule adherence (specific sun sensitivity, threshold, etc.).

**Heat level range.** Traditionally open-door. Closed-door vampire romance exists but's minority.

---

## Shifter / Werewolf / Omegaverse

**Expected conventions.** Pack structure, alpha / beta / omega dynamics (or similar ranking), mate bond, scent language, bite as claiming, heat and rut cycles (sometimes), territorial politics.

**Common comps.** *Psy-Changeling* (Singh). *Black Dagger Brotherhood* (Ward; vampire-shifter hybrid). *Lora Leigh's Breeds*. *Ice Planet Barbarians* (Dixon; monster-shifter adjacent). Omegaverse: indie shelf (Kathryn Moon, Raven Kennedy's earlier work, many others).

**Worldbuilding load.** Shift rule, pack hierarchy, mate-bond rules, scent mechanics, heat / rut conventions if used, inter-pack or cross-species politics.

**Reader expects.** A mating bite as central scene. Scent as romance tool. Pack politics as plot. A clear alpha (and omega, in omegaverse). Heat or rut cycle if the subgenre's omegaverse.

**Heat level range.** Usually open-door. Omegaverse often very explicit.

---

## Monster Romance

**Expected conventions.** Obviously non-human love interest (alien, beast, demon, fantasy-variant), specific physical details (size, anatomy, unique features), compatibility considered, language or communication gap sometimes, care and gentleness despite monstrous form.

**Common comps.** *Ice Planet Barbarians* (Dixon). *Morning Glory Milking Farm* (Nascosta). *A Monster Of A Man* (James). Most of the indie monster shelf.

**Worldbuilding load.** Monster biology in detail, monster culture, monster-human political context, specific physical compatibility considerations.

**Reader expects.** Explicit physicality. The monster's gentleness with the heroine. Specific body design. Open-door scenes that take the monster's form seriously. Usually an HEA that resolves where they'll live.

**Heat level range.** Almost always open-door. Often quite explicit.

---

## Dark Romantasy

**Expected conventions.** Morally grey or compromised hero, captivity or similar power-imbalance frame, heavier violence, dubcon or noncon territory, deep trauma, warnings at the front.

**Common comps.** *Haunting Adeline* (Carlton). *The Plated Prisoner* (Kennedy). *Kingdom Of The Wicked* (Maniscalco). *Lightlark* (Aster; lighter dark). *Zodiac Academy* (Peckham and Valenti; bully romance, dark-adjacent).

**Worldbuilding load.** A world's harshness matching the romance's tone, specific consent scaffolding, villain-level dark hero's context, agency arc for the heroine.

**Reader expects.** Honest content warnings. A dark hero with a specific code. A captivity or coercion frame handled with craft. An agency arc for the heroine that ends in earned power. A romance that ends with them together despite (or because of) the darkness.

**Heat level range.** Almost always open-door. Often very explicit. Content warning territory.

---

## Paranormal / Urban Fantasy

**Expected conventions.** Modern or near-modern setting, supernatural hidden or revealed in the real world, protagonist with a profession (PI, bounty hunter, mage, cop), city setting, arc-spanning romance across a series, supernatural politics.

**Common comps.** *Mercy Thompson* (Briggs). *Kate Daniels* (Andrews). *Guild Hunter* (Singh). *A Discovery Of Witches* (Harkness). *Jane Yellowrock* (Hunter).

**Worldbuilding load.** A defined modern setting, rules of the supernatural's relation to humans, magic system (often quasi-scientific), multiple supernatural species with politics, series-arc planning.

**Reader expects.** A named city with texture. A working protagonist. A slow-burn or arc-spanning romance. Supernatural politics across species. An HFN per book with HEA across the series.

**Heat level range.** Varies widely. Paranormal romance leans open-door; urban fantasy leans closed-door or fade-to-black.

---

## Mythology-Based

**Expected conventions.** A named pantheon, gods as characters, myth as plot frame, modern or ancient setting, pairings often drawn from classical myth.

**Common comps.** *A Touch Of Darkness* (St. Clair). *Dark Olympus* / *Neon Gods* (Robert). *Lore* (Bracken). *Circe* (Miller; literary fiction adjacent). *Song Of Achilles* (Miller).

**Worldbuilding load.** Faithfulness to the source myth, pantheon politics, specific mythic settings, gods' nature and power rules.

**Reader expects.** Recognition of the myth. A specific pantheon with rules. A pairing drawn from or inspired by classical pairings. Mythic settings rendered specifically. A consent-awareness of the source myth's problem beats.

**Heat level range.** Varies. Modern retellings often open-door; more literary takes often closed-door.

---

## How To Use This Appendix

- **If you're writing in a subgenre,** read its sheet; confirm you're hitting conventions or breaking them deliberately.
- **If you're pitching,** use the conventions and comps to shape your pitch.
- **If you're deciding between subgenres,** compare two sheets; pick the one whose conventions match your book's shape.
- **If you're worldbuilding,** use the worldbuilding-load line to prioritise what to build first for your subgenre.

Subgenres are rough shelves, not rigid boxes. Many books sit across two or three. Use these sheets as rough maps, not fixed boundaries.
