# 22. Language, Names, Titles, Honorifics

You don't need a conlang. Tolkien did, but most of us aren't trying to publish appendices. What you need is a sense that language, names, and titles are consistent with themselves.

This is a short practical section.

## The In-World Language

Most romantasy is written in English (or whichever language the writer writes in) with a few in-world words scattered through. Enough to flavour; not enough to require a glossary.

What you need.

- **Two or three everyday words** in the in-world language. A common greeting, a term for a loved one, a profanity. Readers learn these by context.
- **A term for magic.** "The gift," "the flame," "the tenebris", "the old blood". One word your characters use instead of "magic" to make it feel specific.
- **Terms for magical objects or creatures.** Your signet, your relic, your warding stone, your wyrm.
- **Oaths and curses.** Short phrases characters use. "By the Mother," "Mother damn you," "burn me". (See section 19.)
- **Terms of endearment.** What a character calls his lover in private. This is one of the most load-bearing small choices you'll make.

Don't coin fifty words. Coin six. Use them consistently.

## The Term Of Endearment

Romance-specific. What does your love interest call your heroine (and sometimes vice versa) when it matters?

- **Recycled English.** Love, darling, sweetheart. Safe; can feel generic.
- **In-world specific.** "Little wildfire," "firebrand," "breath". Something that comes from his language, his culture, or his bond to her.
- **Magical term.** Mate, chosen, bonded, fated one. Carries the metaphysical weight.
- **Private term with a backstory.** A name he gave her, or she gave him, that nobody else uses. Fully earned.
- **A name he won't say.** He calls her "the scribe" or "the witch" or "the heir" because saying her name aloud would mean admitting it. Slow-burn favourite.

Pick one primary. Let your reader hear it enough to associate it with your couple.

## Titles And Honorifics

What do people call each other?

Build a short table.

- Royalty: Your Majesty, Your Grace, Highness, High Lord, Queen, King.
- High nobility: Lord, Lady, Duke, Marquess.
- Lesser nobility: Sir, Dame, Baron.
- Magical office: High Priestess, Seer, Mage, Witch.
- Military: Commander, Captain, Wing Leader, Sergeant.
- Religious: Father, Mother, Brother, Sister, the Venerable.
- Commoner address: first name, Mistress, Master, goodwife, goodman.

Pick your set and stick to it. If your court uses "Your Grace" for dukes but "Your Majesty" for kings, hold both through the book. Drift reads as sloppy.

## The Honorific Load

A romantasy title can feel weighty or light depending on how often it's used.

- Too often: every line drips with "my lord" and "Your Grace" and the prose gets turgid.
- Too rarely: readers forget who's outranking whom.
- The sweet spot: titles appear when rank matters, especially when the power dynamic is in play (a commoner addressing a king; a rival addressing your hero).

Write with ear, not eye. If a title makes the sentence clang, drop it for a beat.

## The Naming Conventions For People

Same palette logic as places (section 15).

- Pick a phoneme palette for each culture.
- Pick a pattern: first name only (common in informal cultures), first plus family (middle in formality), full three-part name (aristocratic), first plus patronymic (Slavic-adjacent).
- Consider nicknames. Does your heroine's hero call her something shorter in private? Nicknames are also Romance Leverage.
- Consider true names (section 37). If your world has them, decide who has one, and whether the love interest knows hers.

A character name is a sound your reader hears every other line. Pick carefully.

## The Insult And Slur Layer

Your world probably has insults. Use them sparingly, and decide whether any are slurs.

- A light insult: fool, weakling, cur, simpleton.
- A mid insult: bastard, whoreson, dog.
- A world-specific insult: half-blood, mortal, shadow-slave, sunwalker.
- A slur: what the higher species calls the lower, what the winning faction calls the losing. Handle with care; these have moral weight and can reveal the speaker's character.

Your heroine hearing a slur, and choosing not to flinch, is a scene. Your love interest using a slur and then regretting it is a scene. Decide what your world's harsh language is.

## Honorifics And Titles Cheat Sheet

A reference for building your world's address layer. Pick one name per row; don't invent a title you won't repeat.

| Rank | Standard English | Fantasy Variant | Romance Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Supreme ruler | Your Majesty | High King, High Lord, Emperor, Holy One | Unreachable; the one to rebel against |
| Monarch | Your Grace | Sovereign, Crown | Distant; court-wide deference |
| Prince / Princess | Your Highness | Heir, Dawn-born, Bright One | Potentially marriageable; political target |
| Duke / Duchess | My Lord / My Lady | High Seat, Warden, Keeper | Second-tier rival; potential ally |
| Count / Countess | My Lord / My Lady | Lord of X, Count of X | Rural power; land-rich |
| Baron / Baroness | Lord / Lady | Lord of X | Minor nobility; upwardly-mobile |
| Knight | Sir / Dame | Sworn-sword, Shield | Martial honour; love interest's rank often |
| High priest/ess | Your Reverence | The Oracle, Voice of X, Mother | Vow-wielder; villain or mentor |
| Court mage | Master, Magister | Court-gifted, Seer | Knows the real rules |
| General | General, Sir | Lord Marshal, Captain-General | Authority figure; parent-rival |
| Commander | Commander, Sir | Wing Leader, Squad Captain | Direct superior romance |
| Captain | Captain | Swordmaster, Blade | Mid-rank authority |
| Sergeant | Sergeant | None standard | Gruff uncle figure |
| Commoner by noble | (First name, or none) | "Girl", "boy", "mortal" | Power gap in dialogue |
| Noble by commoner | Milord, Milady, Your Grace | Same, in fantasy flavour | Deferred power |
| Peers to peers | Given names in private | Same | Intimacy marker |
| Bonded partners | Given name, pet name, private term | "Mate", "heart", in-world term | Private intimacy |

Pick seven or eight to use. Write them in your book's voice list. When you draft a scene and aren't sure how a character addresses another, check the list before improvising.

## Naming Convention Reference

A quick reference for the naming structures your world might use. Pick one per culture and hold.

| Structure | Pattern | Example | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|
| First name only | Given name, no family name | Feyre, Rhysand, Cassian | *ACOTAR* |
| First + family | Given + surname | Violet Sorrengail, Xaden Riorson | *Fourth Wing* |
| First + patronymic | Given + parent's name | Eyja Bjornsdottir | Slavic / Norse-coded |
| First + locative | Given + place of origin | Aelin of Terrasen | *Throne Of Glass* |
| First + epithet | Given + earned title | Jude the Cruel, Cardan the King | *The Cruel Prince* |
| Three-part aristocratic | Given + middle + family | Daemon Blackwood Targaryen | High-court coded |
| Given + clan | Given + clan or house | Gavriel of the Wolf House | Shifter / clan-coded |
| Single epithet | Just a title or descriptor | The Suriel, The Weaver, The Bone Carver | Mythic creature names |
| Secret / true name | Public and private forms | Public: Nix. True: [hidden] | Bargain magic |
| Number-based | Birth-order or generation | Eighteenth of Ten | Rare, exotic-coded |
| Hyphenated modern | Two-family name | Mor-Andromache | Rare; feels modern |

Mix conventions for cultural contrast: your fae court uses single first names, your mortal kingdom uses first-plus-family, your clan-people use given-plus-clan. The convention becomes a cultural marker without a word of explanation.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Coin five in-world words. Greeting, magic-term, endearment, profanity, magical object or creature. Write them down; use them consistently.

**[L1]** Pick one term of endearment your love interest uses for your heroine. One sentence of its origin or meaning.

**[L1]** Write your honorifics table. Ruler, high noble, lesser noble, magical office, military, religious, commoner address.

**[L2]** Pick a naming convention for your main culture. First only, first plus family, full three-part, patronymic. Write ten names in that convention.

**[L2]** Name one slur used in your world. Who uses it about whom? What does it signal about the speaker?

**[L3]** Write a scene of dialogue that uses three of your in-world words naturally, without exposition. Can a first-time reader understand what's happening from context? If no, simplify or signpost.

**[L3]** Rewrite a scene removing every instance of titles and honorifics. Does the scene still work? Now add them back only where the rank matters. Compare.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's in-world language is thin but specific. "Signet" is her magic term. "Venin" is her magical enemy. "Bond" is overloaded to mean both the dragon-rider bond and the mate bond. Xaden's endearment for Violet ("violence," a pun on her name, used early and rarely, then meaningfully) is a private term with a backstory. Titles in Navarro (Wing Leader, Squad Leader, Commander, General) are institutional and used when rank is in play. She coins little; what she coins, she repeats.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses almost no in-world language. Her magic term is "power" or "magic"; her honorifics are standard (High Lord, Lady, Milord). Her endearments are her strength: "darling" from Rhys is ironic for half the book and sincere for the other half; her Illyrian language gives her "cursed" and other battle-cries in moments of high emotion. Her restraint is a choice: she lets the English carry the flavour, and reserves language-coinage for moments that need to land hard.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The name your love interest calls your heroine when no one's listening is one of the most-quoted lines in any romantasy. Pick it carefully. Introduce it specifically. Let it carry the weight of the private version of their relationship. If the nickname changes across the book, that change is a scene.
