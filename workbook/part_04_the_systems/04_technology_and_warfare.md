# 21. Technology And Warfare

Most romantasy uses pre-industrial technology. Swords and arrows, horses and ships, quills and candles. That's the default. You can break from it, but you need a reason.

This section is about getting your tech level and your fighting style coherent enough that your reader stops noticing and starts living in the world.

## The Tech Level Question

Pick one primary.

- **Bronze Age or earlier.** Pre-iron. Primal, mythic. Rare in romantasy; used in some dark and mythology books.
- **Iron Age / early medieval.** Swords, leather armour, basic metallurgy. Feels ancient.
- **High medieval / late medieval.** Plate armour, crossbows, castles, guilds. Romantasy default. ACOTAR, most fae courts, most kingdom fantasies.
- **Renaissance / early modern.** Firearms (often limited or banned by magic), sailing ships with cannon, printing presses. Some romantasy here.
- **Industrial or later.** Steam, trains, electricity, modern weapons. Rare but not unheard of (steampunk-adjacent romantasy).
- **Modern with magical overlay.** Paranormal / urban fantasy default. Cars, phones, laptops, guns, and magic.

Your tech level should match your reader's expectations for the subgenre. A fae court with cellphones breaks the genre unless you've set up a magical reason.

## Magic As Technology Substitute

Magic often does what technology does elsewhere.

- Messages via mind-bond or magical bird (replaces telegraph, phone).
- Healing via magic or potions (replaces surgery, antibiotics).
- Light via magical orbs, candles, or mage-light (replaces electricity).
- Travel via portal, winnowing, dragon (replaces trains, planes).
- Climate control via weather magic (replaces heating, cooling).

A magical world rich enough to replace technology can feel plausibly pre-industrial. A magical world that also has regular technology can feel inconsistent unless you've built the overlap intentionally.

## The Warfare Layer

Every romantasy has a war, a potential war, or a recently-ended war. How do people fight?

- **Who fights.** Levies of peasants? Standing armies? Trained knights? A conscripted cadet academy? Pack warriors? Summoned magical creatures?
- **Weapons.** Swords, bows, crossbows, spears, pikes, firearms, magic, beasts?
- **Cavalry or mounts.** Horses, war-beasts, dragons, griffons?
- **Logistics.** How do armies feed, move, and shelter? (Most fantasy hand-waves this; the strongest books commit to it.)
- **The strategic unit.** Is your army fighting in legions, warbands, paired riders, small guerrilla cells?
- **Rules of war.** Quarter given or not? Flags of truce honoured? Magical weapons restricted?

Your book probably has one big battle and several small fights. Build to that scale.

## The One Big Battle

If your book climaxes with a battle, design it.

- Where is it fought? (Geography matters: a plain, a keep, a mountain pass, the sea.)
- Who's attacking, who's defending? What are their numbers?
- What's the victory condition? (Kill the commander? Hold the line? Break through the gate?)
- Who's in the battle on the main cast's side? Who's missing?
- What's the cost? (A romantasy battle costs someone something.)

The battle should be a scene your couple experiences differently. If they're fighting side by side, the shared risk is the engine. If they're separated, the desperation to reach each other is the engine. If one is fighting for a faction the other opposes, the battle is the climax of the central obstacle itself.

## The Skirmish And Small Fight

Between big battles, your book will have small fights. Duels, ambushes, training spars, sparring-turned-serious.

- A duel at court (a rival, an insult, a formal challenge).
- An ambush on the road (a nameable enemy, a bandit, a monster, a rival faction).
- A training spar (academy books, military books, shifter books).
- A night-fight for survival (dark romance, monster romance).

Small fights let you show your magic, your combat style, and your couple's dynamic without committing to a battle scene. Write at least two small fights that reveal character.

## The Weapon As Character

Your heroine's weapon should say something about her.

- Violet's dagger-work is fast, precise, and suited to her body. Her weapon and her magic (lightning) are fast-killing companions.
- Feyre's hunting bow in book one signals survival, practical competence, and the caste she came from. Her later Fae-magic arsenal signals her transformation.
- The Cruel Prince's Jude learns to fence, which is a class move: she's acquiring the nobility's weapon to survive among them.

If your heroine uses a weapon, pick one. Let it mean something. Don't give her three swords and a bow and a dagger with no rationale.

## The Love-Interest Warrior Beat

If your love interest is a warrior, the "watch him fight" scene is one of the genre's reliable beats.

- First time she sees him fight, his competence is revealed.
- He fights for her, kills for her, or holds the line so she can escape: the moment he chooses her over the battle.
- He's wounded and she has to tend to him afterwards: the body-tender beat.
- He teaches her to fight: the training scene that's also a courtship.

Pick at least two of these. They're not optional; they're what the reader came for.

## Tech Level Reference Table

A quick reference for picking your tech level and knowing what comes with it. Romantasy clusters at late medieval / early modern with magical overlay; know where you're deviating.

| Level | Core Markers | Weapons | Transport | Romance Angle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bronze Age | Bronze, pottery, early agriculture | Spears, clubs, early bows | Foot, donkey, small boats | Mythic, primal, tribal romance | *The Priory Of The Orange Tree* (partly) |
| Iron Age / Early medieval | Iron tools, timber halls, druidic | Swords, axes, shields | Horseback, longboats | Warrior-coded, oath-bound | *The Jasad Heir* |
| High medieval | Plate armour, castles, guilds, Latin | Swords, crossbows, pikes | Horses, heavy ships | Court romance default | *ACOTAR* |
| Late medieval | Longbows, early firearms, printing | Early muskets, rapiers | Coach, galleon | Regency-adjacent, rapier duels | *The Cruel Prince* |
| Early modern | Gunpowder standard, telescopes | Muskets, cannon, duelling pistols | Stagecoach, tall ships | Pirate romance, regency-coded | *Daughter Of The Pirate King* |
| Industrial / Steampunk | Steam engines, factories, early electric | Rifles, revolvers, steam weapons | Trains, airships | Corset-and-clockwork romance | *Soulless*, steampunk romantasy |
| Modern with magic | Cars, phones, internet, plus magic | Guns, knives, magic | Cars, planes | Paranormal romance default | *Twilight*, *Crescent City* |
| Post-modern magical | Magic-saturated modern life | Hybrid, magic-tech | Portal and car | Urban fantasy | *Crescent City* |
| Post-apocalyptic | Ruined industrial with magic return | Scavenged tech | Foot, beasts | Survivor romance | *Shatter Me* (adjacent) |

Your tech level caps what your characters can do. A love letter sent by pigeon moves the plot differently than a text message. A wound treated with magic binds differently than one treated with surgery. Pick deliberately, because each choice disqualifies the others.

## Warfare Reference Table

A quick reference for scale and style. Pick your war's size before you write the battle.

| Scale | Participants | Unit | Signature Scene | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Duel | Two | Personal | Formal court challenge; first blood | *The Cruel Prince* |
| Skirmish | 10-50 | Warband, squad | Ambush on the road; raid on a keep | Various |
| Pitched battle | 1,000-10,000 | Company, regiment | Field battle; banner-fall; commander duel | *Throne Of Glass* |
| Siege | 10,000 on defence | Castle garrison | Starvation arc, breach, wall-fight | *A Song Of Ice And Fire* |
| Aerial battle | Paired riders, wings | Wing of riders | Sky-battle; dragon-to-dragon | *Fourth Wing* |
| Magical duel | Two mages | Personal | High Lord vs. High Lord | *ACOTAR* |
| Naval battle | Ships, crews | Fleet | Broadside, boarding, sinking | *Daughter Of The Pirate King* |
| Rebellion / guerrilla | Small cells | Cell of 5-20 | Sabotage, assassination, recruit | *Throne Of Glass* later books |
| Army campaign | Full nation's force | Legion / division | Long march, supply lines, multiple battles | *The Priory Of The Orange Tree* |
| Total war | Civilizations | Everyone | Nation-scale; the world changes | *A Court Of Wings And Ruin* |

Pick one climax scale for your book's biggest battle. Pair it with at least one smaller-scale fight your couple participates in earlier (a skirmish, a duel, a magical duel) so the reader has seen them fight before the stakes arrive.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your tech level. Name one thing your magic replaces that technology does in our world.

**[L1]** Name the big battle or war your book lives in. One sentence of the geography, the sides, and the stakes.

**[L2]** Design the weapon and fighting style of each of your leads. One sentence each. Include one fighting beat each gets in the book.

**[L2]** Write the small fight scene that reveals character: not the climax, but the early skirmish. Who fights, who watches, what does the reader learn about whom?

**[L3]** Draft the big battle scene's opening. 500 words. Place your couple (together, separated, opposed) and let the battle start. Don't write the resolution yet.

**[L3]** If you removed all magic from the warfare of your world, would the combat still feel distinct? If yes, your combat style is doing work. If no, your magic is the combat; decide whether that's what you want.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros writes high-medieval warfare with dragons and signet magic layered over. Riders fight individually and in pairs; the unit is a wing; the strategic scale is outpost and border defence. Weapons are daggers, swords, crossbows, and claws (the dragon's). Violet's dagger training is her physical-combat style; her lightning is her magic-combat. Xaden's sword work and shadow-magic are his. The couple's combat styles pair: his close-range shadow plus her long-range lightning is what makes them uniquely deadly together. The fighting pairs the romance.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas runs a blended medieval-magical warfare. Illyrian warriors use swords, daggers, and aerial combat; magic is deployed at high level by High Lords, who can reshape battlefields. The strategic scale shifts from personal trials (Under the Mountain) to army-scale war (later books) without losing coherence. She writes her biggest battles slightly off-screen, centring her couple's choices within them: Rhys's sacrifice at the end of *A Court of Wings and Ruin* is the emotional climax, and the wider battle is the setting, not the focus. Worth studying for how a romance-first war-book balances the two.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The battle your book builds to is the emotional climax of the relationship, not just the military one. Before you write it, ask: who does your couple lose, what does each pay, and which of them is willing to die for the other? Those three answers are the battle. The swords are decoration.
