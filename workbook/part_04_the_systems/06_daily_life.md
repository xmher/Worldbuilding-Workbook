# 23. Daily Life

Romantasy happens in specific places. A bed, a kitchen, a dining hall, a bath chamber, a training yard. If your reader can't see the room, she can't stay in the scene.

This section is about the small texture of your world: food, clothing, buildings, hygiene, the domestic details that make a place feel lived in.

You won't use every detail. You'll use enough to make your reader trust that every detail exists.

## Food

What do people eat in your world?

- **The common table.** What does a peasant eat? What does a soldier carry on campaign? What does the academy feed cadets?
- **The noble table.** What's served at court? What's exotic? What's seasonal?
- **The ritual food.** Is there a food served only on holy days, at weddings, at mourning meals?
- **A regional signature.** What does your heroine's home region serve that nowhere else does? This is the taste of homesickness.
- **A forbidden or rare food.** Something only the wealthy or magical have. The oyster no commoner has ever tasted; the dragon-meat no rider is allowed to eat.
- **A shared food between your couple.** What they eat together. Breakfast in bed. The stew she cooks the first time he's in her kitchen. The food becomes shorthand for the intimacy.

Don't build a feast menu for every meal. Build one signature food per region or culture. Let the rest be described in terms of textures and smells ("roasted meat, fresh bread, honey wine").

## Clothing

Clothing carries class, culture, season, and sometimes magic.

- **Your heroine's everyday wear.** What does she dress in on page one? The dress tells the reader her class before she speaks.
- **Formal wear.** What does she wear to court, to the rite, to the wedding?
- **Her first gift of clothing.** Romantasy loves the "he orders her a new wardrobe" moment. Design the dress. The colour, the cut, the fabric, the message it sends.
- **Culture differences.** How do different kingdoms, courts, or species dress differently? A Night Court look differs from a Spring Court look.
- **Military or uniform wear.** If your book has a military, what does the uniform look like? What marks rank?
- **Magical clothing.** Cloaks of invisibility, dresses that shimmer, armour that remembers its wearer. Pick one; make it matter.

Decide three wardrobe beats your book uses: the everyday, the formal, and the transformation wear (the night she shows up in a dress that signals she's someone new).

## Buildings

Where do people live? A short list per culture.

- **Peasant dwelling.** A one-room hut? A shared longhouse? A cottage with garden?
- **Noble manor or keep.** Stone walls, many rooms, gardens, a solar, a hall.
- **Royal palace or court seat.** Grand. Built to impress. With specific named rooms.
- **Temple or holy site.** What does worship space look like?
- **Academy or institutional building.** Layout, dorms, training grounds.
- **Rural inn or roadhouse.** The travel stop. Where characters meet strangers.

Pick one or two of these per scene location your book visits. Name the rooms. A fireplace, a balcony, a gallery, a solar, a scullery. Named rooms feel more solid than "a castle".

## Hygiene And Bathing

Small but load-bearing. Romantasy loves a bath scene.

- **Bathing customs.** Public baths (Roman-coded)? Private tubs filled by servants? Magical warm pools? Quick wash with basin and cloth?
- **Who bathes whom.** A lady's maid draws her bath. A servant attends. Or she bathes alone. Or a love interest (carefully, consensually) helps.
- **Scent and grooming.** Oils, soaps, perfumes, hair treatments. What does your heroine smell like? What does your love interest notice?
- **The bath scene.** If your book has one, design it. Who's in the water? Who walks in on whom? What's revealed?

A bath scene is a vulnerability beat. Use it deliberately.

## The Daily Rhythm

What does a day look like in your heroine's world?

- When does she wake? Is there a dawn ritual, a breakfast of porridge, a morning prayer?
- What's her work? Hunting, studying, training, attending court, sewing, healing?
- What's lunch? When, with whom, where?
- What's the afternoon? More work? A court session? A rest?
- What's evening? Dinner, dancing, intrigue, a training bout, bed?
- When does she sleep, and what's the room she sleeps in like?

Write her ordinary day once, in a page or less. You'll have answered a dozen small questions about her world that would otherwise ambush you mid-scene.

## The Love Interest's Daily Rhythm

Do the same for him.

- His waking hour (earlier than hers, usually, if he's a warrior).
- His work (training, council, court, patrol).
- His meals (often taken alone, often late).
- His evenings (often political, sometimes private).
- His bed (often a room the reader will eventually see her in).

The overlap between their rhythms is where the romance happens. Where do his mornings meet her evenings? Where does his training schedule leave her free to be where he'll find her?

## The Domestic Scenes

The best romantasies include domestic scenes. Breakfast. A fireside. A bath. A morning tea. A walk in a garden.

These scenes let the reader settle into the couple's reality, separate from plot. They're what make the reader believe in the happy ending you'll deliver. A book that's all plot and no tenderness exhausts readers; one that lets the couple have a quiet breakfast in chapter eighteen has earned its climax.

Plan at least three domestic scenes. Make them specific to your world.

| Royal / High Lord wear | Silk, gold, magical-textile | Signature court colour | Power display; heroine outside her class |

| Mourning wear | Black, grey, or cultural-specific | Culture-coded | The funeral; the reveal of grief |

| Magical garment | Shimmering, self-fitting, enchanted | Otherworldly | Signals a transformation |

## Daily Rhythm Reference

A quick reference for when things happen in different culture types. Pick the one your book leans toward.

| Hour | Medieval court | Academy / military | Rural village | Fae court |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-dawn | Servants at work | Running, drills | Livestock tending | Night-court still active |
| Dawn | Court not yet awake | Breakfast, first drill | Market opens | Night-court sleeps; Day-court wakes |
| Mid-morning | Private audience | Classes, training | Field work | Court rises |
| Noon | Large dinner | Mess hall meal | Simple meal in field | Midday feast |
| Afternoon | Hunting, music, intrigue | More training | Work continues | Gardens, music |
| Late afternoon | Dressing for evening | Evening drill | Evening meal prep | Dressing for court |
| Evening | Supper, dancing, court | Mess, study, free hour | Supper, early bed | Court in full swing |
| Late evening | Political gambling, affairs | Curfew; some break it | Asleep | Revels begin |
| Night | Small hours of intrigue | Night drills, watch | Asleep | The fae-thin hours |

Pick your culture's rhythm. Your heroine's day rides this rhythm until the plot disrupts it. The disruption is the hook.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name three foods: peasant staple, noble delicacy, couple's shared dish.

**[L1]** Describe your heroine's everyday wear in one sentence. Now describe her formal wear in one sentence.

**[L1]** Write your heroine's ordinary day in five sentences. Wake, work, lunch, evening, sleep.

**[L2]** Design one domestic scene that will appear in your book: a breakfast, a bath, a morning-after, a quiet hour. Where, what, who's there?

**[L2]** What does your love interest smell like? What does your heroine smell like? Three sensory words each.

**[L3]** Write a 300-word scene in your couple's private space: kitchen, bed, library, garden. No plot. Just two people and the room. Does the scene feel like your world?

**[L3]** Pick one small domestic detail (the way tea is served, the brand on a bread loaf, a particular way candles are lit) and commit to using it consistently through the book. Small details repeated feel like the hand of a writer who knows her world.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's daily life is academy-coded: cadet meals in a dining hall, military uniforms with rank insignia, shared dorms, training yards, flight drills. The domestic layer is thin by design (the book's pace is military urgency) but specific: shared quarters, the mess hall routine, Violet's dagger-harness, Xaden's shadow-marked uniform. Her world feels lived-in because the institutional life is drawn with detail.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's courts each have a distinct domestic signature. Night Court: the House of Wind (mountain palace with kitchens, bathing pools, a library), the Velaris town house (cosy domesticity, a kitchen where Feyre bakes), the formal chambers where court is held. Her food scenes (Elain's gardens, the cinnamon-warm breakfasts, the faerie wine) carry atmosphere. Her clothing scenes (Mor dressing Feyre for Starfall, the leathers for battle) mark transformations. Domestic texture is one of the reasons readers move into her world.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The scene your couple has in their own kitchen, on an ordinary morning, with no one attacking them, is the scene readers will recite back to each other. Write that scene before you write your climax. If you can't imagine it, you don't know them together yet.
