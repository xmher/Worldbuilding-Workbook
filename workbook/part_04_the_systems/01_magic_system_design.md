# 18. Magic System Design

Your magic system is the thing your book is most likely to over-build and under-use.

Writers spend weeks on the metaphysics of their magic, then use it for three scenes. The writers whose magic feels essential are the ones who built less of it, but built it specifically, and let it do romance work.

This section is about designing magic that serves your couple.

## Sanderson's Three Laws, Adapted

Brandon Sanderson's three laws of magic are the reliable scaffold. Romantasy uses them with a romance-specific twist.

1. **The reader's ability to trust the magic is based on how clearly the rules are understood.** If you want magic to solve the climax, readers need to know its rules before the climax.
2. **Limitations and costs are more interesting than powers.** What magic can't do is more useful story-wise than what it can do.
3. **Before adding new magic, expand what's already there.** One well-used system beats three loosely-used ones.

The romantasy addition: every magic rule should be able to do romance work. If it can't, cut it or redesign.

## The Hard-To-Soft Spectrum

Magic sits somewhere on a spectrum.

- **Hard magic.** Explicit rules. Measurable costs. Readers can predict outcomes. Works for plot-solving scenes. *Fourth Wing*'s signets are hard magic: one per rider, fixed ability, predictable use.
- **Soft magic.** Vague rules. Numinous. Often tied to prophecy or divine force. Can't solve plot problems directly; creates atmosphere and awe. Tolkien's magic is largely soft.
- **Blended.** The most common approach. Hard rules for what the heroine and her immediate world can do; soft mystery at the edges (ancient gods, forbidden zones, the old magic nobody remembers).

Pick your blend. A bonded pair's telepathy should be hard magic (the reader has to trust when it works). A prophecy's effects on the world might be soft. Both can coexist if the hard bits are named and the soft bits stay atmospheric.

## The Source Question

Where does magic come from in your world? This is the theology of your system.

- **Ambient.** Magic is everywhere, drawn from the air, the land, the ley lines. Anyone can potentially tap it.
- **Inherited.** Magic runs in bloodlines. Some families have it, others don't.
- **Bestowed.** A god, a bond, a creature gifts magic to specific chosen ones.
- **Pact-based.** Magic comes from deals with supernatural forces. Always costly.
- **Pulled from a creature or object.** Magic lives in dragons, relics, familiars, or specific beings, and flows through their bonded human.

Different sources mean different politics. Inherited magic creates aristocracy. Ambient magic creates democracy of a kind. Bestowed magic creates a religious hierarchy. Pact magic creates outcasts. Pick the source that matches your class structure.

## The Cost Question

Every magic needs a cost. Free magic is boring magic and, worse, ends your book's tension.

Costs romantasy uses.

- **Physical exhaustion.** The user collapses after big spells.
- **Nosebleeds, migraines, fainting.** Visible, bodily, show-not-tell costs.
- **Magical drain.** Ambient magic needs time to refill.
- **Burnout or permanent damage.** Over-use costs the mage pieces of their mind, body, or years of life.
- **Corruption.** Magic changes the user, morally or physically.
- **Bargain-specific costs.** Magic works but takes what was promised.
- **Community cost.** Big magic requires group ritual; no one person can do it alone.

Pick at least two costs. Apply them consistently. The hero who does spectacular magic without strain breaks the trust.

## The Rarity Question

How common is magic in your world?

- **Rare.** Only a few magical practitioners exist. Each is known by name; magic is news. *Fourth Wing*'s signets are rare (one per rider).
- **Uncommon.** Magic is known but specialised. Trained practitioners, guild-members, priests. Most people are non-magical.
- **Common.** Most people have a small magical ability. The question is how strong.
- **Ubiquitous.** Magic is the water everyone swims in. Every tool, every job, every home uses it.

More common magic needs more rules to stay plot-interesting. Rarer magic needs less rule-building but requires strong character-magic to work.

## The Heroine's Magic

If your heroine has magic, design it specifically.

- **What can she do?** One primary ability, perhaps a secondary.
- **What can't she do?** The limit is the story's engine.
- **How does she learn it?** Training, bond, revelation, crisis?
- **What does her magic cost her?** Physically, emotionally, socially.
- **How does her magic connect to her Wound or Need?** Romantasy magic is often psychologically meaningful. Violet's signet is lightning because she's sharper than her body suggests. Feyre's Fae gifts come from the seven High Lords because she's the heroine who can hold all of Prythian together.

A heroine's magic that reveals something about her is better than a heroine's magic that's just a superpower.

## The Couple's Magic

Romantasy gets extra mileage when the couple's magic interacts.

- **Complementary.** Her magic needs his to work. His magic fails without hers. (Bond-bleed, shared power.)
- **Opposed.** Their magics naturally conflict. Fire and water. Light and shadow. Each cancels the other unless they work together.
- **Paired.** Same magic, different expressions. Both have shadow magic; his manifests one way, hers another.
- **One-sided.** One has magic; one doesn't. The powerless one has to matter as much on the page as the powered one, or the relationship tilts.

Pick the shape that serves your obstacle and payoff. Opposed magic is an engine (they're meant to destroy each other, or cooperate and change the world). Complementary magic is a solver (together they can do what neither could alone).

## Hard Vs Soft Magic Comparison

A quick comparison of the two ends of the spectrum, so you can pick your blend with your eyes open.

| Feature | Hard Magic | Soft Magic |
|---|---|---|
| Rules visible to reader | Yes, named, tracked | Implied, mysterious |
| Used to solve plot problems | Yes, regularly | No, or rarely |
| Costs specified | Exact, measurable | Vague or absent |
| Mood | Tactical, legible | Numinous, awestruck |
| Reader trust mechanism | Consistency of rules | Restraint (used sparingly) |
| Romance use | Bond mechanics, shared combat, telepathy | Prophecy, ancient rite, bond revelation |
| Comp titles | *Fourth Wing*, *Mistborn* | Tolkien, *The Priory Of The Orange Tree* (mostly) |
| Risk if mishandled | Turns into spreadsheet fantasy | Turns into hand-wave fantasy |

Most romantasy blends. Hard rules for what your couple does scene to scene (bond, signet, gift); soft mystery for the world's oldest magic (the Cauldron, the gods' bones, the before-time).

## Magic Source Menu

A browsable list of magic sources your book can draw from. Pick one primary source; optionally pair a secondary for contrast (ambient for commoners, pact-based for the rare exile, etc.).

| Source | How It Works | Romance Angle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ambient / ley line | Magic is in the land; anyone with training can draw | Democratic, low-status heroine can rise | Various academy romantasies |
| Bloodline / inherited | Magic passed through family; purebloods strongest | Class engine, bastard obstacle, marriage politics | *ACOTAR*, *Crescent City* |
| Bonded creature | Magic flows through a bonded beast (dragon, familiar) | Pair-bonded creature mediates couple's magic | *Fourth Wing* |
| Bestowed by god | A deity grants specific gifts to chosen ones | Religious obstacle, priesthood stakes | *A Shadow In The Ember* |
| Pact / bargain | Magic from a deal with a supernatural force | Soul-price, contract-bound love, dark obligation | *The Cruel Prince* (bargains), *Plated Prisoner* |
| Relic / object | Magic lives in a specific item; holder channels it | Quest structure, theft-of-object arc | *Throne Of Glass* (Amulet of Orynth) |
| Ritual / collective | Magic requires group work; no one person can | Coven politics, forbidden solo practice | Witch romantasy |
| Bond-sourced | Magic only exists between two bonded people | Mate bond as magic origin | *From Blood And Ash* lineage |
| Dark / forbidden | Magic from a source the world calls evil | Villain-coded, forbidden-love obstacle | *Fourth Wing* (venin), *Shadow And Bone* |
| Star / sky | Magic tied to celestial events, moons, eclipses | Time-limited magic, ritual-date stakes | Lunar shifter romantasy |

A single-source world is simpler to hold. A two-source world (ambient for most, bestowed for a chosen few) gives you class politics for free.

## Magic Cost Reference Table

Every magic needs a cost. Pick two and apply consistently. Readers feel cheated by costless magic halfway through book one and stop trusting the rules.

| Cost Type | What It Looks Like On The Page | Romance Angle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physical exhaustion | Collapse, tremor, needing sleep for days | Love interest carries her home; bedside care | *Fourth Wing* |
| Nosebleed / migraine | Visible mid-scene signs of strain | Concern scene, tender scene, her hiding it from him | Various |
| Years of life | Each spell shortens the caster's lifespan | Lifespan-gap weight; immortal lover watches mortal diminish | *From Blood And Ash* (partly) |
| Magical drain / recharge | Well runs dry; time required to refill | Forced vulnerability; can't cast when she needs to | Academy romantasy |
| Corruption / moral slip | Each use takes a piece of who the caster is | Villain arc risk; love interest watching her slide | *Shadow And Bone* |
| Bargain cost | The thing promised is taken (name, memory, year) | Dark-love stakes; bond-as-bargain | *The Cruel Prince* |
| Blood | Literal blood required; cutting, bleeding | Blood-sharing as intimacy (vampire-adjacent) | *From Blood And Ash* |
| Pain (hers or his) | The magic hurts to use; scenes land in pain | Him feeling her pain through the bond | *Fourth Wing* |
| Loved one's safety | Using magic endangers someone she loves | The villain threatens; she casts anyway; cost arrives | *ACOTAR* (Under the Mountain) |
| Community / ritual required | Solo casting is impossible; group rite needed | Coven scene, ritual-with-lover scene | Witch romantasy |
| Emotional toll | Casting requires a specific emotion (rage, grief, love) | Love magic; rage magic; grief-powered climax | *Fourth Wing* (some signets) |
| Relic / object cost | The tool breaks, burns, or binds the caster | Sword that drinks; crown that kills | *Throne Of Glass* |

Mix and stack: exhaustion is the cheap default; pair it with one emotional or moral cost for weight. A cost your reader can see (nosebleed, tremor, tear) lands harder than a cost she has to be told about.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name the source of magic in your world. One sentence.

**[L1]** Name two costs of magic. One sentence each.

**[L1]** Does your heroine have magic? If yes, what can she do? What can't she do?

**[L2]** Place your magic on the hard-to-soft spectrum. What's hard, what's soft, and which parts of the plot use each?

**[L2]** How does your couple's magic interact? Pick complementary, opposed, paired, or one-sided. Write the first scene where their magics meet on the page.

**[L3]** Write the rules of your magic as a single in-world paragraph, as a mentor character would teach them to a new student. Read it aloud. Does it sound like wisdom or like a textbook?

**[L3]** If you removed the magic from your book, what would still work? What wouldn't? The things that wouldn't are where your magic is load-bearing. Strengthen those rules. The things that still would are places where your magic is decoration. Consider cutting.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros runs a hard-magic system. Signets are rare, fixed, and legible. Each rider gets one personality-revealing ability. Violet's lightning is fast, deadly, and bodily (matches her physical fragility and her sharpness). The costs are clear (exhaustion, the bleed through the bond). The couple's magic is complementary (his shadows, her lightning). The system is small but it does every job the book needs, and Yarros expands it across books by finding new costs rather than adding new abilities.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas runs a blended system. Each High Fae court has a signature magical flavour (Night = shadow, mind, dream; Spring = growth, sun; Winter = ice). The rules are legible (each High Lord has court-specific powers and personal gifts) with soft edges (ancient magic, the Cauldron, the old gods). Feyre's post-transformation magic is a blend of all seven courts, which is both an earned-transformation payoff and a recurring plot-solver. Her magic scales with her status, which is how Maas keeps the magic interesting across five books.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The magic your couple shares is the thing that makes them specifically them. If any bonded couple in your world has the same magic-pairing, you've built the category, not the couple. Give them something particular. A bleed, a pairing, a limit, a ritual that only works between these two specific people.
