# 20. Government, Courts, Hierarchy

Romantasy runs on hierarchy. Whose word is law, whose oath is taken, whose back you can't turn on. The politics is the pressure system your couple moves through.

You don't need a constitution. You need a clear hierarchy with an interesting succession problem.

## The Government Types

Most romantasy uses one of these.

- **Monarchy.** A king, queen, or their equivalent rules. Hereditary. Common. The kingdom fantasies, the fae courts, the vampire lines.
- **Council of high lords.** Multiple rulers share power, often by region or faction. ACOTAR's seven High Lords of Prythian.
- **Elected or rotating rule.** A rare chief or elder chosen by the powerful. Shifter packs sometimes work this way.
- **Empire with satrap system.** One emperor, many regional rulers who answer up. Useful for colonial-pressure plots.
- **Theocracy.** Priests rule. Political and religious power fused.
- **Academy or institutional rule.** The institution sets the rules; the institution's leaders govern. *Fourth Wing*'s Basgiath.
- **Anarchic or lawless.** No central authority. Plot tension from the absence of rules.

Pick one primary and perhaps one secondary (a monarchy over regional councils, say). Commit.

## The Succession Problem

Every functioning government has a succession rule. Every interesting story has a succession problem.

- **Primogeniture.** Eldest child inherits. The problem: what if the eldest is unfit, dead, or the heroine?
- **Elective succession.** The council chooses. The problem: the council is manipulable.
- **Trial by combat or magic.** Strongest wins. The problem: who decides the trial?
- **Prophetic succession.** The foretold one rules. The problem: prophecies are slippery, and prophecies often name the wrong person on purpose.
- **Bond-succession.** Whoever's bonded to the seat, the throne, the magic, is ruler. Maas uses this for High Lords.

Your succession problem is usually a plot engine. Who's supposed to rule? Who actually will? Is your heroine (or her love interest) in the succession line, and if so, which one of them is a problem for the other?

## The Court

If your book uses a court, design it.

A court has:

- **A ruler.** Named, with an attitude your couple has to navigate.
- **A consort or spouse.** (Or a pointed absence of one, which is often the hook.)
- **A council or inner circle.** Advisors, generals, favourites, whispers.
- **A hierarchy of nobles.** Who sits where at the high table.
- **A faction structure.** Who's aligned with whom, who's rising, who's falling.
- **A court culture.** How they dress, speak, eat, dance, flirt, duel, gossip.
- **A daily rhythm.** When breakfast is taken, when court is held, when the gardens close, when the night revels start.

A court that feels lived-in is a court with small cultural details. What colour does the court wear in mourning? What do you say when you pass the queen? Which topics aren't discussed at dinner? Each detail is a scene handle.

## The Rivalry Layer

Every court has rivalries. Every good romantasy court has the right rivalry on the page.

Think about:

- Who wants your hero's position and what they're willing to do for it.
- Who wants to marry your love interest, and what their pitch is.
- Who has ancestral grievance against your heroine's family.
- Who holds leverage (a secret, a debt, a blackmail) over one of your leads.

A court without rivals is a court without stakes. Pick three rivals your couple has to navigate.

## The Hierarchy Of Address

Court fantasy lives or dies on this. How do people address each other?

Decide.

- How is the ruler addressed? (Your Grace? Your Majesty? High Lord?)
- How are high nobles addressed?
- How are lesser nobles addressed?
- How are commoners addressed by nobles? (First name? Not at all?)
- How do commoners refer to themselves to nobles?
- What are the honorifics for magical offices (high priest, court mage, seer)?
- How do sworn brothers, bonded mates, or blood-kin address each other in public?

These choices are texture but they're also plot. A commoner addressing a High Lord by first name is a scandal or a bond. Let your dialogue hierarchy do work.

## The Pack Or Clan Structure

If your book is shifter, pack politics is government. Same questions apply.

- Alpha, beta, omega roles (if your world uses them).
- Pack heritage (territory, marks, sacred sites).
- How new members join or are born in.
- How leadership passes (challenge, bloodline, council vote).
- How mates fit into pack hierarchy (a mated alpha's authority, a new omega's protection).

Shifter packs are a government with extra teeth. Don't confuse them with informal families. They run on rules; name the rules.

## The Institution Layer

If your book is set in an academy, a military unit, a guild, or a religious order, the institution is your government.

- The curriculum, the rank system, the commands.
- Who disciplines whom.
- How promotions, demotions, and expulsions work.
- What's above the institution (a crown, a council, a god) and how independent the institution is.

Institutions are rich romantasy settings because they have internal politics that don't require the book to leave the campus.

## Government Types Reference

A quick comparison of the most common romantasy governments. Pick your primary; see what the secondary options cost and gain.

| Type | Power Flows | Succession Common | Romance Angle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Monarchy (absolute) | One ruler, hereditary | Primogeniture | Arranged-marriage stakes; forced consort | *The Cruel Prince*, *From Blood And Ash* |
| Monarchy (constrained) | King plus council | Primogeniture with ratification | Scheming council as rival | Regency-coded romantasy |
| High Lord council | Seven or so rulers, equal | Bond-magical succession | Inter-court politics; cross-court romance | *ACOTAR* |
| Empire | One emperor, many vassals | Primogeniture or adoptive | Colonial pressure; foreign-royalty romance | *Priory Of The Orange Tree* |
| Theocracy | Priests rule | Religious election or revelation | Vow-forbidden pairing; god's chosen | *A Shadow In The Ember* |
| Military order | Generals govern | Rank-based promotion | Officer-cadet romance; chain-of-command | *Fourth Wing* (academy layer) |
| Academy / institution | The institution governs | Appointment by faculty | Mentor-student tension; rival cadets | *Fourth Wing*, *Serpent & Dove* |
| Pack or clan | Alpha-led, territorial | Challenge or bloodline | Alpha-mate politics; omega protection | Shifter romantasy |
| Merchant council | Wealth governs | Votes by share or seat | Trade heir heroine; bargain politics | *Six Of Crows*-coded |
| Rebel / revolutionary | Faction-driven | Whoever wins | Traitor-lover stakes; treason kiss | *Throne Of Glass* later arc |
| God-ruler / living god | A divine being rules | None; they don't die | Mortal lover risks all | *A Touch Of Darkness* |
| Anarchic | No central authority | Not applicable | Lawless romance; outlaw lover | *Six Of Crows* (Barrel) |

Most books layer two: a monarchy with an institution inside it (*Fourth Wing*), or a High Lord council with a dark king above (*ACOTAR* later books). The layers let you move scenes between political registers.

## Succession Mechanism Menu

A browsable list of succession rules, with the plot problem each one creates. Pick the one that gives you the best engine for your book.

| Mechanism | How It Works | The Problem It Creates | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primogeniture (eldest) | Eldest child inherits | What if the eldest is unfit, missing, or female (in gendered systems)? | Most medieval fantasies |
| Primogeniture (male-line) | Eldest son inherits, daughters skipped | Feminist heroine as overlooked heir | *The Bridge Kingdom* |
| Equal inheritance | All children inherit a share | Fragmentation of holdings; sibling conflict | Some fae courts |
| Elective | Council or people choose | Manipulable; lobbying arcs | Shifter alpha challenges |
| Trial by combat | Strongest inherits | Who sets the rules? Who referees? | *A Court Of Thorns And Roses* (High Lord magic) |
| Prophetic | The foretold heir rules | Prophecies are slippery and quotable out of context | *Throne Of Glass* |
| Bond-magical | The magic of the seat chooses | Unusurpable; creates deadlock plots | *ACOTAR* |
| Adoptive / named heir | Ruler picks a successor | Favouritism, scheming courtiers, disputed wills | *From Blood And Ash* |
| Matrilineal | Succession through female line | Inverts standard patriarchy | *The Bridge Kingdom* |
| Ultimogeniture | Youngest child inherits | Inverse-of-standard tension | Rare; adds novelty |
| Usurpation legitimised | Whoever takes it, keeps it | Constant war of legitimacy | *A Song Of Ice And Fire* |
| Rotational | Houses or factions take turns | Whose turn, whose grievance | Rare; academy-adjacent |

The succession mechanism is the hinge of your politics. Pick one, commit, and let the problem it creates be a plot engine.

## Court Positions Cheat Sheet

A reference for the court roles your book might need. Name them in your world's language; keep the archetype clear for the reader.

| Role | Typical Title | Function | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ruler | King / Queen / High Lord / Alpha | Rules | The love interest or her obstacle |
| Consort | Queen consort / Prince consort | Married to the ruler; status without rule | Arranged-match target; heroine's fate-to-resist |
| Heir | Crown Prince / Heir Apparent | Next in line | The rival who wants the lover; or the lover himself |
| Regent | Regent | Rules during minority or absence | The scheming figure who won't step aside |
| Chancellor | Lord Chancellor / First Minister | Runs the day-to-day government | The court chess-player; ally or enemy |
| General / War Leader | Lord Marshal / Captain-General | Commands the army | The love interest's direct superior |
| Spymaster | Master of Whispers / Master of Shadows | Intelligence | The morally-grey ally |
| Treasurer | Lord Treasurer / Master of Coin | Wealth and taxation | The merchant-blood character; trade-plot engine |
| High Priest/ess | High Priest / Oracle | Religion | The vow-wielder; conscience; villain-in-robes |
| Court mage | Court Mage / Seer | Magic | The one who knows the real rules |
| Favourite | Royal favourite / Court companion | Personal intimate of the ruler | The erotic-political rival |
| Lady-in-waiting | Lady / Maid of Honour | Personal attendant to the queen | Your heroine's entry into court |
| Master of Ceremonies | Herald / Chamberlain | Runs events and audiences | The gatekeeper at the door |
| Ambassador | Envoy / Legate | Foreign court representative | The visiting love interest from abroad |

Build four or five. A ruler, an heir, a general, a spymaster, and a priest is already a court. Add the others as your plot needs.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your government type. One sentence.

**[L1]** Name the succession rule and the succession problem. One line each.

**[L1]** Name one rival for each of your leads. What do they want?

**[L2]** Describe your central court or institution. Who runs it? Who's the consort (or the notable absence)? Who's the inner circle?

**[L2]** Write the three honorifics your book uses most. Ruler, high noble, commoner. Keep them short enough to repeat in dialogue.

**[L3]** Draft a scene at court: the day the ruler holds audience, and your heroine has to navigate the room. Who does she avoid, who does she approach, who corners her? 500 words.

**[L3]** If your ruler stepped down or died tomorrow, what would happen by the end of the week? Who gains, who loses, who schemes? Your succession problem has to have an answer ready.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Navarrian government is military-monarchic with Basgiath War College as the institutional training ground for the Riders. The succession problem is the throne and the rider leadership, both of which bear on Violet's mother, General Sorrengail. The academy's daily hierarchy (wing leader, section leader, squad leader, cadet) gives Yarros a neat ranking system to rearrange and exploit. Xaden's status as wing leader (above Violet) and his secret position in the rebellion (above the king) let Yarros play hierarchy in two directions at once.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* The Seven High Lords of Prythian is a council-of-monarchs government. Each court is effectively its own kingdom; together, they make collective decisions (or fail to). The succession rule for High Lord is bond-magical: the High Lord's magic passes at death to the named heir, and no one can usurp what the magic refuses. This is a governance rule that's also a plot engine: an unwanted High Lord can't be simply killed and replaced. The political scenes across Maas's series turn on exactly this mechanic.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The court your love interest rules (or would inherit, or rebels against) is the political stage of your romance. If the court has no stakes on the page, the romance loses a layer. Give your court a rival, a threat, a succession crisis, or all three. The court is his Want; sometimes losing it is the cost he pays for her.
