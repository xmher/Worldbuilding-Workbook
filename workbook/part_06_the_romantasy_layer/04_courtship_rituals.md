# 33. Courtship Rituals In Your World

Before two people can be a couple, they have to be seen pursuing each other. That's what courtship is. It's the set of gestures, signals, and stages that a society lets two people move through on the way from strangers to a couple.

Every world has them. Ours included. Whether your romantasy world has high fae waltzes, wolf-pack scent-exchanges, or a quiet Kingdom tradition of leaving pressed flowers on a doorstep, courtship rules are the first obstacle your couple will run into. Following them is safe. Breaking them is plot.

## What Courtship Does For Your Book

A courtship custom lets your reader measure what's changing between your couple. It gives you calibrated beats. If your world says a third dance in an evening is an announcement, then your love interest holding out his hand for the third time is the scene.

Courtship rituals let you:

- **Signal interest without words.** Gestures carry weight when the culture agrees what they mean.
- **Measure escalation.** Reader-visible rungs on a ladder. Gift, dance, private word, first touch, permission asked.
- **Create public stakes.** Courtship is often watched. Observers make courtship political.
- **Introduce refusal.** The rules give the pursued a vocabulary for saying no.
- **Deliver betrayal.** The love interest does the wrong step, or the *right* step with someone else.

Rituals without consequences are set dressing. Rituals with consequences are story.

## Five Courtship Mechanics

Most romantasy courtship customs are built from five recurring mechanics.

### 1. Gifts With Meaning

A specific object with a coded meaning. A rose, a blade, a bolt of cloth, a piece of kill from a hunt. The gift's type, source, or manner of presentation signals the courter's intent and rank.

This works hardest when the gift carries cost. Xaden giving Violet a dagger in *Fourth Wing* isn't a gesture, it's a survival tool, and that's the whole point.

### 2. Contests And Tests

The pursuer proves themselves through a trial. A duel, a hunt, a dance-off, a riddle. Common in warrior cultures and fae courts. Tests separate serious suitors from hangers-on and signal to the community that the pursuer earned the courtship.

Works best when the test's stakes extend beyond the courtship. A suitor who has to fight in the king's war to earn permission is a courtship your political plot can lean on.

### 3. Public Sanction

A parent, elder, alpha, or council has to approve. Courtship isn't considered real until the sanction is granted. Denial is cultural death for the pairing; granted permission makes the courtship semi-binding.

Useful for arranged-marriage tropes, political romances, and any book where the couple is going to be told they can't.

### 4. Private Gestures

A specific small action done in private: leaving a token under a pillow, braiding a lock of hair into a cord, calling someone by their childhood name. Intimate because the culture assigns meaning to the gesture, not because it looks like anything from the outside.

The best private-gesture systems let the reader in on a code that other characters in the scene don't share. Dramatic irony is free.

### 5. Permission Asked

The pursuer names what they want, out loud, and asks. Permission to court, permission to touch, permission to address by a first name. Explicit consent is the structural answer to a genre that has often skipped it.

Consent as courtship is where contemporary romance has been moving. Readers are noticing who asks and who doesn't. Design the asking as carefully as you design the kissing.

## Who Gets To Court Whom

Most worlds have rules about who's allowed to pursue whom. Those rules are where your couple's trouble comes from.

- **Class.** A stableboy courting a princess is a plot engine older than English grammar.
- **Species.** A high fae and a human, a vampire and a witch, a shifter and a priestess. The species rule is one of the most versatile obstacles in the genre.
- **Court or faction.** Night Court and Spring Court. Navarre and Poromiel. Capulet and Montague. Rival sides of the Treaty of Hollow.
- **Existing vows.** A suitor who is already betrothed. A priestess sworn to celibacy. A knight oathbound to another's service.
- **Bond status.** In worlds with mate bonds, courting outside your bond is often heresy. In worlds without, courting the unbonded is considered incomplete.

Name the rule that applies to your couple. Then name the rule they're going to break.

## Prompts

**[L1]** How do people signal romantic interest in your world without speaking? Name three specific gestures and what they mean.

**[L1]** What's the first rung on the courtship ladder? What's the last before the ceremony in section 34? List the rungs in between.

**[L1]** Who is your heroine allowed to court, under the rules of her world? Who is she not?

**[L2]** What private gesture between your couple does the rest of the cast not understand? Use it three times across the book: once innocently, once freighted, once as a wound.

**[L2]** When your love interest breaks a courtship rule for the heroine, which rule is it, and who sees him do it? (The witness matters as much as the act.)

**[L3]** Write a short scene in which a minor couple in your world goes through a full, rule-following courtship in miniature. Use it to calibrate the reader for how much of a rule-break your main couple's courtship is.

**[L3]** What courtship rule does your world have that your heroine's mother broke in her own youth? Most mothers have a story. It shapes how she thinks about her daughter's choices.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Navarre courtship is loose at the Basgiath level. Cadets don't court on the dance-floor; they court in corridors, in shared drills, over strategy maps. What *Fourth Wing* uses as its courtship ladder is smaller and harder: shared risk. Xaden holds back information, then offers it. He protects Violet, then trusts her. The courtship beats are survival beats, which is what gives them weight. The world has no ceremonial courtship code; the couple invent one as they go.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Spring Court courtship is formal, flower-laden, and heavy on feast-night dancing. Night Court courtship is quieter and more intimate: stars drawn in kohl, small knives, permission asked. The books use the contrast to say something about which court loves the heroine well. By the time Rhysand is asking Feyre's permission to do small specific things, the reader can measure the distance from Tamlin's default assumptions without a single line of commentary.

> **Romance Leverage:** Which courtship rule does your couple break, and who sees them break it? That's your midpoint scene.
