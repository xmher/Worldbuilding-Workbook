# 37. True Names, Bargains, Geas, Oaths

Alongside bonds, romantasy has a second magical technology: the contract. A spoken word with teeth. A promise your body enforces whether you like it or not.

Four recurring mechanics belong here. True names. Bargains. Geas. Oaths. Each one is a way for your world to make a character do something, or not do something, when nothing else would.

For romance, contracts are gold. The genre's best scenes of this kind are when a character is caught between what a bond wants and what a contract forbids.

## The Four Mechanics

### True Names

A true name is the secret, given-at-birth (or earned-at-coming-of-age) name of a person or creature. Knowing it grants a measure of control.

- **In fae worlds.** Speaking someone's true name is a command. Holly Black's *Folk of the Air* uses this with Cardan: Jude holds his name and uses it against him. Discomfort, politics, and power flow from who knows whose name.
- **In monster worlds.** Naming a demon binds it. Unnaming sets it loose.
- **In god worlds.** Gods have secret names that mortals must not know.

Good plot work: who told the love interest their name, and when? Who doesn't know it yet?

### Bargains

A verbal agreement between two parties, made with magical force, that compels compliance and punishes breach. Often visible as a tattoo or mark on the skin.

*ACOTAR's* bargain mechanic is the genre's reigning template. The two parties speak terms; a tattoo appears on both; neither can break it without consequence. Rhys and Feyre's early bargain ("call my name and I'll come") is a plot engine through the entire series.

Bargains work because they externalise a promise. Your reader doesn't have to trust the characters to keep their word; they trust the magic.

- **What can be bargained.** Favours, lifetimes, memories, silences, virginities, children, kingdoms.
- **Who can make one.** Fae usually. In some worlds, anyone can, with a witness and a word.
- **How binding.** Until complete, forever, or until a specific release condition.
- **What breach costs.** Magical sickness, loss of power, death, the opposing party gaining the bargain's worth.

### Geas

A fae-oath that compels or forbids a specific behaviour, enforced by magical backlash on breach. Older and blunter than a bargain. Often one-sided (laid on one person by another) rather than mutual.

Seanan McGuire's *October Daye* uses this extensively. In Celtic mythology a geas is a kingly prohibition, broken at the king's peril.

- **Laid by.** A fae elder, a god, a dying mother's last words, a cursed object.
- **Enforced by.** The magic itself; no court needed.
- **Breaking cost.** Physical pain, loss of power, death. Often slow and escalating.
- **Narrative use.** Excellent for "I can never tell her X" plots. The character would tell if they could, and can't.

### Oaths

A spoken vow, often blood-sealed, that binds the speaker. Can be sworn to a person (fealty), to a cause (knightly oath), to a god (priestly vow), or to a spouse (marriage oath).

Blood oaths specifically (Maas's *Throne of Glass*, Harris's Sookie Stackhouse) carry physiological consequences: the oath-sworn can sense their liege's emotions and location, and often die of magical shock if the liege does.

- **Voluntary.** The speaker chooses to swear. Makes the plot turn on whether to swear.
- **Binding.** The magic enforces it. Breaking is costly.
- **Dischargeable.** Completion, release by the one sworn-to, or magical severance can end it.

Oaths are the contract mechanic with the cleanest narrative cost. A character who has sworn is a character who can't act freely on the thing they've sworn to.

## How Each One Serves The Romance

Contracts let your couple be bound to things that aren't each other.

- **A lover who can't tell her what he knows.** Geas. The most reliable "pining in silence" engine in the genre.
- **A lover who owes a bargain to someone else.** Mid-book bomb: the bargain was made before he met her, and now the other party is calling it in.
- **A lover whose true name was taken long ago.** She can find the holder and free him. Classic quest-arc.
- **A lover who swore a blood oath to an enemy.** Blood oath plus mate bond equals structural impossibility. A genre favourite.

Stacking a contract on a bond is one of the most effective moves in romantasy design. The bond says yes; the contract says no; the book is the working-out of how they reconcile.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Does your world have bargains, true names, geas, or oaths? List which.

**[L1]** Does your love interest owe, know, hold, or carry any contract that predates the romance? Name it.

**[L2]** What contract lies between your couple? (Either one binds the other, both bind a third party, or a third party binds them both.) Write one sentence.

**[L2]** What's the cost of breaking each type of contract in your world? Different mechanics should have different costs, so the reader can feel which kind is in play.

**[L3]** Draft the exact wording of the contract that matters most in your book. Read it aloud. Is the language formal, archaic, plain? Does the phrasing carry traps?

**[L3]** Who in your world is famous for having broken a contract and survived? What did it cost them? Your characters know the story; so should you.

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas runs bargains as the operational magic of Prythian. The tattoo is the marker, the word is the trigger, and the stakes are always legible. The bargain Rhys offers Feyre in book one ("a week with me, every month") carries both of them through the rest of the series: it's how she finds him, how she learns to distrust him, and eventually how she learns to love him. A contract placed early, cashed in slowly, across three books.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros doesn't use bargain-magic or true-name magic. What she does use is the relic-scar as a visible record of a bond made under duress, which functions bargain-adjacent: you don't choose the relic, and you can't refuse it. Her relic is to contracts what her dragon bond is to choice: the book accepts that some things happen to a character rather than being decided by them. Worth noting for a writer who wants contract-weight without contract-machinery.
