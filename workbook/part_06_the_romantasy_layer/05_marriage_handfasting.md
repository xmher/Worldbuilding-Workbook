# 34. Marriage, Handfasting, Bonding Ceremonies

The ceremony isn't the bond. This is the first thing to understand.

A bond can exist without a ceremony. Feyre and Rhysand are mated by the Cauldron long before their cabin-and-ritual-meal marriage. A ceremony can exist without a bond: every arranged political marriage in the genre is one. What a ceremony does is witness. It takes something private and announces it to a world that now has to accept or reject what it's been shown.

In your book, the ceremony scene is where your world has its say.

## What A Ceremony Puts On The Page

Weddings work hard in romantasy because they're structured, public, and consequential.

- **They make commitment readable.** The reader sees the pair say the vow, not just feel it.
- **They test the world.** Who attends? Who boycotts? Who stops the ceremony?
- **They set expectations.** Vows taken in the ceremony are rules the book will make characters keep or break.
- **They force presence.** Characters who've been avoiding each other have to stand together in the same light.
- **They stack with the bond.** A bonded pair who also ceremonially marry are doing something different from a bonded pair who don't.

A ceremony that doesn't do at least two of these jobs is a pretty scene. Real ceremonies trouble the plot.

## Historical Templates Worth Stealing

Real-world wedding traditions give you ready-made structure. Pick pieces from any of these.

- **Handfasting.** A cord wraps around the pair's joined hands. Vows are spoken. Celtic-coded; currently the most borrowed structure in romantasy. Holly Black, Melissa Marr, and many indies use it.
- **Blood oath.** A cut, an exchange of blood, a phrase spoken in an old language. Used everywhere from Sookie Stackhouse to *Throne of Glass*.
- **Ritual meal.** The pair prepare and share food together, sometimes unwitnessed, sometimes before the whole community. The *ACOTAR* mating ceremony uses this.
- **Consummation rite.** Sex as the act that seals the union. Private, or publicly expected.
- **Seclusion.** The newly-married pair retreat from the community for a fixed time (a night, a week, a season). Dramatises absence, trust, and return.
- **Public vow.** Words spoken before witnesses that the world will enforce.
- **Title transfer.** The marriage conveys lands, rank, name, or power. The ceremony is the moment the title changes hands.
- **Binding object.** A ring, a necklace, a stole, a tattoo, a ribbon. A thing one of them now wears.

Most romantasy ceremonies stack three or four of these. Pick yours deliberately.

## The Five Elements To Decide

Every ceremony in the genre answers five questions.

### 1. Who Officiates

A priest? A king? A grandmother? A dragon? The officiant's authority tells you whose law the marriage answers to. *ACOTAR* has no priest at the mating ceremony; the bond itself officiates. That's a choice with theological weight.

### 2. Who Witnesses

Family only? The court? The whole town? No one? A ceremony with no witnesses is a different kind of promise than one the whole realm saw. Secret weddings are a plot engine. So are weddings everyone must attend.

### 3. The Exchange

Vows, blood, bites, tokens, names, lands. Something passes from one to the other. Usually several of these, in sequence.

### 4. Where It Happens

Under a specific tree. In the throne room. In a sea-cave. At dawn on a specific mountain. The location binds the ceremony to place, and place binds the marriage to a particular god, ancestor, or memory.

### 5. When It's Done

At solstice, at midnight, during a moon-tide, on the couple's death-day (yes, in some worlds). Timing gives the ceremony its flavour. A wedding at dawn reads differently than a wedding at moonrise.

## Legitimacy And Who Gets To Marry Whom

Not every ceremony in your world is considered valid by everyone else in your world. This matters.

- **Church and crown.** In worlds with organised religion, does the state accept religious marriages? Do the religious accept civil ones?
- **Interspecies.** A fae marrying a human. A vampire marrying a witch. A shifter marrying a Kingdom citizen. Which ceremonies recognise the pairing, and which void themselves on contact with it?
- **Same-sex.** Which traditions perform them? Which refuse? Which invented a parallel ceremony that carries its own flavour?
- **Rank.** Can a commoner marry a royal in full ceremony, or only in a secondary rite?
- **Mated versus unmated.** If your world has bond magic, does a bonded pair *need* a ceremony? Does an unbonded pair get one anyway?

Each answer is a wall your couple might climb or knock down.

## Prompts

**[L1]** What's the standard wedding ceremony in your world's dominant culture? Sketch five elements: officiant, witnesses, exchange, place, time.

**[L1]** What binding object (ring, tattoo, cord, scar) does your world use? What does it look like? Who puts it on whom?

**[L2]** Does your world recognise a secondary or "lesser" ceremony (handfasting-for-a-year, trial marriage, informal binding)? Who uses it? What does using it signal about the couple?

**[L2]** If your couple marries in-plot, which ceremony do they use, and which would their enemies call illegitimate?

**[L3]** Write the oldest wedding vow in your world, in the original phrasing. Now write the modern translation people actually use. What got lost in translation? What did the modern version quietly add?

**[L3]** Is there a ceremony in your world that two people can refuse to finish mid-rite? What happens if they do?

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* The Night Court mating ceremony: the male builds a cabin, the pair prepare and share food, vows are exchanged, and consummation seals the bond. The bond doesn't require the ceremony, but the culture does. Feyre and Rhysand perform it in *A Court of Wings and Ruin* with deliberate slowness because the ceremony is where the court reads the marriage as real. It's also where the political message lands: a Night Court mating now includes a human-born High Lady, and the realm watches.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* No large-scale wedding ceremonies in the Empyrean books. Riders make bond-adjacent commitments through small private rituals: shared tokens, relic scars from the dragons, quiet exchanges in corners of Basgiath. Yarros declines to give her world a big wedding scene, because Navarre is a wartime culture and the book's romantic commitment is proven in combat instead. Worth studying for what it *doesn't* do: sometimes the strongest choice is to leave the ceremony off the page.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Which ceremony's legitimacy is the fight your couple can't win with their world as it stands? Change the world in your climax, or change the ceremony. Don't leave it unaddressed.
