# The Academy Romantasy Worldbuilding Reference

A craft-focused reference document for writers designing magic schools, war colleges, secret societies, and dark academia institutions for romance-driven fantasy. Organised into nine reference sections culminating in 50+ workbook prompts.

---

## Section 1 — The landscape of modern academy romantasy

Academy romantasy is a dominant commercial force. Publishers Weekly reported fantasy book sales up 62% through the first nine months of 2024; Bloomberg estimated romantasy at $610 million in sales that year, up from $454 million in 2023. *Onyx Storm* (January 2025) sold **2.7 million copies in its first week**, the fastest-selling adult title in BookScan's recorded history. The school-set corner of the genre inherits from Hogwarts but actively rejects its gentility: adult stakes, onscreen sex, casual mortality, morally grey love interests, and institutions that are explicitly corrupt rather than accidentally so. What follows is a comparative anatomy of the books that define the current craft conversation.

### Basgiath War College — Yarros's *Fourth Wing* / *Iron Flame* / *Onyx Storm*

**Distinctive setting.** A militarised cliffside academy in the kingdom of Navarre, training dragon riders against a Poromielian threat that turns out to be a cover for a deeper venin war. Basgiath is less a school than a selection apparatus: most of the worldbuilding energy is spent on survival logistics — the Parapet crossing, barracks hierarchy, squad mess politics — rather than classroom instruction. Reviewers consistently note pedagogy thins after year one; Grimdark Magazine's *Onyx Storm* review and multiple Goodreads threads observe the trilogy shifts from training to "active duty" arc by book three.

**Admissions.** Conscription. Children of Navarrian officers are expected to enter; "marked ones" (children of executed separatists) are compelled. Entry requires crossing the Parapet, a sheer stone bridge above a fatal drop. First-day attrition is the premise. This is conscription-as-admissions, not selection.

**Faction structure.** Four quadrants — Riders, Scribes, Infantry, Healers — but the narrative privileges Riders so hard that the other three read as flavour. Yarros establishes class segregation (Riders hold near-caste prestige) but rarely dramatises interquadrant tension beyond Violet's origin as a Scribe-track daughter forced into Rider selection by General Sorrengail. **Craft lesson:** if three of your four factions don't matter to the plot, they don't matter to the worldbuilding; readers noticed.

**Curriculum.** Flight training, signet manifestation, the Gauntlet (an obstacle course testing dragon-rider synchronicity), the Threshing (dragon-rider bonding — dragons choose riders, not the reverse), War Games (squad vs. squad combat exercises). In book two, weapons and tactics. Forbidden knowledge concerns venin and Tyrrish magic; *Iron Flame*'s interrogation-room plot turns on who gets to know what.

**Stakes of failure.** Death. Not expulsion. Bodies on the parapet. Yarros uses attrition as background radiation. In *Onyx Storm*, even named secondary characters (Quinn, Trager) die, though reviewers have argued she protects the core cast to the point of predictability.

**Romantic dynamics.** Year-gap (Xaden Riorson, second- then third-year "wingleader"; Violet, first-year), political enemies (his father was executed by hers), forced proximity via squad assignments, protect-the-rival escalation through War Games. The rebellion-revelation reframes their romance as treason. Sex scenes are explicit and frequent — a deliberate rebuttal to the chaste magic-school tradition.

**Craft lesson.** Use a selection event that is **also a death event** (the Parapet does both) to compress introduction, worldbuilding, character-sorting, and first-conflict into a single chapter. But beware quadrant bloat: if a faction doesn't generate plot, cut it.

### Zodiac Academy — Peckham & Valenti

**Distinctive setting.** Solaria, a fae kingdom with astrological magic; a boarding school where every student has a zodiac sign determining partial access to an "Order" (dragon, pegasus, vampire, werewolf, siren, harpy, etc.). Reviewers describe it as "Harry Potter by way of CW teen drama" — extremely high book count (ten main novels plus spinoffs), serialised escalation, enormous BookTok following.

**Admissions.** Inheritance-plus-manifestation: fae students come into their Order at adolescence and Zodiac Academy is where they learn to wield it. The Vega twins, Tory and Darcy, enter as outsiders raised in the human world and discovered to be the lost princesses of Solaria.

**Faction.** Zodiac signs as personality backbone; Orders as magical subgroups; and cross-cutting them, the four Celestial Heirs (Darius, Caleb, Seth, Max) — students whose fathers seized the throne after the Vegas were deposed. The Heirs are therefore politically positioned to destroy the protagonists on arrival. **This is the engine of the bully romance.**

**Curriculum.** Astrology, order-specific training, elemental magic, "Pitball" (the series' Quidditch-analogue). Described by critics as thin relative to the romantic drama; a ejbookblog series review notes protagonists "only read Darius and Tory chapters" and skip class scenes.

**Stakes of failure.** Social annihilation, expulsion, loss of throne claim. Until the final books, stakes are political rather than lethal for protagonists, though they're lethal for minor characters.

**Romantic dynamics.** The flagship of contemporary bully romance. Darius and his fellow Heirs waterboard Tory in a pool, publicly humiliate her, try to force her expulsion. They then become love interests. Peckham & Valenti stretch the redemption over ten books; detractors (widely quoted on Goodreads and Reddit r/romantasy) argue the abuse is too severe to recover from narratively, and that Tory's eventual love for Darius reads as trauma bonding rather than earned evolution. Defenders argue that Darius's own abuse by his father Lionel Acrux is foregrounded, that Tory pays back violence with violence (she's explicitly not a passive victim), and that the mate-bond mechanic is used to *complicate* rather than excuse the dynamic.

**Craft lesson.** The series reveals what happens when "enemies to lovers" collapses into "abuser to lover": readers split violently. The redemption arc requires page-count most romances can't afford, and the violence must scale to the redemption, not exceed it. See Section 3 for the full bully-romance craft discussion.

### The Atlas Six / Atlas Paradox / Atlas Complex — Olivie Blake

**Distinctive setting.** The **Alexandrian Society**, a secret order of magical academicians who guard the actual library of Alexandria (which, per Blake's conceit, faked its destruction to protect itself). The "school" is a year-long initiation: six recruits live in a London manor with access to the archive, one will die, five will be initiated.

**Admissions.** Recruitment by the Caretaker (Atlas Blakely). Six "medeians" are chosen every decade — specialists whose talents make them uniquely valuable. This is elite tap-on-the-shoulder selection, closer to Skull and Bones than to boarding school.

**Faction / cohort.** Not houses — specialisations, and they read as genuinely distinct because each character's magic is narratively consequential: Libby Rhodes and Nico de Varona (physicists, matter-controllers, unwilling romantic tension via oppositional power); Reina Mori (naturalist); Parisa Kamali (telepath); Callum Nova (empath); Tristan Caine (the one who sees reality's substructure, the narrative's structural key).

**Curriculum.** Independent research into "subjects of impossibility": time and space, thought and luck, life and death. Students study what they are drawn to and present findings. This is the graduate-seminar model transposed into fantasy, and it's what makes Atlas Six work for readers who find undergraduate magic schools repetitive.

**Stakes of failure.** Death. Literal: five are initiated because the sixth's death *powers* the initiation ritual. The core horror is that the cohort is told "one of you will be eliminated" and assume it means expulsion; it means sacrifice.

**Romantic dynamics.** A six-way rotating lattice — every pairing is considered. Libby/Nico (rival academic twins), Parisa/Callum (empath/telepath mutual-predator), Tristan/Libby (sleep-with-the-enemy), and the triangular Libby/Nico/Ezra subplot. Blake writes desire as strategy as often as feeling; sex happens, but it's usually tactical.

**Craft lesson.** Specialisation is a faction system in disguise. By making each character's magic unique, Blake never has to do the "here are the four houses" scene — the cohort's structure is fractal. Also: **kill the pretence of schooling**. Atlas Six is only nominally a school; it's a pressure cooker with a library.

### The Scholomance trilogy — Naomi Novik

**Distinctive setting.** A sentient magical school built in the Void by the Manchester Enclave in the late 1800s to protect wizard children from maleficaria (mals) — magical predators that hunt adolescent wizards. **There are no teachers.** Curricula are delivered by the school itself. Students arrive at 14 and leave (if at all) at 18 via the graduation hall: an unwarded corridor populated with hundreds of mals. Half of students die before graduation; half of survivors die at the graduation run. The official motto, *In Wisdom, Shelter*, is laced with irony.

**Admissions.** A magical lottery for enclave-sponsored children; since the London Enclave's coup, open to independent wizards (indies) — explicitly framed as cannon fodder so that enclave children have targets to outrun.

**Faction.** Enclave vs. independent; language tracks (students slot into Mandarin, Spanish, Hindi, English, etc. cohorts based on their training mages' native languages, which affects access to spell libraries); alliance clusters formed in junior year for the graduation run. **Alliance-making IS faction-making**, and Novik drops the pretence that factions are essential rather than political.

**Curriculum.** Shop, alchemy, incantation, history, mundane subjects — but the defining pedagogy is that the school *gives each student spells it thinks they need*, and El, the narrator, is offered apocalyptic spells because her destiny is mass destruction. **Pedagogy is characterisation; curriculum is prophecy.** Homework is plot.

**Stakes of failure.** Death, obvious. But Novik's real innovation is moral stakes: El could survive trivially by drawing *malia* — magic from unwilling living beings — and refuses. The Scholomance is therefore not just a survival test; it's a temptation test.

**Romantic dynamics.** El Higgins and Orion Lake: the bully's mirror. Orion is the school's hero, killing mals for everyone (rather than leaving enemies for the weak). He's universally beloved; El resents him on principle, discovers he's socially disabled because adults trained him as a weapon, and falls in love with him as an act of seeing-past-use-value. It's study-partner-to-lovers, rival-to-lovers, and inverted chosen-one romance simultaneously.

**Craft lesson.** **Remove the adults and the stakes become social.** Without teachers, the political ecology of students *is* the plot. Also: pedagogy as life-or-death makes every classroom scene narratively active. Every spell El learns is either a weapon, a trap, or a clue.

### The Little Palace / Grishaverse — Leigh Bardugo

**Distinctive setting.** The Ravkan royal palace's Grisha training facility, attached to (and subservient to) the Crown. Part school, part barracks, part court. Grisha (magical soldiers) are discovered, tested, trained, and deployed into the Second Army. This is state-run magical conscription dressed in finery.

**Admissions.** Testing by itinerant examiners who tour villages with silver probes identifying latent Grisha. Alina is tested early, suppresses her power, and is only identified in her late teens — the premise of *Shadow and Bone*.

**Faction.** Three **Orders** with sub-disciplines: Corporalki (body — Heartrenders, Healers) in red kefta; Etherealki (summoners — Squallers, Tidemakers, Inferni, Sun Summoners) in blue; Materialki (fabricators — Alkemi, Durasts) in purple. Kefta colour is identity, instantly legible. Within each order, prestige hierarchies pit Heartrenders against Healers, Squallers against Tidemakers.

**Curriculum.** Small Science ("like calls to like"), combat, etiquette; specialised training per Order. Education is tightly woven with indoctrination — the Darkling's Second Army is the destination for all students.

**Stakes of failure.** Political and ethical rather than immediately lethal during training. Failure to conform risks being made an *oprichnik* (enforcer) or worse.

**Romantic dynamics.** The Darkling is a headmaster-figure weaponised as love interest — a **cautionary student-teacher archetype**. He is centuries old, holds absolute institutional power over Alina's life, and the narrative uses their romance to horrify rather than celebrate. When Alina feels drawn to him, Bardugo makes clear readers are watching a grooming operation. This is the craft-correct version of the trope: the romance is *legible* as coercion even before the characters name it.

**Craft lesson.** If you're writing a powerful mentor figure who has romantic charge with a student, **the text must know what the reader knows**. Bardugo lets Alina enjoy the Darkling's attention and marks it as danger simultaneously; the two acts are not contradictory.

### Vampire Academy — Richelle Mead

**Distinctive setting.** St. Vladimir's Academy, training ground for Moroi (living vampires), Dhampirs (half-human guardians), and the enemy Strigoi (undead). Class and race are inseparable: Moroi rule, Dhampirs serve.

**Admissions.** Inherited species; royal and non-royal Moroi families send children; Dhampirs train as bodyguards.

**Faction.** Species + royal Moroi clans. The class politics are foregrounded — Rose Hathaway is a Dhampir, therefore a servant-in-training to her best friend Lissa, the royal Moroi she protects.

**Curriculum.** Combat training (for Dhampirs), elemental magic (for Moroi), guardianship ethics, royal comportment. Year-by-year progression with field training in later books.

**Stakes of failure.** Expulsion to the margins of Moroi society; as the series advances, Strigoi attack becomes lethal stakes.

**Romantic dynamics.** The defining **student-teacher romance handled well**. Rose/Dimitri Belikov: Dimitri is her combat instructor, seven years older, Dhampir. Mead spends five books on the ethics. They do not consummate while he's her instructor in a classroom sense; she ages up; the professional relationship ends before the romance fully begins; her agency is foregrounded; his internal conflict is narrated, not hand-waved. When Dimitri becomes Strigoi in book three, the series explicitly stages their relationship as traumatic before romantic resolution is permitted.

**Craft lesson.** Student-teacher can work, but only if the author pays the cost: the teacher must stop being the teacher before the consummation, the text must acknowledge the power imbalance in-dialogue, and the ethics must be argued, not assumed. See Section 3.

### Harry Potter — the legacy influence

Hogwarts is the foundation modern academy romantasy builds on **and revolts against**. What it codified:
- **The Sorting** as destiny-revelation scene
- **Four-house architecture** as personality cosmology
- **Headmaster-with-secrets** (Dumbledore)
- **Forbidden Forest** as wild zone adjacent to civilised space
- **Restricted Section** as institutional secrecy made manifest
- **Prefects, Head Boy/Girl, student hierarchy**
- **Quidditch** as magic-school sport archetype
- **Triwizard Tournament** as high-stakes academic event
- **Hogsmeade** as village-beyond-the-gates
- **The Great Feast** as ritual punctuation
- **The headmaster's death** as Act II capstone
- **The inevitability of return** (every book begins with boarding the Hogwarts Express)

What academy romantasy rejects:
- **Chaste childhood.** Hogwarts is almost asexual until Goblet. Romantasy starts at 18+, puts sex on the page, and treats desire as primary engine.
- **British boarding-school cosiness.** Yarros, Peckham, and Novik write military, Gothic, or dark-academic settings where the school is dangerous by design, not accidentally.
- **The moral headmaster.** Dumbledore is flawed but benevolent. The Darkling, Atlas Blakely, General Sorrengail, Professor Orion in Zodiac Academy are ambiguous or outright villainous. The modern academy headmaster is a political actor with an agenda.
- **Essentialist sorting.** Modern romantasy is suspicious of personality-as-destiny; protagonists often refuse or subvert their sorting.
- **Happy graduation.** Hogwarts students graduate into careers. Basgiath students graduate into a war front. Scholomance students may not graduate at all.
- **The reading of Rowling's author politics.** The current romantasy community largely distances itself from Rowling, which accelerates the reframing of what Hogwarts represents.

**Craft lesson.** Know which Hogwarts elements you're using, which you're refusing, and which you're inverting. The reader knows Hogwarts; the reader is checking your work against it.

### Ninth House / Hell Bent — Leigh Bardugo

**Distinctive setting.** Yale's real secret societies (Skull and Bones, Scroll and Key, Book and Snake, etc.) reimagined as practicing working magic, with a ninth house — **Lethe** — tasked with policing the other eight. Alex Stern, a trauma survivor who sees ghosts, is recruited to Lethe.

**Admissions.** Tap-on-the-shoulder plus magical aptitude. Alex's seeing-ghosts ability makes her valuable; her trauma makes her exploitable.

**Faction.** Eight houses, each with a signature magical speciality (prognostication, portals, glamour, etc.), plus Lethe as oversight. Each house is identifiable by crest, ritual, endowment — faction-as-brand, which Bardugo leans into because Yale's real societies are brands.

**Curriculum.** Alex learns by necessity; there's no formal education structure. The magical crimes she investigates are her syllabus.

**Stakes of failure.** Alex is on probation; failure returns her to a fractured home and criminal charges. Institutional failure means ghosts crossing the veil.

**Romantic dynamics.** Low-heat by romantasy standards; Bardugo is writing dark academia with thriller pacing. The Alex/Darlington dynamic is slow-burn rescue-romance with heavy guilt overlay.

**Craft lesson.** **Base institutions in real-world analogues to get worldbuilding for free.** Bardugo's Yale does half her atmosphere work. For romantasy, note that thriller-pacing and slow-burn romance can coexist if the external stakes are dense enough.

### Babel — R.F. Kuang

**Distinctive setting.** The Royal Institute of Translation at Oxford, 1830s. Silver bars engraved with translation pairs — a word in one language, its inexact equivalent in another — power the British Empire. Translation is magic; imperial knowledge extraction is literal. The tower is a character.

**Admissions.** Recruitment from colonised regions: Robin Swift (Cantonese), Ramy (Calcutta), Victoire (Haiti), and Letty (English aristocrat). The empire farms colonies for native speakers; children are taken young and raised bilingual for silverwork.

**Faction.** The cohort of four; the broader Oxford class structure; the clandestine Hermes Society (anti-imperial resisters).

**Curriculum.** Classical languages, etymology, translation theory, silverworking. **Every class is plot.** When Professor Lovell teaches Robin about Chinese–English match-pairs, the lesson is both how to kill Lovell and why Lovell must die.

**Stakes of failure.** Removal from Oxford means return to colonised poverty. Failure to silverwork means the empire runs out of magic. The final stakes are revolutionary.

**Romantic dynamics.** Muted. The book is a platonic-love story among the four — Ramy/Robin especially — and Kuang chooses solidarity over romance. **This is deliberate:** the colonial violence is too grave for a conventional romance arc to bear.

**Craft lesson.** Pedagogy can **be** the magic system. Make what is taught indistinguishable from what is at stake. Also: not every romantasy-adjacent academy needs a central romance; thematic density can do the work.

### Indie / self-published academy romantasy

BookTok has created an enormous market for indie academy books. Patterns identifiable from 2023–2025 search trends, BookTok compilations, and the Film 14 / FanBolt / Bank Square Books round-ups:

- **Reverse harem academies** dominate. *Her Guardians* by G. Bailey, the Supernatural Academy series by Jaymin Eve, the Elemental Academy series by Michelle Madow, Nocturne Academy by Amelia Hutchins, Frostfire Academy, Her Fae Protectors, etc. Structural formula: heroine enters an academy with three-to-five love interests, each representing a faction or order; relationship develops toward polyamory; antagonist is usually a patriarchal council or prophetic doom.
- **Fae academies** are a sub-genre. *Heavenly Bodies* (2025 BookTok sensation, Greek-myth and shadow magic), *Quicksilver* by Callie Hart (2024), *Phantasma* by Kaylie Smith, *Powerless* by Lauren Roberts (academy-adjacent). Setting sells the book before the plot does.
- **Bully-academy hybrids.** Zodiac Academy popularised this and spawned imitators; many indie series open with a pool-drowning, car-crash, or public-humiliation hazing scene.
- **Chosen-one-in-denial academies.** The heroine arrives not knowing her heritage, is identified as Special, acquires love interests who were raised to serve or destroy her.
- **Academy of the Apocalypse / Night Elves / Supernatural Bullies and Beasts (Peckham's broader catalogue)** — indie serial approach, high book count, rapid release cadence, continuous cliffhangers.

**Craft lessons from indie patterns.** Speed-to-hook: many indie academies are *on the parapet* (literally or figuratively) within the first chapter. Trope legibility: readers know the formula and want it delivered with variation, not replacement. Cover/title grammar: "X Academy" plus dark aesthetic sells before the reader opens the book. Serialised cliffhangers train reader addiction across 6–10 book series.

### Adjacent touchstones

- **The Magicians by Lev Grossman (Brakebills).** Pedagogy-heavy, Discipline system (students' magic specialises into focused subfields), written as literary fantasy critique of Hogwarts. Craft value: Discipline as a worked example of forced specialisation that replaces house sorting.
- **Carry On by Rainbow Rowell (Watford).** Simon/Baz rival-to-lovers is a direct response to Drarry fanfic culture. Demonstrates how shared-dorm roommate-to-lovers functions when both parties are supposed to hate each other and the school structure forces proximity.
- **A Study in Drowning by Emily Lloyd-Jones.** Dark academia with folklore undertone; short, atmospheric.
- **The Name of the Wind by Patrick Rothfuss (the University).** Pedagogy done rigorously: Kvothe's studies in sympathy and naming are load-bearing. Demonstrates "specialisation narrows the magic system."
- **Xiran Jay Zhao.** Worth naming the honest limit: Zhao's *Iron Widow* is not academy romantasy but mecha/political YA. *Zachary Ying and the Dragon Emperor* is middle-grade adventure. Including Zhao in the academy canon is a miscategorisation; the craft influence is more diffuse (genre-busting Asian fantasy that remade commercial expectations for 2020s romantasy-adjacent fiction).

### Cross-cutting patterns — what modern academy romantasy does that Hogwarts doesn't

1. **Admissions are violent.** Crossings, cullings, lotteries, sacrifices. The gate is the first conflict.
2. **Graduation is a ticking clock, often a death sentence.** Deployment, initiation-by-murder, the graduation hall.
3. **Headmasters and professors are untrustworthy by default.**
4. **Institutions serve explicit political agendas.** Military, imperial, aristocratic.
5. **Mortality is on-page.** Named characters die; the school is a filter.
6. **Sex is on-page.** Desire is primary engine, not subtext.
7. **Morally grey love interests.** The reader is trusted to hold contradictions.
8. **Factions are political, not merely temperamental.**
9. **Chosen-one tropes are interrogated, not celebrated.**
10. **The school is sometimes alive, or haunted, or sentient.**

## Section 2 — Core academy worldbuilding elements

### Admissions systems

**Lottery / induction.** Scholomance assigns students by a system the school itself operates; no parent controls the date of induction. Creates a shared trauma for the cohort — no one chose to be there. In indie academy romantasy, "I got my acceptance letter" scenes remain the default opener despite being a Rowling borrowing.

**Inherited / legacy.** Babel recruits from colonised regions where empire trains children for silverwork. Ninth House taps students whose family wealth or utility makes them useful. Vampire Academy simply requires species membership. Legacy systems generate *class* conflict — Draco Malfoy, Darius Acrux, Callum Nova all embody the entitled-legacy archetype.

**Testing / manifestation.** Grisha examiners tour Ravka with silver probes; Brakebills in *The Magicians* administers a written-and-practical exam so difficult the candidates don't remember taking it. Harry Potter's letter is magical-detection based. Testing systems produce "we found you" narratives and the protagonist's double consciousness (I am ordinary / I am extraordinary).

**Prophetic / marked selection.** Fourth Wing conscripts marked ones by edict. Prophetic selection is the purest form of agency-theft and therefore useful for romance: the protagonist didn't choose the institution and owes it nothing.

**Tournament qualification.** Less common, more associated with sports or combat systems. Works when the story wants competitive stratification built into admission.

**Conscription.** Basgiath is the flagship. Combined with prophetic marking (the "marked ones"), conscription delivers the adult conscript-against-her-will protagonist that contemporary romantasy loves.

Craft principle: **your admissions system is your protagonist's first political stance**. Did she choose to be here? Was she forced? Did she lie her way in? The answer colours every subsequent scene.

### House / faction / quadrant systems

A faction system works when each faction has distinct (i) **value system**, (ii) **aesthetic**, (iii) **methodology**, (iv) **blind spot**, (v) **internal prestige hierarchy**, (vi) **stereotype vs. truth gap**.

- **Hogwarts** codified four-house trait essentialism (Gryffindor brave, Slytherin cunning, Ravenclaw clever, Hufflepuff loyal). The system is criticised for flattening — real people carry all four, and the Sorting as Destiny pretends personality is legible at 11.
- **Fourth Wing** has four quadrants but runs them as prestige castes (Riders high, the rest low). The Riders get all the methodology; the other three quadrants read as demographic facts.
- **Divergent's factions** (Dauntless, Erudite, Amity, Candor, Abnegation) demonstrate what happens when factions become totalising: the novel's own thesis is that the faction system is incoherent, which is both the point and the weakness — the worldbuilding undoes itself.
- **Grisha orders** work because they're built on a magic-system logic (body / elemental / material) rather than on personality. Colour-coded kefta make them instantly legible; the subdivisions (Heartrender vs. Healer) generate internal prestige.
- **Atlas Six** uses six specialisations that are each a distinct *methodology* — how a naturalist gathers knowledge differs structurally from how an empath or physicist does.
- **Zodiac Academy** layers zodiac signs and Orders, which creates combinatorial identity (Darius is a Leo Dragon, Tory a Gemini Phoenix). Combinatorial faction systems produce micro-tribes.
- **Ninth House's eight societies** succeed because Bardugo bases them on real Yale societies with documented history — the worldbuilding is free.

Craft principle: **make one faction the protagonist doesn't fit, and make it the one she's assigned to.** The friction between sorted-identity and self-identity is the psychological engine.

### Curriculum structure across years

Year one typically covers: the magic system's basics, history, combat or physical skills, a survey course in everything. Senior years specialise. Forbidden knowledge (locked archives, classified spells, restricted sections) appears as foreshadowing in year one and as climax payoff by year three.

- **Hogwarts** runs seven years, with OWLs and NEWTs as checkpoints.
- **Fourth Wing** runs three years (first, second, third) with the Gauntlet in year one, War Games in year two, deployment in year three.
- **Scholomance** runs four years (freshman through senior). Senior year is graduation preparation.
- **Brakebills** (*The Magicians*) runs five years with Discipline selection mid-stream.
- **Babel** operates on the Oxford undergraduate-then-research-fellowship model.
- **Atlas Six** compresses education into a single-year initiation.

Capstones: Triwizard, War Games, graduation-hall run, initiation ritual, fellowship thesis, Grisha deployment, senior prank that's a magical duel. The capstone is where pedagogy becomes plot.

Craft principle: **year structure is pacing.** A seven-year curriculum lets you write seven books. A one-year initiation compresses everything. Pick the length that matches the arc you want to write.

### Faculty hierarchy

- **Dean / Headmaster.** Dumbledore (warm but manipulative), Atlas Blakely (warm but conspiratorial), General Sorrengail (cold, the protagonist's mother), the Darkling (charismatic, deceiving). The headmaster is almost always an antagonist or an ambivalent ally; in contemporary romantasy, they are nearly never straightforwardly heroic.
- **Heads of house / quadrant.** McGonagall, Snape, etc. — figures who embody a house's values. In modern work these become squad commanders, signet-bonded officers, Grisha masters.
- **Professors.** Defence Against the Dark Arts as the "rotating slot" where new antagonists are introduced annually. The "one cool mentor" trope: Lupin, Dimitri Belikov, Professor Lovell (inverted), Baba Yaga/Mama Aleksander. The mentor who sees the protagonist's true power and names it.
- **Secret faculty societies.** Lethe's oversight role in Ninth House; the Alexandrian Society's initiated fellows; the Death Eater professors at Hogwarts in the war years. Faculty who have their own conspiracy parallel to the school's.

Craft principle: **give your headmaster a secret the protagonist will discover in book two or three.** The secret is the character's load-bearing beam.

### Tournaments, trials, tests, graduation stakes

- **Triwizard (Harry Potter):** model of the interscholastic tournament — each champion represents an institution; tasks escalate; a death changes the tone.
- **Fourth Wing's Threshing** (dragons choosing riders; students who are rejected die — incinerated by dragons), **Gauntlet** (obstacle course with magical hazards), **War Games** (squad combat). Threshing is selection; Gauntlet is ordeal; War Games are training-with-violence.
- **Scholomance graduation hall.** The single most consequential set-piece in the genre: a corridor of mals that students must run through. What makes it work is that it's *predictable*, *prepared for*, and *designed to be gamed.* Students form alliances, stockpile mana, practice routes. The plot of book two is collectively reinventing how graduation works.
- **Grisha summoning demonstrations.** Public performance of power; political theatre.
- **Babel's translation exams.** Intellectual trials with political weight — a translation pair can kill.
- **Ninth House observations.** Students witness a ritual and must intervene if it goes wrong.

Craft principle: **a trial scene lands when the test reveals character AND changes power relations.** Violet crossing the Parapet reveals her physical fragility AND establishes her as a rider; Harry's Triwizard first task establishes his broomwork AND places him in the tournament spotlight; El refusing malia under pressure reveals her ethics AND weakens her position in her alliance.

### Physical infrastructure

Dorms: Hogwarts common rooms (house-coded, fireplace, warmth), Scholomance cell-rooms (narrow, dangerous, moving), Basgiath barracks (shared, military, public), Little Palace chambers (ornate, surveilled), Atlas Six London manor (domestic, six-bedroom, intimate). **Dorm type predicts romance type.** Shared dorms produce forced-proximity; private rooms produce clandestine meeting; common rooms produce semi-public tension.

Cafeterias / halls: the Great Hall as ritual space; Basgiath's mess as class-segregated; Atlas Six's communal dinners as civilised theatre of conflict; Scholomance's cafeteria where food itself is magically dangerous.

Towers, dungeons, fields: the tower (knowledge/power/isolation — Ravenclaw, Babel), the dungeon (secrecy, threat — Slytherin, Vampire Academy), the flight field (Fourth Wing, Quidditch pitch), the training yard (combat — Vampire Academy, Basgiath), the library (always, and always with restricted sections).

Craft principle: **design space to generate scenes.** If you need a confession scene, design a tower. If you need a brawl, design a training yard. The architecture is a script.

### The forbidden library / restricted section

The trope works because it **physicalises institutional secrecy**. Rules mark the boundary; breaking rules reveals plot. The Hogwarts restricted section; the Scholomance reading rooms where senior seminar books literally bite; Babel's silver workrooms; the Atlas Six reading room with The Book at its centre; Vampire Academy's classified Moroi histories; the forbidden section at Brakebills where Dean Fogg keeps the Niffin records.

Deploy it by: making the boundary legible (a locked door, a gated stack, a ward), making the breach consequential (detention, ghost-manifestation, discovery of a conspiracy), and making the content **both useful and forbidden because it is useful** — the knowledge the institution hides is the knowledge that could unmake it.

### Secret societies and student politics

Skull and Bones is the real-world ur-source: Yale, 1832, fifteen seniors tapped per year, alumni network weaponised across American institutions. Bardugo's Ninth House is direct homage; Blake's Alexandrian Society is a continental European cousin; Zodiac Academy's Heirs are aristocratic-dynastic; the Death Eaters function retroactively as a Hogwarts student society gone terrorist.

Student government, prefects, head students, squad leaders — visible political structure. What makes secret societies *work* narratively: **tiered knowledge**. The protagonist learns the society exists in act one, sees its rituals in act two, learns its true purpose in act three. Each tier raises stakes.

### Hazing, bullying, attrition, death tolls

This is the axis where contemporary academy romantasy most diverges from Hogwarts. Fourth Wing's parapet casualties, Scholomance's 75% cumulative mortality, Zodiac Academy's violent first-week hazing, Vampire Academy's training-room injuries, Babel's expulsion-as-deportation-back-to-colonised-poverty.

The line between grimdark and gratuitous is roughly: **does the death change something about the survivors, or is it a set decoration?** Yarros has been criticised for casualty inflation without character cost; Novik is praised because every Scholomance death affects an alliance, a mourning, or a plan. The Zodiac Academy hazing is the most contested example — some readers see it as genuinely escalating stakes, others see it as torture porn because the abusers become love interests.

Craft principle for bullying culture: **if the bully is a love interest, the on-page violence must be survivable and accountable.** If you show him waterboarding her in chapter two, you are writing a redemption arc that requires at least half the book. Plan accordingly or excise the scene.

### What graduates become

Basgiath → officers deployed to the Poromielian border or (for the marked ones) worse postings. Hogwarts → Ministry, Auror corps, private careers. Babel → imperial translation service. Alexandrian Society → lifelong initiated fellows with esoteric wealth. Scholomance → enclave membership or independent precarity. Grisha → Second Army. Vampire Academy → guardians for Moroi royals or royal duties.

**The post-graduation destination is the romance's final obstacle.** Xaden and Violet face deployment. El and Orion face enclave-vs-independent paths. Alina faces leadership of an army. Robin Swift faces revolutionary exile. Where the graduates go shapes what the couple must fight to stay together.

## Section 3 — Romantic dynamics unique to academy settings

### Cross-faction / cross-house forbidden romance

The Romeo-and-Juliet-in-uniform dynamic: institutional structures make the romance treasonous. Draco/Hermione fanon (popularised but non-canonical) taught a generation of romantasy readers to crave cross-house pairings. Simon/Baz in Rainbow Rowell's *Carry On* runs on Chosen One / Vampire Heir rivalry. Alina/Darkling crosses Grisha/royal. Xaden/Violet crosses Rider-rebel-family/Rider-loyalist-family within the same quadrant, which is structurally interesting because the *same* institution produces enemies.

Mechanics: the setting generates **oath conflicts** (whose side are you sworn to?), **divided loyalty** (telling one secret to protect the other), **betrayal stakes** (when one reports on the other), and **cover-identity dread** (what if your friends discovered who you are sleeping with?). Cross-faction romance is load-bearing for plot because every public appearance becomes a scene.

### Roommate-to-lovers, study-partner-to-lovers, rival-to-lovers

The academy makes forced proximity **non-consensual** from the characters' perspective: they didn't pick their dorm, their squad, their lab partner. The romantic tension is therefore guilt-free for the reader — the institution is responsible for the collision.

- **Roommate-to-lovers.** Small rooms; accidental intimacy; the changing-into-pyjamas scene; the nightmare-woken-by-your-roommate scene. Rhiannon/Violet as friendship-in-quarters; Fourth Wing squad arrangements; Brakebills co-ed dorms.
- **Study-partner-to-lovers.** Shared problem sets; the hand-brushes-hand moment; the after-hours library; El and Orion's reluctant alliance in Scholomance.
- **Rival-to-lovers.** Competition for academic standing (Libby/Nico in Atlas Six), for captaincy (Xaden/Violet's early tension), for Quidditch/Pitball placement. The rivalry provides plausibility: they had to engage with each other anyway.

Craft principle: **forced proximity is the scaffolding; desire is the framing.** The setting puts them in the same room; the writing makes it electric.

### Student-teacher taboo

Honest reckoning. This is the trope with the most active community discourse as of 2024–2025. Boundaries roughly agreed on by craft-conscious romance discourse (per BookTok, Reddit r/romantasy, LitReactor, Ex-Puritan essays, She Reads Romance Books blog, and multiple author-interviewed sources):

**What reads as well-handled:**
- Rose Hathaway / Dimitri Belikov (*Vampire Academy*). Acknowledged power gap; the romance is named as forbidden in-text; consummation occurs after Dimitri's professional authority is suspended; Rose's agency is foregrounded; other characters confront the pair about it.
- Alina / the Darkling (*Shadow and Bone*), which is handled as *horror*, not aspiration. The narrative's frame is essential; the character named the coercion.
- Darcy Vega / Orion (Zodiac Academy), which readers divide on — some find the power gap handled by making Orion nearly the same age and ultimately bound to Darcy rather than in authority over her, others find the secrecy-and-desire framing normalising.

**What reads as abuse (from contemporary craft discourse):**
- Any pairing where the teacher uses grades or institutional access as leverage.
- Any pairing where the student is younger than consent age or the power differential is not addressed in-text.
- Any pairing where the relationship is framed as aspirational without interrogation.

**The age-gap complication.** A 4–6 year gap between a 21-year-old graduate student and a 26-year-old TA is a different ethical beast from a 200-year-old immortal mentor and an 18-year-old Grisha. The immortal-mentor trope ("centuries old but looks 25") is now widely discussed as potentially using age-cosmetic to smuggle in grooming dynamics. The craft-conscious solution: make the age gap mean something *in the character's behaviour* — not just the aesthetic.

**Craft principles:**
1. The teacher must stop being the teacher before consummation.
2. The text must acknowledge the power differential in dialogue.
3. Another character — friend, parent, institutional figure — must name the ethical problem.
4. The student's agency must be visible on page.
5. Consent cannot be contingent on a magical bond that overrides choice, or the bond itself must be interrogated as a coercion mechanism.
6. The romance must survive the removal of the power gap, not depend on it.

The Ex-Puritan essay "An Education" and Michael Forman's *Ethics of Teacher-Student Romantic Relationships* are useful craft references: both argue the real ethical concern is not age but *lasting professional leverage* — a lover should not be able to assign you grades or end your career.

### Forced proximity through curriculum

Training partners (Fourth Wing squad), paired research (Atlas Six physicist twins), shared thesis, laboratory partners, dorm assignments — the institution engineers collision. Plot implication: **every pairing the protagonist is forced into should do plot work.** A training partner who only trains is wasted; a training partner who also holds a secret, runs a competing faction, or is the love interest's rival is doing three jobs.

### Competition / tournament romance

Fourth Wing's War Games put Xaden and Violet on the same side *within* the competition and in conflict *outside* it; Triwizard's task-partnership between Harry and Cedric is platonic but runs on the same mechanic. Protect-the-rival dynamics: the lover must outperform the beloved to survive the tournament but fail to outperform them far enough to draw institutional suspicion. The arithmetic is load-bearing.

### Graduation as ticking clock

Last-semester urgency. Deployment-after-graduation separation. The certainty that after graduation the lovers will be sent to different postings creates compressed romance in the final book of a series. Yarros deploys this (book four of Empyrean is expected to feature deployment tension); Mead deploys it in the fifth Vampire Academy book; it's the structural engine of the "one more summer" beat that romance readers crave.

### Bully romance in academy contexts

The current contested territory. Zodiac Academy is the flagship. The defence (per Peckham interviews and fan forums on Fandom/Reddit): Darius is also abused by his father Lionel, the Vegas return violence with violence, the narrative does not frame the bullying as admirable. The critique (per ejbookblog series review, multiple Reddit r/romantasy threads, BookTube reviews): the violence is too severe and too early for the redemption arc to support, and the mate-bond reframes coerced reconciliation as romance.

**The craft distinction between enemies-to-lovers and abuse-to-lovers:**
- **Enemies-to-lovers** pits the pair on opposite sides of an *external* conflict (house, faction, nation, mission) where they have roughly equal power to harm each other. They may fight; they may insult; the fight is *matched*. Think Simon/Baz in *Carry On*, Xaden/Violet, Libby/Nico.
- **Abuse-to-lovers** places one party with institutional or physical power that they use to harm the other, without genuine risk of retaliation. The power is not equalised within the relationship. Think the first two books of Zodiac Academy — Darius as Heir, Tory as outsider princess with no power allies. The story can become enemies-to-lovers only when the power evens out.

**Craft principles for writing a genuinely antagonistic love interest without romanticising abuse:**
1. **Equal power, or power that equalises rapidly.** The protagonist must be able to hurt back, not just survive.
2. **Acknowledgment of harm in-text.** The abuser's internal monologue or dialogue must name what they did as wrong, not just as "complicated."
3. **A genuine atonement arc, not just a change of heart.** Atonement costs something — rank, friendship, safety.
4. **Narrative pushback from other characters.** Someone whose judgment the reader trusts must say, *this was cruel.*
5. **Consent issues foregrounded.** Sex following violence must not paper over the violence.
6. **The abuse cannot be the foundation of the romance.** If you cannot tell the love story without the early cruelty, you are telling a trauma bond, not a romance.

The Colleen Hoover discourse (not academy, but romance-adjacent) taught the community to ask *whose POV are we in during the violence, and whose POV are we in during the reconciliation?* If the victim's POV is only granted to forgive, the framing is coercive.

### Year-gap romance

Senior/junior dynamics: Xaden/Violet (he's a third-year; she's first), Rose/Lissa (same year, different species), transfer students who arrive with backstory (often senior-level), repeat years (Orion Lake's strange relationship to time in Scholomance book two). The year gap provides *authority without institutional power* — the senior knows the ropes, can warn/protect/threaten, but is not grading the junior. Safer territory than student-teacher, and narratively rich.

## Section 4 — The "school with no actual teaching" problem

The recurring critique: academy fantasy shows so little pedagogy that by book three, the reader cannot name a single class the protagonist attended. Yarros's Empyrean is regularly accused of this from book two onward; Harry Potter was accused of it in every year except Remus Lupin's Defence class; most indie reverse-harem academies skip to the romance and set classes offscreen.

### What works

**Scholomance.** Curriculum is life-or-death. Every spell El is offered by the sentient school is characterisation (it sees her as a future maleficer) and plot (the spells become weapons she refuses to use). Every classroom scene is either an allocation (she's given a text), a temptation (the text is dangerous), or a negotiation (she trades with classmates for safer allocations). Novik treats homework as a resource-management subsystem and makes pedagogy the game.

**Babel.** Translation theory is the magic. When Professor Playfair lectures on the etymology of *tr-anslate* as "carried across," the lecture is both the magic-system-reveal and the colonial indictment and the set-up for the final act's translation-pair destruction. Pedagogy **is** the thematic argument.

**Fourth Wing (book one).** Flight training, signet manifestation, the Gauntlet — Yarros shows the work in the first book. The Gauntlet is the clearest example: a sequence of obstacles that require both physical training and the rider's magical signet manifestation. Every training scene is a setup for a later deployment. Critics contend book two and three abandon this structure in favour of action-sequence pacing.

**Atlas Six.** Each character's research is their arc. Parisa's telepathy lessons teach the reader how her power works and seed her later mind-invasions of her rivals. Reina's botany classes seed her arc with the school itself. The specialised research lets every class be plot-unique to one character.

**The Name of the Wind.** Kvothe's sympathy class, his lute lessons, his Archives research — Rothfuss spends chapters on mastery of skill, and every lesson is set-up for a scene chapters later. The pedagogy is slow but load-bearing.

**Harry Potter (arguably).** Defended mostly by lesson-cameos: Expelliarmus in Duelling Club (book two) pays off through all seven books; the Patronus lesson from Lupin becomes Harry's defining spell. Even when most lessons are flavour, the significant lessons are plot seeds.

### Craft principles

1. **Every class scene must do one of four things:** teach a skill the character uses within 50 pages, reveal character, seed conflict, or establish stakes. If it does none, cut it.
2. **Pedagogy as characterisation.** What the character struggles with tells the reader who they are. Violet is small and fragile, so her flight training reveals her ingenuity; El is powerful and ethical, so her spell allocation reveals her moral structure.
3. **The specialisation principle.** A narrow magic system, revealed through specific pedagogy, is more vivid than a broad magic system handled in broad strokes.
4. **Pedagogy as metaphor.** Babel teaches translation because the book is about colonial violence. Scholomance teaches spell-hoarding because the book is about scarcity ethics. Let the syllabus *argue* something.
5. **Avoid flavour pedagogy.** If the protagonist attends Arithmancy, Ancient Runes, and Divination but nothing that happens in those classes affects anything else, you have decorated the school at the expense of inhabiting it.
6. **Let failure be instructive.** A failed exam, a mislearned spell, a misunderstood text — failure is pedagogy's sharpest tool because it reveals both the content and the character.
7. **Have the protagonist teach someone once.** The act of explaining forces the character (and the author) to articulate what the magic actually is. Hermione teaching Harry Accio; El tutoring freshmen in *The Last Graduate*; Nico coaching Libby.

## Section 5 — Institutional politics

### Who funds the school

- **Basgiath.** The Navarrian crown. The war with Poromiel is the budget. General Sorrengail sits near the apex of military funding. When the wardstone fails and the venin war opens, the institution's finances become the empire's finances.
- **Babel.** The British Empire via silver trade. Oxford's Royal Institute of Translation is simultaneously an academic institution and the central bank of imperial magic.
- **The Alexandrian Society.** Self-funded endowment, secret wealth accumulated across millennia, concealed inside Wrenfare-scale fortunes and older quiet money. Funding is the first mystery the initiates investigate.
- **Scholomance.** Collectively funded by enclaves in an uneasy cartel. Seats are apportioned by enclave contribution; London coup opened slots for independents partly as cannon fodder.
- **Hogwarts.** Ministry oversight but largely autonomous, with founding endowment and alumni contributions. The financial ambiguity is why the Ministry's intervention in book five reads as a *takeover*.
- **The Little Palace.** Ravkan crown, though the Darkling's faction wields it like a private estate.

### Whose agenda does the school actually serve

Beneath the public mission is always the real mission. Basgiath claims to train defenders; it conscripts the children of executed rebels as expendable officers. Babel claims to translate; it strip-mines colonised languages for magical capital. The Alexandrian Society claims to preserve knowledge; it demands human sacrifice for initiation and operates as a knowledge-gatekeeping cartel. Scholomance claims to protect; it functions as a lottery where some children survive and others are fed to mals. Little Palace claims to uplift Grisha; it weaponises them for the royal wars.

Craft principle: **the public mission and the real mission must be legibly distinct for plot to work.** The protagonist's arc is often the journey from believing the first to confronting the second.

### Political factions in the student body and faculty

Basgiath: marked ones vs. loyalists vs. ordinary conscripts; senior officers aligned to the crown vs. officers with rebel sympathies. Atlas Six: backers who sent each initiate (corporate Wrenfare via Libby's family ties, governmental intelligence via Parisa, Society-internal via Tristan, independent drift via Reina). Babel: the four students from different colonised relationships to empire; faculty split between institutional loyalists and Hermes Society sympathisers. Hogwarts in the war years: pureblood supremacists, half-bloods, Muggle-borns; Death Eater faculty, Order of the Phoenix faculty, neutrals.

Craft principle: **a student body without factions is decoration.** Factions inside the school are the institution's politics in miniature; give every major student a legible political position, even if they haven't named it yet.

### Is it a front for conscription or something darker

Often, yes. The most interesting academies *lie about what they are for*. Scholomance's enclave politics conceal that the school eats a fixed percentage of children every year — parents send children knowing this is a 25% mortality improvement on the 95% mortality of keeping them home. Atlas Six's initiation is a murder. Basgiath is a war-factory with a training programme.

This conversion — "the school I thought I attended is a grinder" — is one of the genre's defining plot moves. It can't be the twist if it's underbaked; seed the truth from chapter one so the protagonist's slow realisation feels earned.

### Reputation if graduates fail or defect

The institution is a brand. When Xaden's separatist riders defect, Basgiath's political capital implodes. When Robin Swift strikes at the silverworks, Oxford loses its centrality. When Death Eater alumni are exposed, Hogwarts faces Ministry seizure. **Alumni defection is institutional crisis** — which means alumni are a third-act device. Know who graduated before book one; the graduates are the institution's collateral.

## Section 6 — Academy traditions, uniforms, rituals, games

### Uniforms as worldbuilding

Clothing is fastest characterisation. Hogwarts robes with house ties — students become legible as houses at thirty paces. Grisha kefta, colour-coded by order (red Corporalki, blue Etherealki, purple Materialki) with embroidered rank and distinction. Basgiath leathers — functional, military, with squad patches. Babel undergraduate gowns over street clothes — the academic uniform over the colonial body is its own image. Ninth House grey. Vampire Academy's tailored school uniforms.

Craft rule: **your uniform system answers three questions — what house, what rank, what role.** If it answers all three in a single glance, it is doing the work of ten pages of exposition.

### Rituals

Opening feast (Hogwarts), Parapet crossing (Basgiath), Threshing and dragon-bonding ceremony, oath-taking (Grisha oaths to tsar and Darkling), induction into the Alexandrian reading room. The Scholomance's conspicuous *absence* of an opening ritual — students simply appear in their cells on induction day — is itself a worldbuilding choice: the school is a trap, not a welcome.

### Games and sports

Quidditch as the archetype that every academy romantasy has to answer. Zodiac Academy's Pitball is a direct response (similarly fast-paced, magically aerial, with betting culture around it). Fourth Wing's War Games function as sport-plus-war-game. Brakebills' welters (a living-chess game) is a literary-fantasy answer. The key design principle: **the sport must be playable with the magic system, watchable without rules-knowledge, and dangerous enough to kill.**

### Coming-of-age ceremonies

First kill (Dhampirs, dragon riders), first summoning (Grisha), first bond (Fourth Wing dragon, fae mate bonds), signet manifestation (Empyrean). These are the romance trope's narrative midpoint — the character becomes *themselves* and the love interest sees it.

### Graduation traditions

Triwizard Cup, the graduation run, the Alexandrian initiation, commissioning ceremony. **Graduation is the genre's emotional climax** — survived, earned, or refused.

### Founder / patron lore

Hogwarts founders (Godric, Salazar, Rowena, Helga) and their foundational schism; Basgiath's imperial founding; Babel's 18th-century commission under George III; the Scholomance built by Sir Alfred Cooper Browning of Manchester Enclave in the 1880s; the Alexandrian Society's mythic founding with the (staged) destruction of the Great Library. **The founder's crime is usually the institution's original sin.**

### Songs, chants, in-group slang

Hogwarts school song. Grisha oaths. Basgiath's chants. In-quadrant terminology (Rider slang for dragons, scribes' archival shorthand). Slang is cheap, powerful worldbuilding — one or two consistent terms ("mal" in Scholomance, "signet" in Empyrean, "kefta" in Grishaverse) carries pages of exposition.

## Section 7 — Beyond-school worldbuilding

### The town beyond the gates

Hogsmeade is the archetype — a buffer zone where students are briefly free. Its absence is also meaningful: Scholomance has no town because Scholomance is not connected to the world except through the graduation gate. The village near Basgiath and the Oxford town around Babel do structural work: they are where students who cannot afford further loneliness find company; they are where faculty have homes; they are where the institution's spillover effects become visible.

### Families

Violet's mother General Lilith Sorrengail is both parent and institutional authority — the single most structurally consequential parent in current romantasy. Xaden's father's execution by her mother defines the star-crossed axis. Robin Swift's aunt Piper and the revealed Professor Lovell as father — the family secret is the imperial secret. Alex Stern's fractured home as absence that makes Lethe her chosen family. Draco's Malfoy Manor as legacy-politics. The Weasleys as warmth; the Malfoys as menace.

Craft principle: **the protagonist's family is the institution's shadow.** The family's values either align with the institution (she's legacy) or contest it (she's a rebel's daughter), and either makes plot.

### Break / holiday worldbuilding

Christmas at Hogwarts for the orphan-by-choice Harry; summer between Fourth Wing years; the notable *absence* of breaks in Scholomance (the characters are stuck in the school); Babel's long vacations during which the political organising happens; Brakebills' Fillory summer. Breaks are where character growth happens offscreen and reappears onscreen — the "summer they didn't see each other" trope.

### The outside world's view of the school

Prestige, fear, myth. The *Daily Prophet*'s Hogwarts coverage, Navarrian propaganda about Basgiath's heroism, Oxford's public-facing grandeur vs. Babel's secret silverworks, the Ravkan populace's ambivalent awe of the Little Palace. What the public *thinks* the school does is almost never what it does.

### Alumni networks

Skull and Bones as the real-world ur-network. Ninth House's Lethe alumni. The Death Eaters as a Hogwarts alumni network. Basgiath's veterans. The Alexandrian Society's initiated fellows. **Alumni are the institution's third act.** They fund it, protect it, and sometimes betray it. In romantasy, they are also the protagonist's future community.

### What happens during summer / off-season

Who stays (orphans, punished students, faculty on contract); who's sent home; training intensives; the empty school's rituals. The absent student is as significant as the present one.

### Homesickness as emotional engine

First-year homesickness is different from senior-year reluctance to leave. The academy as chosen family emerges through the friction between missing home and realising the school is now home. The love interest often becomes the anchor: *she* is his home now, and that substitution is the romance's emotional centrepiece.

### Parents who want students home vs. parents who sent them to die

Violet's mother sent her to die (knowingly; Lilith Sorrengail is the series' moral question). Robin's guardian *owned* him. The Malfoys' pride in Draco's school placement vs. the Weasleys' worry over every letter home. The Scholomance parents' cold mathematical calculation that 25% mortality in Scholomance beats 95% mortality at home. This axis — parental agency over the child's education — is one of the genre's most politically charged and most under-used by indie authors.

## Section 8 — Common academy clichés and how to subvert them

Each cliché below is followed by why it tires and **two-to-three actionable subversion strategies.**

### 1. The chosen one who's bad at everything except the one thing

Tires because it's over-used (Harry Potter, Fourth Wing, half of indie romantasy) and because the "bad at everything" framing is cosmetic — the character is always secretly good at what matters.
- **Subvert by making the "one thing" structurally useless without the ordinary skills they lack.** Violet's frailty makes her dragon-bond precarious; give your chosen-one a talent that requires other people's competence to activate.
- **Make the character genuinely incompetent, with real costs.** Show her failing, not her struggling-prettily.
- **Give her a rival who's better at the "one thing." Let her lose the face-off and have to find another way.**

### 2. The wise old headmaster with a secret

Dumbledore fatigue is real.
- **Kill the headmaster before book one and let the void be the mystery.** The school under crisis management.
- **Make the headmaster a mid-career striver with imposter syndrome** — the "wise" is performance.
- **Make the headmaster young and the protagonist's peer, complicating the authority**, as in some indie academies.

### 3. The mean girl clique

Flattening, sexist, and boring.
- **Give the "mean girls" actual motives that are legible as political strategy, not just personality.** Pansy Parkinson is a Slytherin protecting house prestige, not a catty stock.
- **Let the mean girl become an ally mid-book** without an apology scene — just competence recognised competence.
- **Swap the gender of the clique.** Mean boys are less clichéd and often more interesting.

### 4. The ancient prophecy discovered in year one

Wish-fulfilment structural cheat.
- **Make the prophecy a forgery or a misreading.** El's destiny-of-destruction in Scholomance is real but misunderstood.
- **Have the prophecy be about someone else and the protagonist is the mistake.**
- **Show the prophecy's author had a political motive.** It was a propaganda tool, not a cosmic truth.

### 5. The forbidden forest

Every school has one.
- **Put it above, not below or beyond.** A forbidden sky, a forbidden sea.
- **Make the forbidden zone part of the curriculum** — students are required to enter, and the forbiddance is cosmetic.
- **Remove it entirely.** Scholomance has no forest and suffers not at all.

### 6. The evil professor reveal

Quirrell-was-the-villain is now the expected twist.
- **Make the suspected professor actually be the hero** and the trusted one be the villain — without hiding the clues from rereaders.
- **Reveal that the professor is compromised rather than evil** — blackmail, family hostage.
- **Have the professor be the villain *and* the love interest's sibling*, and make the relationship rather than the reveal the crux.

### 7. The tri-faction / four-house sorting moment

Formula.
- **Let the protagonist refuse sorting.** Show what the institution does when its architecture is challenged.
- **Sort twice, with a contradiction.** Which reading is correct?
- **Remove the sorting and let students self-select**, as Atlas Six does — the initiates choose their subjects of impossibility.

### 8. The underfunded orphan beats the rich legacy student

Harry/Draco template.
- **Make the legacy student the better person**, without irony. Let her deserve her wealth because she *worked* within it.
- **Make the underfunded protagonist's poverty a consequence of a choice her parents made**, not a blameless misfortune, and let her have to forgive them.
- **Blur class. The legacy student is secretly broke; the orphan is secretly royal.** Explore the performance of each status.

### 9. The grumpy hot senior love interest

Xaden-archetype fatigue.
- **Make him genuinely grumpy, not decoratively grumpy.** Show him unpleasant to strangers, clumsy in friendship.
- **Give him a rival who is warm and equally compelling** — the love triangle as character test.
- **Make the grump expire.** He warms in book two; in book three, his grumpiness is his old trauma showing, not his personality.

### 10. The training session that's actually a date

Fine once; tired ten times.
- **Make the session genuinely about training and the attraction a cost of the training.** They both hate that they're attracted; the work suffers; the teacher calls them on it.
- **Have a third person train with them.** A trio training session changes the dynamics entirely.
- **Make the session public.** An audience changes everything.

### 11. The forbidden room / restricted section infiltration

Every book.
- **Let the infiltration fail.** The protagonist is caught and gains an unexpected ally among the faculty.
- **Make the forbidden content boring or useless.** The mystery is why the institution forbade it.
- **Have the restricted section be a trap** that catches students who break in — and the faculty are using the catches for something worse.

### 12. Exams that are actually life-threatening trials

Triwizard fatigue.
- **Make the trial bureaucratic.** Paperwork, committee review, plagiarism accusations. The violence is institutional, not physical.
- **Make survival the point but the test graded on *how* the student survived.** Did they sacrifice an ally? Did they use forbidden magic?
- **Give the test a no-win condition** the protagonist must reframe, as El does in *The Last Graduate*.

### 13. The rival sport / tournament that's really about the romance

Pitball, Quidditch, War Games.
- **Let one lover genuinely lose to the other and not recover gracefully.**
- **Have the sport resolve the romance in the wrong direction.** The win drives them apart.
- **Make the sport the friend-subplot's centre, not the romance's.** Let the protagonists' *friendship* be the team.

### 14. Secret society initiation

Alexandrian, Lethe, Heirs.
- **Have the initiation be administrative.** Paperwork, oaths, a handshake. The horror is that it wasn't horrific.
- **Let the initiation reject the protagonist.** She was going to say yes; the Society says no. Why?
- **Invert: the protagonist initiates the Society into something.** She is the outside and the Society is the incomer.

### 15. The mentor dies to raise the stakes

Dumbledore, Mal (*Shadow and Bone*), Professor Lovell.
- **Let the mentor live and become a burden.** The protagonist has to carry them.
- **Make the mentor's death a relief**, and have the protagonist grapple with that.
- **Kill the mentor offscreen before book one.** The lingering is the point.

## Section 9 — Workbook prompts

Fifty-plus prompts, organised into nine sub-categories. Each is designed to force a decision that will affect plot and romance — not to fill in flavour.

### Academy design prompts

1. Who built your academy, when, and why? What crime or compromise was foundational to its construction, and which present-day faculty member is the last person alive who knows?
2. In a single sentence, state what your academy claims to teach and, in a second sentence, what it actually produces. If those two sentences are the same, revise until they are not.
3. Pick a real architectural style (Gothic, Brutalist, imperial Chinese, Mughal, mid-century modern). Describe your academy's physical form in that style and explain what political era of your world produced the choice.
4. Where is your academy located — geographically — and who benefits from that location? What nearby power would lose most if the school moved?
5. How does your academy handle the student who refuses to be there? Is there a legal apparatus, a magical one, a social one? What happens to the first student who successfully refuses?
6. What is the academy's relationship to its surrounding town or region? Does it feed the economy, fear it, exploit it, or pretend not to see it?
7. If the academy burned down tomorrow, who would fund its rebuilding and on what conditions? What changes would they demand?

### Faction / house / quadrant design prompts

8. For each faction in your school, answer these six questions in one sentence each: What do they value most? What do they wear? How do they solve problems? What is their blind spot? What is their internal prestige hierarchy? What is the stereotype outsiders hold about them, and how is it wrong?
9. Which of your factions would a contemporary reader underestimate, and what scene will force them to take it seriously?
10. Your protagonist is sorted into the faction she least identifies with. What trait does she have that the sorting system is rewarding, and does she know she has it?
11. Pick two factions. Describe a piece of slang that exists only in one and how the other uses it as a slur.
12. If your faction system removed one faction entirely, what political collapse would follow within the school, within a year? Within the state, within a decade?
13. What does your faction system conceal about your magic system? (If the answer is "nothing," your factions are cosmetic and may be cut.)

### Curriculum design prompts

14. Name every class your protagonist takes in year one. For each, write in ten words or fewer what it teaches, how it affects plot, and what her grade is. If any class has no plot function, cut it.
15. What is the capstone assignment of your academy, and what is the student who writes the only honest version punished for?
16. What forbidden knowledge exists in your school's library? Who placed the ban, what would happen if the ban were lifted, and who in the current administration is secretly already reading it?
17. What is the single most important spell, theorem, or combat technique your protagonist must master, and in what chapter does she finally use it — and against whom?
18. Design a class scene that does all four of: reveals character, teaches a skill used within fifty pages, seeds a conflict, and establishes institutional stakes.
19. Your protagonist fails a class. Which one, why, and what does she lose because of the failure — not merely emotionally, but materially?
20. What does your school *refuse* to teach, and who in the surrounding world teaches it instead?

### Tournament / trial design prompts

21. What is the set-piece event that ends year one, and what is the casualty count? How does the administration publicly account for the deaths?
22. Design a trial that your protagonist would fail if she played honestly and win if she cheated. Which does she choose, and what does the choice cost her in the romance?
23. What is the selection event that decides which students are deployed to the most dangerous postings after graduation, and who fixes the selection — administration, fate, the students themselves?
24. Your tournament pits the protagonist against her love interest. They cannot both win. Describe the aftermath of each outcome.
25. What magical or physical ritual marks the transition from first-year to second-year at your school, and what percentage of students do not survive it?
26. During a trial, your protagonist must choose between saving a stranger and saving an ally. Which decision does she make, and how does it change her standing with her love interest, her faction, and herself?

### Romantic-integration prompts

27. What feature of your academy's institutional structure — dorm assignment, squad pairing, class seating, sparring rotation — forces your protagonist and her love interest into proximity they did not choose? Write the scene where they both notice the structure is forcing them.
28. Your love interest has institutional authority over your protagonist. Describe the first scene where the authority is exercised against her, and the first scene where he refuses to exercise it. Which scene comes first, and why?
29. Where in the school can your lovers go to be unobserved, and what does the institution do when it discovers them there?
30. Your academy has a rule that, if broken, ends careers. Write the scene where the lovers break it for the first time. Now write the scene where someone else finds out.
31. Graduation will separate your lovers — to which postings, and how far apart, and what is the one thing they can carry with them?
32. What academic task must the lovers complete together that neither can complete alone? Is this task intimate, practical, or dangerous? Is it all three?
33. Your protagonist's romantic rival is her academic rival. What happens when the rival offers her help — on the exam, not on the romance?
34. Write the scene in which your protagonist's love interest is forced to grade her, assess her, or evaluate her professionally. Describe the document he submits and the document he does not.
35. Your lovers have a year until graduation. On the first day of that year, what does each of them secretly decide?

### Institutional politics prompts

36. Trace the flow of money into your academy. Whose gold pays for the dragons, the silverwork, the potions, the dormitories? Which benefactor will, in book three, demand something the school cannot give?
37. Name three factions in your student body with distinct political alignments, and name three faculty members whose loyalties do not match the faction they lead.
38. Your school produces graduates who will populate a specific institution — an army, a bureaucracy, a secret order. What does that institution do to a graduate who, upon arriving, refuses to serve?
39. What does your academy's public motto say, and what is the sardonic version students mutter among themselves?
40. A senior faculty member has been leaking information. To whom, and what do they want?
41. If the truth about your academy's hidden purpose reached the public, which three groups would demand its closure and which three would defend it?

### Traditions / rituals prompts

42. Describe the opening ritual that welcomes first-years. Now describe the version of it the senior students perform privately, which the administration pretends not to know about.
43. What is your academy's sport, what is the body count per season, and who profits from the betting?
44. Design a uniform that, at a single glance, communicates faction, rank, and year. Describe what one student wears when they are attempting to conceal one of those three facts.
45. What song, chant, or oath is taught to every first-year, and what is the line that has been altered from its original by administrative edit?
46. What is the founder's most famous saying, and what was it originally, before the institution sanitised it?
47. Invent three slang terms used only inside the school. One is affectionate, one is derogatory, one is obscene. How does a student become fluent?

### Beyond-school prompts

48. What is the nearest town to your academy, and what does its economy rest on? Which students go there for freedom, and which go there to work?
49. Your protagonist receives a letter from home that, if delivered to the administration, would destroy her place at the school. What does it say, who sent it, and what does she do with it?
50. During the winter break, three students stay behind. One is an orphan. One has been punished. One has a secret reason. Describe each and what they learn about the empty school.
51. What do your academy's alumni do in the decade after graduation, and who among them has become powerful enough to threaten the school itself?
52. Your protagonist's family either wants her home or sent her to die. Which is it, and what does she tell her love interest about it — and what does she lie about?
53. The wider world has a perception of your school that is substantially wrong. Describe the news article, pamphlet, or ballad that propagates the falsehood, and the detail it has closest to right.

### Subversion prompts

54. Write a scene in which the Chosen One is genuinely bad at the one thing she is supposed to be good at — and remains bad at it through the end of the book. What does she do instead?
55. Your headmaster is a mid-career administrator with imposter syndrome. What does the protagonist discover when she breaks into his office — not power, but what?
56. The "mean girl" is the smartest person in the room. Write the scene in which your protagonist realises this, and the scene in which she asks for help.
57. The ancient prophecy discovered in year one turns out to be a forgery planted by a faculty member with a political motive. Whose motive, and what does it cost the protagonist to uncover it?
58. Remove the forbidden forest from your school. Where does the wild, uncivilised, mystical encounter happen instead?
59. The evil professor is not the villain. The beloved mentor is. Write the scene in which the protagonist realises, and the scene she doesn't yet realise she has misread.
60. Your protagonist refuses to be sorted and the institution does not break. Describe how the administration absorbs her refusal, and what her refusal costs her socially.
61. Write a romance in which the academic rivalry is genuinely unequal — one of the lovers is, by any objective measure, the better student — and make the love survive that inequality without condescension or resentment.

## Conclusion: what academy romantasy is becoming

The genre that launched from Hogwarts has stopped apologising for its appetites. Modern academy romantasy is adult, political, sex-positive, and morally complicated. Its academies are no longer safe; its headmasters are not to be trusted; its love interests are not uncomplicatedly good. The craft conversation now centres on questions Hogwarts never asked: when does forced proximity become coercion, when does a mentor become a predator, when does an institution become a grinder, and when does the reader's pleasure become the author's responsibility?

The writer designing an academy romantasy in 2026 inherits three decades of worked examples, half a decade of sharp BookTok discourse, and a readership that has read everything. The craft task is not to invent from nothing; it is to inherit deliberately. Choose what you take from Hogwarts, what you take from Scholomance, what you take from Babel, and what you refuse. Know the difference between enemies and abusers, between rivals and tormentors, between mentors and predators. Build institutions that serve visible political functions. Let pedagogy be plot. Let graduation matter. Let the reader see the grinder before the protagonist does, or at exactly the same moment, or — most devastatingly — just after. These are the choices the workbook is for.
