# 8. What They Give And Cost

A romance works when the couple changes each other. Not vaguely. Specifically.

He gives her something she couldn't give herself. She gives him something he couldn't give himself. He costs her something she didn't know she cared about. She costs him something he didn't know he had.

The gift and the cost. Four exchanges. Most romantasy couples have all four running.

## The Four Exchanges

- **What she gives him.** A thing only she can give. Often a truth, a safety, a mirror.
- **What he gives her.** A thing only he can give. Often a choice, a strength, a world.
- **What she costs him.** A thing he had and now can't keep. A throne, a plan, a vow, a family.
- **What he costs her.** A thing she had and now can't keep. A home, a certainty, a former self.

Romance Leverage is highest when all four are visible on the page. The gifts make the couple earned. The costs make the stakes real.

## Varieties Of Gift

Some common shapes of the gift a romantasy couple gives each other.

- **A name.** She gives him a version of himself nobody else calls him. He gives her a name that isn't her Wound's name.
- **A future.** She gives him a reason to want tomorrow. He gives her a tomorrow she can picture.
- **A mirror.** She gives him the first honest reflection he's seen in years. He gives her a reflection that isn't the one her family gave her.
- **A body's peace.** She lets him touch her (after violence). He lets her touch him (after grief).
- **A competence.** She teaches him to read, fight, dance, or trust. He teaches her to stand, speak, fly, or survive.
- **A family.** She gives him a found family. He gives her a court, a pack, a throne, or a forest.

The gift has to be something the giver can uniquely give. If any other character could hand it over, the romance isn't load-bearing yet.

## Varieties Of Cost

Some common costs a romantasy couple pay for being together.

- **A throne.** He had an inheritance; loving her makes him unfit, or she makes him unfit to want it.
- **A vow.** One of them swore an oath. Loving the other breaks it.
- **A family.** Her family disowns her. His family wants her dead.
- **A kingdom.** The war they were preventing lights up because they chose each other.
- **A self.** The version of her that existed before him doesn't exist anymore. She mourns it, a little, even as she wouldn't go back.
- **A future she'd planned.** Not her HEA, her former HEA: the life she'd pictured before she met him.

The cost should be something the loser genuinely had. A throne the hero never wanted isn't a cost. A throne he'd trained for since childhood is.

## The Honest Ledger

Write it out. Four rows.

- What she gives him: _______
- What he gives her: _______
- What she costs him: _______
- What he costs her: _______

If any row is blank, your romance is missing an exchange. If any row is weaker than the others, that's the one to strengthen.

Not every exchange has to be equal. Some books give her more than he gives her (she's been through more, she needs more). Some books cost him more than her (he had more to lose). What matters is that all four are there.

## The Scene Where It Lands

Every one of the four exchanges has a scene where it's made visible on the page.

- The scene where she gives him the gift only she can give (often a midpoint intimacy beat).
- The scene where he gives her his gift (often earlier, establishing why he's the one).
- The scene where he pays his cost for her (often the Act Two low point, the lost throne or broken vow).
- The scene where she pays her cost for him (often the choice-point near the climax).

If you can name those four scenes, you have four pillars of your plot. Every other scene can flex around them.

## The Costs Your World Imposes

In romantasy, the costs aren't usually the couple's alone. The world imposes them.

- The war she interrupts to save him.
- The court he burns to marry her.
- The species feud their child will carry.
- The prophecy one of them fulfils by falling for the other.

This is where Part 3 (world skeleton) starts earning its keep. The costs land hardest when the world is the one levying them. A couple who pay their own emotional costs are a contemporary romance. A couple who pay the world's costs are a romantasy.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Fill in the four-row ledger above. One sentence per row.

**[L1]** Name the one thing only your heroine can give your love interest, and the one thing only he can give her.

**[L2]** For each cost (his and hers), name what the cost would have been worth in their lives before the book opened. If either cost is abstract, make it concrete.

**[L2]** What's the exchange your reader will remember three weeks after finishing the book? The one she'll cry about in the shower. That's the beating heart of the book. Write it in a sentence.

**[L3]** Write the scene where one of the four exchanges lands. Pick the one you most want to write. 500 words. Don't edit; just draft.

**[L3]** If you removed your magic system from the book, could the four exchanges still happen? If yes, your magic isn't working hard enough yet. If no, your magic is load-bearing. Either answer tells you something true about where the worldbuilding work is.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Violet and Xaden's exchanges are legible. She gives him a version of himself not defined by his father's execution. He gives her a future where her body isn't a death sentence. She costs him the secrecy that kept his rebellion alive. He costs her her trust in her mother. All four are paid on the page; all four are tied to the world (the college, the rebellion, the throne). The romance is the engine because the exchanges can't happen in any other setting.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Feyre and Rhysand's exchanges take longer because the first book is a misdirect. Once the real pair is on the page in book two: Feyre gives Rhys the first person in centuries who sees him past his Under the Mountain mask. Rhys gives Feyre literacy, flight, and a court that wants her whole. Feyre costs Rhys the secret of his mate bond (he has to carry it alone). Rhys costs Feyre her betrothal to Tamlin, her sense of her own sanity, and her original idea of love. All four exchanges ladder into the climax.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If you could cleanly swap your love interest for a different man with the same skills and the same job, and the exchanges would still happen, your love interest isn't load-bearing yet. He should be unexchangeable. The book should only work with him.
