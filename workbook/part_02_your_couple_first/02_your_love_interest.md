# 7. Your Love Interest

The same worksheet, for him. Or her. Or them.

Your love interest gets the same three cores: Want, Need, Wound. He also gets one extra beat your heroine didn't need: the Why Her. What is it about this specific woman that breaks him open? A love interest without a Why Her is interchangeable with any other brooding prince on the shelf.

## The Three Cores For Him

- **His Want.** What he's chasing when she meets him. A throne, a war won, a brother avenged, a curse lifted. Concrete.
- **His Need.** What he has to learn. Often the mirror of hers. She needs to trust; he needs to be trustworthy. She needs to let herself be loved; he needs to let himself love.
- **His Wound.** What happened before he met her. Usually bigger than hers on the plot-scale, because he's been alive longer or been burned harder.

## The Why Her

The scene where he first looks at her and the reader understands why she, specifically, matters.

Some working Why Her moments.

- She doesn't flinch. (Works for brooding loners.)
- She sees through him. (Works for charming manipulators.)
- She saves him. (Works for proud warriors.)
- She reminds him of someone he loved. (Works if the book interrogates this honestly.)
- She's the one person his magic, curse, or bond points at. (Fated mate shortcut. Valid if paid for elsewhere.)
- She makes him laugh when he hasn't in years. (Works for grief-carriers.)
- She's the wrong woman. (Works for dutiful characters. The Why Her is that she's the one thing he shouldn't want.)

The Why Her isn't love at first sight (unless your bond mechanic is). It's the moment the story commits to this pairing specifically. Without it, your reader wonders why he doesn't just pick any of the other courtiers.

## His Surface

Same practical list.

- **Name.** In the voice of your world.
- **Age.** And whether it matches hers.
- **Status.** Prince, general, rider, shifter alpha, exile, outlaw, cursed bastard, god's son.
- **Physical presence.** One line. Romantasy loves a specific feature: a scar, a particular build, an eye colour, a habit. Pick one.
- **Magic or ability.** Roughly, not fully designed yet.
- **One thing he's good at.** His competence.
- **One thing he's bad at.** His vulnerability.

## The Darkness Dial

Every romantasy love interest sits somewhere on the darkness spectrum.

- **1. Warm and steady.** A good man who's been through something. Mostly sweet romantasy.
- **2. Haunted but tender.** Brooding on the surface, soft underneath. Mainstream default.
- **3. Morally grey.** Does dark things for reasons that make sense to him. Enemies-to-lovers common setting.
- **4. Villain-coded.** He's the antagonist of someone else's story, the hero of hers. Dark romantasy territory.
- **5. Genuine villain.** He's done and will do things the reader has to reckon with. Dubcon and dark romance territory. Demands the most consent scaffolding (section 48).

Pick the number. Stay on it. A love interest who drifts from 2 to 5 across chapters without a plan reads as inconsistent. A love interest who's built at 4 and opens up at 2 in private is built on purpose.

## What He Knows About Her (That She Doesn't)

A useful romantasy trick: the love interest usually knows something about the heroine before she does.

- He knows she's his mate (and she doesn't).
- He knows her father killed his father (and she doesn't).
- He knows she's a princess in exile (and she doesn't, quite).
- He knows her magic is about to manifest (and she has no idea).
- He knows the bond will kill him if she rejects him (and she's going to try).

This gap in knowledge is an engine. Pick one thing he knows she doesn't. Pick the moment in the book where she finds out. That's a midpoint beat for free.

## His Own Supporting Web

Give him at least three people in his life who aren't her.

- A loyalty (a brother-in-arms, a cousin, a captain).
- A wound-carrier (the person who was with him when his Wound happened).
- A rival or enemy who isn't the heroine.

A love interest without his own web reads as a prop. His web is also where your couple's inner circle (section 63) starts.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name him. Age, status, one physical detail, one magic or ability.

**[L1]** His Want, Need, Wound. One sentence each.

**[L1]** His darkness number on the 1-5 scale. Commit.

**[L2]** Write his Why Her. When does he first look at her and understand she's the one this book is about? Four sentences.

**[L2]** What does he know about her that she doesn't? When does she find out?

**[L3]** Write a scene from his POV that the heroine never sees. What is he doing when she isn't in the room? Who does he tell what?

**[L3]** Write the single conversation he had with his most-trusted person (brother, mentor, captain) about her, before the book starts. What did they warn him? What did he ignore?

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Xaden's cores: Want (survive the Riders Quadrant without revealing his secret), Need (stop treating love as a liability), Wound (his father's execution and the relic-bond that came with it). His darkness dial sits at 3. His Why Her is that Violet is the last person he should love, because her mother killed his father. The hidden-knowledge runs both ways: he knows her mother's role; she knows his family name. Yarros makes them both carriers of secrets, which is a why-now engine.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Rhysand's cores: Want (keep the Night Court alive through the long con of Amarantha's reign), Need (let himself be known, especially by someone he loves), Wound (centuries of trauma Under the Mountain, his mother's and sister's deaths). His darkness dial sits at 3 (he does villain-looking things for reasons that parse). His Why Her is extraordinary: he recognises her mate bond and doesn't tell her, because he refuses to choose for her. That's a love interest built with a Need that's romance-specific.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If your love interest's Wound and your heroine's Wound are mirrors, their healing has to be mutual. If they're different shapes, they heal each other by being specifically each other. Either works. Decide which you're writing.
