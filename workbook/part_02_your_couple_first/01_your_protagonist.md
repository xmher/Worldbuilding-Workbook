# 6. Your Protagonist

Most worldbuilding workbooks start with a map. This one starts with the woman who'll walk across it.

Your protagonist isn't a blank you'll fill in once the world's done. She's the reason the world exists. Every worldbuilding decision we make after this page is going to press on her in some way, and the pressure only matters if we know who she is.

This section is a short character-core worksheet. Three things, three pages. We'll come back to her in Part 8 and audit whether every worldbuilding choice has earned its weight against her.

## The Three Cores

A romantasy heroine needs three things a reader can feel by the end of chapter one.

1. **A Want.** What she's consciously chasing. Usually external. A throne, a rescue, a return home, an escape.
2. **A Need.** What she actually has to learn or become. Usually internal. To stop running, to trust, to choose herself, to let herself be loved.
3. **A Wound.** What happened before page one that set the Want and Need in motion. A mother's death, a betrayal, a rejection, a single conversation she never recovered from.

The trick in romantasy is that the love interest is often the person who threatens the Want (initially), meets the Need (eventually), and knows the Wound (halfway through). The couple's arc runs along these three rails.

## Her Want

What does she think she wants when we meet her?

Some working examples.

- To keep her sisters alive through another winter (Feyre, ACOTAR).
- To survive the Rider Quadrant long enough to graduate (Violet, Fourth Wing).
- To win her father's approval (common).
- To avenge her brother (common).
- To go home (common).
- To be left alone (common).

Keep the Want concrete. "To be happy" isn't a Want; it's a wish. "To get my sister out of the Illyrian camps before the equinox" is a Want.

## Her Need

What does she actually need to learn, at the soul level, for the book to end well?

Some working examples.

- To let someone help her (after a lifetime of self-reliance).
- To believe she's worth saving.
- To stop apologising for her power.
- To choose a future instead of recreating the past.
- To trust that the love interest's love is real.

The Need is usually quieter than the Want, and often in tension with it. She wants a throne; she needs to learn that wearing it will cost her the love she doesn't yet know she wants.

## Her Wound

What happened before the book started that formed her?

Romantasy heroines usually carry at least one of these.

- **A parent's death or abandonment.** The genre's most common wound. Creates trust issues and a hunger for chosen family.
- **A betrayal by someone she loved.** A fiancé who cheated, a friend who sold her, a sister who chose the wrong side.
- **A survived violence.** Often off-page, often alluded to, always carefully handled.
- **A caste or class humiliation.** Being the lowest, the smallest, the unwanted. Creates ambition and armour.
- **A public shame.** Something she did, or something done to her, that the world knows.
- **A private shame.** Something she did that only she knows.

The Wound is where the love interest eventually gets past her defences. Her defences were built for a reason. The romance works when he earns access to the wounded place, not when he storms it.

## Her Surface

Alongside the cores, know these for practical writing.

- **Name.** Pick it in the voice of your world.
- **Age.** (And whether your world ages the same as ours.)
- **Status.** Princess, peasant, scribe, thief, recruit, courtesan, priestess, exile.
- **Physical presence.** One line. Height, build, a defining feature. Not a full inventory.
- **Magic or ability.** If she has one. Don't design the whole system yet; just know what she can do.
- **One thing she's good at.** Her competence beat. Readers love watching a heroine be good at something.
- **One thing she's bad at.** Her vulnerability. Readers love watching a heroine struggle with something.

## The Competence And Vulnerability Beat

Romantasy heroines work best when the reader sees them both excel and fail early. A heroine who only ever wins is unbelievable; a heroine who only ever fails is unbearable.

Pick one skill your heroine is world-class at. Not "good at", world-class. Then pick one skill she's genuinely hopeless at, in a way her love interest (or rival, or enemy) will see.

Violet is the smartest cadet in her year and physically the frailest. Feyre can hunt a wolf at nineteen and can't read. Both characters are built on this scales: one weight of competence, one weight of vulnerability. The balance is what makes them breathe.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your heroine. One sentence of status. Keep it short.

**[L1]** Her Want, Need, Wound. One sentence each. Don't get ornate; get specific.

**[L1]** One thing she's world-class at. One thing she's hopeless at.

**[L2]** Write the single memory from her past that, if she told her love interest, would change how he saw her. Don't write the scene. Write the memory in four sentences.

**[L2]** What would her best friend say about her that she wouldn't recognise as true? That's her blind spot.

**[L3]** Write the first page of your book from her POV. Not the whole chapter, just the first 250 words. Do her Want and Wound come through? If not, what could the opening paragraph do differently?

**[L3]** Write a short letter she'd never send. To whom? About what? The letter she can't send tells you what she most needs to say, which tells you what the book is eventually going to let her say.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Violet's cores: Want (survive Basgiath), Need (stop treating her body as a limit), Wound (her father's death, her mother's ruthlessness). Her competence is tactical intelligence; her vulnerability is her body itself. The romance with Xaden works because he sees her competence first (the scribe who outthinks him) and earns access to her vulnerability only when she chooses. Yarros's three cores are always visible on the page.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Feyre's cores: Want (keep her family fed), Need (accept being loved), Wound (the bargain her father didn't honour, the hunger she promised her mother she'd solve). Her competence is hunting; her vulnerability is literacy and emotional expression. The moments Rhys teaches her to read are doing Romance Leverage work: he's addressing the vulnerability she hides from Tamlin.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The scene where your heroine is loved while doing the thing she's worst at is the scene the book is building toward. If you don't know what she's worst at, you don't know which scene you're writing the whole book for.
