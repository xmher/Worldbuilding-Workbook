# 3. Your Three Comp Titles

Every writer needs three comp titles. Three books your book would sit next to on a bookshop table. Not because you're copying them, but because you're in conversation with them, and your reader is going to pick your book up because she loved theirs.

Comp titles do four practical jobs.

1. **They name your audience.** Readers of X and Y and Z will want to read you.
2. **They teach you the form.** Your subgenre's conventions live in these books.
3. **They fuel your pitches.** "For fans of X who want more of Y" is a working sentence.
4. **They keep you honest.** If you can't name three comp titles you'd be proud of, you haven't read enough in the genre yet.

This section is a worksheet. Pick your three. Note what each one does. You'll refer back here through the rest of the workbook.

## Picking Your Three

Three rules.

- **Recent.** Published in the last three to five years, ideally. Romantasy moves fast. A 2012 title is a different market.
- **Successful.** Bestseller list, big Goodreads count, visible BookTok presence. If your reader hasn't heard of it, it can't anchor your pitch.
- **Close enough.** Same subgenre. Same tone. Same heat level (approximately). Same reader demographic.

You're not picking your three favourite romantasy novels ever. You're picking three your book would sit next to. They can overlap with your favourites, but only if your book is genuinely like them.

## The Three Roles

Each comp does a slightly different job.

- **Comp A: Shape.** The book whose plot structure most resembles yours. Same subgenre, same central obstacle, same ending shape.
- **Comp B: Tone.** The book whose voice and mood your reader would recognise in yours. Same heat level, same darkness, same emotional pitch.
- **Comp C: Reach.** The book that sold well to the reader you want to reach. Not necessarily the most similar, but the one whose audience you most want.

If one book can do two roles for you, fine. Don't force three separate ones if the stack works with two.

## For Each Comp, Know

Fill this in for all three. Writing it down is the point.

- **Title and author.** Obvious.
- **Subgenre.** Primary and any secondary.
- **Publication year.** For context.
- **Approximate length.** (Yours should probably be within 30% of theirs.)
- **Heat level.** Closed door, fade to black, open door, explicit. (See section 4.)
- **POV.** First person, third limited, multi-POV, dual.
- **What it does best.** One line. Why this book is on the list.
- **What you'd do differently.** One line. Where your book diverges.

A comp list without the last two lines is a favourites list, not a comp list.

## What Counts As A Comp

- **Another novel in your subgenre.** The standard comp.
- **A series.** Name a book in the series, not the series itself, unless you're comping a series-shape.
- **A book outside romantasy.** Rare, but sometimes useful. "My book is *Fourth Wing* meets *The Secret History*" is a working pitch if the reader can read both.
- **A TV show or film.** Acceptable in a pitch, not in a market comp. *Merlin* meets ACOTAR is a fine vibe; it won't tell your publisher what shelf you're on.

## What Doesn't Count As A Comp

- A book you haven't read.
- A book that sold poorly.
- A classic (your book isn't *Pride and Prejudice*; it's *The Bridge Kingdom*, or whichever modern retelling lives on your shelf).
- A book from the wrong subgenre because you liked the tone. If your book is a vampire romance, comping a fae court book confuses everybody.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your three comps. Just the titles. Don't overthink.

**[L1]** For each, fill in the eight-line profile above. Half a page per book is enough.

**[L2]** Write your pitch sentence: "My book is _______ meets _______, for readers of _______." Three comps, three slots, one sentence.

**[L2]** What does each of your comps do that your book won't? Name one thing per comp. This is where your differentiation lives.

**[L3]** Read the one-star reviews for your Comp A. What did the angry readers say? Is any of their complaint also a risk for your book? If yes, plan now how you'll address it.

**[L3]** Write the back-cover blurb for your book using the exact conventions of your Comp B's back cover. Same structure, same punch. Your book should pass the blurb test.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's comps are obvious on the page: *Harry Potter* meets *Eragon* meets *A Court of Thorns and Roses*, minus the sweet academy tone and plus military grit. Her book sits in the dragon rider tradition (Pern, *Eragon*), draws its academy structure from HP, and takes its romance pacing from Maas. If a writer comping Yarros today names all three influences, she's told her reader exactly where her book lives.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas at the time of *A Court of Thorns and Roses* was comping *Beauty and the Beast* retellings and fae court fiction like Holly Black and Julie Kagawa. Two of her comps were structural (the retelling frame) and one was genre (court fae). That's a useful pattern: don't feel obliged to pick three books that are exactly your book. Pick the three that triangulate it.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If your three comps are all sweet, your reader will be furious when your midpoint turns dark. If your three comps are all dark, your sweet ending will land soft. Your comps tell the reader what tone to expect. Pick them honestly.
