# 5. The Promise Of The Premise

Every romantasy has a one-sentence hook. It's the thing you'd tell a friend at a party. It's the thing an Amazon category page needs to catch a reader's eye in three seconds. It's the thing your marketer writes on the top of the back cover.

If you can't say what your book is in one sentence, you either don't know yet, or you know and you're afraid to commit. Either is a problem. This section fixes it.

## The Shape Of The Hook

A working romantasy hook has three parts.

1. **The heroine and her situation.** Who she is at page one. One or two words.
2. **The love interest or the world's demand.** What or who changes everything. One or two words.
3. **The stakes or the twist.** What makes this book's version different. A clause.

Example shapes.

- "A mortal huntress is taken to a fae court where the High Lord might be her salvation or her ruin." (ACOTAR)
- "A fragile scribe is conscripted into a dragon rider war college where her closest threat is the boy whose father killed hers." (Fourth Wing)
- "A thief indentured to the high court of Faerie falls for the wicked prince who's been tormenting her." (The Cruel Prince)

Your hook is the answer to: "What's your book about?" When you hear yourself start with "Well, there's a kingdom, and a magic system, and...," you haven't written the hook. You've written a synopsis.

## The Three-Part Formula

Use this as a scaffold, not a prescription.

> When [inciting situation], [heroine] must [action or choice], or [stakes].

Fill it in.

- Heroine: who she is, not what she looks like
- Inciting situation: what the world does to her
- Action or choice: what she has to do
- Stakes: what's at risk if she fails

Rewrite three times. Tighten. Say it aloud. If it doesn't scan as a sentence, it's not there yet.

## The Romance In The Hook

A pure fantasy hook can skip the romance. A romantasy hook can't. Somewhere in the sentence, your love interest or your bond needs to show up.

Three ways to do this.

- **Name him.** "The High Lord who saves her," "the prince who tormented her," "the rider who won't speak her name." He's in the hook by role.
- **Name the bond.** "The mate bond she can't refuse," "the bargain she can't break," "the dragon that's chosen her."
- **Name the obstacle.** "Even if her kingdom calls it treason," "even if the court will kill for it."

A hook without a romance cue reads as fantasy. That's fine if you're writing fantasy. We're not.

## Testing The Hook

Three tests.

- **The friend test.** Say it aloud to a friend who doesn't read your genre. Does she nod, or does she ask questions that show she didn't follow? If she asks what a High Lord is, that's fine. If she asks what's at stake, your hook's missing a beat.
- **The cover test.** Close your eyes and imagine the cover. Does the cover art in your head match the hook? If the hook says "bloody dragon war" and your mind's eye shows a pastel lavender fae cover, one of them is lying.
- **The shelf test.** Walk into a bookshop. Would a reader who likes your three comp titles pick up a book with this hook? If she'd glance and pass, the hook is too far from its comps.

## The Longer Version

Your hook is the top of a pyramid. Below it sits the back-cover blurb (100-150 words), below that the synopsis (one to two pages), below that the full book.

Get the hook right first. The blurb is easier once the hook is tight. The synopsis flows from the blurb.

Some writers work the other way: they write the book, then cut down to the blurb, then to the hook. That works if you're a pantser with time. If you're trying to know what you're writing before you write it, the hook-first direction is faster.

## The Promise You're Making

Your hook is a contract. Your reader buys the book expecting what the hook said. If you promise a dark romance and deliver a sweet one, she'll be disappointed. If you promise dragons and deliver politics, she'll feel lied to.

Don't promise what you won't deliver. Don't deliver what you didn't promise. Your hook is what your blurb, cover, and opening chapter all align to.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Write your hook. One sentence. Use the three-part formula above.

**[L1]** Read it aloud. Rewrite it. Read it again. Do this until you stop making changes.

**[L2]** Write three variants: a pulpy version, a literary version, a trope-loaded version. Which one matches the book you want to write? (It's probably the one that felt hardest to write.)

**[L2]** Show your hook to someone who doesn't read romantasy. What did they hear? What did they miss? Revise.

**[L3]** Write a two-line version where line one is your hook and line two is your Romance Leverage: the one worldbuilding element that makes this couple's love impossible or beautiful or both.

**[L3]** Write the back-cover blurb. 120 words. Use your hook as the first sentence. Use your stakes as the last sentence. Fill the middle with the world.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's hook writes itself: a scribe-bodied heroine conscripted into a lethal dragon rider school, where her only allies might also be her enemies. The three beats are there: the heroine's situation (fragile, conscripted), the world's demand (dragon rider war college), the stakes (she isn't supposed to survive). The romance cue is implicit in "enemies might be allies". It reads as a book, not a synopsis.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's original hook leaned on the *Beauty and the Beast* retelling frame: a mortal huntress kills a fae, is taken to his lord's estate as payment, and falls for him. The three beats work, the romance is foregrounded (he's the lord she falls for), and the retelling hook borrows reader familiarity. First books often benefit from a retelling anchor. Her later books don't need it.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If your hook doesn't gesture at the couple or the bond, your book might be a fantasy with a love story. The moment your hook puts the love interest on the page, your reader knows she's in romantasy territory. Name him. Or name the thing that binds them.
