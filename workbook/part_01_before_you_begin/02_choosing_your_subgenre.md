# 2. Choosing Your Subgenre

Romantasy isn't one genre. It's ten. Each one arrives with a different set of reader expectations, a different shelf of comp titles, and a different toolkit of conventions.

You don't have to lock in a subgenre before you write. Some writers draft for months before they know what their book is. But you do need to lock in a subgenre before you market, because the cover, the blurb, the tags, and the tropes are all subgenre-specific. And your worldbuilding will differ, meaningfully, depending on which one you're building for.

This section helps you figure out which subgenre you're writing. If you already know, skim and move on.

## The Ten Subgenres

Each of these has a dedicated deep-dive in Part 7. This list is the orientation.

1. **Fae / Faerie Court.** Courts, bargains, true names, mortal-fae relations. ACOTAR, *The Cruel Prince*, *From Blood and Ash*.
2. **Dragon Rider.** Bonded mounts, military schools, aerial combat. *Fourth Wing*, Pern, *Eragon*.
3. **Academy / Magic School.** Students, rivals, magical curriculums, coming of age. *A Deal With the Elf King*, *Zodiac Academy*.
4. **Kingdom / Court / Royalty.** Thrones, successions, arranged marriages, political marriages. *The Bridge Kingdom*, *Crown of Gilded Bones*.
5. **Vampire.** Blood, immortality, sire bonds, the night. *Black Dagger Brotherhood*, *A Discovery of Witches*, *Crescent City*.
6. **Shifter / Werewolf (including omegaverse).** Packs, mates, heats, hierarchies. Lora Leigh's Breeds, Nalini Singh's Psy-Changeling.
7. **Monster Romance.** Non-human partners, size difference, body horror made tender. *Ice Planet Barbarians*, *Morning Glory Milking Farm*.
8. **Dark Romantasy.** Captivity dynamics, villain love interests, morally grey leads. *Haunting Adeline*, *Kingdom of the Wicked*, *Plated Prisoner*.
9. **Paranormal / Urban Fantasy.** Modern-world setting with magical layer. *Mercy Thompson*, *Kate Daniels*.
10. **Mythology-Based.** Retellings, pantheons, living gods. *Dark Olympus*, *A Touch of Darkness*, Alexis Hall's Greek retellings.

A book can live in two. A fae book with dragons is fine. A vampire book set at an academy is a stack you've seen on shelves. But every stack costs shelf real estate, so pick your primary and treat the second as the flavour.

## The Quick Quiz

Answer honestly. There's no wrong answer; there's just the answer that matches the book you actually want to write, which isn't always the one you think you're writing.

**1. Your love interest is, primarily:**
- a) A fae royal with a bargain to settle (Fae Court)
- b) A rider with a dragon and a grudge (Dragon Rider)
- c) A student who shouldn't be talking to her (Academy)
- d) A king or his heir (Kingdom)
- e) An immortal who shouldn't need her (Vampire)
- f) A man with a second shape (Shifter)
- g) Not human-shaped (Monster)
- h) The villain (Dark)
- i) A detective with something hidden (Paranormal)
- j) A god or the son of one (Mythology)

**2. Your heroine's biggest problem is, primarily:**
- a) She's on the wrong side of a wall or a court
- b) She's been chosen by something she can't refuse
- c) She's at a school that will kill her
- d) She's been promised to the wrong person
- e) She's mortal and he isn't
- f) She's human and he's not, and his kind notice her
- g) She's small and he's large, in a world that judges that
- h) She's been taken, bought, or bound
- i) She's investigating something that's looking back
- j) A god has picked her

**3. Your favourite scene to imagine is:**
- a) A ball. A masque. A court intrigue.
- b) A training yard. A spar. A flight.
- c) A classroom. A duel. A dorm at midnight.
- d) A throne room. A negotiation. A coronation.
- e) A night alley. A bite. A long conversation across centuries.
- f) A run through woods. A shift. A scent-mark.
- g) A first touch with someone whose hands are wrong in all the right ways.
- h) A locked room. A bargain at knifepoint. A slow corruption.
- i) A coffee shop with a portal in the back.
- j) A temple. A vision. A god refusing to look away.

If your answers cluster on one letter, that's your primary subgenre. If they split, your book is probably two subgenres in conversation. That's allowed. Name both.

## Subgenre Conventions You Can't Ignore

Every subgenre has conventions readers expect. Violating them is a choice; ignoring them is a mistake. A few key ones.

- **Fae Court.** A bargain plot. Named courts. A High Lord or equivalent. Mortal-fae distinction.
- **Dragon Rider.** A bonded mount. A training institution. Aerial combat. Losing the dragon equals death (usually).
- **Academy.** A curriculum. A rival. A mentor. A test the protagonist isn't supposed to survive.
- **Kingdom.** A succession problem. A political marriage. A court with factions. Someone who shouldn't be king becoming king.
- **Vampire.** A blood rule. A bite scene. An age gap worth discussing. A daylight problem.
- **Shifter.** A pack structure. A mating mechanic. A second form that matters on the page.
- **Monster.** A physical difference that's treated with care. A consent conversation. A culture the reader doesn't recognise.
- **Dark.** A consent scaffold (see section 48). A morally grey lead. A book that knows what it's doing.
- **Paranormal.** A contemporary grounding. A detective or investigator heroine. A magical community.
- **Mythology.** A recognisable pantheon. A retelling that earns its changes. A god who reads as a god.

When readers say "it didn't feel like a fae book," what they mean is your book didn't deliver the conventions they bought the book for. You can subvert them. You can't forget them.

## What If Your Book Is Two Subgenres

Fine. Most are. The question is which one leads.

Pick the one that governs:

- Your cover
- Your first three chapters
- Your primary reader pitch
- Your Amazon categories

The other one is flavour. You still build it; you don't market on it.

A dragon-rider book with fae politics is a dragon-rider book. An academy book with vampires is an academy book. The lead is whichever one your opening scene commits to.

## Prompts

**[L1]** After the quiz, write one sentence: "My book is primarily a _______ romantasy, with elements of _______."

**[L1]** Name the three conventions of your primary subgenre you're planning to deliver on.

**[L2]** Name one convention of your primary subgenre you want to subvert. What's your plan for replacing the reader expectation with something that satisfies it differently?

**[L2]** What's the scene from your subgenre you've read ten times and want to write your own version of? Write its setting in one line.

**[L3]** Find the one-star reviews for a recent book in your subgenre. What did the angry readers say the book didn't deliver? That's the list you can't afford to miss.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros writes dragon rider with academy layered on top. The dragons are primary (every decision on the cover, in the marketing, and in the opening chapter commits to dragons). The academy is the setting, not the selling point. If she'd tried to sell the book equally as both, the pitch would have diluted. She picked the lead and flavoured with the rest.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas writes fae court, cleanly. Every book in the series leans harder into the court conventions: courts with seasonal identities, High Lords with political weight, a mortal-fae wall that's doing geographic and cultural work at once. The romance is load-bearing; the fae machinery is what the book sells on.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The conventions of your subgenre aren't a checklist. They're the permission the reader gives you to tell an old story in a new way. Every convention you deliver buys you one bold choice you can make elsewhere.
