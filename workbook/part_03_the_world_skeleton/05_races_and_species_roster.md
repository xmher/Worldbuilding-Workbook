# 16. Races And Species Roster

Not every romantasy has non-human species. Some are all-human kingdom fantasies. Most aren't.

If your book has fae, vampires, shifters, dragons, or any other non-human group, you need a species roster. Not a Tolkien-scale encyclopedia. A working list of who lives in your world, what they can do, and how they relate to each other.

This section is a scaffold. Fill in the parts your book uses; skip the rest.

## What Counts As A Species Or Race

Three tiers.

- **Species.** Biologically distinct. Fae and humans in ACOTAR. Shifters and witches in most PNR. They can interbreed sometimes; their differences are more than cosmetic.
- **Race or lineage.** Within a species, a distinct group with cultural or magical differences. High Fae vs. lesser fae. Werewolves vs. other shifters. Day-walkers vs. night-walkers among vampires.
- **Subgroup or faction.** Within a race, a group bound by politics, training, or magic. A court, a clan, a pack.

Not every book needs three tiers. Many use just species plus faction. Some use all three.

## The Species Roster Worksheet

For each species in your book, answer these.

- **Name.** What they call themselves; what others call them. (Often different; sometimes insulting.)
- **Physical signature.** One sensory marker that tells the reader which species a character is. Pointed ears. Fangs. Scales at the temple. A scent the hero can't unsmell.
- **Magical or biological abilities.** What can they do that another species can't?
- **Lifespan.** Mortal, long-lived, immortal. A number or a range.
- **Social organisation.** Courts, packs, covens, guilds, empires?
- **Relationship with other species.** Allies, enemies, wary peace, hidden war, trade partners.
- **A cultural marker.** A food, a ritual, a taboo, a material the reader associates with them.

Three or four species is usually plenty. More and your reader starts losing track.

## The Species Your Couple Belongs To

Decide this cleanly.

- **Same species.** Romance runs on the normal obstacles (class, faction, oath). Mostly simpler.
- **Different species.** Romance runs with an extra obstacle layer: biology, lifespan, social prohibition, compatibility (section 40), fertility (section 39). Genre-default for much of romantasy.
- **One mixed-species character.** She's half-fae. He's a dhampir (half-vampire). Creates a character with a foot in two worlds and the politics of neither welcoming her. Rich engine.

If your couple is cross-species, the worldbuilding has to cover: how their species see each other, whether they can touch without harm, whether they can produce children (section 39), whether their lifespans match (section 41), and whether the world will tolerate them.

## The Lesser Species Question

Romantasy inherits a tradition from high fantasy in which there are "higher" and "lesser" species. High Fae and lesser fae in Maas. Alpha and beta shifters. Pureblood and turned vampires.

This hierarchy does useful story work (class obstacles, mixed-blood heroines, political gatekeeping) but carries weight. Some of the genre's worst-read books are ones where the hierarchy felt uninterrogated. Some of the best (ACOTAR, The Cruel Prince) use the hierarchy as a problem the heroine challenges, not as a neutral fact of the world.

If your world has a species hierarchy, decide: does your book endorse it, interrogate it, or dismantle it? The answer shapes the ending.

## The Creature Roster

Beyond the species your characters belong to, your world has creatures.

- **Mounts.** Dragons, horses, griffons, worgen, giant cats, beasts that bond.
- **Monsters.** Things that hunt your heroine. Often tied to the magic system or the cursed zones.
- **Familiars or spirits.** Small-scale magical creatures bound to witches, priestesses, or shapers.
- **Mythical beasts.** The unique creature of your world. The thing your reader will put on fanart.

You don't need dozens. You need the ones your plot uses. If your book has dragons, build your dragons deeply (section 50). If your book has one weird spirit who appears twice, name it and move on.

## The Cross-Species Love Question

If your book is cross-species, pay special attention to:

- **Biology.** Can they physically be together? (See section 40.)
- **Fertility.** Can they have children, and what happens if they do? (See section 39.)
- **Lifespan.** Will one outlive the other, and is the book solving that? (See section 41.)
- **Social cost.** Who disapproves? What's the price of being seen together?
- **Magical compatibility.** Does a bond work across species, or does the species difference break the bond?

You don't need an answer to all five in chapter one. You need to know the answers before you write the scenes that invoke them.

## Romantasy Species Cheat Sheet

A quick reference for the genre's most common non-human species. Use it to make sure you know the conventions before you bend them, or to pick a pairing your book hasn't considered.

| Species | Physical Signature | Lifespan | Key Ability | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| High Fae | Pointed ears, preternatural beauty, court-specific magic | Centuries or immortal | Elemental or court-inherited magic, glamour | Mate bond, lifespan gap, fae bargains (*ACOTAR*) |
| Lesser fae | Smaller, animal or plant-touched, less socially privileged | Long-lived, variable | Specialised minor magics | Class-below-class romance; servant or forest-dweller heroine |
| Dragon (bonded) | Winged reptile, scaled, elemental breath | Millennia | Flight, fire, mental bond with rider | Human-dragon telepathy; dragon's mate drives rider's love (*Fourth Wing*) |
| Dragon shifter | Human-form by day, dragon-form at will | Long-lived | Shift, flight, fire | Possessive claiming; hoard instinct; mate-for-life |
| Vampire | Fangs, cool skin, pallor, night-coded | Immortal, turning others | Speed, strength, thrall, blood-bond | Blood-sharing as intimacy; sire bond; sun-limit (*From Blood and Ash*, Twilight-lineage) |
| Shifter / werewolf | Human form, animal form, often alpha/beta hierarchy | Long-lived | Shift, pack-bond, heightened senses | Mate scent, heat cycle, pack politics (omegaverse-lineage) |
| Witch / sorceress | Human-shaped, magical lineage | Human to long-lived | Ritual magic, herb-craft, covens | Coven politics; witch-hunt stakes; familiar bond |
| Demon | Horned, tailed, heat-radiating, sometimes-monstrous | Immortal | Pact-bargaining, fire, corruption | Contract-bound love; soul-price; monster-romance end (*Plated Prisoner*-adjacent) |
| Angel / celestial | Winged, radiant, sometimes-androgynous | Immortal | Healing, sight, wrath | Falling-for-a-mortal; blasphemous love; wings as intimacy |
| Elf | Pointed ears, graceful, forest- or city-bound | Very long | Bow, magic, woodcraft | Classic pairing; slightly more Tolkien-coded than fae |
| Merfolk / siren | Tail below water, legs above (by magic), song-gifted | Long-lived | Song, water-command, drowning glamour | Voice-as-bond; land-sea divide; transformation romance |
| Orc / ogre | Tusked, massive, often tribal | Human-like | Raw strength, tribal magic | Monster-romance; cross-species acceptance arc |
| Monster (unique) | Scaled, multi-limbed, other-shaped | Varies | Varies | Beauty-and-beast template; full monster romance (section 46) |
| Gods / demigods | Varies, often mortal-passing | Immortal | World-shaping, domain-specific | Mortal-immortal gap; divine lover's cost; prophecy (*A Shadow in the Ember*) |
| Ghosts / revenants | Partial body, cold presence | Post-mortal | Haunting, possession, memory | Love across death; resurrection arc; necromancy-adjacent |
| Human | Human | 70–90 years | None, or learned magic | The grounding species; the reader's mirror |

Pick your couple's species from this list, or build a variant. Name what's different about yours. ("My vampires can walk by day if a witch blesses them.")

## Prompts

**[L1]** List the species in your book. For each, one line of physical signature and one line of magical ability.

**[L1]** Which species does your heroine belong to? Which does your love interest belong to? Is it the same?

**[L2]** If your couple is cross-species, answer the five-point list above (biology, fertility, lifespan, social cost, compatibility). One sentence each.

**[L2]** Pick two species in your world that are in conflict or wary peace. What's the source of the conflict? One line of history.

**[L3]** Write the first line of in-world folklore about one of your species. "It is said that the fae..." How does your human culture talk about them? What's feared, what's desired, what's misunderstood?

**[L3]** Consider the version of your book where your couple is same-species. What would change about your plot? If the answer is "not much," your species difference isn't pulling weight. If the answer is "most of it," the species is load-bearing.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros keeps her species roster lean: humans (divided into Navarrian, Tyrrish, and Poromish populations), dragons (six species with distinct personalities and abilities), gryphons, and venin (the magic-eating dark threat). No fae, no vampires. The dragon hierarchy is her central species design, and it does class and romance work at once (the bond-match between dragon and rider is a status marker). She doesn't overbuild the roster; she builds the one species family her plot needs.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's roster has High Fae, lesser fae (a wide variety of types with distinct magic and appearance), humans, and creatures from beyond the courts (Bone Carver, Suriel, Weaver). The High/lesser split is a class engine; the human/fae split is the central cross-species romance obstacle. Feyre's transformation from human to High Fae is one of the book's most consequential plot beats and is only possible because Maas committed to the species as biologically distinct.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your couple's species pairing is one of your biggest worldbuilding decisions. It governs fertility, lifespan, compatibility, social approval, and magical bond. Pick the pairing that forces the most interesting obstacles for the specific couple you're writing. If you can't answer "why this pairing specifically," reconsider.
