# 13. Climate, Biomes, Travel

Your geography is the map. Your climate is the weather on it. Your biomes are what grows. Your travel rules are how long it takes to get anywhere.

All three sit beneath a single question: what's it like to move through your world?

A book that answers that question specifically feels lived in. A book that doesn't feels like a stage set.

## Climate As Atmosphere

Climate is the first sensory thing your reader experiences. Before they know the politics, they know whether the air is cold.

Three kinds of climate decisions.

- **Stable climate.** One climate, one feel. The Night Court is cold and star-bright. The Spring Court is warm and petal-scented. Simple, strong atmosphere.
- **Seasonal climate.** Normal seasons, normal cycling. Most realistic-feeling. Tracks time for the reader.
- **Magical climate.** Weather that doesn't follow physics. Endless winter (Winternight, Narnia). Unnatural storms. Dead zones. Skies that show two moons or three.

Pick the climate that does work for your couple. A frozen north separates more harshly than a warm south. A seasonal climate lets you use calendars, festivals, and time's passage. A magical climate tells the reader magic is unbounded in your world.

## Biomes As Culture

What grows tells the reader what the culture lives on.

- **Forest people.** Wood, fur, smoke, venison, berries, clay. A forested biome shapes buildings (timber), religion (tree spirits, deep woods gods), cuisine (foraged, hunted), warfare (ambush, archery).
- **Plains people.** Grain, herds, horses, wide skies. Buildings open (low walls, big halls), warfare cavalry-centric, religion often sky-facing.
- **Coastal people.** Fish, salt, ships, trade. Wealth from the sea, buildings weather-braced, politics tied to port cities.
- **Mountain people.** Stone, goats, cold, isolation. Defensive culture, strong ancestral ties, mining economies.
- **Desert people.** Caravan, water-law, silk or wool, stars for navigation. Strong hospitality codes (because strangers must be fed), sacred water.
- **Island people.** Isolation, mist, boat-faring, narrow resources. Often magic-rich because separation preserves old ways.

Pick your couple's biome because it shapes them. Feyre hunts because her family lives on the forest edge. Violet reads because she's a scribe's daughter in a military academy. Biome is character-forming.

## Travel As Time-Cost

How long does it take to cross your world?

This is the question most romantasy writers under-think. They want the heroine in her own kingdom by chapter ten, then at court by chapter eleven, then back home by chapter fifteen. Geography doesn't usually allow that.

Decide your standard travel speeds.

- **Horse.** About 30-40 miles a day on good roads; less on rough terrain.
- **Ship.** Variable, wind-dependent. Think in days for coastal hops, weeks for crossings.
- **Foot.** 15-20 miles a day, slower in mountains or forest.
- **Dragon or mount.** Whatever your magic allows, but commit to a number (Yarros commits to one day's ride for the dragon bond range in *Fourth Wing*).
- **Magic.** Portals, winnowing, teleportation. Decide the cost: magical exhaustion, a bargain, a ritual site required.

Write down the fastest way across your continent and how long it takes. Every plot decision checks against that number.

## The Magical Transport Trap

Magical transport solves travel time and kills stakes. Be careful.

If your heroine can portal anywhere, your geography doesn't separate. If your love interest can winnow into her bedroom, your wall is decoration. The genre deals with this in three ways.

- **Limit the magic.** Only certain people can do it, only to certain places, only at certain times, only at a cost.
- **Forbid the magic in some zones.** The wall blocks winnowing. The cursed forest eats portals. The academy grounds are warded.
- **Let the magic exist but make it socially expensive.** Appearing in someone's bedchamber by magic is a court-level scandal.

Pick at least one limit. Your reader will thank you.

## The Sensory Signature

Every biome in your book should have a sensory signature your reader can feel.

For each place your couple visits, name:

- A smell (petrichor, salt, smoke, incense, pine, char).
- A temperature (the dry cold that bites the lungs, the humidity that softens the linen).
- A sound (gull-cry, wolf-howl, chant, wind across grass).
- A texture underfoot (flagstone, moss, sand, snow, glass).

Three sensory details per scene is enough. More is purple; less is sterile.

## The Weather As Plot Device

Weather can do plot work.

- A storm traps the couple in a cabin.
- A cold snap means she freezes if she doesn't share a bed.
- A dry season makes the river crossing possible.
- A fog hides the army's approach.
- A blood moon powers the ritual.

Decide when weather is a device and when it's a backdrop. A device-weather moment lands harder if most of the book's weather is backdrop. Save your storms.

## Travel Time Reference

Here's a rough reference for how long it takes to move people across a map. Don't obsess over realism. Do commit to numbers your book will hold to.

| Mode | Miles Per Day | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|
| On foot, walking | 15–20 | Slow travel, lots of talk, shared meals |
| On foot, forced march | 25–30 | Exhausted intimacy, injury, close-quarters care |
| Horseback, steady | 30–40 | Daily rests, inn scenes, saddle-soreness |
| Horseback, hard ride | 50–70 | Urgent, short, sweat-and-wind |
| Changing horses (posts) | 80–120 | Fastest mortal method, used by couriers, spies |
| Carriage, paved road | 30–40 | Forced proximity in a box, knees touching |
| Ship, coastal | 60–100 | Deck scenes, cabin scenes, weather risk |
| Ship, open water | 100–150 (wind permitting) | Weeks-long voyage, confined quarters |
| Dragon flight (cruise) | 150–300 | Pair-bonded riding, wind-in-face, shared power |
| Dragon flight (push) | 400–600+ | Exhaustion after, forced overnight landing |
| Winnowing or teleport | Instant | Usually limited, costly, or socially scandalous |
| Portal network | Instant within network | Requires a key, a bargain, or a master |

A healthy adult makes these numbers. Children, the wounded, the elderly, and anyone carrying a lot of goods are slower. Terrain slows everyone. Weather stops everyone.

Pick your standard speed. Write it down. When your plot asks how long a journey takes, check the number.

## Weather-As-Plot-Device Menu

Here's a menu of weather events that earn their scene. Pick two or three for your book. Save the rest for later titles.

- **Thunderstorm, shelter required.** Couple trapped in a cabin, cave, or single-bed inn.
- **Blizzard or snow-in.** Road closed, a week indoors, body heat as survival.
- **Heatwave, water scarce.** Tempers fray, bathing in questionable places, the thin shirt.
- **Drought, river low.** A crossing becomes possible that usually isn't; or tension over water.
- **Flood, bridges out.** Forces a detour into hostile territory, a longer route, more time together.
- **Fog, visibility nil.** Ambush weather; also the weather for sneaking past a guard.
- **Eclipse or blood moon.** Magic surges; a ritual happens; a bond flares.
- **Unnatural storm.** Magical origin, often the villain's work; the couple learns who's hunting them.
- **First frost.** Signals the end of a safe season. Travel becomes harder. The deadline tightens.
- **Thaw.** Rivers re-open; exile can finally travel home; an army can march again.
- **Wildfire.** Forces evacuation; destroys a safe place; gives you a rescue scene.
- **Windstorm grounds dragons.** *(Fourth Wing*-coded.) Mounts grounded means the couple travels by less glamorous means, which makes the journey longer and closer.

Each of these is a pre-built obstacle or intimacy engine. Match the weather to the scene's need.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name the climate of your heroine's home. One sentence.

**[L1]** Name the climate of your love interest's home. If it's different, what's the first thing that surprises her when she arrives?

**[L1]** How long is the journey from one to the other by the fastest means in your world? Commit to a number.

**[L2]** Pick three biomes your book touches. For each, name the smell, temperature, sound, and underfoot texture.

**[L2]** Does your world have magical transport? Write the limits of it in one paragraph. If you can't find limits, your magic is too strong.

**[L3]** Write a travel scene: your heroine crossing a border or a long distance for the first time. 500 words. Include weather, biome, and one conversation with whoever she travels with.

**[L3]** Plan your book's weather. Which three scenes need the weather to do work? What's the weather in each? Is the weather consistent with your climate rules?

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Basgiath is mountainous, cold, and stormy; the parapet is literally windswept. The climate is doing character work (Violet's fragility is tested by every wind gust) and plot work (storms ground dragons, which limits training and forces scenes). The biome is military-mountain, which shapes the food, the buildings, and the pace of daily life. Yarros's climate is small-scope and heavy-duty.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Each of Prythian's seven courts has its own climate, untethered from normal seasons. The Spring Court is perpetual spring; the Winter Court is perpetual snow; the Night Court shifts between starlit peaks and a glittering city. Maas uses climate as identity: travelling between courts feels like travelling between worlds. The rule (climates are court-magical, not seasonal) is established early and held throughout. A strong atmospheric move that required a clear magical rule to support.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Weather is free obstacle material. A cold snap, a flood, a magical storm, a dry season: any of these can keep your couple trapped, travelling, or apart. Plan three weather events in your book and put each where it will cost your couple something.
