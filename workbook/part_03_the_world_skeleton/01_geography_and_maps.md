# 12. Geography And Maps

A map is a visual aid that some writers find clarifying and others find a distraction from the drafting itself, which is why the question of whether to draw one is less important than the question underneath it. What every romantasy writer needs, whether she draws a map or not, is a clear sense of the physical shape of her world, meaning a working knowledge of what's where, what lies between, and what keeps the couple apart or forces them together.

This section treats geography as an obstacle engine, because landscapes that separate, gather, or threaten your couple are the landscapes that do plot work, whereas landscapes that only look nice are decoration and will not carry a story on their own no matter how carefully the writer has described them.

## The Three Jobs Geography Does For Your Romance

Romantasy geography tends to do one of three jobs, and knowing which job your geography is doing in a given scene is one of the clearer signs that your world has been set up to support your romance rather than simply surrounding it with scenery.

The first job is **separation**, which describes the situation where your couple cannot easily reach each other because something physical is standing in the way. That obstacle might be a wall across the continent that only certain people are permitted to cross, or a sea that takes weeks to traverse and costs money your heroine doesn't have, or a mountain range that is shut by snow for half the year, or a political border enforced by guards, search warrants, and an unfriendly relative at the gate. Separation geography does its work by making every shared moment expensive in some way, so that the reader registers the effort of each meeting and understands that the crossing was never going to be free of cost to the person who made it.

The second job is **forced proximity**, which describes the situation where your couple cannot get away from each other because they are sharing a space that has no easy exits. That space might be a training school they have both been conscripted into, a long carriage ride through bandit country, a ship in the middle of a voyage, a siege they are both trapped inside, or a country inn where a storm has closed the roads and nobody is able to leave until morning. Proximity geography removes the option of walking away from a difficult conversation, which is the option your characters would normally take in order to avoid the tension that is building between them.

The third job is **threat**, which describes the situation where the land itself is trying to kill your characters. A storm coast where ships don't come back, a wood that takes travellers and doesn't return them, and a mountain pass where the cold can kill faster than any enemy are all places where survival and romance become the same scene, and the reader's anxiety about whether both characters will come out the other side carries the tension of the sequence without the writer having to build additional conflict on top of the landscape.

Most romantasy uses all three of these jobs, layered across the arc, so that the book opens with separation (she is on her side of the border, he is on his), moves into forced proximity (they are stuck together on the road, in the school, or at the court), and climaxes in threat geography (the final confrontation happens somewhere that could kill them independently of any enemy's plans). If you can name at least one feature of your world that does each job, your geography is starting to carry real weight. When you cannot name a feature for a given job, you have more sketching to do before you begin drafting Chapter One.

## The Features That Earn Their Keep

Not every landscape element on the page needs to pull plot weight, but the ones you name repeatedly should, and any feature that recurs across multiple chapters needs to earn those recurrences by doing something for the plot rather than appearing as background. Here are the features romantasy uses most often, with a note on what each one tends to be good for in a romance.

**Walls and borders** are the simplest separators, and a crossing of one is a scene the reader will pay attention to without needing to be told in advance why it matters. The mortal-fae wall in *ACOTAR*, the titular bridge in *The Bridge Kingdom*, and the perimeter of any closed-campus school all do the same kind of work, because they turn a crossing into a decision with consequences that follow the character afterwards into the rest of the book.

**Seas and oceans** offer larger-scale separation that is slower to cross and usually culturally loaded, because the other side of a sea tends to be foreign, suspicious, or enemy territory in the reader's imagination as much as in the characters' assumptions. A sea in romantasy often carries the weight of exile, so that being on the wrong side of it becomes a condition your heroine has to live inside for a significant portion of the story rather than a distance she can simply travel across when she feels like it.

**Mountain ranges** are defensive, isolating, and seasonally unreliable, which is why they make natural faction boundaries. They are hard to cross year-round, and the passes that do cross them become chokepoints that a ruler can guard, a rival can block, and an ambushing party can hide alongside when the plot requires it.

**Forests** have earned their reputation in this genre, because forests hide things such as monsters, hermits, lovers, and secrets, they bend time in ways that open ground does not (a day in the fae wood is a year outside), and they hold magic that roads and villages don't seem able to keep in one place for long. A forest is also a reliable place for a character to get lost, either physically or in some other sense, which is usually the reason the writer has sent her there in the first place.

**Deserts and dry country** make for harsh passage, and they are where characters tend to get tested, where exiles end up once everyone else has given up on them, and where the old magic still lives because nobody else wanted the land enough to fight over it. A desert scene is slow and thirsty by default, and that default rhythm is what gives the eventual arrival at an oasis, a waystation, or a city its emotional weight when the journey finally ends.

**Rivers** do triple duty as travel routes, borders, and pause-points for scenes, which is why they appear so frequently in the genre. Rivers are where caravans stop for the night, where duels take place at first light on a gravel bank, and where boats float your couple downstream while they finally have the conversation they have been avoiding for six chapters. A river running through your book gives you a line along which you can walk your characters through several chapters of rising tension without having to invent new terrain for each scene.

**Cities** are the populated hearts of your world, where the court lives, where the school sits, where gossip travels faster than any rider does, and where your heroine can be seen with the wrong person by the worst possible witness at exactly the wrong moment. Every named city should have its own feel, and if two of your cities are interchangeable in the reader's memory, you have either scoped for one city too many, or one of the two needs a reason to exist that the other does not already provide.

**Cursed and forbidden places** are useful in the later section of a book, because a valley nobody enters, a high place where something terrible happened once, or a ruin the locals will not name after dark all arrive pre-loaded with menace, which means the climactic scene set in one of those locations can skip the tension-building work that would otherwise require most of a chapter of setup.

You do not need all of these features, and you almost certainly should not try to fit all of them into a single book, but you do need enough named geography to give your couple somewhere to be kept apart, somewhere to be forced into close contact, and somewhere that is actively dangerous to both of them at the same time.

## The Starting Points Question

In most romantasy, the couple start the book in different places, which means the distance between them is the distance the story has to close over the course of the arc, and naming both of those starting points precisely is one of the most productive hours of worldbuilding you can do before you begin drafting.

Ask where your heroine lives at the opening of Chapter One, whether that is a village, a court, a barracks, a convent, or a merchant's household with counting rooms on the ground floor. Ask where the love interest lives at the same moment, and ask what lies between them, whether that is a border, a sea, a city, a rival court, a species line, or an old war that is still being fought in the background of everyone's family stories.

Once you have both starting points, ask the practical question of how long a crossing would take at the fastest speed your world actually allows, because the answer determines how your plot can move through its chapters. A healthy horse covers roughly thirty miles on a good day, a hard ride with changed mounts stretches that figure to around a hundred, a ship depends on wind and tide, and a creature such as a dragon might do a three-week overland ride in an afternoon, although creatures on that scale tend to be expensive in other ways, whether through political visibility, bond strain, or the simple cost of keeping something that large fed. A portal could close the distance instantly, but portals are supposed to cost something, and the cost of a portal is what keeps it interesting rather than making it a shortcut that flattens the obstacle your separation geography was built to create.

If the crossing turns out to be instant and free, your geography is not separating anyone, and if the crossing turns out to be genuinely impossible, your book cannot happen in the shape you have currently drawn. The useful space for your plot to live in is a crossing that is costly but possible, where the trip can be made but only at the price of money, time, political capital, or a piece of someone's health that will not fully grow back before the next obstacle arrives.

## The Scale Problem

Most romantasy writers over-scope their first world, sketching three continents, twelve kingdoms, and forty cities before they have written Chapter One, and then discovering halfway through the draft that they cannot actually use any of this material, because the reader cannot hold onto more than a few named places at once, particularly when she is reading in ten-minute bursts before bed.

A useful test is to count the named places your book visits across Act One. One or two places means you have scoped for a single-setting story, which is often the strongest shape for a debut and for a romance that wants the couple in each other's sightline through most of the book. Three to five places means you have scoped for a travelling story, which works well when the pace is brisk and each stop earns its chapter. Six or more places means you have scoped for an epic, which can work when you have the word count to support it and when you are willing to commit to building each location at full depth rather than half-sketching several of them.

You can, and probably should, have more places existing in your world than your couple actually visits during the book, because the places that stay offstage add depth and give your reader the sense that the world continues past the edge of the page rather than ending at the border of the map. The places that do make it onto the page, however, need to pull their weight, which means each named place should be distinct enough that the reader remembers which is which a hundred pages later, because a blurred setting is effectively the same as no setting once the reader has to go searching for it in her memory.

## The Map-Drawing Question

Draw a map if it helps you, and do not draw one if it does not, because this is one of the few worldbuilding decisions where personal preference genuinely matters more than craft advice does.

A map can force you to commit to distances, which is useful because vague distances tend to stretch and shrink to whatever the current scene wants from them, and a map can also expose geography you have not actually built, because the blank space on the page is the question you have not yet asked yourself about your world. When you try to fill that space in, you find out whether your world has an answer ready or whether you have been relying on a general impression of how everything fits together.

What a map cannot do is make your geography load-bearing on its own, because a carefully drawn map of a useless world is still a useless world with a picture attached to it. The map is a tool for clarifying decisions you have already begun making about what your landscape does to your couple, and when those decisions are not being made elsewhere, drawing the map will not prompt them for you on its own.

If you do decide to draw one, keep the first version rough and functional, because the designed publication-ready version of your map happens later in the process, either with a cartographer or through a dedicated tool. Your first map only needs to be honest about what sits where, how far apart things are, and what your characters have to cross in order to reach each other.

## The Scene-Level Geography

Everything covered so far works at world-scale, but most of your book actually happens at scene-scale, inside a specific room, garden, corridor, or clearing, and scene geography needs to answer the same questions that world geography does, only in smaller and more immediate spaces.

A balcony separates two characters by a few feet and a social code, a locked door separates them by two inches of oak and a law that neither of them particularly wants to break in front of witnesses, and a shared carriage presses them together for a hundred miles of conversation that both of them would rather not be having. A bedroom with a lit fire functions differently from a bedroom without one, because the warmth of the space functions as permission for intimacy and the cold of it functions as a signal of distance, and the reader registers that difference in atmosphere without needing it to be spelled out for her in dialogue.

When the room your scene is set in doesn't actually matter, you should pick a different room, because the walls and furniture should be working alongside the dialogue rather than standing in quietly as background fill.

## A Short Reference Of Features That Do Plot Work

If you want a checklist to test your world against, here are the features romantasy uses most often for obstacle work. You do not need all of them, so the useful approach is to pick the features your plot actually requires rather than collecting the full set out of habit or completeness anxiety.

Consider a wall your couple find themselves on opposite sides of, a sea one of them has to cross for the other, a forest that few travellers return from in one piece, a mountain pass that closes in winter and strands whoever has been caught on the wrong side, a border guarded by a court that would rather see her arrested than allow her through, a capital she cannot return to without being recognised, a village her family was driven out of for reasons the elders will not discuss, a confined space that forces her and the love interest together despite their intentions to keep their distance, and a ruined site where the climactic scene can happen without the writer having to build atmosphere from scratch.

Once you have chosen two or three features from that list, build them with specificity by giving them names and sensory signatures, so that the wall smells of iron rust and sea salt, the forest's silence feels wrong because the birds are not singing there, and the mountain pass is cold enough to crack the breath in your heroine's throat before she reaches the shelter on the other side. Those specific, named features are what your plot will hang on through the middle of the book, and everything else on your map should be treated as texture, which is useful in small amounts but cannot be asked to carry the story on its own once the reader starts asking questions about how the world actually functions.

## Your Starting Points Worksheet

Fill this in before you begin drafting Chapter One, because every answer here is a plot decision that you would otherwise end up making by accident somewhere in the middle of Act Two, at which point reversing the choice becomes significantly more expensive in terms of word count.

| Question | Your Answer |
|---|---|
| Where does your heroine live when the book opens? | |
| Where does your love interest live when the book opens? | |
| What lies between them (wall, sea, forest, court line, species line)? | |
| How long does a crossing take at the fastest speed your world allows? | |
| What does that crossing cost (money, magic, permission, injury, time)? | |
| Who or what prevents an easy crossing? | |
| What's the first crossing that actually happens in your book? | |
| Who crosses first, toward whom, and why at this particular moment? | |

If you have written "instant" or "free" in the cost column, your geography isn't yet doing obstacle work, and the corrective question is to ask what would make the crossing cost something the character would notice paying.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name the single geographic feature that most keeps your couple apart, in one sentence that is specific enough that a reader could picture the feature without needing further explanation.

**[L1]** Name the single geographic feature that most forces them together, in one sentence.

**[L1]** Where does each of them live at the book's opening? Answer in two sentences, one for each character.

**[L2]** List the named places your book visits in Act One, and for each one, write a sentence of sensory detail that makes the place impossible to confuse with any of the others, because when two of them sound similar in the reader's memory, one of them is redundant and should either be cut or given a reason to exist that the other doesn't already provide.

**[L2]** Pick the most important city, court, or settlement in your book, and write a paragraph about its layout, including where the heart of it sits, where its most dangerous district lies, and where within it your couple first meet each other.

**[L3]** Draw a rough map on whatever surface is to hand, whether that is paper, a tablet, or the back of an envelope, marking both starting points as well as the route between them. The map does not need to be pretty, but it does need to be honest about distances and about what lies in the gaps between the named places.

**[L3]** Pick one feature of your world and ask whether the book would change if this feature weren't in it, because when the answer is "not very much," the feature can probably be removed, and when the answer is "the whole book falls apart without it," the feature needs to be built with more care than it has been given so far.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros keeps her world deliberately small, because most of the book happens within the walls of the Riders Quadrant at Basgiath, with book two expanding the frame slightly through outpost missions while the war beyond those walls is gestured at rather than shown in detail. The confinement is doing proximity work on the romance, because Violet cannot walk away from Xaden when she is inside a military school built into a mountain fortress and the school's design doesn't allow cadets to leave at will, which means the two of them are forced to keep running into each other in the same stairwells, corridors, and training fields throughout the book. The political map of the provinces sits in the background, keeping her mother's regime and his rebellion in visible tension without ever requiring a grand continental tour, which makes efficient use of offstage geography to support the on-page stakes of the romance.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses a courts-and-cardinal-directions layout, with a wall between the fae and mortal lands and a significant mountain location where political power is centralised in the first book, and each court within the fae side carries its own distinct sensory signature so that the reader learns the world by travelling across it from book to book. The geography does three different jobs simultaneously, acting as separator (the wall between mortal and fae), threat (Under the Mountain), and cultural marker (each court feels different before the reader is told anything about its politics), and every instalment of the series visits new places, so that the reader's sense of the world deepens by accretion as the map fills in across the course of the series.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Every feature of your map is a potential obstacle or reunion engine waiting for the plot to pick it up and use it. The sea is where she is exiled to, the mountain is where he hides while she is hunting for him, and the wall is what one of them crosses for the other when the book finally asks them to prove what they've been claiming to each other. When you choose your map's features, choose them for the work they can do on your couple, and leave decoration for the later stage of the build, once the load-bearing elements have been set into their places.
