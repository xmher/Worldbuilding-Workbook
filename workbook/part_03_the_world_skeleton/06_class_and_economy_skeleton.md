# 17. Class And Economy Skeleton

Class is the silent engine of most romantasy plots. Who holds power, who holds land, who holds gold, who holds none of the above. The gap between them is the romance obstacle half the time.

Economy is the physical expression of class. What's traded, what's scarce, what's taxed, what's given freely. A reader feels economy through what characters can and can't afford.

You don't need a GDP. You need a skeleton.

## The Class Pyramid

Every romantasy world has one, explicitly or implicitly.

- **Top tier.** Royals, High Lords, gods' sons, ruling council, whoever sits the throne.
- **Upper nobility.** Dukes, counts, court favourites, minor royals.
- **Lower nobility.** Knighted families, landholders without titles, cousins of cousins.
- **Gentry and merchants.** Wealthy non-nobles, traders, successful craftsmen.
- **Commons.** Farmers, soldiers, servants, tradesmen. Most of your population.
- **Underclass.** Exiles, serfs, the enslaved, the shunned.

Every book needs to decide where each character sits, and how mobile the pyramid is. Can a commoner become a noble? Can a noble be demoted? The rules of mobility tell your reader how big your world's obstacle is.

## The Class Obstacle

If your couple is class-crossed, the worldbuilding has to support the obstacle.

Ask yourself:

- **How wide is the gap?** Princess and stable boy is a wider gap than baron's daughter and knight. The wider the gap, the harder the earn.
- **What forbids the pairing?** Law, custom, marriage rules, family obligation, political expectation?
- **What's the cost of crossing?** Exile, disinheritance, execution, social ruin, war?
- **Who objects, specifically?** Fathers, councils, the church, the court, the people?
- **Is crossing possible?** Can class be dissolved by marriage, by bond, by war, by magic?

A class obstacle without a named enforcer is soft. Readers need to see who's going to punish the couple for their choice.

## The Mobility Question

Some romantasy worlds are rigid: your birth decides your whole life. Some are fluid: a commoner can rise to the court. Pick one.

- **Rigid.** Feels like medieval Europe, feudal China, or a strict caste system. Class obstacle is large; dismantling it is a major arc.
- **Somewhat mobile.** Most fantasy worlds. Commoners can become court mages, knights, captains. Marriage between classes is rare but possible.
- **Fluid.** Merchant-class or meritocratic. Academy-based worlds (*Fourth Wing*) often use this. Class matters but doesn't determine.

Yours will probably sit in the middle. Name what's rigid and what's mobile.

## The Economy Skeleton

You need enough economy to make the world feel real without building spreadsheets.

Six questions.

1. **What's the main currency?** Coin (gold, silver, copper)? Trade goods? A credit system? Something magical?
2. **What's the main export or wealth source for your kingdom?** Grain, salt, iron, wool, magic, dragons, court favour?
3. **What's scarce?** Food, water, magic, good weather, peace? Scarcity drives plot.
4. **What's abundant?** The commoner's staple. Bread, fish, wine, certain herbs.
5. **Who taxes whom?** The crown taxes the nobility, the nobility taxes the peasants, the church takes a tenth. Follow the money up.
6. **What's the underground economy?** Smuggling, thieving, magic black market, court bribery. Where does your rogue character work?

Five-minute answers are enough. You're not writing a textbook.

## The Daily Experience Of Class

Class shows in small things.

- What a character wears (fabric, dye, condition).
- What she eats (grain-heavy peasant fare vs. imported delicacies).
- How she travels (on foot, horseback, carriage, litter).
- How she's addressed (first name, "my lady", full honorific).
- What she can read (literacy is class-coded in most fantasy worlds).
- Who sits while she stands, and vice versa.

A heroine who eats cheese and bread and walks to market is a different class than one who eats quail and rides in a closed carriage. Show class through sensory detail; don't announce it.

## The Wealth Of The Couple

Where do your two leads sit?

- Same-class couple: the romance obstacle is elsewhere (faction, species, bond). Class is texture.
- Class-crossed couple: the romance obstacle includes class itself. Worldbuilding has to carry it.
- Outside-class couple: both are outsiders (exiles, academy students, mercenaries). The obstacle is partly that they can't return to their own class.

Romantasy loves the second option. A high-status lover and a common-born heroine (or vice versa) is one of the genre's evergreen engines.

## The Social Mobility Event

If your book ends with class resolved (a commoner becomes a queen, a disinherited prince regains his title), there has to be a mechanism. Not deus ex machina. A mechanism.

- War (who's alive at the end gets the throne).
- Magic (a true name, a bond, a prophecy fulfilled).
- Marriage (cross-class marriage legitimised).
- Rebellion (the commoners win).
- Adoption into nobility (earned, gifted, or magical).

Name your mechanism by the midpoint. If your reader can't see how the class gap closes, she won't trust the HEA.

## Romantasy Class Cross Pairings

A browsable reference of the genre's common class-cross pairings, with comp titles for each. Steal one, or pick the shape your book is closest to.

| Her Class | His Class | The Obstacle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commoner / villager | Prince, High Lord, alpha | Forbidden by birth; court objects | *ACOTAR*, *From Blood and Ash* |
| Scribe, scholar, cadet | Heir, general's son, exiled noble | Ambition vs. inherited rank | *Fourth Wing*, *The Shadows Between Us* |
| Bastard, disinherited | True-blood heir | Legitimacy politics; inheritance | *Crescent City*, *Priory of the Orange Tree* |
| Thief, smuggler, outlaw | Lawman, prince, captain | Law itself forbids them | *The Cruel Prince*, *Six of Crows* |
| Kidnapped / captive | Captor, enemy king | Power gap; consent weight | *Captive Prince*, *Plated Prisoner* |
| Merchant's daughter | Court noble | Trade vs. blood nobility | Regency-coded romantasy |
| Priestess / temple-bound | Secular warrior or royal | Vow-forbidden pairing | *The Jasmine Throne*, *A Shadow in the Ember* |
| Human | Fae, vampire, god | Species is also class | *ACOTAR* (reversed direction), *A Touch of Darkness* |
| Fae noble | Human scholar or artisan | Species-down pairing; lifespan gap | Less common; emerging strand |
| Pack omega / low-rank | Alpha or pack leader | Omegaverse class politics | Shifter romantasy |
| Rebel / outlaw | Crown official | Political class; treason stakes | *Fourth Wing*, *Throne of Glass* |
| Exile / outsider | Native royalty | Not-of-this-world obstacle | *The Bridge Kingdom*, *A Court of Thorns and Roses* |

Romance angle: two of these pairings stacked (exile daughter of a disgraced general paired with an exiled son of an executed rebel) is *Fourth Wing*-calibre layered obstacle. Stacking is where romantasy class work gets interesting.

## The Class Mobility Mechanisms

If your book closes the class gap, you need a mechanism. Here's a menu.

- **War victory.** She's on the winning side; the old order falls; she rises by default.
- **Royal decree.** The king (or a new king) legitimises the pairing. Political, revocable, tense.
- **Magical transformation.** Bond, Cauldron, ascension, god-blessing. She becomes of his kind (section 41).
- **Marriage with dispensation.** Cross-class marriage with a specific waiver. Usually morganatic (children can't inherit).
- **Adoption into nobility.** A patron raises her. Debt attached; she owes.
- **Rebellion wins.** The commoners take the throne; class itself is remade.
- **Return of lost heritage.** She was secretly highborn all along. The birthright reveal.
- **Exile together.** The gap doesn't close; they leave the system. (See section 47, the rule-holds-they-defy ending.)

Pick your mechanism by the midpoint. Ratify it on the page. The reader needs to see the gap close, not just be told it did.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Where does your heroine sit on the class pyramid? One line.

**[L1]** Where does your love interest sit? One line.

**[L1]** Is your couple class-crossed? If yes, what forbids the pairing, specifically?

**[L2]** Answer the six economy questions above. One line each.

**[L2]** Pick three sensory details that show your heroine's class in the opening chapter. What she eats, wears, walks on, sits on, or says.

**[L3]** If your book closes a class gap, name the mechanism that closes it. Write the scene, or at least the shape of the scene, where the gap is paid.

**[L3]** Consider your world's underclass (exiles, slaves, the shunned, the broken). Who are they? Does your book acknowledge them? Romantasy that ignores its underclass often reads as unexamined. A single scene, a single line of dialogue, a single glimpse gives your world moral weight.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's class system is military-academic and fluid in principle (cadets are ranked by merit and dragon-bonding), rigid in practice (politics flows from family alliances). Violet is a general's daughter; Xaden is a disinherited son whose father was executed. Their class obstacle is partly inherited (her mother killed his father) and partly institutional (their futures in the war depend on whose faction wins). The mobility is real but costly.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses a clean class structure: High Fae (court-ruling), lesser fae (within-court servants and warriors), and humans (outside the fae hierarchy entirely). Feyre's human-to-High-Fae transformation is also a class leap; becoming High Lady of the Night Court is the class gap resolved in full. The mechanism is magical (the Cauldron's rebirth) and political (Rhys naming her his equal). Both required. A useful reminder that a class gap resolution needs both a cause and a ratification.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** A class obstacle that the world doesn't enforce isn't really there. If your heroine is a peasant and your love interest is a prince, someone in your book has to try to stop them. A disapproving father, a scandalised court, a rival noble, a scheming queen. Name the enforcer. She's a character you haven't built yet.
