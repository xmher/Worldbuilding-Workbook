# 49. Fae And Faerie Court

Fae romantasy is the flagship of the genre. ACOTAR built the modern shelf. Holly Black, Julie Kagawa, Grace Draven, and a hundred indie writers feed it. Your reader arrives with a deep set of expectations, some of which she wants fulfilled, some of which she wants subverted.

This section is about designing your fae court so it feels both familiar and yours.

## The Conventions Your Reader Knows

Before you design, know what she's carrying.

- **Beauty and danger together.** Fae are beautiful. Fae are dangerous. A fae who's only one or only the other reads wrong.
- **Bargains that bind.** A deal struck with a fae is law. The word given becomes the rule.
- **Names have power.** A true name, freely given, is a kind of surrender.
- **The lie rule.** Many fae traditions say fae can't lie. They can mislead, twist, omit, but not speak a flat untruth.
- **Iron hurts.** The metal of the mortal world wounds them.
- **Time moves differently.** A night in the fae court is a year in the mortal realm. Or a century. Or none at all.
- **Food binds.** Eat the food, drink the wine, and you're tethered to the court.
- **Glamour hides.** Fae show you what they want you to see. The true face is another thing.

You don't need all eight. Pick four or five and build them with specificity. Name which conventions you're using, which you're bending, and which your book is quietly refusing.

## The Court Structure

Most fae romantasy organises fae into courts. Decide your shape.

- **Seelie and Unseelie.** The old split. Summer and Winter, Light and Dark, Bright and Shadowed. Moral-coded or not, as you choose.
- **Seasonal courts.** Four or eight, each with its season, its weather, its politics.
- **Element courts.** Earth, water, fire, air, shadow, light.
- **Directional or regional courts.** North, South, East, West, with their own lords and traditions.
- **A single court.** Simpler, but loses the faction play that court-romantasy loves.

Your court count is a plot choice. Seven courts gives you seven potential rivals, seven possible alliances, seven settings, seven High Lords. One court gives you a tighter chamber piece.

## The Ruler Title

Your fae ruler has a title. It matters.

- **High Lord, High Lady.** Maas's term, now widely borrowed.
- **King, Queen.** The older, plainer register. Holly Black uses it.
- **Prince, Princess of the [Court].** A title that suggests hierarchy above.
- **Archon, Regent, Warden.** Less borrowed; room for you to make it yours.

Whatever you pick, decide the plural. High Lords gather in council. Fae Kings don't; they send envoys. The title shapes the politics.

## The Bargain

Your most useful fae device. Design it.

- **What seals a bargain.** A spoken word? A handclasp? Blood? A kiss? A drink shared?
- **What breaks it.** Fulfillment. Death. Sometimes nothing breaks it, ever.
- **What happens if it's broken.** A mark that appears. A geas that compels. A death that comes.
- **Who can strike one.** Any fae? Only High Lords? Anyone invited to court?

Your heroine's bargain with your love interest is probably a plot rail. What did she promise? What did he? What's the way out? Design the bargain before you write the scene where it's struck.

## The True Name

True names bind. Decide yours.

- **Does every fae have one?** Or only the old ones, or only royalty?
- **What can you do with a true name?** Command? Summon? Kill? Merely locate?
- **How's it learned?** Freely given, stolen, guessed, inherited?
- **Can it be changed?** Some fae traditions say a fae can set aside his true name through ritual. Some say never.

A love interest who gives his true name to your heroine is a romance beat at the scale of a wedding. Don't waste it. Earn it.

## The Lie Rule

If your fae can't lie, that's a rule you'll use constantly.

- **What counts as a lie?** A direct untruth? A deliberate misdirection? A promise one doesn't intend to keep?
- **What are the workarounds?** Implication, silence, technical truth, changing the subject, answering a different question.
- **Can half-fae lie?** Can humans who've lived long in court?
- **Does the rule bind in writing, or only spoken word?**

A fae who can't lie is a fae whose every sentence carries weight. Your reader learns to watch his phrasing. Your heroine learns to listen for the gap. That's a whole layer of tension.

If you're not using the lie rule, decide that clearly. A fae who can lie freely is a different creature, and your reader needs to know.

## The Glamour

What does your fae look like, and what does she let you see?

- **Default glamour.** The everyday disguise (often a plainer version of the true self, pointed ears hidden, eyes softened).
- **Court glamour.** The ceremonial version (full beauty, full strangeness, on display).
- **War glamour.** The terrifying version (claws, fangs, wings extended, voice changed).
- **The unglamoured self.** What she looks like when she's not hiding anything. Usually seen only by those she trusts.

The moment your love interest drops his glamour for your heroine is a love scene without a kiss. Design it.

## The Iron Question

If iron hurts your fae, decide how much.

- **Fatal on touch.** Rare in modern romantasy; very restrictive.
- **Burns on touch.** Your fae avoids iron but can endure brief contact.
- **Weakens at proximity.** Your fae loses power in iron's presence.
- **Not a rule in your world.** You've dropped the convention. State it clearly.

The heroine who carries an iron dagger in a fae court is a different heroine from one who doesn't. Decide before you arm her.

## The Time Distortion

Does time move differently in your fae realm?

- **Yes, and it's dangerous.** Mortals who enter return decades displaced.
- **Yes, but it's managed.** Certain crossings are timed; certain paths hold time steady.
- **Only in deep fae.** The surface courts run on mortal time; the old places don't.
- **No.** You've dropped the convention. Time's the same. State it.

If your heroine crosses into fae and returns, has her world moved on without her? That's a plot rail. If it hasn't, your reader needs to know the convention's been set aside.

## The Food Rule

Does eating fae food bind the eater?

- **Yes, permanently.** One bite and the mortal is changed, tethered, fading.
- **Yes, for a time.** Eating binds the eater to the court for a season, a year, a bargain's length.
- **Only specific food.** Certain fruits, certain wines. The rest is safe.
- **No.** Eat freely. The rule's been set aside.

The first meal your heroine takes in fae hands is a scene. Design the rule, then stage the moment.

## The Mortal-Fae Question

Is your heroine fully fae, fully mortal, or somewhere between?

- **Fully mortal.** Her mortality's a plot issue. She ages; he doesn't. The grief is built in.
- **Half-fae.** She moves between worlds and belongs fully to neither.
- **Changed.** She started mortal and became fae (the ACOTAR model). The change is the cost of love.
- **Fully fae.** A same-species romance; the mortal world is backdrop, not axis.

This choice drives half your plot. A mortal heroine with a fae love interest is a ticking clock. A fully fae heroine can be a political player on equal footing. Pick the version your story needs.

## The Court Politics

Fae court means court politics. Know the layers.

- **The inner circle.** Who's in it? How do you join it? What binds it?
- **The rivals.** Other courts, other High Lords, other factions within your own court.
- **The throne.** Who's on it, who wants it, who's been passed over, who's returning?
- **The old wrongs.** Every fae court has centuries of grievance. Name two or three that matter to your book.

A fae court without politics is a fae court without plot. Stack these layers thickly; your reader expects it.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your court or courts. If multiple, sketch the essence of each in one line.

**[L1]** Pick which four or five conventions from the list at the top of this section your book is using. Name which you're bending.

**[L1]** Write the ruler title. What's the plural? What's the form of address?

**[L2]** Draft the bargain your heroine and love interest strike early in the book. What does each promise? What's the cost of breaking it? Three paragraphs.

**[L2]** Write the glamour-drop scene. Your love interest shows your heroine his true face. Where, why, what does she see? 300 words.

**[L3]** Design your court's political web in half a page. Three courts or factions; two old wrongs; one recent crisis. This is your plot backdrop.

**[L3]** Write the food rule scene. The first meal your heroine takes from a fae hand. What does she know about the rule, what does she risk, what does she choose? 500 words.

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's seven courts (Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter, Day, Dawn, Night) give her a mappable board with seven High Lords, seven politics, seven settings. Her bargains are tattooed marks; her true names are weapons; her mortal-to-fae transformation is the engine of the first book's climax and the second book's grief. She uses glamour sparingly (Rhys's real power, hidden behind the Under-the-Mountain performance) but when she deploys it, she deploys it hard. Her lie rule is softer than the older fae tradition (Prythian fae can lie), which frees her dialogue and shifts the tension to bargains and bonds.

*Case study: The Cruel Prince.* Holly Black holds the older rules. Her fae can't lie. Her bargains are binding. Her iron hurts. Jude is a mortal raised in fae court, and her disadvantage (she can be lied to, she can be bound by food, she bleeds on iron) becomes her weapon: she learns to wield these rules against the beings who own them. Cardan, her love interest, is fae and can't lie directly; the scenes where he must answer her honestly are some of the sharpest in the trilogy. Black's fae are dangerous in a way Maas's aren't: they feel like the old folklore wearing court dress. A useful study if you want the older register.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The moment the fae love interest tells the mortal heroine a truth he can't lie about, in language he's tried to avoid, is the midpoint of your book. The rule did the work. The bargain, the true name, the glamour drop, the lie he couldn't tell: each of these is a romance beat your world gives you for free. Build the rules. The scenes come built in.
