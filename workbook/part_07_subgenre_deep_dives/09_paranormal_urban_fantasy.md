# 57. Paranormal And Urban Fantasy

Paranormal romance sits adjacent to romantasy, sharing readers, conventions, and shelves. *Mercy Thompson*, *Kate Daniels*, *Guild Hunter*, *Hollows* series. The world's usually ours, with supernatural layered in. The romance is central but often arc-spanning across a series rather than a single book.

If your book is paranormal or urban fantasy, you're in a neighbouring craft tradition. Know what the shelf wants.

## The Setting

Paranormal and urban fantasy usually sit on or near our world.

- **Our world, hidden supernatural.** Magic and creatures exist; most humans don't know. A masquerade.
- **Our world, open supernatural.** The paranormal is known, public, politically present.
- **Our world, post-reveal.** Something happened; the supernatural came out. The book's set in the aftermath.
- **Alternate earth.** Close to ours but diverged. Magic's always been present.
- **Secondary-world urban.** Not our earth, but recognisable urban register (streets, apartments, coffee shops, modern professions).

Pick one. The masquerade frame gives you the classic paranormal setup. The open frame lets you write supernatural politics directly. The post-reveal frame is its own sub-shelf with its own tensions.

## The City

Urban fantasy in particular is often tied to a specific city. Pick yours.

- **A real city.** New Orleans, Seattle, Atlanta, London, Prague. The city's real geography's available to you.
- **A named stand-in.** A fictional city modelled on a real one, with freedom to invent.
- **A fictional city with specific flavour.** Built from scratch; memorable shape.
- **A nomadic book.** The protagonist moves; the city changes.

The city's a character in this subgenre. Your reader wants to feel the fog of this specific Seattle, the summer of this specific Atlanta. Pick one you can write with confidence.

## The Protagonist's Profession

Urban fantasy heroines usually have a job. Define yours.

- **Investigator, PI, or detective.** Mysteries structure the book.
- **Bounty hunter or mercenary.** Contract work gives you episodic plot shape.
- **Witch, mage, healer.** A magical practitioner whose profession is the magic itself.
- **Cop or federal agent.** Institutional power, conflict with it or from within it.
- **Shifter, vampire, or creature with day job.** A supernatural with an ordinary profession (Mercy's a mechanic).
- **Reluctant specialist.** She didn't ask to be the expert, but she's the one in the room who knows.

The profession gives the book structure. A PI book runs on cases. A bounty hunter book runs on contracts. A witch book runs on consultations or covens. Pick one that gives you episodes or through-lines.

## The Supernatural Community

The world behind the masquerade, or beside it.

- **Vampires.** Almost always present. Often organised (clans, courts, nests).
- **Shifters.** Wolves, big cats, bears. Pack-structured.
- **Witches and mages.** Covens, orders, traditions.
- **Fae.** Hidden courts, often with the older folklore register.
- **Demons, angels, divine beings.** A religious layer.
- **Ghosts, the dead.** Often present; usually limited.
- **Necromancers, psychics, empaths.** Specialist humans with power.

Pick which exist in your world. Three or four species is usually enough. Give each a political position and a rough relationship to the others. A vampire-werewolf truce is a paranormal staple; use it if you want it; decide if you don't.

## The Rules Of Magic

Urban fantasy tends to have specific magic rules, often published or semi-public. Build yours.

- **Who can do it.** Genetic, trained, cursed, chosen?
- **What it costs.** Energy, blood, years, sanity, specific components?
- **What it can and can't do.** Healing? Killing? Scrying? Compelling? Necromancy?
- **How it's regulated.** Is there a council? Is there law? Is there tradition only?
- **How it intersects with technology.** Does magic work near computers? Does the masquerade extend to keeping magic off the internet?

Your magic rules should be slightly more laid out in urban fantasy than in secondary-world romantasy. The reader wants to understand the system, because the world's close enough to ours that inconsistency jars.

## The Romance Arc Across A Series

Paranormal and urban fantasy often use arc-spanning romance rather than one-book-one-romance.

- **Slow-burn across the series.** The romance develops over three, five, ten books. The first book may barely contain a kiss.
- **HFN per book, HEA across the series.** Each book closes its arc; the final book resolves the romance.
- **One romance, one couple, ongoing.** They're together early; the series is their continuing adventures and challenges.
- **Multiple romances, rotating.** Different books centre different couples; the world's the throughline. (Black Dagger Brotherhood shape.)

Pick your series arc. It shapes everything about the pacing of your first book. A slow-burn-across-five-books needs a first book that doesn't close the romance; a one-couple-ongoing needs a first book that establishes the pair and builds series texture.

## The Stakes That Escalate

Urban fantasy plots escalate across a series. Plan.

- **Book one.** Local. A case, a monster, a crisis in the protagonist's neighbourhood.
- **Book two or three.** Regional. The city, its supernatural politics, a wider threat.
- **Book four or five.** National or continental. Governments, major institutions, large-scale conflict.
- **Book six and on.** Cosmic. Gods, ancient threats, world-shaking stakes.

A book one that opens with the apocalypse leaves you nowhere to go. Start small; let the stakes grow. Your romance's intensity can echo the stakes' escalation: book-one flirtation, book-five bond, book-ten mate.

## The Day-Job Texture

Urban fantasy's best texture is the ordinary life running alongside the supernatural.

- **The coffee shop.** Where she works, eats, meets clients.
- **The apartment.** Small, specific, lived-in. Her wards, her salt lines, her cat.
- **The car.** Her vehicle's a character (Mercy's VW, Kate's Prokopi horse).
- **The neighbours.** Human, supernatural, aware or not.
- **The phone, email, bills.** The modern administrative layer. Her inbox has vampires in it.

The texture of ordinary life making room for the extraordinary is what the subgenre reads for. Build it. A werewolf standing in the cereal aisle is the genre's whole shape.

## The Paranormal Romance-Paranormal Versus Urban Fantasy Split

A distinction worth knowing.

- **Paranormal romance.** Romance-centred. A love story with supernatural elements. Usually one couple per book. Romance dominates the plot. Closer to the romance shelf.
- **Urban fantasy.** Plot-centred. A heroine with a case or mission, in a supernatural world, with a romance often running as a thread. Closer to fantasy shelf.

Your book sits somewhere on this spectrum. Closer to paranormal romance, the romance is the whole plot. Closer to urban fantasy, the romance is a major subplot. Both are valid. Decide which you're writing; your shelf placement depends on it.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Setting: our world hidden, our world open, post-reveal, alternate, secondary urban?

**[L1]** Pick your city and your protagonist's profession.

**[L1]** Name your supernatural species roster. Three to five.

**[L2]** Write your protagonist's apartment or home in specific detail. Wards, mess, the cat, the kitchen, the thing on the shelf. 300 words.

**[L2]** Plan your series romance arc. One book? Three books? Ten? Where's the first kiss? Where's the HEA?

**[L3]** Draft book one's escalating stakes. What's the local case? What bigger thing does it connect to? How does book two inherit the problem? One page.

**[L3]** Write a day-in-the-life scene: an ordinary morning in your heroine's supernatural life. 500 words. The texture's the point.

---

*Case study: Mercy Thompson series.* Patricia Briggs's Mercedes Thompson is a VW mechanic in the Tri-Cities, a coyote shifter raised by werewolves, caught between pack politics and her own quiet life. Briggs's world is layered: werewolves, fae, vampires, gremlins, ghosts, with distinct politics and intersecting threats. Her romance with Adam, the pack alpha, develops across several books before committing; the mating bond is earned across pages. Briggs's texture is her signature: the garage, the car she's working on, the way a day-to-day paranormal life looks. The escalation across the series is visible: book one's local problem opens onto book six's continent-scale threat. A clean model for writers building a long urban-fantasy series with a single ongoing couple.

*Case study: Kate Daniels series.* Ilona Andrews writes a post-shift Atlanta where magic and technology alternate in waves, and Kate's a mercenary with a hidden bloodline that eventually threatens the world. The romance with Curran, the Beast Lord, is slow-burn across two books and then the centre of the series for the next eight. Andrews's world is tightly built: the shifter Pack, the Masters of the Dead (vampires), the Guild, the Order of Merciful Aid, each politically distinct. Her escalation is steep (book one: a kidnapping; book ten: a god war), and her romance arcs parallel the stakes arc with precision. A useful study in pacing a decade-long romance inside an urban-fantasy frame.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** In urban fantasy, your romance has to survive the case. Your heroine doesn't get to stay in bed. She gets up, goes to the scene, deals with the client. The romance that fits inside her life (not the one that replaces it) is the one the subgenre wants. Write him into her work, her mess, her 3 a.m. calls. Let him meet her on her ground, because she isn't leaving it.
