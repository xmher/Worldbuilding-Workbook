# 26. Taboos And Prohibitions

Every culture has rules that aren't laws. Things you don't say. Topics you don't raise. Doors you don't open. Dishes you don't serve. Words you don't speak in certain rooms.

Taboos are where your world gets texture the reader can't fake her way through. They're also where your couple gets into interesting trouble.

## Why Taboos Matter

Four reasons your book needs them.

1. **They make your world specific.** Every culture has a quiet list of "we don't do that". Name yours, and your reader can tell your world from a generic fantasy.
2. **They generate scenes.** A taboo broken is a scene. A taboo narrowly avoided is a scene. A taboo weaponised by a rival is a scene.
3. **They mark the outsider.** A character who doesn't know the taboo announces she's from elsewhere. Your heroine's first embarrassing error tells the reader where she's from and where she's not.
4. **They show character.** Who breaks taboos casually, who clings to them, who weaponises them, who quietly ignores them.

Every romantasy should have three to five named taboos in play.

## The Types Of Taboo

- **Speech taboos.** Don't speak a name. Don't discuss the king's succession at table. Don't ask a fae their true name. Don't say "luck" before a battle.
- **Behaviour taboos.** Don't stand while the queen speaks. Don't offer your left hand. Don't cross the threshold uninvited. Don't touch a bonded's marked skin.
- **Relational taboos.** Don't marry within the bloodline. Don't love across the species. Don't bed your brother's widow. Don't court the priestess.
- **Sacred taboos.** Don't enter the grove. Don't look upon the god's face. Don't eat the fruit. Don't write the true prayer down.
- **Magical taboos.** Don't use the old magic. Don't summon what can't be dismissed. Don't take a name without giving one. Don't break a bargain.
- **Practical taboos.** Don't whistle below deck. Don't eat meat on the sacred day. Don't pass a bowl with your left hand. Don't wear red at a funeral.

Your world can have a mix. Pick at least one from three different types.

## The Taboo Your Book Uses

Pick the taboo your plot breaks, or nearly breaks. This is usually a relational or magical taboo.

- Loving across the species line (fae-human, shifter-human, vampire-human).
- Loving across the faction line (war-factions, rival courts, sworn-enemy families).
- Using forbidden magic.
- Speaking a name nobody's supposed to know.
- Crossing a boundary (physical, sacred, political) that's not meant to be crossed.

Your central obstacle (section 10) usually includes a taboo. Make sure the taboo has teeth: who enforces it, what's the cost of breaking, who's watching.

## The Enforcement Layer

Taboos only matter if they're enforced.

- **Social enforcement.** Shunning, gossip, lost reputation, broken alliances. Low-grade but persistent.
- **Legal enforcement.** Fines, imprisonment, exile, execution. Depends on whose law.
- **Religious enforcement.** Excommunication, curse, purification ritual, loss of magical status.
- **Magical enforcement.** The magic itself punishes. The bargain breaks; the true name stops working; the old blood rises and strikes.
- **Personal enforcement.** A specific character enforces the taboo. A father, a mother, a rival, a sworn guardian.

Name the enforcer of your central taboo. That's a character your book needs.

## Small Taboos As Texture

Beyond the big plot-driving taboo, your world should have a few small ones that signal culture.

- At the Night Court, it's bad manners to ask a High Lord about his mother.
- At Basgiath, you don't say a rider's name aloud before they've been presented to their dragon.
- In the Seelie court, you don't offer your name to a stranger.
- Among the Valley clans, you don't accept food offered by your left hand.

These small taboos can be mentioned once, broken by your heroine in chapter two, and referenced again in chapter fifteen. Each use is free cultural weight.

## The Outsider Scene

If your heroine is an outsider to her love interest's culture, her first faux pas is a scene.

- She offers her left hand and his sister flinches.
- She asks after his mother, and the whole court goes quiet.
- She sits in the wrong chair, and his brother gives her a look that promises trouble.
- She eats the wrong thing at the banquet, and he has to lean over and whisper the correct etiquette.

Romantasy loves the outsider-discovers-the-culture scene. Plan two or three.

## The Insider Scene

Equally useful: a scene where your heroine knows the taboo her companions don't. She's among strangers and understands, better than they do, what's dangerous to say, to do, to wear.

This flips the power. She's suddenly the guide. The love interest notices.

## The Taboo As Flirtation

Some taboos, broken deliberately, are flirtation.

- Using her first name in public (when only lovers do so).
- Touching her elbow (when only bonded partners touch).
- Offering her food from his plate (when the culture treats shared plates as intimate).

A love interest who breaks a small taboo to make a claim on your heroine is a scene the genre loves. Put one in.

## Enforcement Menu

A reference for who or what enforces your taboos. Pick the enforcer type for each taboo; name at least one specific person or power.

| Enforcer | How It Works | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|
| Social (gossip, shunning) | Reputation lost; alliances break | The court whispering arc; the cold-shoulder scene |
| Legal (law and court) | Fines, imprisonment, exile, execution | Trial scene; sentencing; rescue |
| Religious (priest, god) | Excommunication, curse, loss of grace | Priest's threat; god's wrath |
| Magical (self-enforcing) | The bargain breaks; the bond burns; the name stops working | Cost-of-magic scene; the price is paid |
| Personal (named enforcer) | A specific character punishes | The father-rival; the rival queen |
| Institutional (academy, order) | Rules of the body; expulsion | Expulsion scene; dishonour arc |
| Family (patriarch, matriarch) | Domestic authority enforces | The disapproving-parent obstacle |
| Pack / clan | Pack rule; alpha enforces | Omega protection; pack exile |
| Crown / sovereign | Royal decree; sovereign's word | The king's line in the sand |
| No one (drift taboo) | Nobody enforces; the rule persists by habit | Breakable without cost; signal of culture change |

Name your enforcer by midpoint. A taboo without an enforcer is a soft obstacle. "My father will disown me" is harder than "this is just not done."

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name three taboos in your world. One each from three different types (speech, behaviour, relational, sacred, magical, practical).

**[L1]** Which taboo does your couple's romance break, or threaten to break? Name the enforcer.

**[L2]** Write the outsider scene: your heroine (or her love interest, if he's the outsider) commits a taboo breach by accident. Who notices? What's the reaction? One paragraph.

**[L2]** Write the insider scene: your heroine correctly navigates a taboo her companions miss. Who's impressed? What does it tell them about her?

**[L3]** Write the scene where a small taboo is broken deliberately as flirtation. Two or three hundred words. What's the taboo? Who breaks it? How does the other react?

**[L3]** List the taboos of a culture your book doesn't centre on (the rival kingdom, the neighbouring court, the lost country). Your world should have more depth than the reader ever sees; this is one way to create that depth.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's taboos are institutional: you don't kill your squadmates in public (you do it on the sly); you don't touch another rider's dragon; you don't speak the king's name lightly; you don't wear relics where they can be seen by the wrong people (Xaden's relic-marked skin is one of his most carefully-guarded taboos, and its eventual reveal is a plot beat). The enforcement is mixed: academy rules, magical consequence (touching a dragon who doesn't want you touched costs you), and political reprisal.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's taboos shift by court. In the Night Court, you don't let outsiders see Velaris (a secret-city taboo whose breach would be catastrophic). In the Spring Court, you don't question the High Lord's rule. In the Day Court, you don't disturb a scholar at work. The mate bond itself is partly governed by taboo: a High Lord who hides his mate bond from her (Rhys hiding the bond from Feyre) is breaking a cultural expectation that the genre handles through Mor's later scolding. The taboo isn't magical; it's cultural, and Maas uses that distinction well.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Pick one taboo your couple breaks together and pays for. Not narrowly avoids, breaks. The scene where they do it is the scene where their commitment becomes real: they've chosen each other over the culture that raised them. Write that scene before the climax.
