# 25. Festivals, Holy Days, Rituals

A festival is a calendar beat plus a plot engine. You named the days in section 14. Now you design what happens on them.

Rituals are the smaller version: private, family, bond-specific. A courtship ritual, a funeral rite, a mating ceremony (section 34), a mark-giving (section 35).

Both give you structured scenes with built-in meaning. Your reader feels the weight before the scene does any work.

## What A Festival Does

Three kinds of work.

1. **It gathers characters.** A festival brings everyone who matters into one place. Useful for court books, ensemble books, and reunion scenes.
2. **It lowers the guard.** People who wouldn't meet do meet, because the festival opens doors that are usually closed. Strangers dance with strangers. Enemies drink from the same cup.
3. **It raises the magic.** Many romantasy festivals are when the magic surges, the veils thin, or the bond-pulls strengthen. The magic does work that ordinary time doesn't allow.

Your book doesn't need five festivals. It needs two, maybe three, chosen for what they let you do.

## The Festival Types

- **Sacred day.** Religious ceremony. Prayers, sacrifices, blessings. The community renews its relationship with its gods.
- **Seasonal turn.** Solstice, equinox, harvest, first snow. The wheel of the year, expressed as celebration.
- **Mating rite.** Calanmai in ACOTAR, spring pack gatherings, the fae Rites. Explicit or implicit, the festival where pairs form.
- **Court gathering.** The annual council, the summit, the summer-at-the-capital. Political, hierarchical, alliance-forming.
- **Victory or commemoration.** The anniversary of a battle, a king's ascension, a liberation. Tells the reader the history.
- **Private festival or custom.** A family's tradition, a household's annual feast, a pack's yearly hunt.

Pick the festival types that serve your plot. A mating rite is doing very different work than a court gathering. You can have one of each. You don't need every type.

## Design Your Primary Festival

The single most-important festival in your book. Design it.

- **Name.** What do people call it?
- **When.** What day, what season, what cycle?
- **Scale.** Local, regional, realm-wide?
- **Ritual structure.** Morning ceremony, day activities, evening feast, night ritual. What happens in what order?
- **Who attends.** Everyone? Only certain classes? Only the bonded, only the unbonded, only the faithful?
- **What's forbidden.** Every festival has taboos. Work, speech, weapons, certain names, certain colours.
- **What's required.** Every festival has obligations. Prayer, dress, song, contribution, a specific act.
- **What changes after.** The festival marks a transition. What's different when it's over?
- **Its scene in your book.** Where in the plot does it land? What happens to your couple during it?

A festival page in your notebook is half a scene already written.

## The Calanmai Move

ACOTAR's Calanmai is worth studying. It does the following at once.

- Anchors the world's magical cycle (Fae magic surges at the Spring equinox).
- Introduces the love interest (Rhysand first meets Feyre at the Rite, masked).
- Creates a moral complication (the High Lord's rite involves choosing a partner; the consent-questions are live).
- Establishes a returnable beat (the Rite happens every year; Maas returns to the idea in later books).
- Gives the reader a dense scene of cultural specificity (the fires, the masks, the music, the wine).

A festival that does all five jobs is a festival pulling its weight. Aim for at least three.

## The Ritual (Smaller Scale)

Not every scene needs to be a festival. Some are rituals: smaller, private, specific.

- **Courtship rituals.** The proposal dance, the hand-kiss, the formal asking, the exchanged gift (see section 33).
- **Bonding rituals.** Handfasting, blood-oath, mating vow, mark-giving (see section 34).
- **Mourning rituals.** The washing of the body, the night-wake, the burial, the year-ending day.
- **Naming rituals.** Child-naming, coming-of-age, the taking of a warrior name, the revealing of a true name.
- **Pack or court admission.** The oath to the alpha, the fealty to the High Lord, the matriculation to the academy.

Design three rituals your book uses. Name them. Write the shape.

## The Ritual Form

Most rituals follow a pattern. Your reader recognises it even across cultures.

- **The opening.** A word, a gesture, a gathering sign.
- **The offering.** Something given (a vow, an object, a drop of blood, a name).
- **The binding.** A response (the god's, the community's, the magic's, the lover's).
- **The mark.** Something that records the ritual (a scar, a tattoo, a gifted ring, a spoken memory).
- **The closing.** A word, a gesture, a dismissal.

A ritual missing one of these five beats feels incomplete. Your reader's ear picks it up even when she can't name why.

## The Forbidden Ritual

Every world should have at least one ritual nobody performs anymore. Outlawed, dangerous, the old way.

- A blood binding that required three willing souls.
- A mating rite that involved a god.
- A summoning that opened a door that hasn't been closed.

A forbidden ritual is Act Three material. The climax of a romantasy often involves doing something the world has forgotten how to do. Plant the forbidden ritual early. Use it late.

## Ritual Form Reference

Every ritual your book uses should hit these five beats, or the reader's ear picks up the absence. Use this as a quick structural check.

| Beat | What It Looks Like | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | A word, a gesture, a gathering sign | The priestess strikes the bell; the torch is lit |
| Offering | Something given: vow, object, blood, name | A drop of blood on the altar; a ring placed in the bowl |
| Binding | A response: god's, magic's, community's | The flame rises; the witnesses say "It is so" |
| Mark | A recorded trace: scar, tattoo, ring, memory | The couple wear matching bands; a line of paint on her cheek |
| Closing | A word, a gesture, a dismissal | The priestess lowers her hands; "Go, and be well" |

Design your book's key rituals using this spine: name each step, the words or gestures, the thing given, the mark left. A ritual without the five beats feels rushed or fake.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's primary rituals are institutional: Parapet Day (the gauntlet), Threshing (the dragon choice), Presentation, War Games, and Graduation. Each is structured, dangerous, and plot-central. The rituals are the academy calendar, not a religious calendar; they do the gathering and the guard-lowering jobs without needing fae festivals. Her one private ritual is the mating flight of the dragons, which Violet and Xaden experience as a bond-bleed. The institutional beats carry the festivals' work.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Calanmai, Starfall, Winter Solstice, Summer revels, the Rite of the Autumn Court, the Winter Court's celebrations: Maas gives each of her seven courts its own festival, which is partly why her world feels so alive. Starfall (the Night Court's falling-star ritual) is a key romance-beat festival in book two. Calanmai (the Spring Court's equinox rite) is the book one midpoint festival. Each is specific, each does the magical-surge job, and each gives the couple a scene they couldn't have anywhere else.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your festival is where the public world sees your couple in a way they can't undo. A claiming at the rite; a betrothal at the solstice; a scandal at the masquerade. A festival scene is a commitment scene with witnesses. Use it once, use it well.
