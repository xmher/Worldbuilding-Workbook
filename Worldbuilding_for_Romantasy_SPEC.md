# Worldbuilding for Romantasy — Final Outline & Spec
**PlotBrew**

Legend for exercise formats:
- **[TABLE]** = fill-in table (columns specified). Where there are several of a thing (peoples, species, factions), the table ships with 2–3 rows/slots by default — we never tell the user to "duplicate this page."
- **[LINES]** = one or more open-response lines for a sentence/short answer
- **[BOX]** = a larger free-write box for a longer answer or sketch
- **[CHECK]** = checkbox / tick-list selection
- **[R]** = romance-angle prompt (styled differently in layout)
- **Show it** = a marked, skippable block on a strong world-fact question, asking the writer to render that fact as one concrete image (no scene, no couple). Styled as a set-off box in layout.
- **Try a scene** = a rarer marked, skippable block on a strong romance beat, asking for a few lines between the leads, demonstrated via the running sample couple. Styled as a set-off box in layout.

Voice spec (locked): warm first-person PlotBrew voice; British English; contractions; no em dashes; plain not flat; connected sentences, no clipped fragments for drama; no AI filler ("it's worth", "broad strokes", "in a vacuum", anaphora triples). Run every draft through the plain-language discipline before showing.

Design principle (locked): build a real fantasy world first; bring in the romance lens only where it genuinely applies; never tack it on. Romance-relevant worldbuilding (laws, customs, taboos, vows, rituals around love/marriage) IS essential and carries the romantasy weight — not a spice meter.

Companion product note: a separate comprehensive Notion template will cover every exhaustive detail, so THIS workbook stays lean without guilt.

## SECTION 0 — Primer: How to Use This Workbook
*Purpose: set the lens and grant permission to skip. ~One page. No exercises.*

- **Opening framing** — Explainer only.
  - This is a worldbuilding workbook first; good romantasy needs a world that stands on its own, not a thin backdrop. You'll build the world, and where it's genuinely useful we'll look at how it puts pressure on the romance. Romance prompts are marked; skip them, or anything else, freely. Change answers anytime.
  - Genre bar (plain line): if you could remove either the fantasy or the romance and still have a whole story, it isn't romantasy yet.
  - Scope reassurance: readers want a world to sink into, not an encyclopaedia. Build enough to make scenes real.
  - Flag: *Ours*

## SECTION 2 — Lands & Geography
*Purpose: the physical world as the ground everything sits on.*

- **Intro** — geography shapes who lives where and who fights whom; you don't need every river. Canonical light coverage: landforms & water, climate, biomes, settlement. Story-first, not physical-realism-first.
- **How Big Is Your World?**
  - [LINES] One country, several, or somewhere smaller?
  - [TABLE] Region/Nation | Known for  (3 rows default)
  - *Shared*
- **Naming Places** — *RESOLVED: keep build-your-own, anchored in SOUND & FEELING, not -ton/-ford suffixes. Doubles as light language coverage.*
  - Explainer: a name's sound tells the reader who lives there; harsh/consonant vs soft/flowing; a name only has to sound right, not mean something.
  - [TABLE] Sound or part | Feeling it gives | Used in a name  (3 rows)
  - *Ours (reframed)*
- **Climate & Terrain**
  - [LINES] Climate/terrain of main regions and how it shapes daily life (food, building, fears).
  - *RESOLVED: keep weather-shapes-architecture as a short, light note.*
  - *Shared*
- **Resources & Trade** — *light economics coverage.*
  - Explainer: what a land has and lacks drives trade, wealth, war.
  - [TABLE] Region | Has plenty of | Needs from elsewhere | Trades with whom  (3 rows)
  - *Shared*
- **Borders & Barriers**
  - [LINES] What separates regions, and how hard is it to cross?
  - [R] [LINES] A place that would keep your leads apart, or trap them together?
  - One-line example: incompatible geographies (sea-folk vs sky-riders) as a lovers-divided device.
  - *Ours*
- **Do You Need a Map?** — *RESOLVED: keep, with a romantasy angle SoP doesn't have.*
  - Explainer: no, you don't need one; tools exist; but mark the places that carry weight.
  - [BOX] Optional sketch space, prompted to mark emotionally/romantically loaded places (where they meet, where they're forbidden to go, the border between their homelands) — not just rivers and mountains.
  - *Ours (reframed)*

## SECTION 4 — Magic
*Purpose: the full magic system. Our richest romance section.*

- **Intro** — magic needs limits or it kills tension; doesn't need to be complex. Name "worldbuilder's disease" lightly: a handful of meaningful rules beats a hundred spells. Plain Sanderson rule: the more magic solves problems, the more the reader must understand it.
- **Where Magic Comes From**
  - [LINES] Where does magic come from?
  - [CHECK] Source: innate / learned / granted / artifact / bargain / other
  - *Shared*
- **The Rules & The Cost** — costs that recur: physical, mental, resource, social, time. The cost makes the tension.
  - [LINES] What can your magic NOT do?
  - [TABLE] Power/Spell | What it does | What it costs  (3 rows)
  - *Shared*
- **Who Has Magic, Who Doesn't**
  - [LINES] Who can use it, and how do the magicless live alongside them?
  - *Shared*
- **Magic & Power**
  - [LINES] How does having magic affect your standing in society?
  - *Shared*
- **Magic That Pulls People Together or Apart** [R] — THE differentiator. Levers, each spelled out:
  - reacts to emotion; shared/transferred between two people; forbidden (every use a risk); needs physical contact.
  - **The bond / fated-mate link gets its own prominent prompt** (genre-defining): what it does, can it be resisted, what happens if it breaks/backfires, destiny vs choice.
  - [CHECK] Which levers fit your system? (multi-select)
  - [LINES] How could your magic force your leads close, or drive a wedge between them?
  - [R] [LINES] If there's a bond between your leads, what does it do — and can they fight it?
  - *Ours (strongest unique section)*

## SECTION 8 — Religion & Belief
*Purpose: gods/faith and what people hold sacred. Kept its own section, placed beside Culture. NOTE: we deliberately place religion late, not first as some guides advise — most romantasy isn't religion-driven.*

- **Intro** — religion shapes language, custom, law; you don't need a full theology. *Shared*
- **What Do They Believe?** — *RESOLVED: avoid the "god of fire, check" checklist trap. Columns earn their place; add the no-gods option.*
  - Explainer: one god, many, or none; local vs global; do the gods visibly act or is it faith; gods with wants and tempers beat a list of domains.
  - [LINES] What do your people believe, and (if there are gods) what do they want from people? If there are no gods, what's held sacred instead?
  - [TABLE] Deity or sacred power | What they're god of | What they ask of followers | How they're worshipped  (3 rows)
  - *Ours (reworked columns)*
- **Faith in Daily Life**
  - [LINES] How does belief show up day to day? (festivals, taboos, clergy, how devout)
  - *Shared*
- **Vows, Marriage & the Sacred** [R] — oaths/true-names/sacred vows as fresh beats; a vow can be more intimate and more dangerous than a kiss.
  - [LINES] What does marriage or a binding vow mean here, and what would it cost your leads to defy or break one?
  - *Ours*

## SECTION 9 — Culture & Daily Life
*Purpose: the warm sensory detail that makes scenes vivid. The fun section.*

- **Intro** — tip-of-the-iceberg detail readers actually see; the most enjoyable to fill in. *Shared*
- **Food & Drink** — *RESOLVED: lighter than SoP, fun, with a faint scene angle.*
  - Explainer: food signals class, region, occasion; in romantasy it mostly earns its place in scenes (shared meals, a drink pressed into a hand, a dish one lead doesn't know how to eat).
  - [LINES] A comfort food here / what a feast looks like / a drink they're known for.
  - A couple of playful questions (e.g. what would horrify an outsider at the table; what's pressed on guests).
  - *Shared (lightened, angled)*
- **Fashion & Appearance**
  - [LINES] What does dress say about who someone is here?
  - *Shared*
- **The Sound of Things (Language & Arts)** — *light language + arts coverage, folded together.*
  - [LINES] What does the language sound like, and one word/curse/endearment they'd actually say?
  - [LINES] What do people do for fun, and what art or music are they known for?
  - *Ours (folding language+arts into one light section)*
- **Courtship & Customs** [R] — a taboo kiss, an exchanged true name, a courtship ritual generate beats.
  - [LINES] How do people court and marry here? What's expected, what's taboo?
  - [R] [LINES] How do these customs help or hurt your leads specifically?
  - *Ours*
- **Festivals & Gatherings**
  - [LINES] Name a festival or gathering and what happens there.
  - [R] note: a natural setting for a key romance scene.
  - *Shared*

## SECTION 10 — Capstone: Finding the Friction
*Purpose: flip back through the built world and mine it for romantic pressure. The payoff.*

- **Intro** — now the world exists, find the pressure already in it; friction works both ways, often the same feature doing both. Genre principle, plainly: the world shouldn't decorate the love story, it should pressure it, reveal it, or change it.
- **The Central Obstacle**
  - [LINES] In one sentence: the main thing standing between your leads.
  - *Ours*
- **Per-Element Friction Sweep** — the concrete heart of the capstone.
  - [TABLE] World element | Could pull them together... | ...or drive them apart
    - Pre-labelled rows: Geography/distance | Magic & its cost | The bond (if any) | Law | Class | Species/peoples | Religion & vows | History/old wounds | Court/factions
  - *Ours*
- **The Cost of Choosing Each Other**
  - [TABLE] Character | What they lose by choosing the other  (2 rows)
  - *Ours*
- **The Pressure Point**
  - [LINES] When does the pressure peak and force a decision?
  - *Ours*

Audited all 11 sections. Two genuine gaps found and filled: Magic (added what it looks/feels like in use, how it's learned/controlled, what failure looks like, and two bond questions on chosen-vs-forced and what the bond exposes) and Peoples (added a physical-sketch question and a craft/livelihood question before the "what others misjudge" nuance). Question total 97 → 102; Magic 9 → 12 (richest-romance section no longer the thinnest), Peoples 13 → 15. ✔
11. Show-don't-tell layer (post-draft, for absolute-beginner buyers): added because the audit showed plenty of "what it means" and "what it's for" guidance but almost no "what to do with the answer," and a worldbuilding workbook is fundamentally telling — the hard leap for new writers is turning a fact in a box into a moment on the page. Solution is a one-page "Showing, Not Telling" warm-up placed right before The Big Picture, plus two recurring marked/skippable block types (see legend): "Show it" (turn a world-fact into one concrete image, no scene/couple — placed on barrier, peoples-appearance, magic-cost, scandal, taboo) and "Try a scene" (a few lines between the leads on a strong romance beat — placed on the bond, the class divide, the sacred vow). Not applied to a strict number; placed where they make sense and individually removable in layout if a page feels heavy. Sample couple for the "Try a scene" examples: a Hades/Persephone *riff* (not a lift) — a smuggler who runs cargo into a failing realm of the dead, and the buyer she's traded with for years through go-betweens, who turns out to be the one bound to her when an old law catches them both. Chosen after rejecting healer/archivist/warden as AI-cliché; the trade-route-IS-the-relationship structure embodies the brand thesis (worldbuilding carries the romance). Kept mostly as "the smuggler"/"the buyer" so buyers project their own couple. Draft ~6,800 → ~7,850 words; still in SoP's band. ✔

## Competitor measurement (resolved; earlier PDF-extraction attempt had failed)
Counted all four competitors on one method (open question = distinct prompt ending in ?; words exclude page furniture/answer-line underscores). Result: ours ~6,800 words / 102 open questions sits level with School of Plot on length (~6,900 words) but with fewer, deeper questions (SoP ~163, many one-word-answerable e.g. "do they drink milk? from which animal?"; ours nearly all open and consequence-seeking). Well ahead of PlannerPalette Ultimate Fantasy (sparse, ~1,400 words, ~12 real questions). ydb (~9,800 words) is wordier but is a plot/character outline, not worldbuilding. Verdict: lean by design, not thin by accident; the deeper-not-more question style is the defensible differentiator. ✔
