# Show, Don't Tell — A Craft Report for a Romantasy Worldbuilding Workbook

## TL;DR
- **"Show, don't tell" is a useful corrective, not a law.** It rightly stops beginners from info-dumping, labeling emotions, and explaining what should be experienced — but treated as absolute it produces bloated, slow, melodramatic prose. The craft consensus (Lubbock, Gardner, Burroway, Le Guin, plus modern editors and Brandon Taylor) is that strong fiction *alternates constantly* between showing and telling, often within one paragraph.
- **The real skill is choosing among six tools — Show, Tell, Summarize, Imply, Delay, Cut —** based on whether a moment changes emotion, conflict, choice, romance, or consequence. Showing slows the reader on what matters; telling/summary compress connective tissue; implication/delay create curiosity; cutting protects momentum. Use the **narrative camera**: zoom in for the moments that matter, zoom out for transitions, move on past what doesn't matter, cut what serves only the author.
- **For romantasy:** reveal the world *through* the romance and plot, never in lore lectures that bury the tension readers came for. "Lush does not mean slow; immersive does not mean exhaustive." The workbook should reframe its show/tell section around emphasis and pacing, give explicit permission to tell/summarize/imply/delay/cut, and label every prompt by technique so beginners never believe they must dramatize everything.

## Key Findings (Craft Foundation)

**Origins & the famous misquote.** The show/tell distinction descends from Henry James ("Dramatize, dramatize!") and was codified by Percy Lubbock in *The Craft of Fiction* (1921) as "picture" vs. "drama." The Chekhov line ("Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass") is **not actually Chekhov's** — it is a 20th-century paraphrase of his 1886 letter advising *selective significant detail*: "seize on small details, grouping them so that when the reader closes his eyes he gets a picture." Chekhov told and summarized constantly. The misquote matters because it props up the false absolute.

**What the advice prevents (its ~60% of usefulness):** info-dumping; emotion-labeling ("she was sad"); explaining what should be experienced; and abstraction where concrete sensory detail would create belief. Janet Burroway (*Writing Fiction*, the most-used US creative-writing text) frames the goal not as banning words but moving "through these [words] to felt experience."

**Where beginners misunderstand it:** they hear "never tell" / "telling is lazy." Some tools (e.g., AutoCrit: "in 99 percent of cases it's better to show") reinforce the overcorrection. Result: prose that dramatizes neutral logistics and narrates every breath.

**Why it's harmful as a law:** Editor Arlene Prunkl calls it "the big myth of fiction writing." Novelist Brandon Taylor reversed his own former belief that exposition was "cowardly": "exposition is indispensable… absolutely essential to getting at that human resonance that makes the characters of James, Wharton, Austen, and Zola so utterly unforgettable." Austen, Tolstoy, and Dickens are full of telling. An all-showing novel would be unreadable.

**Why they're complementary:** Burroway's metaphor — **summary is "the mortar," scenes are "the bricks."** You can't build with bricks alone. Telling compresses time, orients, and handles connective tissue; showing slows the reader and makes a moment felt.

**Beginner-friendly translation:** *"Show don't tell" really means "don't flatten the moments that matter." Slow down and dramatize what carries emotion, conflict, or meaning; stop explaining feelings you could let the reader feel. Telling is how you move quickly through the dull parts so you have room to slow down on the parts that count. Your job isn't to show everything — it's to know what deserves the spotlight.*

## Details

### 1–3. The Pacing Dial and Sentence-Level Craft

**Showing/telling is a pacing dial, not a quality measure.** John Gardner's **psychic distance** (*The Art of Fiction*, 1983) gives the vocabulary: the "distance the reader feels between himself and the events," sliding from far ("It was winter of the year 1853") to intimate ("Snow. Under your collar… plugging up your miserable soul"). It works exactly like a camera zoom — and *careless, unmotivated shifts* are the real error, not telling itself.

- **SHOW** slows the reader (expand time across many words). **TELL** moves them forward. **SUMMARY** compresses long spans. **IMPLY** trusts the reader (iceberg). **DELAY** creates curiosity. **CUT** protects momentum (Le Guin's "leaping": "what you leave out is infinitely more than what you leave in").

**The narrative camera (workbook centerpiece):** Zoom in (SHOW) for moments that matter — first touch, broken vow, costly magic. Pull back (TELL/SUMMARIZE) for travel, weeks passing, routine, orientation. Move on/skip (CUT) past the trivial. Le Guin: keeping the story "full" is *crowding*; skipping the irrelevant is *leaping* — both are skills.

**Over-showing = purple prose:** "writing so excessive… it breaks the flow." Symptoms: "the pace slows to a crawl… melodrama takes over… the voice sounds artificial." Fix = the **lighthouse technique**: illuminate one or two specific details brightly, leave the rest to imagination.

**Sentence-level test (per sentence/short paragraph):**
1. What is this doing? (emotion/tension/clarity/movement/atmosphere/character/romance/consequence/rhythm) — no nameable job may = filler.
2. Slow down or speed up here? Match technique to intent.
3. Loaded or neutral detail? Could one loaded detail replace five neutral?
4. Am I dramatizing something neutral (unlocking a door)? Tell it or cut it.
5. Am I labeling an emotion I could let the reader feel?
6. Does this sentence lead to the next? (Le Guin: a sentence's "chief duty [is] to lead to the next sentence.")

**A detail "earns its weight"** when it's both *concrete* (a sense) and *significant* (an idea/judgment) — Burroway: "The window sill was green" (concrete) vs. "shedding flakes of fungus-green paint" (concrete + significant). Loaded detail does double work; filler does none.

**Three original example paragraphs:**

*Over-shown (everything at full zoom):*
> Her trembling, ink-stained fingers curled around the cold brass handle of the heavy oaken door, and slowly, achingly, with a groan of ancient hinges that screamed like dying things, she pulled it open, her heart hammering against the cage of her ribs as the candlelight guttered and threw writhing shadows across the cold grey flagstones beneath her soft leather boots.

*Over-told (the moment flattened):*
> She opened the door and went into the throne room. She was nervous because the queen might have her executed. The queen looked angry. It was tense.

*Balanced (telling carries logistics; one loaded detail carries emotion):*
> She opened the door and stepped into the throne room. The queen did not look up from the letter in her hands — and that, more than any drawn blade, told her she was already condemned.

### 4. When to SHOW Worldbuilding
Show (dramatize/embody/slow) when worldbuilding: creates emotional impact the reader must *feel*; reveals character (values/fear/desire/status/shame); creates conflict or consequence; affects the romance (bond, taboo, power imbalance, obstacle); changes plot understanding; reveals culture through *behavior* (ritual, etiquette, law, food, clothing, architecture, magic, religion, hierarchy, economics); or fulfills the genre promise (lush magical aesthetics at key beats). Editor Lauren Atkin's test: is the worldbuilding "integrated or inserted"? The world "needs to feel inhabited rather than explained."

**Critical clarification — "showing worldbuilding" is usually NOT a full scene.** It can be **one loaded detail** (gloves never removed in public → a touch taboo), **one consequence** (a flinch at the mention of magic), **one line of dialogue** ("Careful — the temple doesn't forgive the uncovered face"), **one gesture/reaction** (a room going silent at a name), or **one touch, sensory impression, choice, or cost.** A burned hand teaches the danger of fire magic faster than a page of rules.

### 5. When to TELL Worldbuilding
Tell when info is functional but not emotionally important; when showing would waste scene time; for quick orientation; to summarize travel/history/logistics/geography/transitions/routines; to prevent confusion; when the POV character already knows it (avoiding "As you know, Bob"); when an explanation beats an artificial scene; for pacing control; or when over-showing would bloat/melodramatize. **Telling can be elegant and immersive** — Le Guin: "crafty writers don't allow Exposition to form Lumps. They break up the information, grind it fine, and make it into bricks to build the story with" (*invisible exposition*). Filter telling through a character's attitude, bias, humor, or fear and it carries voice: *"The dragon-courts kept their old cruelties dressed in silk; that was the first thing the capital taught her."*

### 6. When to SUMMARIZE
Summary is its own tool. Burroway distinguishes **sequential** (compress a specific span in order) and **circumstantial** (how things *generally* go) summary; its jobs: "give information, fill in background, let us understand a motive, alter pace, create a transition, leap moments or years." "A well written summary can be as good as a scene" and can carry concrete detail. Swain/Bickham: pacing is controlled by the **ratio of scene to summary/sequel.**

Romantasy uses: weeks of court lessons compressed ("For a month she learned which forks to fear and which smiles to fear more"), then zoom in on the humiliating lesson; travel between kingdoms in three sentences; repeated magical training summarized, then dramatize the success/the burn; recurring political dinners summarized to set a pattern, then slow for the dinner where an alliance breaks; the litany of failed curse-breaking attempts; rules stated briskly *before* the scene where breaking one is catastrophic. **When to shift back to scene:** Burroway's most common beginner error is "to summarize events rather than to realize them as moments" — never summarize the confession, the kiss, or the betrayal.

### 7. When to IMPLY or DELAY
Rests on Hemingway's **iceberg**: omit what you know and "the reader… will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them." Imply when the world feels bigger unexplained, when partial understanding creates intrigue. Delay when the reader doesn't need the answer yet or a character can confront it later more dramatically. **Let characters treat their world as normal while the reader catches up** — they don't lecture themselves. *Caution:* delay must come from story logic, not authorial coyness (Helen Lowe calls the "you're not ready to know that yet" mentor trope worse than the info-dump); and implication must not tip into confusion (Section 19).

Original examples: a taboo no one names (eyes lowered, subject changed); a court ritual no one explains (two fingers to the lips before the throne); a forbidden bond hinted through others' reactions (a servant gasps and flees); a magical caste shown by who is served first / never touched; a dragon court's fear of phoenix fire (guards flinch from a brazier); a temple custom shown through a refused handshake; political danger revealed through a question no one answers.

### 8. When to CUT
**Inventing a detail doesn't entitle it to page space.** "Worldbuilder's pride" exposition is identifiable by a tell: deleting it costs the story nothing. Cutting *increases* immersion — explaining everything shrinks a world; gaps make it feel vast. Distinguish the *author's* need to know (justifies a bible/appendices, like Tolkien's) from the *reader's* need to know (justifies page space).

**Cut Test:** (1) Does removing it change plot/character/romance/stakes/theme? No → cut. (2) Am I including it because the story needs it or because I'm proud of it? Pride → cut. (3) Need it now/later/never? Never → cut; later → delay. (4) Could the reader infer it? Yes → cut, let implication carry it. (5) Does it belong in an appendix/map/private notes? Move it. (6) Did I slow the story to deliver it with no emotional gain? → cut.

### 9. Worldbuilding That Usually Should NOT Be Shown in Full
These categories default to **tell/summarize/imply/delay/cut** unless they touch plot/character/romance/stakes/theme — at which point show the *one* part that does:
- **Currency/economics:** tell in a line; show only when a price reveals scarcity or status (a coin bearing a new queen's face).
- **Calendars/naming conventions:** imply through use; never lecture.
- **Long histories/genealogies/family trees:** summarize the one relevant thread; appendix the rest. Show only the ancestor whose deed shapes the present stakes.
- **Political structures/court titles/military ranks:** imply through behavior (who waits at thresholds, who is saluted); show only when a rank creates a romantic or political obstacle.
- **Magic taxonomies/rules:** state the rule briskly *before* it matters; show the rule only at the moment its breaking has consequence.
- **Travel logistics/geography/trade routes:** summarize ("the crossing took three days"); show only the arrival that carries emotion.
- **Clothing construction/meal prep:** imply; show only when a garment or dish reveals taboo, status, or intimacy.
- **Religious doctrine/legal systems/social customs/species classifications:** reveal through ritual, refusal, and reaction — show the *one custom* that creates tension, tell the framework.

### 10. Worldbuilding That MUST Be Shown / Embodied
Categories where telling alone feels flat — the reader must *feel* it (but still efficiently, via one loaded detail, not a scene): oppression/class hierarchy; fear of ruling power; danger of magic; allure of forbidden romance; cost of a curse/bond; cultural taboos; religious devotion/dread; court politics and social consequence; power imbalance; war trauma/occupation; scarcity/poverty/exploitation; the emotional meaning of home/exile/belonging/identity; the lived experience of being nonhuman/shifter/mage/fae; romantic/sensual atmosphere; consequences of breaking law; and the emotional cost of being chosen/cursed/bonded/exiled/hunted/hidden. **Show these through a single embodied unit:** the flinch, the lowered eyes, the refused touch, the price paid, the option a character no longer has.

### 11. How to TELL Well (avoiding info-dump)
- **Voice:** filter exposition through the character's attitude/bias/humor/resentment/longing/grief/fear/judgment.
- **Selective specificity:** one vivid fact, not an encyclopedia.
- **Consequence over catalogue:** "because of this, the character must now…" — Carol Berg-style snippets of *consequence* ("the only two defensive spells I knew were wholly out of reach") rather than rules lectures.
- **Telling during motion / inside conflict:** deliver info while something is happening; let disagreeing characters explain positions naturally.
- **Avoid fake exposition scenes and "As you know" dialogue.**
- **Before:** *"As you know, our magic requires blood, and stronger spells need more, which is why the guilds regulate it."* **After:** *"How much will you need?" / "Enough that I'll be useless for a week." / "The guild will fine you." / "Let them. Those rules were written for peacetime."*

### 12. How to SHOW Efficiently
One loaded detail vs. five neutral; show through consequences, social friction, what characters avoid saying, and choices under pressure; sensory detail only when it affects emotion/mood/conflict/meaning; outsider-vs-insider contrast; rituals/rules/broken expectations; romantic beats (touch, restraint, taboo, vulnerability, jealousy, protection, betrayal); changed options; what a character *can't* safely do; what a character notices *because of* desire/fear/resentment/attraction/trauma. Avoid cinematic over-description.
- **Before:** *Their lips met in a fiery explosion of desire, hearts beating as one in the eternal dance of passion.* **After:** *She hesitated, her breath catching as his hand brushed her cheek. When their lips finally met it was slow, tentative — as if neither was sure they were ready for the storm they were about to unleash.*

### 13. Romantasy-Specific Guidance
Romantasy readers want lush atmosphere, emotional intensity, romantic tension, magical aesthetics, beauty, danger, and intimacy — but the genre bogs down if every court/species/rule/kingdom/war/prophecy/bond/faction is over-explained. **Reveal the world and the romance together.** As author Maizie Bennett frames it, the world "exists to create opportunities for romance, raise emotional stakes, and provide obstacles that force your characters closer together. It's not the star of the show — it's the stage where the romance performs," and "when in doubt, choose the emotional beat over the world-building detail every single time." Editor Lauren Atkin: the two arcs "need to move in tandem… when one races ahead of the other, the story loses its defining tension." **Lush does not mean slow; immersive does not mean exhaustive — romantic tension depends on selective focus.**

Quick guidance by element:
- **Court politics:** imply danger through silence; show the *one* rule that threatens the couple.
- **Magical bonds:** show through forced proximity, involuntary reaction, and cost; delay the full mechanics.
- **Curses:** state the rule briskly; show the romantic consequence.
- **Forbidden romance:** show through what they can't safely do; tell the law in a voice-driven line.
- **Shifter/fae/dragon/vampire/phoenix societies:** reveal through behavior, hierarchy, and fear (a flinch from fire), not species taxonomies.
- **Arranged marriages/bargains/royal bargains:** dramatize the moment terms are struck; summarize the negotiations around it.
- **Power imbalance/class hierarchy:** show through who serves whom, who waits, who is touched.
- **Court rituals:** show the one that creates intimacy or taboo; imply the rest.
- **Magical training:** summarize the grind; show the breakthrough/disaster.
- **War/rebellion, exile/captivity:** show the emotional cost in one embodied detail; summarize logistics.
- **Prophecy/destiny, soulmates/fated mates/bonding customs:** delay and dole out one cryptic line at a time.
- **Temples/markets/prisons/palaces/battle camps:** one lighthouse detail of sensory atmosphere, not a tour.

## Decision Frameworks

### 14. Scene-Level Decision Tree (SHOW / TELL / SUMMARIZE / IMPLY / DELAY / CUT)
Ask, in order:
1. **Does this change what the character wants/fears/risks/chooses/misunderstands, affect the romance, or create conflict/danger/temptation/intimacy/shame/status/consequence?** If NO → it's logistical → **TELL, SUMMARIZE, or CUT.**
2. **Will the reader be confused without it right now?** If NO and it's not emotional → **DELAY** or **CUT.** If they'd be lost → keep it.
3. **Is it emotionally important or just informational?** Informational → **TELL/SUMMARIZE.** Emotional → continue.
4. **Would dramatizing make a genuinely better scene, or just add pages?** Just pages → **TELL.**
5. **Can it be one detail / one vivid sentence?** Yes → **SHOW small** (don't build a scene).
6. **Would withholding it create useful tension?** Yes → **IMPLY or DELAY.**
7. **If none of the above, does the story lose anything without it?** No → **CUT.**

**Use-when summary:** SHOW = the moment matters emotionally and the reader must feel it. TELL = functional info, voice-driven, fast. SUMMARIZE = compress uneventful or repeated time. IMPLY = the world feels bigger unexplained. DELAY = withholding builds curiosity and a later payoff. CUT = removing it costs nothing.

### 15. Sentence-Level Framework (ZOOM IN / ZOOM OUT / MOVE ON / CUT)
Do a **"sentence pass"**: mark each sentence SHOW / TELL / SUMMARY / IMPLICATION / DELAY / CUT. Then for each: name its job (emotion/tension/clarity/movement/atmosphere/character/romance/consequence/rhythm); decide whether it should slow *on purpose* or move efficiently; check meaningful vs. neutral detail; ask whether it could combine or replace five neutral actions with one, turn a flat explanation into voice, or be cut.

*Marked-up original example:*
> She had never seen the floating palace before. **[TELL — orientation]** For three days the airship climbed, the clouds thinning until the world below was only a blue smear. **[SUMMARY — compress travel]** Then the spires broke through the mist, white and impossible, and her breath stopped. **[SHOW — the moment that matters]** No one had told her it would be beautiful; perhaps no one here believed it still was. **[IMPLY — hints at a jaded court without explaining]**

## Recommendations (Product Development — Section 22 synthesis)

**What the workbook's current "show don't tell" section should emphasize:** Reframe the entire section from a *rule* to a **pacing-and-emphasis dial.** Lead with: "Show the moments that matter; tell the connective tissue." Add the principle: **"Worldbuilding earns page space when it changes emotion, conflict, choice, romance, or consequence"** — and **"Not every action deserves a camera close-up"** and **"Lush does not mean exhaustive."**

**Warnings/caveats to add:** State plainly that telling is valid and often stronger; that showing can become over-showing (purple prose); and that you needn't dramatize everything you invented. Add a sidebar: "Mystery ≠ confusion."

**Where show-don't-tell prompts may mislead:** Any prompt that says "show this" without a size cap invites beginners to build a whole scene for a one-line detail. **Fix:** phrase prompts as "Show this in *one loaded detail or one line of dialogue*," and pair every SHOW prompt cluster with TELL/SUMMARIZE/CUT prompts so the technique is always a *choice*.

**Recommended frameworks to print as worksheets:**
- **The Six-Tool Compass:** SHOW / TELL / SUMMARIZE / IMPLY / DELAY / CUT, each with a one-line "use when."
- **The Narrative Camera:** ZOOM IN / ZOOM OUT / MOVE ON / CUT for the sentence pass.
- **The Cut Test** and **The "Earns Its Weight" Test** (concrete + significant).

**How to teach prose rhythm without overwhelming beginners:** One idea only — "long sentences and dwelling = slow/emphasis; short sentences and summary = fast/movement; vary on purpose." Anchor with Le Guin's read-aloud test.

**Suggested section titles:** "The Dial, Not the Rule"; "The Narrative Camera"; "Show the Moment, Tell the Bridge"; "Does This Detail Earn Its Place?"; "Lush, Not Long."

## Caveats
- **Sources disagree on degree, not direction.** Tools like AutoCrit still push "show" as near-universal; classic teachers like William Sloane claimed *everything* must be scene. The weight of authority (Burroway, Le Guin, Gardner, Brandon Taylor, working editors) supports balance — but a beginner Googling "show don't tell" will hit the dogmatic version first, which is exactly why the workbook's corrective framing is valuable.
- **The Chekhov quote is apocryphal** — attribute it as "often attributed to Chekhov" and cite the real 1886 letter to avoid repeating a popular error.
- **Scene-and-sequel theory (Swain/Bickham) is genre-specific** to plot-driven popular fiction; cite it for pacing mechanics, not as a universal law (critics note it can produce formulaic "pot-boilers" if followed rigidly).
- **Romantasy craft sourcing is thinner and more recent** than the literary canon; the strongest named voices found were practicing authors and developmental editors (Maizie Bennett, Lauren Atkin, Savannah Gilbo) rather than peer-reviewed scholarship. No on-record quote from the genre's biggest names (Maas, Yarros) on this specific craft question was located; treat published bestsellers as high-level illustrations only.
- **All example paragraphs in this report are original inventions** for teaching and must remain so in the product; do not quote or closely imitate any copyrighted work.
