Character Development Romantasy

What This Is

You're here to build characters for romantasy. Not just attractive people with magic powers, but complex individuals whose inner wounds drive every beat of the love story.

In romantasy, character development isn't separate from romance. The trust issues, the past betrayals, the desperate need for control that crumbles when real intimacy shows up — these aren't backstory details. They're the engine of your story.

This workbook helps you build characters whose psychology creates romantic tension by design.

Who This Is For

You want to build characters who feel psychologically real. You want internal obstacles that challenge and test relationships. You want to understand how wounds, lies, flaws and needs create romantic conflict.

Whether you are writing your first romantasy or your tenth, regardless of whether you plan out your entire plot beforehand or discover your characters as you go — this workbook is designed to meet you where you are.

How This Workbook Is Structured

Each section has two parts.

Part One: The Foundation

This is who your characters are before the romance begins. Their wounds, their false beliefs, their patterns, their desires. This foundation exists independent of the love story.

Readers can tell when a character has real psychological depth versus when they're a collection of appealing traits assembled around the romance.

Part Two: [Topic] & Romance

This is where character becomes romantic tension. You'll learn how to mine each topic for obstacles, intimacy, stakes, and conflict.

A character wound isn't just about what happened to them. It's about why they can't let the love interest in. Why vulnerability feels like death. Why they keep sabotaging the very connection they crave.

You need both parts. Skip the foundation and your characters feel thin. Skip the romance application and you're not using your characters to their full potential.

How to Use This Workbook

You Don't Have to Do Everything

This workbook is comprehensive. However, not all sections may apply to your story. Not all questions may pertain to your story.

Skip freely. If your characters don't have significant power dynamics, skim that section. If tropes aren't central to your approach, move past them.

The goal isn't to fill every blank. The goal is to build what your specific story needs.

You Don't Have to Go in Order

The sections are numbered for reference purposes only. There is no requirement to complete the sections in the order that they are presented.

Some authors begin with the wound as that is the core of the character. Other authors begin with the love interest as that relationship is typically the easiest to conceptualize. Some authors establish the supporting cast first as the dynamic created among the supporting cast reveals the protagonist.

Begin with the area where you have the greatest energy. As you complete the section, allow that section to influence the other sections.

Focus on What Creates Stakes

As you complete each section, continue to ask: How does this create obstacles for the romance?

Not all of the character development that you create will necessarily place direct pressure on the love story — however, if you find that you are creating an abundance of detail regarding a character's background that never influences the romance, you are likely creating character for character's sake.

The most effective romantasy character development serves two purposes: It provides a sense of realism to the characters AND it creates a sense of impossibility in regard to the love story (prior to the realization of the love story).

Do This For Both Leads

Both your lead and your love interest require equal amounts of psychological complexity. A brooding attitude is not equivalent to actual character development.

You can complete both sets of sections at the same time or you can complete the sections for the lead first and then revisit the sections for the love interest. Section 4 walks you through the process of establishing your love interest; however, he/she/they should receive the same foundational work as the lead.

Examples Are Guides, Not Rules

Throughout this workbook, you'll find examples from popular romantasy novels — A Court of Thorns and Roses, Fourth Wing, Six of Crows, The Cruel Prince, and others.

These examples are not templates for you to copy, but they should help you gain insight into the type of thinking required to provide answers to the questions posed within the workbook; then develop answers that support your original story.

A Note on Tropes

Romantasy has a lot of tropes. And I'm not saying this is bad — I think it's good and super fun to play with!

Readers come to romantasy looking for morally gray love interests, enemies to lovers, grumpy/sunshine pairings, "there's only one bed," and protagonists who have no idea how powerful they are.

One of the purposes of this workbook is to help you successfully implement these tropes.

The main reason why some tropes can be seen as clichés while others are beloved by readers is not the trope itself, but rather how well the writer executed it. The fact that there is a brooding love interest is unremarkable if he is simply mysterious because the author did not develop his personality.

On the other hand, a brooding love interest whose walls were built from a very specific psychological cause makes for a more compelling love interest.

Enemies-to-lovers is simply not just about them being mean to each other. Enemies-to-lovers is about the wounds, fears and beliefs that prevent both parties from trusting each other.

Forced Proximity isn't just about them being stuck together. Forced Proximity is about the psychological barriers that make getting close to each other scary enough even though escape is not possible.

If you build your characters around your tropes, your tropes will take center stage.

The Foundation

The wound-lie-flaw framework isn't arbitrary — it mirrors how real people work. We get hurt. We draw conclusions from that hurt. Those conclusions shape our behavior. Understanding this chain helps you create characters who feel psychologically real, whose actions make sense even when they're self-destructive.

In romantasy specifically, this framework does double duty. It explains why your character struggles with intimacy, trust, or vulnerability — the very things romance requires. The walls they've built aren't random obstacles for the love interest to charm their way past. They're survival mechanisms that made sense once, even if they're now causing harm.

The Chain Reaction

Here's how it works in practice:

Element What It Is Example
Wound The painful event that shaped them Abandoned by their mother at age seven
Lie The false belief they formed "People always leave. I can't count on anyone."
Flaw The behavior pattern that results Pushes people away before they can leave first
Want What they're consciously chasing Power and self-sufficiency — to never need anyone
Need What would actually heal them To trust that someone will stay

Notice how each element flows from the one before. The wound creates the lie. The lie produces the flaw. The want is often an attempt to solve the problem the lie has identified — but it's the wrong solution, because the lie itself is wrong. The need is the real answer.

Why This Matters for Romantasy

Romance requires vulnerability. It requires letting someone in, trusting them with your soft places, believing they won't hurt you. For a character carrying a wound, this feels dangerous — maybe impossible.

This is why the best romantasy isn't just about two attractive people meeting. It's about two people who have every reason not to trust, not to open up, not to risk being hurt again — and watching them do it anyway.

The Core Truth
The wound creates the stakes. The lie creates the obstacle. The flaw creates the friction. And love becomes the force that finally breaks through.

How the wound becomes self-perpetuating behavior.

The Wound Something painful happened
The Lie / Misbelief They drew a false conclusion to protect themselves
The Flaw The lie drives harmful behavior patterns

Wound Type What Happened How It Blocks Romance
Abandonment Left by a parent, lover, or someone who should have stayed Fear of trusting anyone to stay
Betrayal Trusted someone who hurt them deliberately Difficulty believing anyone's intentions

Common Lies in Romantasy

The lie is the conclusion your character drew from their wound. It felt true at the time — maybe it even protected them. But now it's become a cage.

The most powerful lies are specific. "Love is dangerous" is weaker than "If I let someone matter to me, they'll be used against me." The more precisely you can articulate what your character believes, the more clearly you'll see how it affects their behavior.

If the Wound Was... The Lie Might Be...
Abandoned by a parent "Everyone leaves eventually. Don't get attached."
Betrayed by someone trusted "If someone seems trustworthy, that's when they're most dangerous."
Rejected for who they are "My true self is unlovable. I have to hide who I really am."
Loved only conditionally "I'm only worth something if I'm useful."
Powerless to save someone "Love makes you weak. Caring about people gives your enemies weapons."
Isolated or outcast "I don't belong anywhere. Connection isn't for people like me."
Used as a weapon or tool "I'll only hurt the people I care about."

Notice how many of these lies are partially true in certain contexts — which is what makes them so hard to shake. Someone did leave. Caring about people can give enemies leverage. The lie isn't pure delusion; it's an over-generalization.

The Lie in Your World

In romantasy worlds, the lie is sometimes actually true — at least partially. If your world genuinely punishes vulnerability, if enemies genuinely target loved ones, then your character's defensive beliefs aren't irrational. They're survival.

The question becomes: Is the love interest the exception? Or does the character need to change themselves and their world?

The Flaw: How the Lie Shows Up

The flaw is the lie in action. It's not just what your character believes — it's what they do because of what they believe.

Lie Resulting Flaw
"Everyone leaves" Pushes people away first; refuses to rely on anyone
"I'm only valuable if I'm useful" Overworks; can't accept help; sacrifices self-worth for achievement
"Love makes you weak" Emotionally distant; refuses to admit feelings; sees vulnerability as failure
"My true self is unlovable" Wears masks; never lets anyone see the real them; sabotages intimacy
"I'll only hurt people I care about" Keeps everyone at arm's length; runs when things get serious
"I don't belong anywhere" Never commits; always keeps one foot out the door; rejects connection
The Key Insight
The best flaws are strengths taken too far.

Protectiveness becomes controlling. Independence becomes isolation. Caution becomes cowardice. Passion becomes destruction. This makes them feel human — we can see why they developed this trait, even as we watch it cause damage.

Flaws should cause problems. If a flaw doesn't create friction in the plot or the relationship, it's not really a flaw — it's just a quirk. The most effective flaws are the ones that hurt the character and the people around them, especially the love interest.

Common Mistakes with Wound-Lie-Flaw

Mistake #1: The Unconnected Flaw

What it looks like: The character has a tragic backstory AND a flaw, but one doesn't lead to the other. Their parents died, and they're also stubborn — but the stubbornness doesn't come from the loss.

Why it hurts your story: It feels like two separate characterizations stapled together.

How to Fix It

Ask why. Why does this specific wound create this specific flaw? If you can't draw a clear line, change one to fit the other.

Mistake #2: The Easily Shed Flaw

What it looks like: The character's flaw disappears as soon as they meet the love interest. They were distrustful, but the love interest is so attractive that suddenly trust isn't a problem.

Why it hurts your romance: It cheapens both the wound and the relationship. If the flaw was real, it shouldn't evaporate on contact with a pretty face.

How to Fix It

Make the flaw a genuine obstacle throughout the story. It can crack, soften, and eventually transform — but it should fight back. The love interest should have to earn trust, not just receive it automatically.

Mistake #3: The Unchanging Flaw

What it looks like: The character has the same flaw at the end as at the beginning. They still push people away, still refuse to trust, still self-sabotage — they just do it while in love now.

Why it hurts your romance: Romance should transform both characters. If they're exactly the same at the end, the relationship hasn't actually affected them.

How to Fix It

The flaw doesn't have to disappear, but it should evolve. Maybe they still struggle with trust but can now choose to trust despite the fear. Maybe they still have the instinct to push away but can catch themselves. Growth is more interesting than cure.

Mistake #4: The Flaw That's Not Really a Flaw

What it looks like: The character's "flaw" is something cute or endearing — they're too loyal, too caring, too passionate. It never causes real problems.

Why it hurts your romance: Flaws are supposed to create friction. A flaw everyone secretly admires isn't a flaw.

How to Fix It

Push the "flaw" until it becomes genuinely problematic. Too loyal? They refuse to see that someone they love is hurting them. Too caring? They neglect their own needs until they collapse. Too passionate? They destroy things in their intensity. Find where the trait causes actual damage.

Mistake #5: The Announced Flaw

What it looks like: The character tells us they're cold, distant, and unable to love — then immediately acts warm, connected, and loving.

Why it hurts your romance: Actions speak louder than self-description. If we're told they're one thing but shown another, we believe what we see.

How to Fix It

Show the flaw in action before showing it cracking. Let us see them push someone away, refuse help, or shut down emotionally. Then the moments when they don't do this land with more impact.

The Heart & Romance

Want and need are where character psychology meets story structure. The want drives the plot — it's what your character is actively pursuing, the goal they'd tell you about if you asked. The need is what would actually heal them — but they usually can't see it yet, because the lie is in the way.

In romance, the love interest often represents the need, or at least holds the key to it. They embody what the character is missing, offer what the character has been denying themselves, or simply refuse to accept the lie the character has been living by.

The tension between want and need is the engine of romantic conflict. The character is chasing the wrong thing — and falling in love is what redirects them.

Want vs. Need: The Core Tension

Want Need The Conflict
To be powerful enough that no one can hurt them To trust that someone will protect them too Love requires accepting help, which feels like weakness
To prove they're worthy through achievement To believe they're enough as they are Love interest values them for themselves, which they can't accept
To remain independent and self-sufficient To let someone in Falling in love threatens everything they've built
To get revenge on those who hurt them To let go and build something new The love interest represents moving on — which feels like betrayal
To return home / reclaim what was lost To find a new home The love interest is here; what they're chasing is there
To fulfill their duty / keep a promise To choose their own path The love interest represents forbidden freedom

Notice that the want isn't wrong, exactly. It's often a reasonable goal. But it's not enough. Achieving the want without addressing the need would leave the character hollow. This is the "hollow victory" — and it's worth writing out what that would look like for your character.

How Romance Complicates Want and Need

In a standard character arc, the protagonist pursues their want, discovers their need, and reorients. Romance adds a layer: the love interest is tangled up in this process.

Sometimes the love interest represents the need directly — they offer the trust, the belonging, the vulnerability the character has been avoiding. Sometimes they obstruct the want — they're the enemy the character is supposed to defeat, the distraction from the mission, the reason duty and desire are suddenly at odds.

The best romantasy weaves want, need, and love interest together until choosing one means reckoning with all of them.

Comparing Your Leads

The deepest romantic tension comes from how the two characters' inner worlds interact. Sometimes they share similar wounds and recognize something in each other. Sometimes their wounds are opposites, and they each have exactly what the other lacks. Sometimes one character's coping mechanism directly triggers the other's wound — creating friction that's painful but also revelatory.

How does your protagonist's want/need compare to your love interest's?

Are they chasing similar things or opposite things? Do they need the same thing, or does each one have what the other needs?

Do they have similar wounds? Opposite ones?

Characters with similar wounds might recognize each other immediately — "You understand." Characters with opposite wounds might clash, each one inadvertently poking the other's sore spots.

How does one character's coping mechanism affect the other's wound?

For example: If one character copes by withdrawing and the other's wound is abandonment, every time the first character needs space, it feels like abandonment to the second. This kind of friction is painful but creates opportunities for growth.

Common Mistakes with Want and Need in Romance

Mistake #1: The Love Interest as Need

What it looks like: The character needs love. The love interest provides love. Done.

Why it hurts your romance: It reduces the love interest to a function rather than a person. And it makes the character's arc about receiving rather than growing.

How to Fix It

The need should be something internal — the ability to trust, the willingness to be vulnerable, the belief that they're worthy. The love interest can help them get there, but they have to do the actual growing.

Mistake #2: The Abandoned Want

What it looks like: The character spends the whole book chasing their want, then forgets about it once they fall in love. The want wasn't important after all.

Why it hurts your romance: It makes the plot feel like a distraction from the "real" story. It also suggests that love replaces goals rather than transforming how we pursue them.

How to Fix It

Integrate the resolution. Maybe they achieve the want but in a different way. Maybe they redefine it. Maybe they sacrifice it for something better. But don't just drop it.

Mistake #3: The Need That's Obvious to Everyone

What it looks like: The character's need is clear from page one, and we spend the whole book waiting for them to figure out what we already know.

Why it hurts your romance: It creates frustration rather than tension. We're ahead of the character with nowhere to go.

How to Fix It

Either obscure the need better (give us the puzzle pieces without the picture) or complicate it (we think we know what they need, then discover it's deeper or different than we thought).

This is what separates romantasy magic from epic fantasy magic. In epic fantasy, magic might be a tool, a weapon, a resource to be managed. In romantasy, magic is intimate. It reveals character. It creates vulnerability. It becomes a language for emotional truths that are hard to say out loud.

This section is divided into two parts:

Part One: Magic as Identity — How your character's magic reflects who they are. The connection between their powers and their psychology, what their magic reveals about them, and how their relationship with their own abilities mirrors their internal struggles.

Part Two: Magic & Romance — How magic shapes the love story. The obstacles magic creates, the intimacy it enables, and how the fantasy elements of your world interact with the romantic tension between your leads.

You need both. Magic that's just cool powers without emotional resonance feels shallow. But magic that's all metaphor without considering how it actually works in your world — and how it affects what's possible between your characters — will leave gaps in your story logic.

A Note Before You Begin

Magic systems vary enormously across romantasy.

If your protagonist doesn't have magic, that absence is still identity. Being powerless in a world of power, being the ordinary one among the extraordinary, being unable to access something everyone else has — this shapes a character just as much as having abilities. Focus on what their lack of magic means for who they are and how they relate to those who have it.

If magic is subtle in your world, the questions still apply — just scale them appropriately. Even small magics can carry emotional weight and create romantic obstacles.

If you haven't figured out your magic system yet, this section can help you develop it through a character lens. Instead of starting with rules and limitations, start with what would be emotionally meaningful for your protagonist — then build the system around that.

Come back after drafting. Sometimes the right magical metaphor only becomes clear once you've written scenes. If something here doesn't click yet, move on and return later.

Magic as Identity

The most resonant romantasy magic works on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, it's a power with rules, costs, and applications in the plot. Beneath that, it's a mirror — reflecting something true about the character who wields it.

This doesn't mean magic has to be a heavy-handed metaphor. It means there should be an emotional logic to why this character has this power. A character terrified of losing control might have volatile, unpredictable abilities. A character who hides their true self might have magic they must keep secret. A character who fears their own capacity for destruction might wield something genuinely dangerous.

When magic and psychology align, everything reinforces everything else. The character's internal struggles manifest externally through their powers. Their growth arc and their magical arc become the same arc.

The Emotional Logic of Magic

Ask yourself: Why does this character have this specific power?

Not the in-world explanation — the storytelling reason. What does this magic let you explore about who they are?

If the Character... Their Magic Might Be... Because...
Fears losing control Volatile, unpredictable, dangerous when emotions run high External power mirrors internal fear
Hides their true self Secret, forbidden, or visibly marks them as different Magic forces the question of exposure
Feels powerless despite strength Immense but costly, or powerful but forbidden to use External power can't solve internal powerlessness
Believes they hurt everyone they love Genuinely dangerous to those nearby The lie is made literally true

Common Mistakes with Magic & Identity

Mistake #1: The Disconnected Power

What it looks like: The character's magic has no relationship to their personality, wound, or arc. They're struggling with trust issues, and they can... move objects with their mind. The two elements never interact.

How to Fix It

Find the connection. Maybe telekinesis becomes relevant to control issues — they can move everything except themselves. Look for where magic and psyche can speak to each other.

Mistake #2: The Metaphor That's Too On-the-Nose

What it looks like: The character who's cold and distant has ice powers. The angry character has fire. The connection is so obvious it feels like a children's book.

Why it hurts your story: It's reductive and predictable. Real people are more complex than a single trait, and their magic should reflect that complexity.

How to Fix It

Add contradiction or complication. The cold, distant character has fire powers they're terrified of — their magic reveals the passion they're suppressing. The angry character has healing abilities — they're so angry because they do care, intensely. Let magic reveal hidden depths, not just confirm surface impressions.

Mistake #3: The Power Without Cost

What it looks like: Magic is purely beneficial. The character has cool abilities with no downside, no limitation, no price to pay. There's nothing about their power that creates problems for them.

Why it hurts your story: Costless magic creates no tension. And it misses the chance to connect magical limitations to emotional limitations.

How to Fix It

Add costs that resonate with character. Physical costs (exhaustion, pain, shortened lifespan) create stakes. Emotional costs (must feel certain emotions, must relive memories) create vulnerability. Social costs (marked as different, hunted, feared) create isolation. The best costs connect to the character's wound or lie.

Mistake #4: Magic That Solves Emotional Problems

What it looks like: The character uses magic to shortcut emotional work. Telepathy means they don't have to learn to communicate. Truth-telling magic means they don't have to learn to trust. The power removes the romantic obstacle instead of complicating it.

Why it hurts your romance: The obstacles are the story. Magic that removes them removes the tension.

How to Fix It

Make magic complicate emotional problems instead of solving them. Telepathy means they know the love interest cares but still can't make themselves believe it. Truth-telling magic confirms words but can't read hearts. The magical information doesn't help until they do the emotional work.

Mistake #5: The Static Magical Relationship

What it looks like: The character's relationship with their magic never changes. They start the book afraid of their power or unable to control it, and they end the same way — or they're in full control from the start.

Why it hurts your story: It's a missed arc. The character's relationship with their magic should evolve alongside their emotional growth.

How to Fix It

Tie magical growth to emotional growth. As they heal, their magic changes. As they learn to trust, powers that require vulnerability become accessible. As they accept themselves, abilities they've suppressed emerge. The magical transformation is the emotional transformation made visible.

Common Mistakes with Backstory

Mistake #1: The Trauma Competition

What it looks like: The character's backstory is relentlessly terrible — abuse, loss, betrayal, hardship piled on hardship. Every possible bad thing happened to them.

How to Fix It

Quality over quantity. One well-developed formative experience is more powerful than a list of tragedies. Let the backstory breathe.

Mistake #2: The Backstory Dump

What it looks like: The character's history is delivered in a block — pages of flashback or a monologue where they tell the love interest everything.

Why it hurts your story: It stops the present story cold. And readers won't retain information that isn't yet relevant.

How to Fix It

Reveal backstory through present-moment triggers. A smell brings a flash of memory. A situation echoes something from their past. The love interest asks a question and we get a glimpse, not the whole story. Let backstory emerge in fragments that illuminate present behavior.

Mistake #3: The Backstory That Doesn't Connect

What it looks like: The character has detailed history that never affects present behavior. We know everything about their childhood, but none of it explains why they act the way they do now.

Why it hurts your story: It's worldbuilding without function. Backstory should be an engine, not decoration.

How to Fix It

Work backward. Start with present behavior — what are they like in relationships, what's their flaw, what do they struggle with? Then build backstory that explains it. If a piece of history doesn't connect to present characterization, either cut it or find the connection.

Mistake #4: The Perfectly Remembered Past

What it looks like: The character has complete, accurate access to their history. They remember everything clearly and understand exactly why it affected them.

Why it hurts your story: Real people's memories are incomplete, biased, and often wrong. We misremember. We don't understand our own patterns until someone points them out. Perfect self-knowledge is unrealistic — and removes the possibility of discovery.

How to Fix It

Let the character have gaps, distortions, and blind spots. Maybe they remember a parent as loving when evidence suggests otherwise. Maybe they've blocked out key moments. Self-understanding can be part of their arc.

Mistake #5: The Backstory That Excuses Everything

What it looks like: The character behaves badly, but their backstory is presented as justification. They hurt the love interest, but it's fine because look what happened to them.

Why it hurts your romance: Explanation is not excuse. Understanding why someone developed harmful patterns doesn't make those patterns okay. If the narrative treats backstory as a free pass, the character never has to actually grow or take responsibility.

How to Fix It

Let the backstory explain without excusing. The character can understand where their patterns came from AND recognize they need to change. The love interest can have compassion for the wound AND hold boundaries around the behavior. Both things can be true.

Make the flaw genuinely problematic. The love interest should do things that hurt the protagonist, make mistakes because of their lie, push the protagonist away in ways that cause real damage.

Mistake #4: The Love Interest as Reward

What it looks like: The love interest exists primarily as the prize the protagonist wins by completing their arc. They don't have their own growth; they're just there to be claimed.

Why it hurts your romance: It reduces them to an object and misses the opportunity for transformative mutual growth.

How to Fix It

Give them an arc that runs parallel to the protagonist's. They should be different at the end than at the beginning. The relationship should change them, not just be something they're eventually willing to enter.

Mistake #5: Backstory as Justification for Bad Behavior

What it looks like: The love interest treats the protagonist poorly, but it's presented as acceptable because they have trauma. The narrative excuses cruelty, manipulation, or control because "they've been hurt."

Why it hurts your romance: Explanation isn't excuse. A love interest who damages the protagonist without accountability isn't romantic — they're harmful.

How to Fix It

Let the love interest have wounds AND be held accountable for their behavior. They can struggle because of their past and still apologize when they hurt the protagonist. Growth means taking responsibility, not getting a free pass.

A Note Before You Begin

Power imbalance in romance is not inherently bad.

Some of the most beloved romances have significant power gaps. Beauty and the Beast. Pride and Prejudice. Countless romantasy novels where one character could destroy the other with a thought. Power imbalance creates tension, and tension creates story.

The question is how you handle it. Does the powerful character respect the less powerful one's autonomy? Does the power shift over time? Is the less powerful character genuinely choosing the relationship, or are they coerced by circumstance?

Know what you're writing. Some authors intentionally write dark romance with power dynamics that aren't meant to be healthy. This is valid, but it requires awareness. The problem isn't writing unhealthy dynamics; it's writing them accidentally, or framing them as aspirational when they're not.

Reader expectations vary. What one reader finds thrillingly transgressive, another finds disturbing. You can't please everyone, but you should know what you're offering and be consistent about it.

Navigating the Imbalance

The same dynamics that create delicious tension can, if handled carelessly, romanticize control or present unhealthy relationships as aspirational. The question isn't whether to have power imbalance — it's how to handle it intentionally.

The Choice Test

At every point, ask: Is the less powerful character genuinely choosing this relationship?

  • Could they survive without this relationship?
  • Do they have other options (even if less appealing)?
  • Is their "yes" free from coercion, desperation, or lack of alternatives?
  • If they said "no," would that be respected?

If the answer to any of these is "no," you're writing a power dynamic that requires very careful handling — or that crosses into territory many readers will find troubling.

Common Mistakes with Power Dynamics

Mistake #1: The Oblivious Imbalance

The narrative doesn't seem to notice the power dynamics it's created. The immortal fae lord is presented as an unproblematic love interest without any acknowledgment that he could incinerate the protagonist and everyone she loves.

Why it hurts: Readers notice even when the text doesn't. Ignoring obvious power imbalances feels naive at best, and at worst, like you're normalizing dynamics that deserve scrutiny.

How to Fix It: Acknowledge the imbalance within the story. Characters can notice it, discuss it, navigate it consciously. "You could destroy me" can be said out loud. The power gap should be seen.

Mistake #2: The Instant Trust

The less powerful character immediately trusts the powerful one despite having every reason not to. The captive falls for the captor without any realistic hesitation.

Why it hurts: It erases the very tension the power imbalance creates, and it makes the less powerful character seem naive or even foolish.

How to Fix It: Let trust be earned. The less powerful character can be attracted and still wary. The powerful character has to prove themselves — and that takes time and consistency.

Mistake #3: The Benevolent Dictator

The powerful character is controlling but it's fine because they're right. They make decisions for the less powerful character, override objections — but they're always correct, so it's romantic.

Why it hurts: Being right doesn't make control okay. Autonomy matters even when we'd make bad choices. "I know what's best for you" is controlling whether or not the controller is correct.

How to Fix It: Let the powerful character be wrong sometimes — and let the less powerful character's judgment be vindicated.

Section 5 Power & Balance

Common Mistakes with Power Dynamics (continued)

Mistake #4: The Meaningless Agency

The narrative insists the less powerful character has agency, but their "choices" don't affect anything. They get to have opinions that are then ignored. They make decisions that the powerful character overrides.

Why it hurts: Agency isn't just the appearance of choice — it's choice that matters. Token agency is almost worse than no agency because it's dishonest.

How to Fix It: Give the less powerful character choices that stick. Things they decide that the powerful character respects, even when they disagree. Moments where their judgment determines outcomes.

Mistake #5: Exit as Threat

The less powerful character could theoretically leave, but leaving is framed as abandonment, betrayal, or cruelty. The narrative punishes the desire for exit, making staying feel obligatory rather than chosen.

Why it hurts: If leaving is wrong, then staying isn't really a choice. "You could leave but you'd be terrible if you did" is manipulation.

How to Fix It: Let exit be a genuine option — not punished, not framed as betrayal. The character stays because they want to. If they do leave (temporarily or otherwise), that choice is respected.

Common Mistakes with Mutual Transformation

Mistake #1: One-Sided Growth

The protagonist has a rich transformation arc. The love interest is the same at the end as at the beginning — they were already perfect, or they're a static catalyst for the protagonist's change.

Why it hurts: It makes the relationship feel unequal. One person is doing all the growing; the other is doing all the helping. That's not partnership.

How to Fix It: Give the love interest an equally specific arc. What do they believe at the start that's wrong? What do they learn to do differently? How are they changed by loving this person?

Mistake #2: Told, Not Shown Transformation

We're told at the end that the characters have changed, but we haven't seen the moments where change happened. "She realized she could trust him now." But when? Through what scenes?

Why it hurts: Transformation we're told about doesn't land emotionally. We need to experience the shifts.

How to Fix It: Identify specific scenes for each beat of transformation. The first crack. The deepening. The setback. The breakthrough. Ensure each one is dramatized, not summarized.

Mistake #3: Transformation as Cure

The characters' wounds are completely healed by the end. They're no longer anxious, no longer distrustful, no longer haunted. Love fixed everything.

Why it hurts: It's unrealistic, and it undersells the real achievement. Loving someone doesn't cure your trauma. It gives you a partner to navigate it with.

How to Fix It: Let transformation be growth, not erasure. The character still has the wound — they've just learned to live with it differently. The scar remains; the bleeding stops.

Section 6 How Leads Change Each Other

Common Mistakes with Mutual Transformation (continued)

Mistake #4: Simultaneous Epiphany

Both characters have their breakthrough at the exact same moment, in the same scene, as though transformation is synchronized.

Why it hurts: It feels artificial. Real growth happens unevenly. Usually one person gets there first and then helps or waits for the other.

How to Fix It: Stagger the arcs. Let one character crack open first, creating vulnerability that then affects the other. Let there be tension when one has grown and the other hasn't yet.

Mistake #5: Growth Without Cost

The characters transform, but it doesn't cost them anything. They get to keep their walls, their old life, their comfortable patterns — and also get the relationship. Nothing has to be given up.

Why it hurts: Transformation without sacrifice is just addition. Real growth requires letting go of something — the lie, the protection, the false self.

How to Fix It: Identify what each character loses by growing. What do they have to give up to become capable of this love? The loss might be giving up emotional distance, the comfort of expecting nothing, the protection of believing they're unlovable.

Final Thought: Love as Catalyst, Not Cure
The deepest romantic transformations don't happen because love heals wounds. They happen because love creates conditions where healing becomes possible.

The love interest isn't a magical fix for the protagonist's damage. They're a person whose presence, challenge, and care create an environment where the protagonist can finally risk changing. The same is true in reverse.

This is why both characters have to transform. When both are changed by knowing each other, when each one's growth enables and challenges the other's, the romance becomes what it should be: two people who are more themselves together than they could be apart. Not because they complete each other. Because they catalyze each other.

Common Mistakes with Tropes

Mistake #1: The Broken Promise

The book promises a trope but doesn't deliver. The enemies barely feel like enemies. The slow burn resolves in chapter three.

Why it hurts: Readers came for a specific experience and didn't get it. This feels like bait-and-switch.

How to Fix It: Know what you're promising and commit. If you're writing enemies to lovers, make them real enemies with real stakes.

Mistake #2: Trope Without Character

The trope is executed, but the characters feel interchangeable. Any two enemies could have this enemies-to-lovers arc.

Why it hurts: Tropes are frameworks, not fill-in-the-blank templates. The specific characters should make the trope feel fresh.

How to Fix It: Filter every trope beat through your specific characters. How does *this* character experience the hostility phase?

Mistake #3: The Checklist Approach

The story hits trope beats mechanically — here's the banter, here's the one bed, here's the dark moment — without organic flow.

Why it hurts: Readers can see the machinery. The story feels engineered rather than lived.

How to Fix It: Let beats emerge from character and situation. If a beat feels forced, it probably is.

Mistake #4: Subversion Without Satisfaction

The writer subverts the trope, but the subversion leaves readers without the emotional payoff they came for. The enemies stay enemies. The slow burn never ignites.

Why it hurts: Subversion is only satisfying if it provides something better than what was expected. Pure negation of reader expectations rarely works.

How to Fix It: If you're going to subvert, offer something in place of the expected satisfaction. Or subvert one element while delivering on others.

Mistake #5: Too Many Tropes, No Depth

The book crams in every popular trope: enemies to lovers AND forced proximity AND fated mates AND only one bed AND touch starved... Each one is surface-level.

Why it hurts: None of the tropes get enough development to deliver their full emotional payload. It's a buffet with no satisfying dishes.

How to Fix It: Choose your primary trope(s) and commit. Better to deliver deeply on two tropes than superficially on seven.

The deep work of wound and lie gives your character structure. The small work of habits and voice gives them life. Give your characters both, and they'll step off the page as people readers feel they know.

Error #1: The Convenient Vulnerability

The guarded character suddenly opens up because the scene needs them to — not because anything has changed. The walls come down when it's romantic, go back up when tension is needed.

Error #3: The Selective Wound

Section 11 Consistency Check

Error #4: The Power Slider

The character's abilities scale to whatever the scene needs. They're powerful enough to defeat armies in one chapter, struggling against a single opponent in the next.

How to Fix It: Establish clear limitations. If abilities vary, explain why. Exhaustion, emotional state, and specific conditions should govern power levels consistently.

Error #5: The Personality Override

The character does something completely out of character because you need them to for plot. The careful planner rushes in without thinking. The coward suddenly stands and fights.

How to Fix It: If the plot needs them to act against type, create circumstances that would genuinely push them there. What would make a careful person rush? Build to that.

  • Lie interpretations: Should govern how they read events until challenged
  • Power levels: Should remain stable unless explained
  • Relationship state: Should reflect accumulated interactions
  • Voice: Should be recognizable even under stress
Final Thought: Consistency Serves Character
The goal isn't rigid consistency — it's meaningful consistency. Characters can and should change. But each change should be earned by what came before.

When a guarded character opens up, readers should feel it was inevitable. When someone who never backs down retreats, they should understand why. Consistency isn't about stasis — it's about making every shift feel like the only thing that character could do.

Section 12 Agency Check

Agency is the difference between a character who drives their story and a character who is driven by it. Readers want to root for characters who choose — even when those choices are constrained, even when the options are terrible.

A Note Before You Begin

Agency isn't the same as power.

A captive can have agency. They can choose how to respond, what to resist, when to comply strategically. Their options are constrained, but they're still making choices.

Agency is about choice, not capability. Can your character affect their situation through their decisions? Do their choices matter?

Section 14 Appendix

Craft Techniques for Character

Show, Don't Tell (But Know When to Tell)

Instead of stating "she had trust issues," show her counting the exits, watching people's hands, flinching at sudden movements. But telling has its place — sometimes a direct statement is more efficient than elaborate showing.

Contradiction Creates Interest

Characters who contain contradictions feel real. The warrior who writes poetry. The cynic who believes in love. The villain who loves their cat. Look for where your character can surprise.

Behavior Over Backstory

Readers learn character through action, not exposition. Don't explain why they're guarded — show them being guarded. The backstory can come later, after readers already know who this person is.

Dialogue as Character

How someone talks reveals who they are. Word choice, sentence structure, what they avoid saying — all of it characterizes. Each character should have a distinct voice.

Change Through Action

Character growth is proved through behavior. If your character has learned to trust, show them trusting — taking a risk, accepting help, being vulnerable. The change must be visible.

Section 14 Appendix

Free Indirect Discourse: Speed and Intimacy

Free Indirect Discourse (FID) merges the narrator's voice with the character's thoughts, creating psychological intimacy without slowing the pace. Instead of:

"She thought that the castle looked ominous."

FID removes the filtering:

"The castle's towers clawed at the sky. Nothing good happened in places like this."

This technique lets you deliver psychological context simultaneously with action. A character fleeing a monster doesn't pause to think "I am terrified because this reminds me of my childhood trauma." Instead:

"The beast's claws were the same rusted iron color as the cage in the cellar. It smelled of wet earth and old blood — the smell of home."

Deep POV: Being Inside the Character

Deep POV eliminates "filter words" — verbs of perception like saw, heard, felt, realized, wondered. The text doesn't report that a character feels pain; the text is the pain.

Standard POV Deep POV
Elara felt a surge of panic as she looked at the closing gates. The gates slammed shut with a finality that vibrated in her teeth. Air refused to enter her lungs. Trapped.
He heard the love interest laugh and realized he wanted to hear it again. Her laugh cut through the noise of the tavern. He'd sell secrets for that sound.
She saw him watching her and felt heat rise to her cheeks. His eyes were on her. Her face burned.

Strategic depth: Modulate POV depth based on emotional stakes. During high-tension romantic encounters or danger, deepen the POV to capture every micro-expression and heartbeat. During transition scenes, pull back for faster pacing.

Section 14 Appendix

Subtext: The Triangulation of Truth

The most compelling character moments use the gap between what characters say, think, and do:

Layer Example
Dialogue "I don't need your help."
Internal thought Please don't leave me.
Body language She crossed her arms, shielding her trembling hands.

Readers perceive the truth the character is hiding. This creates dramatic irony and emotional yearning — we see what they can't admit.

Somatic Empathy: Showing Emotion Through Body

Instead of naming emotions (a cognitive, reflective act), describe the physiological state:

Telling (Named Emotion) Showing (Somatic)
She felt afraid Her stomach dropped; her mouth went dry
He was attracted to her His eyes dropped to her mouth, and the air in the room seemed to thin
She was angry Heat crawled up her spine; her jaw ached from clenching
He felt guilty Something heavy settled in his chest, pressing against his ribs

The romantic gaze: Sexual tension is conveyed through the effect of looking, not the label. "She felt the weight of his stare like a physical touch on the back of her neck."

12 Common Romantasy Character Mistakes

1. The "Not Like Other Girls" Protagonist

The protagonist is defined by being different from (and implicitly better than) other women. She doesn't wear dresses, doesn't care about her appearance, isn't "dramatic."

How to Fix It: Define her positively, not in contrast. What does she care about, not just what doesn't she care about? And let other women be complex too.

2. The Brooding Blank Slate Love Interest

He's described as mysterious and complex, but there's nothing underneath. Dark past referenced but never developed. Brooding substitutes for personality.

How to Fix It: Do the same psychological work for him that you do for her. Wound, lie, flaw, specific desires. Mystery should hide something real.

3. Wound Without Consequences

The character has a tragic backstory, but it doesn't actually affect their present behavior. They function normally despite trauma that would realistically shape everything.

How to Fix It: Trace the wound to present behavior. How does it affect how they interpret events, what they avoid, how they relate to others?

4. The Personality Transplant Arc

The character's arc involves becoming a completely different person. The shy introvert becomes a confident leader. The prickly loner becomes warm and open. Their original personality was just a problem to fix.

How to Fix It: Growth should be expansion, not replacement. The introvert can learn to speak up when needed while still being introverted. Core personality should remain.

Section 14 Appendix

12 Common Romantasy Character Mistakes (continued)

5. The "Just Talk To Each Other" Conflict

The romantic conflict relies on characters not having a simple conversation that would resolve everything. If readers are screaming "just talk!" the conflict isn't deep enough.

How to Fix It: Root conflict in psychology, not miscommunication. Even if they talked, their wounds should make resolution difficult.

Section 14 Appendix

Mistake #6: The Flaw That Disappears

What It Looks Like

The character has a stated flaw — they're impulsive, controlling, jealous — but the flaw only appears when it's convenient. It creates a problem once, then doesn't come up again. Or it's mentioned by other characters but never actually demonstrated. The flaw is a fact about the character rather than a pattern of behavior.

Warning signs:

  • You can name the flaw but struggle to list scenes where it causes problems
  • The flaw appears in early chapters then vanishes
  • Other characters talk about the flaw but we don't see it
  • The flaw never affects the romance
  • The flaw is "fixed" by one conversation or realization

Why It Happens

Writers know characters need flaws but don't want their protagonists to be too unlikeable. So the flaw exists in theory but gets minimized in practice. It's also hard to consistently write flawed behavior without losing reader sympathy.

How to Fix It

Make the flaw visible in multiple scenes. A character who is jealous should show jealousy repeatedly, in big and small ways. Not once — consistently.

Let the flaw damage things. The flaw should hurt the romance, hurt friendships, hurt the character's goals. If the flaw has no consequences, it's not really a flaw.

Connect flaw to wound. The flaw exists because of the wound. When you understand why the character is this way, you can write it with empathy even when it's causing harm.

Maintain the flaw (evolved) even after growth. The character might manage their jealousy better by the end, but it shouldn't completely disappear.

Ask: Does this flaw make the character's life harder throughout the story? If no, it's not functioning as a flaw.

Section 14 Appendix

Mistake #7: The Perfect Partner Reveal

What It Looks Like

The love interest seems problematic early on — cold, cruel, dangerous — but it turns out they were secretly good all along. Every bad thing is explained away. The redemption isn't earned through change; it's revealed through backstory. "He was never really bad; he was just hurting/protecting/pretending."

Warning signs:

  • Bad behavior is justified by sad backstory rather than addressed
  • The love interest doesn't actually change, just gets explained
  • Harmful actions are reframed as protective or necessary
  • The protagonist learns the "real" love interest was always good
  • No genuine accountability or apology occurs

Why It Happens

Writers want to use the appeal of dark/morally gray love interests without doing the work of actual redemption. Backstory explanation is easier than demonstrating change. It also feels romantic to "see the real person underneath."

How to Fix It

Separate explanation from excuse. Understanding why someone behaves badly doesn't make the behavior okay. The backstory explains; the character still needs to change.

Show actual change. If the love interest was cold, show them learning warmth. If they were cruel, show them choosing kindness when it's hard. Don't just reveal they were secretly warm all along.

Require accountability. The love interest should acknowledge what they did, understand the impact, and make amends. Not just "I'm sorry I hurt you" but genuine reckoning with their actions.

Let the protagonist be legitimately affected. If the love interest did harmful things, the protagonist should need time to process that. Quick forgiveness based on explanation undermines both characters.

Ask: Does the love interest actually change, or are they just explained? Redemption requires change, not revelation.

Section 14 Appendix

Mistake #8: The Sidekick Love Interest

What It Looks Like

The love interest exists entirely in relation to the protagonist. They have no goals, friends, or concerns outside the relationship. Their entire purpose in the story is to fall in love with, support, and validate the protagonist. When they're not with the protagonist, they're thinking about the protagonist.

Warning signs:

  • The love interest has no scenes independent of the protagonist
  • You can't describe what they want outside the relationship
  • They have no important relationships besides the protagonist
  • Their skills and abilities exist only to help the protagonist
  • In their POV sections, they mostly think about the protagonist

Why It Happens

It's natural to build the love interest around the romance, since that's their primary story role. But characters who exist only for the protagonist feel more like wish fulfillment than people.

How to Fix It

Give them independent goals. What did they want before the protagonist arrived? What do they still want that has nothing to do with the relationship?

Give them other relationships. Friends, family, rivals, mentors. People who matter to them, whose opinions they care about, who want things from them.

Give them scenes that aren't about the protagonist. Even briefly, show them doing something, dealing with something, caring about something that doesn't relate to the romance.

Let them have conflict with the protagonist based on their own values. They should sometimes disagree not because of misunderstanding but because they genuinely want different things.

Ask: If the protagonist didn't exist, would this character have a story? If no, they need more development.

Mistake #9: The Interchangeable Best Friend

What It Looks Like

The protagonist has a best friend who provides support, comic relief, and someone to talk to about the love interest. Beyond that, the best friend has no distinctive personality, goals, or story. They could be swapped for any other supportive friend character with no impact.

Warning signs:

  • The best friend's main scenes are conversations about the love interest
  • You struggle to describe their personality beyond "loyal" or "funny"
  • They have no subplot or personal stakes
  • Their dialogue could be given to any supporting character
  • They appear only when the protagonist needs support

Why It Happens

Supporting characters are easy to neglect when the focus is on the central romance. The best friend serves a function (sounding board, comic relief) and stops there.

How to Fix It

Give them their own want. What does the best friend want? It doesn't need a full subplot, but it should exist and occasionally affect the story.

Give them a distinctive voice. Their dialogue should be recognizably theirs. A reader should be able to identify them from speech patterns alone.

Let them have opinions that complicate things. Maybe they're not immediately supportive of the romance. Maybe they have legitimate concerns. Maybe they need something from the protagonist that conflicts with the romance.

Give them at least one scene that isn't about the protagonist. Even a brief moment showing them dealing with their own life.

Ask: If this character left the story, what would be lost besides support for the protagonist? If nothing, they need development.

Mistake #10: The All-Powerful Character Who's Somehow Threatened

What It Looks Like

The character is established as incredibly powerful — unmatched magic, legendary combat skills, political dominance — yet somehow faces stakes and threats. The power level is inconsistent, high when demonstrating their appeal and low when the plot needs danger. Or the threats they face seem too weak to actually challenge them.

Warning signs:

  • Early scenes establish massive power; later scenes forget it
  • Threats seem weak compared to established abilities
  • The character's power level seems to change scene by scene
  • You've written "for some reason, it wasn't working" or similar
  • The character easily defeats some enemies but struggles with weaker ones

Why It Happens

Power is appealing, so writers give their characters a lot of it. But powerful characters are hard to threaten, so the power gets quietly nerfed when stakes are needed. It's also easy to lose track of what a character should be capable of.

How to Fix It

Establish clear limits from the start. The most powerful magic should have significant costs. The greatest warrior should have specific weaknesses. Make limitations as compelling as abilities.

Create threats that target the limitations. If your powerful character's weakness is people they love, threaten those people. If their magic doesn't work against a certain substance, put them up against that substance.

Let power create problems. Great power often creates enemies, responsibilities, costs. The power itself can be a source of conflict.

Be consistent. Whatever you establish, maintain it. Track abilities and limits. Don't quietly adjust power level for convenience.

Ask: Given my character's established abilities, does this threat make sense? If no, either strengthen the threat or better establish limitations.

Mistake #11: The "Everyone Loves Them" Protagonist

What It Looks Like

Everyone the protagonist meets likes them. Enemies respect them. Powerful people are drawn to them. Even minor characters treat them as special. There's no significant conflict arising from characters who genuinely dislike or oppose the protagonist.

Warning signs:

  • Antagonists seem to respect or admire the protagonist
  • The protagonist wins people over quickly and easily
  • Few characters have sustained negative views of the protagonist
  • Social conflicts resolve quickly in the protagonist's favor
  • The protagonist is "chosen" or recognized as special by others

Why It Happens

Writers are told to make protagonists likeable and sympathetic, and overcorrect by making everyone in the story like them. It's also more pleasant to write characters succeeding socially than struggling.

How to Fix It

Let some characters dislike them. Not misunderstand them — genuinely dislike them, for reasons that make sense. Not everyone has to come around.

Let the protagonist be wrong in social situations. Let them misjudge someone. Let them fail to win someone over. Let their approach backfire.

Give the antagonist genuine contempt. The villain doesn't need to secretly respect the protagonist. They can see the protagonist as a pest, a disappointment, a fool.

Earn respect rather than assuming it. When powerful characters respect the protagonist, it should be based on specific demonstrated qualities, not innate specialness.

Ask: Who in this story genuinely dislikes the protagonist, and why? If no one, you may have a likability problem.

Mistake #12: Falling in Love With an Aesthetic

What It Looks Like

The love interest is a collection of appealing images — silver eyes, shadow magic, dark throne, dangerous smirk — without psychology underneath. The writer is clearly captivated by the visual/aesthetic appeal but hasn't developed the person beneath the imagery.

Warning signs:

  • You can describe what they look like better than who they are
  • Their scenes focus heavily on physical presence and appearance
  • Their appeal is more visual than personal
  • You developed their aesthetic before their personality
  • You could make a mood board but not a character study

Why It Happens

Romantasy thrives on aesthetic appeal. It's easy to fall in love with an image — the dark fae prince, the scarred warrior, the silver-haired mage — and mistake that love for character development.

How to Fix It

Do the psychological work. Complete Sections 1 and 4 of this workbook fully. Wound, lie, flaw, want, need. Make them a person first.

Find the psychology in the aesthetic. Why do they present this way? What does the darkness hide? What need does the intimidating presence serve? Let the aesthetic become an expression of character rather than a substitute for it.

Write scenes without the aesthetic. Can you write this character's voice in dialogue without describing them? Can you convey who they are without relying on imagery?

Ask: If I removed all physical description, would this character still be compelling? If no, the aesthetic is doing too much work.

Quick Self-Check

Before you finish your draft, run through this checklist:

  • ☐ Does my protagonist have a personality beyond "not like other people"?
  • ☐ Does my love interest have goals, personality, and depth independent of the protagonist?
  • ☐ Do character wounds actually affect present behavior?
  • ☐ Is character growth earned through specific events, not just declared?
  • ☐ Is the conflict based on character psychology, not just withheld information?
  • ☐ Do stated flaws consistently cause problems throughout the story?
  • ☐ Does the love interest actually change, or are they just explained?
  • ☐ Does the love interest have a life and goals outside the relationship?
  • ☐ Do supporting characters have distinctive personalities and wants?
  • ☐ Are character power levels consistent throughout?
  • ☐ Does anyone in the story genuinely dislike the protagonist?
  • ☐ Is my love interest a person, not just an aesthetic?

If you answered "no" or "not sure" to any of these, go back and address it. These are the character mistakes that separate forgettable romantasy from the books readers remember.

The Core Principle: Characters Are People

Every mistake on this list comes from the same root: treating characters as functions rather than people. The brooding love interest who exists to be mysterious. The best friend who exists to be supportive. The protagonist who exists to be special.

Real characters have interior lives that don't serve the plot. They have contradictions. They want things that aren't narratively convenient. They make choices that complicate the story.

The Key Question
When you catch yourself asking "what does the story need this character to do?" — stop. Ask instead: "What would this person actually do?"

That's the difference between a character who serves the story and a character who lives in it.

Final Thought: Mistakes Are Fixable

Every writer falls into some of these traps in early drafts. Finding them isn't failure — it's part of the process. The goal isn't to write perfectly the first time; it's to develop the awareness to catch these patterns and the skills to fix them.

The fact that you're thinking about these issues means you're already ahead. Now take what you've learned and make your characters stronger.

That's how you write characters readers fall in love with.
