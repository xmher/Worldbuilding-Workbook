# Meet the Two Running Examples

Two books will keep reappearing in the pages ahead. You've probably read them both.

Their job isn't to be copied. They're here to give you two very different answers to the same question every time a prompt asks one, so that when you sit down to answer it yourself, you have shape, contrast, and the permission of already-successful books to lean into what makes your answer strange.

Each example is here because it does one thing better than nearly any other book in the genre. When a section is about bonds, you'll see how Maas handles the mate-snap and how Yarros handles the dragon-split. If a section's about academies, the Empyrean quadrants are in scope. For fae courts, Prythian is the anchor.

## Example One: *Fourth Wing* (Rebecca Yarros)

What it anchors: dragon-bonded military academies, signet-style magic, kingdom politics under wartime strain, a fated mating bleed between dragons and their riders, and, quietly and importantly, a protagonist with an invisible disability whose body is a real and ongoing constraint on the plot.

When a chapter refers to Violet, Xaden, Tairn, Sgaeyl, Basgiath, signets, or the Parapet, Yarros is being used as a case study. *Iron Flame* and *Onyx Storm* are in scope where they're useful. Where the later books disagree with *Fourth Wing*, the examples usually lean on book one, because book one is where the worldbuilding is tightest.

Yarros isn't a model of flawless worldbuilding. Some of her institutional logic is fragile, and critics will tell you at length why a dictatorship arming the orphans of executed rebels is the dumbest military policy imaginable.

But what Yarros does superbly well is weld a magic system (signets) to a romance engine (dragon-forced proximity) to a protagonist's body (her joints, her inventory checks, her planning overhead), in a way that makes every magical decision serve character.

Steal the integration, question the institutional logic, and take what works.

## Example Two: *A Court of Thorns and Roses* (Sarah J. Maas)

What it anchors: fae courts, seasonal and solar courts, the wall between mortal and fae realms, glamour and its consent problems, bargains made with tattoos, mating bonds revealed late, the gap between mortal and immortal lifespans, and the forbidden-love engine of politics across court lines.

When a chapter refers to Prythian, the Night Court, Velaris, the Summer or Spring Courts, Hybern, the mate bond between Feyre and Rhysand, the bargain mechanic, Calanmai, or the Under the Mountain arc, Maas is being used as a case study. The later books (*ACOMAF* through *ACOSF*) are in scope.

Maas isn't a model of flawless worldbuilding either. Her geography draws heavily on Britain and Ireland, her economy is famously absent from the page, and her continent has High Lords but no High King and no clear explanation of how seven autonomous regions avoid civil war.

But what Maas does superbly well is use bonds, bargains, and glamour as both the romantic engine *and* the consent problem, turning the things that bring her lovers together into the things that threaten their ability to consent at all.

Steal the engineering, notice where the governance is handwaved, and decide for yourself.

## How the Two Examples Appear

When a case-study reference appears, it'll be short, specific, and labelled. Look for phrases like:

> *Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros gives Violet's signet as lightning...

> *Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas delays the mate bond reveal until...

Neither example will be referenced on every page. Where one earns its place, it appears. Where it doesn't, it stays quiet.
