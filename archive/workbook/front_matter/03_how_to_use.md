# How to Use This Workbook

This workbook is a working document, not a reference book. It expects to be written in. Keep a pen nearby, and something warm to drink.

## The Shape of the Whole Thing

Ten parts, then appendices. Each part is made of short, standalone pages. Every page is designed to hold up on its own, so you can drop in on page 147, answer what's in front of you, and benefit, without having read the 146 pages before it.

Here's the rough shape.

- Part 1. Before You Begin. Subgenre choice, comp titles, tone, and a one-sentence hook.
- Part 2. Your Couple First. Your heroine and her love interest, before any worldbuilding.
- Part 3. The World Skeleton. Geography, species, races, class, economy.
- Part 4. The Systems. Magic, religion, government, language, daily life.
- Part 5. Culture and Customs. Rituals, taboos, gender, sexuality, death.
- Part 6. The Romantasy Layer. Bonds, mates, courtship, heat, consent, forbidden love. The heart.
- Part 7. Subgenre Deep Dives. Ten subgenres. Read only the ones relevant to your book.
- Part 8. Welding World to Romance. Where every prior decision gets audited against your couple.
- Part 9. Story Framing. A light plotting pass, not full craft, just enough to start drafting.
- Part 10. Red Flags and Troubleshooting. Common mistakes, unsticking prompts, beta-reader questions.

Then appendices: tropes, subgenre cheat sheets, blank worksheets, a one-hour sprint for when you're stuck, and recommended reading.

## The Prompt Tiers, Again Briefly

Every prompt is tagged with one of three levels.

**[L1]** prompts are foundational. Answer these and your world will stand up.

**[L2]** prompts deepen. Answer them where the topic matters to your plot.

**[L3]** prompts are for the specialist who wants to stress-test every decision. Optional.

You don't have to work through all three levels in order. A first-time writer can do only the L1s and finish with a full world. A second-drafter can skip straight to the L3s. There's no wrong way to use the tiers.

## Three Kinds of Callouts to Expect

Throughout the workbook, three boxed blocks will interrupt the main text.

**Romance Leverage.** At the end of every major section, a short boxed prompt that ties the worldbuilding topic directly back to your couple. This is the signature move of the whole workbook. If a section has no Romance Leverage, it's probably a typo.

**Common Mistake.** A flag for errors writers routinely make in that area, with a corrective prompt. Expect two or three per major part.

**Stuck?** A short unsticking prompt every twenty to thirty pages, for when the page is staring back at you and nothing is coming.

## Where to Start

Start wherever you like. A few sensible options, depending on where your head is.

- If you know your couple but not your world, start at Part 2, then move into Part 3.
- With a world but no couple, start at Part 1 and then head to Part 2.
- Stuck on bonds, mates, or consent mechanics? Skip straight to Part 6.
- In the middle of a draft and the worldbuilding is falling apart? Go to Part 8.
- Got an hour and want a sprint? Flip to Appendix F.

## What You'll Need

- A pen. Preferably one that doesn't bleed through paper, if you're printing.
- A notes page or a notebook for the overflow, because you will overflow.
- Your favourite romantasy on the shelf within reach. Re-reading the odd scene is research, not procrastination.
- Permission to be messy. This is a draft workbook, not a museum piece.

## A Quick Word on Pace

There's no deadline. Do five pages today and none tomorrow. Do twenty pages in a weekend and go quiet for a fortnight. The only thing that will actively harm your work is trying to finish the workbook in one sitting because you bought it yesterday and feel behind.

You aren't behind. Start where you are.
