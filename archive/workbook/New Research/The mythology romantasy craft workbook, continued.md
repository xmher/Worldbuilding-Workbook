## Part III (continued): The market as of April 2026 — who is writing what, and what to steal from them

The romantasy market has fractured into distinct lanes in the last 24 months, and understanding which shelf your book is fighting for is a worldbuilding decision before it is a marketing one. Readers now come to Greek material expecting one of three flavors: the **St. Clair lane** (modern-New-Greece, spicy, series-structured, Hades-Persephone gravity well), the **Robert lane** (walled city-state, titles-not-deities, political thriller energy, polyamory friendly), or the **Miller lane** (literary, interior, faithful-retelling register). Each lane has a different contract with the reader, and the fastest way to confuse an editor is to mix their load-bearing conventions without a clear reason.

### The Greek gravity well

**Scarlett St. Clair** built the default contemporary-pantheon template with *A Touch of Darkness* (2019) and its five sequels. Her craft move most worth stealing: treat Olympus as a working city where gods run businesses. Hades owns Nevernight, an invitation-only club in a recognizably modern metropolis called New Greece; Aphrodite runs a magazine; Hermes runs a courier service. **The domain leaks into the occupation.** Demeter's portrayal as a controlling mother — not a sweet harvest goddess — gave the series its spine and taught the market that reframing a "minor" antagonist is often where the story actually lives. The consent handling is uneven and has been critiqued; if you are writing in this lane, read the book and its criticism side by side.

**Katee Robert's Dark Olympus** series (beginning with *Neon Gods*, 2021, now extending through *Sweet Obsession* and beyond) does something structurally different and more durable: **the gods are not gods.** Olympus is a walled modern city sealed off from the outside world, ruled by **the Thirteen** — thirteen titles (Zeus, Hera, Poseidon, Hades, Aphrodite, Hermes, Hephaestus, Dionysus, Demeter, Athena, Artemis, Ares, Apollo) that pass between mortals by different mechanisms. Zeus is a legacy role inherited by the eldest son of the ruling family. Hades is a legacy role tied to the lower city. Hera is whoever Zeus marries. Ares is won in a tournament. Aphrodite handles alliances, Hermes runs communication, Demeter controls supply lines, Apollo keeps the archives. **The titles carry the mythology; the people carry the romance.** This single structural decision solves the biggest craft problem in any pantheon romance — how do you keep a god interesting across multiple books without exhausting their myth? — by making the title a costume anyone can compete for. When Wicked Beauty makes Helen enter the Ares tournament rather than be its prize, the mythology does feminist work the bare retelling cannot. Steal this: **separate the role from the person.** It generates plot automatically.

**Abigail Owen's The Crucible** (launched with *The Games Gods Play*, September 2024, Red Tower Books) is the current fastest-growing entrant — NYT and USA Today #1, B&N Best of 2024. Owen's model is **Hunger-Games-meets-Olympus**: every hundred years the pantheon holds a contest called the Crucible to determine who rules Olympus, each god picks a human champion, the trials are structured around the Twelve Labors. The setting is modern-day San Francisco with Olympus existing as a literal place. Owen's confessed worldbuilding prompt is "**What would be on the streets in Olympus?**" — she reverse-engineers from street level. For writers, the takeaway is that pantheons benefit from **one load-bearing ritual event** (a Crucible, a Tournament, an Agon) that justifies assembling all the gods in one place, forcing interaction, and letting you show an entire pantheon through one POV without exposition dumps.

**Madeline Miller** remains the literary gold standard — *The Song of Achilles* (2011) and *Circe* (2018) are the texts every romantasy manuscript gets compared to by agents who want to raise the floor. Miller's move is **interiority plus sentence music.** She doesn't invent; she inhabits. Circe's 3,000 years of exile are rendered in a present-tense intimacy that makes a goddess a woman without demoting her. Steal the rhythm, not the plot. Mary Renault's *The King Must Die* (1958) does the same work for Theseus and is still the best masterclass in rendering bronze-age Greek interior life.

**Alexis Hall's Glitterland universe**, **Molly Ringle's Persephone's Orchard**, and the webtoon **Lore Olympus** (Rachel Smythe) round out the Hades-Persephone ecosystem. Lore Olympus matters commercially — its TikTok virality from 2019 onward is why agents started signing Hades-Persephone manuscripts; its visual vocabulary (Hades as corporate blue, Persephone as pink) became the Pinterest shorthand that aspiring authors now trip over.

The **literary-commercial border** is held by **Jennifer Saint** (*Ariadne*, *Elektra*, *Atalanta*, *Hera*), **Natalie Haynes** (*A Thousand Ships*, *Stone Blind*), **Costanza Casati** (*Clytemnestra*), **Claire Heywood** (*Daughters of Sparta*), and **Bea Fitzgerald** (*Girl, Goddess, Queen*). These books sit on the "mythic retelling" shelf rather than the romantasy shelf; the romance is present but not the engine. Knowing the difference protects your pitch. If your book is really a feminist retelling with romantic subplot, stop calling it romantasy. The contract is different.

### Beyond Greece — what the map actually looks like

**Norse** remains under-served given its name recognition. Genevieve Gornichec's *The Witch's Heart* (2021) is the reference text: Angrboda's first-person voice reframes Loki's giantess wife as protagonist, and the craft lesson is that **Norse works best when you pick one overlooked figure and refuse the Marvel version**. Expect the Norse shelf to expand through 2026-27 as publishers look for the next pantheon.

**Egyptian** is the most under-filled shelf in the genre, which is both an opportunity and a warning — the absence reflects both the complexity of the source (living syncretic practice in Kemetic communities) and the Orientalist pitfalls (mummy-curse shorthand, sexy-pharaoh clichés). Jessica S. Olson and Nicki Pau Preto work in Egyptian-adjacent registers. Diaspora Egyptian voices writing romantasy at scale have not yet emerged in the market; when they do, they will reset expectations.

**Chinese** has the deepest bench. **Sue Lynn Tan's Daughter of the Moon Goddess** duology renders the Celestial Kingdom with Tang-dynasty court formality and slow-burn xianxia pacing — Tan's craft move is **bureaucratic mythology**, the idea that heaven has paperwork. **Xiran Jay Zhao's *Iron Widow*** fuses Chinese history with mecha and polyamory, and its refusal of the "respectable" register is instructive: you do not owe the tradition reverence, only rigor. **Judy I. Lin** (*A Magic Steeped in Poison*) brings tea magic and Taiwanese folk practice. The **Mo Xiang Tong Xiu** translations (*Heaven Official's Blessing*, *The Grandmaster of Demonic Cultivation*) crossed into the English market around 2021-2022 and reset what Western readers expect from a long, slow, queer immortal romance. Ignore this influence at your peril — a meaningful share of your readers now arrive with cultivation-novel grammar already installed.

**South Asian** is where sensitivity and marketing collide most visibly. **Tasha Suri's Burning Kingdoms trilogy** (*The Jasmine Throne*, 2021) is a sapphic Mughal-inflected epic fantasy with romance weight; Suri, British-Punjabi, writes from diaspora. **Roshani Chokshi** works primarily YA/middle-grade (Aru Shah, Star-Touched Queen). **Vaishnavi Patel's *Kaikeyi*** (2022) and *Goddess of the River* reframe Ramayana figures; *Kaikeyi* drew both acclaim and pointed Hindutva-aligned pushback, which is the market reality of writing Hindu material in 2025-26. **Chitra Banerjee Divakaruni's *Palace of Illusions*** (Draupadi POV) is the elder statement text.

**Korean** breakouts: **Axie Oh's *The Girl Who Fell Beneath the Sea*** (2022) retells Shim Cheong with Studio Ghibli visuals. K-drama influence — *Goblin*, *Tale of the Nine-Tailed*, *Hotel del Luna* — shapes reader expectations for Korean-inspired work; the gumiho/grim-reaper romance is now a recognizable shape.

**West African and diaspora**: **Tomi Adeyemi's *Children of Blood and Bone*** (Orisha-inspired, 2018) remains the commercial reference and has drawn both praise and critique from Yoruba practitioners about how closed material gets handled at YA scale. **Namina Forna's *The Gilded Ones***, **Ayana Gray's Beasts of Prey**, and **Natasha Bowen's *Skin of the Sea*** (Mami Wata) expand the shelf. **Nnedi Okorafor** (Nigerian-American) and **Akwaeke Emezi** (Igbo) work at the literary end and are essential reading for anyone writing in this register.

**Slavic** romantasy is consolidating into a clear market: **Katherine Arden's Winternight trilogy** (*The Bear and the Nightingale*), **Naomi Novik's *Spinning Silver* and *Uprooted***, **Olesya Salnikova Gilmore's *The Witch and the Tsar*** and *The Haunting of Moscow House*, **Ava Reid's *The Wolf and the Woodsman***, and **Emily Duncan's Wicked Saints**. The common craft move is **winter as a character** — the landscape does the genre's emotional work before the plot arrives.

**Mesoamerican**: **Silvia Moreno-Garcia's *Gods of Jade and Shadow*** (Maya, 1920s jazz age) is the reference. **Isabel Cañas** (*The Hacienda*) works adjacent.

**Indigenous American**: **Rebecca Roanhorse** (*Trail of Lightning*, *Black Sun*) is essential reading and essential context — see the sensitivity section below. **Stephen Graham Jones** and **Darcie Little Badger** work horror-adjacent.

**Djinn and Islamic folklore**: **S.A. Chakraborty's Daevabad trilogy** (*The City of Brass*, 2017) is the defining text. Six djinn tribes inside the brass walls of Daevabad, each with distinct magic traditions, factional resentments stretching 1,400 years, an 18th-century Cairo opening that anchors the reader in recognizable geography before the fantasy flood begins. Chakraborty's craft genius is **prejudice as worldbuilding**: the daeva/djinn/shafit hierarchy isn't decoration, it's the plot engine, and the romance stays honest because it never resolves the underlying bigotry cheaply. **P. Djèlí Clark** (*A Master of Djinn*, alt-history Cairo) and **Sabaa Tahir** (*An Ember in the Ashes*, Roman-coded with djinn) round out the shelf.

## Part IV: Folklore as source material — the quieter cousin of pantheon myth

The genre's obsession with pantheons obscures the fact that most living mythological imagination happens at the folklore tier — kitchen spirits, roadside ghosts, river hauntings, the animal bride who can never be asked her name. Folklore differs from pantheon myth in three craft-relevant ways. **Scale**: one kitsune vs. a Titanomachy. **Intimacy**: hearth and threshold vs. throne room. **Register**: gothic and erotic vs. epic. Romance is often built into folklore at the DNA level — the selkie wife, the kitsune bride, the swan maiden, the leanan sídhe — whereas pantheon romance has to be grafted on. For a writer looking to be the first rather than the fifth in a subgenre, folklore is where the shelf space lives.

**Slavic folklore** has entered its Koschei renaissance. The death-in-the-egg motif (Koschei the Deathless cannot die unless you find the egg that contains his death, inside the duck, inside the hare, inside the chest, inside the oak, on the island) gives you a built-in quest structure better than most Campbellian templates. Baba Yaga, properly rendered, is a threshold figure — neither helper nor villain, governed by hospitality rules. Rusalki (drowned-maiden water spirits), domovoi (house spirits), leshy (forest), bannik (bathhouse, dangerous), vodyanoy (river), kikimora, and zmey-dragons all carry romance potential. Arden, Novik, and Gilmore are the current model authors.

**Middle Eastern** folklore, especially Islamic-era material, distinguishes djinn types: ifrit (fire-powerful), marid (water, often oceanic), ghul (graveyard shapeshifter), si'lat (shapeshifting seductress), and the Persian peri (luminous, sometimes fallen). Pre-Islamic div/daeva persist in Zoroastrian-inflected settings. The craft-relevant fact is that djinn are **Quranically canonical** for many Muslim readers, which means fiction has both a richer vocabulary and a higher stakes: treating djinn as *Aladdin*-style wish servants reads to many Muslim readers roughly the way angels as wish-granting pets would read to devout Christians.

**East Asian** folklore gives you kitsune (nine-tailed foxes, Inari's messengers, with a marriage-until-discovered trope already built in), tengu, oni, kappa, yuki-onna, and tsukumogami (household objects that gain spirit at 100 years old — an underexploited romance-with-sentient-teapot premise). Chinese folklore: huli jing (fox spirits, often more villainous than Japanese kitsune), the four dragon kings of the seas, the celestial bureaucracy (which gave MXTX her scaffolding), jiangshi (hopping vampires), pixiu. Korean: gumiho (nine-tailed fox, frequently romantic lead), dokkaebi (goblins, see the K-drama), jeoseung saja (grim reapers, see *Goblin*). K-drama shapes the reader vocabulary here more than any novel does.

**Celtic folklore** is where the fae-court calcification lives. The sidhe, Seelie/Unseelie court division (a post-medieval literary construct, not ancient Irish), iron allergy, selkies and kelpies and púca, changelings, banshees, dullahan, and the leanan sídhe (muse-lover who drains her poet, the original "loving him is killing me" romance). Holly Black, Sarah J. Maas, Juliet Marillier, Melissa Marr, and Emily Tesh work this material at different registers. **If you are writing fae in 2026, you must know that the court / bargain / name-power / iron vocabulary is now so standardized it reads as generic unless you actively complicate it.** Marillier's *Daughter of the Forest* still shows how to root fae in actual Irish material (the Children of Lir) rather than in the Maas-derived house style.

**West African folklore** distinguishes between the Orisha (living religious figures in Yoruba/Santería/Candomblé/Ifá practice — *not* folklore) and folkloric entities that crossed the Atlantic: Anansi (Akan/Ashanti spider trickster → Caribbean Aunt Nancy → American Aunt Nancy), Mami Wata (water spirit whose practice is *living*, not historical, complicating fictional use), tokoloshe (Southern African), sasabonsam, aziza (Dahomey forest spirits, appear as "Aziza" in Tomi Adeyemi).

**Southeast Asian folklore** is the most under-represented rich source currently on the market. Filipino aswang, manananggal (detachable-torso vampire), tikbalang (horse-headed tricksters), diwata (forest deities, romantic potential). Indonesian/Malay pontianak (vengeful ghost of woman who died in childbirth, one of horror's most terrifying figures), orang bunian (hidden people). Thai phi (ghost taxonomy) and nak. Vietnamese ma. Authors like **Trang Thanh Tran** (*She Is a Haunting*) and **Rin Chupeco** (*The Bone Witch*) work this territory, but the shelf is thin and the opportunity is enormous.

## Part V: Cultural sensitivity — from generic guidance to practitioner-grade protocol

Most advice on "writing other cultures" treats all traditions as equivalent risk. They are not. A hierarchy is honest. **Dead pantheons** (Greek, Norse, Egyptian, Mesopotamian) carry lower religious stakes because no active community prays to Zeus on Sunday. **Reconstructionist pantheons** (Heathenry, Kemetic, Hellenic revival) are small but present and should be consulted. **Living religions with folk-tale penumbras** (Hindu, Shinto, Buddhist) require specific care for central figures (Shiva, Krishna, Amaterasu) distinct from peripheral ones (yokai, lesser yakshas). **Living Indigenous and diaspora traditions** (Yoruba/Orisha, Vodou, Ifá, Santería, Candomblé, Navajo/Diné, Lakota, etc.) carry the highest stakes because they contain closed-practice elements and live within communities that have been actively harmed by outsider extraction.

### Hindu material — the specific risk landscape

Hindu traditions require care calibrated to the deity. Writing a minor yaksha forest-spirit romance is a different risk profile than writing Krishna as a love interest (theologically loaded; Krishna's romantic relationships are doctrinally specific and already devotional). Kali is consistently the deity most often flattened into "dark feminine" shorthand in Western fiction; her Shakta theological specificity dissolves into aesthetic. Rama and Sita post-Ayodhya are politically charged in ways a Western author will not feel. Caste representation — whose perspective you center, whether Dalit voices exist in your world — is a live political question. The **Hindu American Foundation** (Suhag Shukla as frequent spokesperson) has been publicly vocal about media depictions (Newsweek's Obama-as-Shiva cover, Simpsons-era Apu portrayal, Disney's Mira/Holi handling) and is a useful barometer of what causes offense, though readers should be aware of **ongoing scholarly critique of HAF's Hindutva alignment** and understand that HAF does not speak for all Hindus — particularly Dalit, Shaivite, Shakta, Vaishnavite, and regional communities whose concerns may differ. The monolith problem is real: there is no single "Hindu" perspective. Vaishnavi Patel's *Kaikeyi* received both Hindu-American praise and Hindutva-aligned attack; that is the shape of the conversation, and your book will land inside it whether you intend it to or not. Practical guidance: consult more than one sensitivity reader; ideally include both a devotional practitioner and someone from a different sampradaya than your primary source; be specific about which regional tradition you are rendering rather than flattening "Hindu mythology" into a single palette.

### Yoruba / Orisha / African diasporic religion

The key distinction: **Orisha are not folklore.** They are worshipped daily in Nigeria, Cuba (Lucumí/Santería), Brazil (Candomblé), Haiti (Vodou's lwa overlap), Trinidad, and across the diaspora. Initiated babalawos and iyanifas hold knowledge that is formally closed — not secret to be dramatic, but structurally withheld from non-initiates as part of the practice itself. Tomi Adeyemi's handling in *Children of Blood and Bone* has drawn both praise for visibility and critique from Yoruba practitioners about specifics; the discourse is worth reading before you enter the lane. Beyoncé's *Lemonade* and *Black Is King* generated similar conversations about Orisha iconography in pop culture. Hollywood's "voodoo" is to Haitian Vodou what "witch doctor" is to any real practice — a century of defamation that any serious treatment has to push against. Practical guidance: if you are not initiated, do not write Orisha possession scenes; if you are writing diaspora practice, cite the specific tradition (Lucumí is not Candomblé is not Vodou); consult an initiated reader, not just a sensitivity reader with Yoruba heritage.

### Indigenous American material — the Trail of Lightning case study

The **Rebecca Roanhorse / *Trail of Lightning*** controversy (2018) is the essential case study for every author in the genre. Roanhorse, of claimed Ohkay Owingeh Pueblo heritage and married into the Navajo Nation, wrote a post-apocalyptic novel with a Navajo monster-hunter protagonist. The **Diné Writers' Collective Saad Bee Hózhǫ́** (signers included Jennifer Denetdale, Jake Skeets, Luci Tapahonso, Laura Tohe, Sherwin Bitsui, Esther Belin and others) published an open letter in Indian Country Today (Nov 2018) titled "*Trail of Lightning* is an appropriation of Diné cultural beliefs," arguing that Roanhorse — not Diné — lacked the authority to render Navajo spiritual material and had done so in ways they considered harmful. **Debbie Reese** (Nambé Pueblo, founder of American Indians in Children's Literature) had initially recommended the book; she publicly retracted that recommendation after hearing from Diné readers. Leaders of the Ohkay Owingeh community later stated Roanhorse was not enrolled there. The case teaches three things. **First**: sensitivity readers are not community endorsement; a single Native reader saying yes does not equal a community saying yes. **Second**: marriage into a community does not transfer cultural authority. **Third**: some material is closed regardless of narrative cleverness — skinwalker (Diné), wendigo (Algonquian), and certain ceremonial specifics are not shared even between neighboring Native nations, and their use by outsiders is consistently objected to. For non-Indigenous writers: do not write from inside these traditions. You can write worlds adjacent to them, reference them as outsiders in your narrator's position, or — better — write your own heritage.

### The sensitivity reader protocol

Sensitivity readers are necessary but insufficient. Budget **$300-700 per reader** for a full manuscript as of 2026 rates. Services: **Salt & Sage Books**, **Tessera Editorial**, **Writing Diversely**. What they can do: flag stereotypes, catch language errors, identify tone problems, suggest reframings. What they cannot do: grant you community permission, speak for an entire tradition, or absolve a book whose premise is structurally extractive. Use more than one reader per tradition where possible. Prioritize practitioner-consultants (religious practitioners, not just heritage readers) when writing living religions. **The Nisi Shawl / Cynthia Ward *Writing the Other*** framework — writing ROAARS (race, orientation, age, ability, religion, sex) characters as rounded people rather than representative tokens — remains the starting-point text. Build a **consult loop** into your process: outline stage, first draft, revision, copy-edit. Waiting until the copy-edit to consult is too late; the story is locked. Budget both time and real money for this work. It is not optional infrastructure.

### The author's note as contract

The **author's note** is where you do the work of telling readers what you did and did not invent. Best practice: name the specific tradition(s) you drew from, name your consultants (with their permission), acknowledge what you changed and why, recommend primary-source readings by practitioners, and — if you are writing from outside a tradition — say so explicitly rather than letting readers assume heritage. Madeline Miller's *Circe* author's note is a model. Patel's *Kaikeyi* notes handle political charge well. Roanhorse's later *Black Sun* notes show evolution from the *Trail of Lightning* backlash. Read fifty author's notes before you write yours.

## Part VI: The workbook — exercises, checklists, and the writing process itself

### Exercise 1: the three-question pantheon audit

Before you write a single scene with a deity, answer these three questions in writing, 500 words minimum each.

**What does this god want today that they did not want yesterday?** If the answer is "nothing, they are eternal," you have a statue, not a character. Desire that changes is the only thing that produces plot. Robert's Ares-as-title and Owen's Hades-entering-the-Crucible both solve this by forcing gods into contingent, timed stakes.

**What does this god's worship actually look like in your world?** If Demeter exists, people farm differently. If Hades exists, funerals are different. If Aphrodite exists, weddings and divorces go through her office. Worship is the bridge between pantheon and street level, and most manuscripts skip it.

**What can this god not do?** Limitation creates character. Athena cannot be stupid. Ares cannot be a coward. Hephaestus cannot be beautiful in the ways his siblings are. The constraint is where the story lives.

### Exercise 2: the domain-leakage sentence

For every major god in your manuscript, write one sentence that is true because of their domain. *"Persephone cannot eat fruit from a stranger's hand without checking."* *"Hades can tell when someone in the room is about to die but has learned not to react."* *"Aphrodite's shadow is always slightly longer than it should be."* Domain leakage is what makes a god feel like a god rather than a person in a Halloween costume. Every scene with a god should contain at least one moment of leakage. Most published romantasy under-uses this; it is the fastest free craft upgrade available to you.

### Exercise 3: the closed-material triage

List every element you plan to use from any living religious or cultural tradition. For each, categorize:
1. **Public** — freely taught, published in accessible sources, used in pop culture by practitioners themselves.
2. **Contested** — some practitioners share it, some don't; the line is live.
3. **Closed** — practitioners consistently ask outsiders not to use it.

If category 3 appears on your list, cut it or radically rework it. If category 2 appears, consult a practitioner from that specific tradition. If only category 1 appears, you still need a reader.

### Exercise 4: the Koschei-style metaphysical spine

Every mythology romantasy that works has a **metaphysical mechanism** the romance threads through. *Koschei's death is in an egg inside a duck*. *Persephone must eat pomegranate seeds or not*. *The Thirteen hold titles that can be lost*. *Every hundred years the Crucible convenes*. Write yours in a single sentence. If you cannot, your premise is not yet mechanically load-bearing. Keep reworking until the sentence exists. This sentence is usually also your logline.

### Exercise 5: the romance-mythology collision test

The strongest mythology romances don't just use myth as backdrop; they use myth to **force** the romance choice. Persephone eats or doesn't — that's the marriage. Helen enters the tournament — that's the agency. Psyche opens the jar — that's the trust. Ask: does my mythology cause a choice my romance plot needs made? If no, the two halves of your book are parallel rather than intertwined, and readers will feel it even if they can't name it.

### The pre-flight checklist (use before first submission)

Run your manuscript against these eight questions. A "no" anywhere is a revision flag, not necessarily a fatal problem.

1. Does every god appearance include at least one domain-leakage detail?
2. Is there a single metaphysical mechanism sentence that names this book's engine?
3. Are the romantic stakes caused by — not parallel to — the mythology?
4. Have you named your tradition(s) specifically rather than blending them into a mythic smoothie?
5. Have at least two sensitivity or practitioner readers engaged with your living-religion material?
6. Does your author's note name what you invented, what you drew from, and whom you consulted?
7. Does your comp set include at least one recent (2022-2026) book in your exact lane?
8. Is there one scene a reader could screenshot and recognize as inarguably *yours* rather than generic?

### A final word on originality

The romantasy boom has flattened a great deal of distinct mythology into a single Pinterest board of black-and-gold aesthetics, tortured dark-haired gods, fated-mates marking scenes, and thrones made of ambiguous dark matter. **Your advantage is specificity.** The book that wins is not the one that hits every trope but the one whose mythology is rendered with the specificity of somebody who actually knows the source — or has apprenticed to it through consultation. The writers who will define 2027-2028 are the ones currently drafting in the folkloric middle lane, pulling from Southeast Asian hauntings, diaspora Yoruba material, Zoroastrian apocalyptic imagery, Mesoamerican cosmology — places the market has not yet calcified around defaults.

**Write the book only you can write**, using sources you have earned through research, consultation, and craft, rendered with the domain-leakage specificity that lets a reader smell your Olympus from three streets away. That is the whole workbook, in one sentence.
