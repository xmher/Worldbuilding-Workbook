## Workbook 1 — School of Plot, World Building Workbook

### 1. Identification
- **Seller:** School of Plot (schoolofplot.com, @schoolofplot on Instagram/TikTok). One-person shop per its own copy.
- **Title:** "World Building Workbook"
- **Format:** Digital PDF, print-ready (the file name in the uploaded copy is "PRINTVERSION"). Fillable but also meant for printing.
- **Approximate page count:** The file is ~60 layout pages as written (each section slug + page break = ~1 page; roughly 50–60 pages printed).
- **Price:** Not specified in the file. School of Plot's general workbook pricing on Etsy sits in the $10–18 range; their worldbuilding workbook typically lists around $12–15.
- **Genre targeting:** Fantasy generalist. Not romantasy. One single romantasy-adjacent prompt ("in-world Romeo & Juliet couples") is the total romantasy content.

### 2. Structural analysis
Element-by-element, sequential but with explicit permission to skip. Twelve topic clusters:

1. Before You Begin (philosophy intro + Terry Pratchett quote)
2. Cities & Lands (place names, maps, travel, natural resources, architecture, geography)
3. History & Politics (history prompts, government types, currency design)
4. Magic System (hard vs soft, types, limitations, politics-integration, spell catalog)
5. Culture (broad culture, social, marriage, children, food, class)
6. Language (basic words, common sounds, register)
7. Timekeeping
8. Fashion (rich, poor, descriptive terms)
9. Religion (how to write, god design, deity tracker, prompts)

Each cluster opens with a teaching page or two, then moves to prompts and/or cheat-sheet tables. Modular in that you can skip any section, but the order assumes a reader going top to bottom.

### 3. Content coverage

**Covers well:** naming conventions and toponymy (prefix/suffix tables), travel-time realism (miles-per-day calculator), natural resources and their cultural downstream effects, architecture-by-climate, government type definitions, soft vs hard magic comparison chart, magic types catalog, magic limitations, fashion vocabulary, religion-culture feedback loops, class relations.

**Covers lightly:** history (prompts are good but thin — three pages), timekeeping (two pages), language (surface-level, one page of word suggestions).

**Does NOT cover but should:** trade and economy beyond currency design (no supply chains, guilds, market structures), seasons and weather patterns as lived experience, warfare and military structures, technology levels and their knock-ons, disability and aging, death and mourning rituals, sexuality and gender systems beyond "is clothing gendered," childhood and coming-of-age detail, prophecy and fate systems, afterlife beliefs, the relationship between magic and the physical laws of the world.

**Romantasy content:** essentially zero. One prompt mentions "in-world Romeo & Juliet" comparisons. Religion section asks how atheists are treated but nothing about how religion views romance, sex, or queerness. Culture section has "what does courting involve?" as a single line. Fashion covers marriage status briefly. This workbook is *fantasy worldbuilding*, not *romantasy worldbuilding* — it's sold adjacent to romantasy writers but was built for fantasy generalists.

### 4. Prompt quality
- **Generative or closed:** Mix. Opening pages of each cluster are often generative ("How might culture and geography shape religion?"). Actual fillables skew closed — tables with "Name," "Known for," "Climate," "Language" etc. The single-line prompts in Culture/Marriage/Food are short-answer.
- **Enough per section:** Magic has ~40 prompts across 8 pages — plenty. Religion has ~20. History has ~9. Language has ~10.
- **Too many:** Magic section is borderline — by page 5 of magic prompts the reader is drained.
- **Difficulty escalation:** Flat within sections. No "start here, now dig deeper" scaffolding. Reader encounters "who were the first users of magic?" and "how does magic influence politics?" with equal weight, no ordering.

### 5. Voice and tone
- **Warm.** The intro ("Dear Writer,") is genuinely affectionate. Closes with "Happy worldbuilding, writer!"
- **Beginner-accessible.** Explains toponymy, soft vs hard magic, government types without assuming prior craft knowledge.
- **Permission-giving, not preachy.** Repeated refrains: "you really don't need a map," "feel free to delete these pages if you don't want to go into this level of detail," "your setting name doesn't need to mean something if it's taking up too much of your energy."
- **Memorable:** Terry Pratchett quote as frontispiece, the "goodbye comes from 'God be with ye'" aside in Religion, the worldbuilding philosophy of peppering detail rather than dumping. Specific enough to stick.

### 6. Visual / layout
Single-column Canva-style layout. Generous white space. Consistent footer branding (@SCHOOLOFPLOT SCHOOLOFPLOT.COM on nearly every page). Tables used for cheat sheets (prefix-suffix, travel calc, soft-vs-hard magic, natural resources). Text pages alternate with fillable worksheet pages — healthy rhythm. Modular page design — each page is self-contained, readable in any order. Probably a neutral cream/brown palette based on the branding aesthetic. Moderate visual variety; no real illustrations or icons shown in the text extract.

### 7. Specific strengths (steal/adapt)
1. **Cheat-sheet tables with real-world grounding.** The toponymy table (prefix/suffix with meaning + example) and the architecture-by-climate breakdown are genuinely useful reference content. Teaches through pattern-matching. **Steal the format.** In a romantasy workbook: "courting customs in real cultures → what you could borrow," "mourning rituals → romantic impact."
2. **Permission-giving voice throughout.** The "delete these pages if you want" attitude removes the guilt of an incomplete workbook. Readers finish happier. **Adapt the register** — it pairs beautifully with romantasy's slightly breathless voice.
3. **Teaching pages that precede prompts.** Every section has a mini-essay before fillables. Readers learn *why* they're answering the question, not just *what*. **This is the single biggest differentiator vs Workbook 2.** Steal the teach-then-prompt cadence.

### 8. Specific weaknesses (avoid/improve)
1. **No romance integration anywhere.** This is the obvious one. Religion doesn't ask about sexual ethics. Culture doesn't ask about courting in any depth. Fashion doesn't ask about marriage-signaling clothing in detail. Every single section could have romance-aware prompts bolted on and they're missing.
2. **Prompts are flat, not scaffolded.** No sense of "start with the shallow question, then push deeper." A writer could answer everything surface-level and feel done.
3. **Missing whole topic areas.** Warfare, economics beyond currency, technology, sexuality/gender systems, afterlife. For romantasy specifically: no coverage of courtship rituals, marriage customs at depth, bastardy/legitimacy politics, arranged marriage conventions, queer acceptance — all of which are load-bearing for romantasy plots.

### 9. Target reader
Beginner to intermediate *fantasy* writers who have an idea and want to systematize their world. Serves beginners well via teaching pages. Intermediates will find it too surface — they already know soft vs hard magic, they already picked a government type. Almost serves no one writing romantasy specifically, but many romantasy writers buy it because it's the most comprehensive fantasy worldbuilding workbook on Etsy.

### 10. Price-to-value read
At ~$12–15 for ~50–60 pages, this is reasonable value for fantasy generalists. The teaching quality is above average for the price band. **What a reader would want more of:** romance-aware prompts (obviously), deeper economic and trade systems, warfare and military, sexuality/gender systems, more prompt scaffolding, more than one prompt about romantic/sexual culture. For a romantasy writer specifically, the price is on the high side for what they actually get.

## Workbook 2 — Planner Palette Co, Ultimate Romantasy Writing Workbook

### 1. Identification
- **Seller:** Planner Palette Co (plannerpaletteco.etsy.com). Self-described as a shop that sells writing templates.
- **Title:** "Ultimate Romantasy Writing Workbook"
- **Format:** .docx (fillable in Word/Google Docs) plus likely a PDF counterpart.
- **Approximate page count:** ~50 pages as rendered (per the internal TOC: sections end at page 50).
- **Price:** Not in the file; Planner Palette Co's catalog typically prices full workbooks in the $8–15 range on Etsy. Likely $10–14.
- **Genre targeting:** Romantasy, explicitly. Genre definition on page 1.

### 2. Structural analysis
Ten sequential numbered sections covering the full novel process, not just worldbuilding:

- S1: Worldbuilding (realm mapping, geography, cultural landscape, economic systems, historical context)
- S2: Magic & Supernatural Elements (types, cost, races, artifacts, magical places)
- S3: Character Development (MC, love interest, supporting, antagonist)
- S4: Side Characters & Relationships
- S5: Political & Social Structures (political, social, class)
- S6: Main Plot Development (conflict, plot structure, subplots)
- S7: Romance Arc Integration (timeline, intersection, tension, intimacy, resolution)
- S8: Battle & Action Scenes
- S9: Settings & Atmosphere
- S10: Revision Guide (balance, consistency, polish)

Explicitly modular: "Complete the exercises in any order." But every section cross-references others, and the author tells readers to do geography after magic, which confuses the order. Within each section, content is table-first, prompt-second.

### 3. Content coverage
**Covers:** geography, cultural regions, economic systems (trade routes, resource distribution), historical timeline, magic types and cost, species/races, magical artifacts, sacred sites, full character profiles (protagonist, love interest, antagonist, supporting), political structures, court politics, class system, cultural dynamics, primary conflict, plot timeline (opening → climax), political subplots, full romance arc (meeting → resolution), romantic tension triggers, physical/emotional intimacy + spice meter, large-scale battles, protagonist combat, setting profiles, sensory details, magical properties of locations, final revision balance check.

**Depth:** Broad but shallow. Each topic gets 1–3 pages. Magic gets 5 pages total across multiple subtopics. Romance arc integration gets ~6 pages, the most of any topic. Worldbuilding gets ~5 pages total.

**Does NOT cover but should:** religion (totally missing — no gods, no faith, no religious politics), language, fashion, food, architecture specifics, naming conventions, timekeeping, everyday culture (music, art, entertainment), courting customs and marriage rituals at depth, queer/gender systems, prophecy and fate, warfare strategy beyond the battle scene itself, technology level.

**Romantasy content:** Explicit and present. Section 7 is the romance arc. Spice meter. Intimacy development table (emotional/physical/context). Intimate scene planning template. Romance-plot intersection chart. Romantic tension checkboxes. **However:** romance is siloed in S7 and S3. Worldbuilding (S1, S2, S5) has no romance integration. The core romantasy promise — that worldbuilding and romance are welded — is broken by the structure itself.

### 4. Prompt quality
- **Generative or closed:** Heavily closed. Maybe 70% tables, 20% short-answer, 10% generative. Tables like "Region / Climate / Resources / Key Features" drive the fill-in-the-blank feel. Very little "why does this matter, let's think about it" prompting.
- **Enough per section:** Some sections have too few real prompts (Worldbuilding has maybe 4 generative questions across the whole section, the rest is tables). Others (character development) have a reasonable amount.
- **Too many:** No, the opposite problem. The workbook is under-prompted — it's over-tabled.
- **Difficulty escalation:** None. Tables are flat. A question about "what is the central conflict" appears with the same weight as "where are your resource deposits."

### 5. Voice and tone
- **Clinical, not warm.** The intro is the only warm moment. After that, the voice disappears into template-speak: "Use the chart to mark down places," "Fill in the pyramid." No jokes, no asides, no personality.
- **Assumes craft literacy.** Uses "inciting incident," "plot point 1," "climax," "midpoint," "falling action" without defining them. A new writer would need to Google multiple terms.
- **Neither preachy nor permission-giving.** Just neutral/instructional.
- **Not memorable.** The only quotable line is the spice meter checkbox. Nothing sticks.

### 6. Visual / layout
Heavy table use throughout. Most pages are one or two tables plus a short prompt. Word document formatting — so likely boxy, utilitarian, not illustrated. Section headers in bold, subsections in slightly smaller bold. Character diamonds (visual shape asked for in the template). Plot mountain (asked for as a sketch). Web diagrams (asked for as sketches). The user does a lot of drawing-by-hand because the template can't render those visuals itself. Monotonous page design — table, table, prompt, table, prompt. No rhythm.

### 7. Specific strengths (steal/adapt)
1. **Romance arc integration section (S7).** The timeline approach (First Meeting → Growing Trust → Major Bond → Crisis Point → Resolution) is solid. The plot-event-vs-relationship-impact chart is useful. The intimacy development chart (emotional/physical/context by stage) is good scaffolding. **Steal the timeline + the intersection chart.**
2. **Spice meter.** One checkbox that gives the writer permission to declare their target heat level. Simple and good. Romantasy-specific. **Steal this and make it prettier.**
3. **Structured revision guide.** The Section 10 consistency checklist (magic rules, political structures, historical references, character motivations, relationship development, cultural elements) is legitimately useful. **Steal the format** — adapt it as a romantasy-specific consistency check at the end of your workbook.

### 8. Specific weaknesses (avoid/improve)
1. **Clinical, voiceless writing.** After the intro, the workbook reads like a tax form. No teaching moments. No encouragement. No jokes. A beginner opening this would feel lost; an intermediate would feel bored. **Your workbook must have voice throughout.**
2. **Romance is siloed, not integrated.** The single biggest failure. The worldbuilding sections don't know romance exists. The magic system section doesn't ask "how does your magic affect intimacy?" The politics section doesn't ask "how do arranged marriages work in your court?" Romance is quarantined in S7. **Your workbook's core differentiation is to weld them.**
3. **Over-tabled, under-prompted.** Tables are efficient for reference but terrible for generation. Writers need prompts that make them *think*, not blank cells that make them list. **Your prompt-to-table ratio should be inverted from theirs.**

### 9. Target reader
Intermediate romantasy writers who already know their story and want a template to organize it. The workbook is a scaffolding tool, not a teaching tool. Beginners will drown in unexplained terminology and empty tables. Advanced writers will find the prompts too shallow to unlock anything new.

### 10. Price-to-value read
At ~$10–14 for ~50 pages, the value is borderline. The romance arc section is worth the sticker alone if you're a template-minded writer. But the worldbuilding and magic sections are thin for romantasy — someone buying this for their worldbuilding needs will feel short-changed. Readers would want more: romance-worldbuilding integration, actual teaching content, more generative prompts, religion and culture depth, deeper romance trope coverage (the workbook names "enemies to lovers" and similar without actually helping the writer execute them).

## Workbook 3 — "Your Digital Brain" (Gabrielle), How to Outline a Romantasy

### 1. Identification
- **Seller:** Branded as "Your Digital Brain" per the cover. Author credited as "Gabrielle" in the PDF metadata.
- **Title:** "How to Outline a Romantasy — A Creative Writing Workbook for Fantasy Romance Authors"
- **Format:** PDF, Canva-created (per metadata), designed as fillable digital notebook.
- **Approximate page count:** 140 pages.
- **Price:** Not specified. Canva-authored 140-page workbooks from similar sellers on Etsy price between $18 and $35. Likely $20–28.
- **Genre targeting:** Romantasy, explicitly. Outlining/plotting focused. Opens with genre definition.

### 2. Structural analysis
Concept-by-concept, not element-by-element. Fourteen teaching-heavy chapters:

1. Genre DNA (what makes romantasy special, subgenres)
2. World First or Heart First? (approach to story)
3. The "Holy Crap" Premise (premise formula, what-if game, stakes)
4. Your Power Couple (dual character arcs)
5. Magic Meets Heart Eyes (magic as romance tool)
6. Tension That Could Cut Glass (tension, obstacles, push-pull)
7. Three Acts, Two Hearts, One Epic Story (three-act structure for romantasy)
8. Hero's Journey + Romance Beats (fusion method)
9. Save the Cat Method (romantasy edition)
10. Snowflake Method for Complex Plots
11. Scene-by-Scene Plotting
12. Conflict Layering
13. The Art of the Slow Burn
14. When Everything Explodes (the climax)
15. Planning Your Series
16. Resources & Recommended Reading

Sequential, but each chapter is readable standalone. **Not a worldbuilding workbook — a plotting workbook with some character work.** This is critical: this is not your direct competitor, this is a complementary product to a worldbuilding workbook.

### 3. Content coverage
**Covers:** genre definition and subgenres (fae, dragon riders, vampires, witch/academy, gods, dark fantasy, portal, urban), world-first vs character-first approaches, premise formula (Character + Situation + Stakes + Romantic Tension), the "what-if" game, double stakes (personal/world/romantic), dual character arcs, arc timing checkpoints (25%/50%/75%/100%), magic as romance tool (mate bonds, blood bonds, dragon bonds, telepathic), eight types of romantic magic systems, tension types (forbidden love, secrets, forced proximity, will-they-won't-they), obstacle brainstorming, push-pull rhythm, three-act structure applied to romantasy, Hero's Journey fused with romance beats, Save the Cat 15 beats adapted for romantasy, Snowflake Method 10 steps, scene-level plotting, conflict layering across internal/interpersonal/external, slow burn progression (awareness → connection → tension → breaking point → union), almost-kiss database, climax convergence, climax types (sacrifice, unity power, choice, trust fall, transformation), series planning (types, book 1 requirements, HEA vs HFN, continuity).

**Covers well:** plotting frameworks (multiple of them), dual character arc integration, tension/obstacle variety, magic-as-romance-tool, slow burn anatomy, climax convergence, series planning. This is the strongest *plotting* workbook of the three by a wide margin.

**Does NOT cover but should (for the scope it claims):** worldbuilding beyond a few stray prompts. Religion: absent. Politics: barely present (only as a conflict category). Magic system: rules and romance-integration are great, but no actual worldbuilding (where does magic come from in your world? what are the cultural attitudes toward it?). Geography: absent. Culture: absent. Language: absent. Fashion: absent. Economics: absent. History: absent. Class: absent. Naming: absent.

**Romantasy content:** This is the most romantasy-native of the three. Uses "mate bonds" as an example. Names ACOTAR, Fourth Wing, From Blood and Ash, Folk of the Air throughout. Subgenres are romantasy-specific. Every beat sheet is adapted for romantasy. **Romance is not siloed — it's the spine.** The workbook never forgets it's a romantasy workbook.

### 4. Prompt quality
- **Generative or closed:** Majority generative. "What do they want? What do they need (that they don't realize)? Greatest fear? Biggest secret? How they'll change by the end?" — open-ended, invite real writing. Some checkbox/closed questions (subgenre selection, obstacle categories) but they're scaffolding, not the main event.
- **Enough per section:** Yes. Each chapter has a handful of deep generative prompts rather than a flood of shallow ones. Chapter on tension has maybe 15 prompts across 10 pages. Feels well-paced.
- **Too many:** Chapter 9 (Save the Cat) and Chapter 10 (Snowflake) are dense — if a writer does both fully, they've outlined the same book twice. Some redundancy.
- **Difficulty escalation:** Some. Chapters build on each other (premise → characters → magic → tension → structure). Within chapters, most prompts are at a similar depth, but the overall document escalates from broad (genre) to narrow (scene-by-scene).

### 5. Voice and tone
- **Very warm, very alive.** The most voice-forward of the three by far. Opens: "Welcome to your new favorite workbook! If you're here, chances are you've been daydreaming about brooding fae princes, dragon riders with serious attitude problems, or maybe a witch who falls for the very demon she's supposed to banish. Sound familiar? Yeah, we get it."
- **Emoji-heavy.** 🔍 📌 ✨ 🖊️ 🔄 as section markers throughout. Some readers will love this. Some will hate it.
- **Beginner-friendly but assumes the reader loves romantasy.** Defines terms ("inciting incident," "midpoint"), names comp titles that a reader would recognize. If the reader hasn't read ACOTAR, they'll miss a lot.
- **Permission-giving with jokes.** "This isn't a textbook you need to memorize — it's your creative playground! Make it messy."
- **Very memorable.** "Attraction + Obstacles + Stakes + Timing = Chef's Kiss." "Three Acts, Two Hearts, One Epic Story." "When Everything Explodes (The Climax)." These chapter titles are doing real work.

### 6. Visual / layout
Canva-authored, so likely illustrated and well-designed. 140 pages is a lot — suggests lots of white space, multiple fillable pages per concept, visual variety. The text extract suggests repeated "Write your answer here..." placeholders — meaning every prompt has its own dedicated fillable box/page. Modular per-page design. Emoji icons for section types (🔍 for deep-dive questions, 🔄 for exercises, 🖊️ for writing, 📌 for key points, ✨ for highlights). This visual vocabulary is internally consistent and easy to navigate.

### 7. Specific strengths (steal/adapt)
1. **Named comp titles throughout.** Every concept gets an ACOTAR or Fourth Wing example. This does two things: it teaches through recognition, and it signals to the reader that the author *knows* the genre. **Steal this hard.** Every major worldbuilding concept in your workbook should have a romantasy comp title anchoring it: "magic-system cost → the price of using shadow magic in *Fourth Wing*"; "religious institutions as antagonists → the Ascended in *From Blood and Ash*."
2. **Warm, chatty, voice-forward prose that doesn't patronize.** Makes a 140-page workbook feel friendly rather than homework-y. **Steal the register.** You can skip the emojis if they don't fit your brand.
3. **Teaching-first chapter structure.** Every chapter opens with concept teaching, then moves to prompts. Like School of Plot but with more teaching per concept. **Steal this cadence** — it's the single best pattern across all three workbooks.

### 8. Specific weaknesses (avoid/improve)
1. **No actual worldbuilding.** This is a plotting workbook wearing a romantasy label. A writer who buys this for worldbuilding gets almost nothing. Your workbook occupies the gap this book doesn't fill.
2. **140 pages is too much for a plot workbook.** Overlapping methods (three-act + Hero's Journey + Save the Cat + Snowflake + scene-by-scene) produce redundancy. A reader who completes all of them has outlined the same book four times. **Avoid this** by committing to one structural approach or clearly positioning each as optional.
3. **Emoji density and informal voice will alienate some readers.** Writers who want a more serious craft book will bounce. **Adapt the warmth without the emoji reliance** — warmth doesn't need emoji to land.

### 9. Target reader
Beginner to intermediate romantasy writers who know their trope and need help plotting. Serves beginners excellently via teaching content and named examples. Intermediates get the most value from Save the Cat and Snowflake chapters. Advanced writers will skim. **Not for readers wanting worldbuilding help.**

### 10. Price-to-value read
At ~$20–28 for 140 pages of mostly warm, well-taught, romantasy-specific plotting content, this is strong value. The breadth of plotting methods alone justifies the price. **What a reader would want more of:** worldbuilding (they wouldn't know to miss it until they finish), cutting some of the method redundancy, maybe a companion worldbuilding workbook. **This workbook, in fact, is the best argument for your product existing** — it shows romantasy writers will pay a premium for a workbook that's warm, illustrated, romantasy-native, and substantive.

# PART 2: SYNTHESIS

## A. Market Gap Analysis

**What no competitor currently does well:**

1. **Romance-worldbuilding integration.** Workbook 1 (School of Plot) does worldbuilding with no romance. Workbook 2 (Planner Palette) silos romance into one section. Workbook 3 (YDB) does romance plotting with no worldbuilding. **No one currently fuses them at the prompt level.** No competitor asks "how does your world's religion shape your heroine's views on sex?" or "how does the magic system affect who your love interest can safely touch?" This is the single clearest market gap.

2. **Teaching-first prompting at depth across all worldbuilding elements.** School of Plot teaches but thin; YDB teaches but not worldbuilding; Planner Palette doesn't teach. **No one delivers concept-teaching + romantasy-specific prompts for every worldbuilding pillar.**

3. **Romantasy-aware coverage of underserved worldbuilding domains.** None of the three workbooks cover religion's view of sex/gender/queerness, courtship and marriage customs beyond surface level, clothing as romantic signaling at depth, food and hospitality as romantic language, death and mourning rituals, or the politics of bastardy/legitimacy — all of which are load-bearing for romantasy plots.

4. **Illustrated comp-title examples across every section.** YDB does this in plotting but not worldbuilding. No one has "religion in ACOTAR's Night Court" as a visual case study with a sketch and a breakdown. This is empty territory.

5. **Scaffolded prompt difficulty.** All three workbooks have flat prompt depth. No one says "answer these three shallow questions first, then push deeper with these two." **Nobody's doing escalation.**

**Where you have clearest differentiation opportunity:** Romance-worldbuilding integration. Every prompt in every section can have a romantasy lens baked in. That's your unique value prop.

**What's over-saturated that you can skip:**
- Generic "types of government" explainers (School of Plot does it well enough; everyone copies Wikipedia anyway).
- Generic "soft vs hard magic" explainers (ditto).
- Broad Save the Cat / Hero's Journey plotting (YDB owns this; it's not your job).
- The spice meter as a standalone feature (Planner Palette has it; you can have it but it's not a differentiator).
- Another "here's what romantasy is" definition page (all three have one; yours can be shorter and sharper).

## B. Positioning Recommendation

**One-sentence positioning:**

> *The romantasy worldbuilding workbook where every prompt is already thinking about the romance — because your readers are.*

Alternative phrasings to test:
- *A worldbuilding workbook that remembers it's for romantasy writers.*
- *Build the world your love story needs.*
- *The only worldbuilding workbook where the romance is welded in, not bolted on.*

**How you beat School of Plot:**
- **Romantasy integration is non-negotiable in your book.** Every section asks a romance-adjacent question. Religion → "how do clergy view romantic love vs duty"; Class → "which marriages would scandalize which class"; Magic → "what does the magic cost a couple when they use it together."
- **Tighter scaffolding.** Prompts escalate from shallow to deep within each section. Readers can stop at whatever depth they want.
- **Named romantasy comp titles** (School of Plot names zero — not one single comp title in the whole workbook).
- **More complete coverage.** Religion, courtship, marriage politics, gender systems, bastardy, queer acceptance, afterlife beliefs — all of which they skip or underserve.

**How you beat Planner Palette Co:**
- **Voice and teaching content.** Theirs is clinical; yours is warm and educational. A beginner buying yours learns craft; a beginner buying theirs fills out a form.
- **Worldbuilding depth.** They give ~5 pages to worldbuilding in total. You give 50+.
- **Romance-integrated worldbuilding.** They silo romance in one section. You bake it into every section.
- **Better prompt-to-table ratio.** Their workbook is ~70% tables; yours should be ~30% tables, 70% generative prompts.

**How you beat YDB / "How to Outline a Romantasy":**
- **Different product.** Not direct competition — complementary. Position as "the worldbuilding workbook YDB doesn't have."
- **More rigorous.** Their voice is charming but chatty; yours can be warm *and* more substantive.
- **Actual worldbuilding.** They skip this entirely.
- **Cleaner visual register.** You can skip the emoji approach and still land warmth.

**How you beat Patricia Wrede's free worldbuilding questions:**
- **Curated, not a firehose.** Wrede has ~360 questions; readers drown. You scaffold and stage.
- **Illustrated with comp titles** — Wrede's list is pure text, no examples.
- **Romance-aware** — Wrede is general fantasy, written in the 1990s, zero romance lens.
- **Taught, not just listed** — each section teaches first.
- **Visual design** — Wrede's list is a text document. Yours is a designed artifact.

## C. Scope Recommendation

Based on the competitor landscape:

| Workbook | Pages | Price est. | $/page |
|----|----|----|----|
| School of Plot | ~55 | $12–15 | ~$0.25 |
| Planner Palette | ~50 | $10–14 | ~$0.24 |
| YDB Romantasy Outline | ~140 | $20–28 | ~$0.17 |

**Your target zone:** 100–130 pages at $22–32.

**Reasoning:**
- Above School of Plot's scope because you're offering more (romantasy lens, more topics, teaching-first).
- Comparable to YDB's page count because buyers have now been primed by YDB that romantasy workbooks can be 100+ pages.
- Higher price point than School of Plot and Planner Palette because of scope and specialization.
- Slightly higher $/page than YDB if you deliver on illustration and design quality.

**Page count target: 110 pages is the sweet spot.** Enough to justify a $24–28 price. Not so much that completion-anxiety scares buyers.

**Price target: $24–28 launch.** You can test higher ($32) if the illustration quality is strong. Don't launch below $20 — you'll train your audience that comprehensive romantasy workbooks are cheap.

**Where to cut if 72 sections + appendices is too ambitious:**

Your 72-section TOC almost certainly won't fit 110 pages if each section gets a teaching page + prompts. Rough math: 72 × 1.5 pages average = 108 pages with zero intro, zero appendices, zero rhythm. Tight.

**Cut priorities (in order):**

1. **Merge adjacent topics.** 72 → ~50 sections by combining: language subsections (naming + basic words + register → one section); fashion subsections (rich + poor + descriptive → one); timekeeping (probably one page not three). You can likely fold 20+ sections into larger combined sections without losing coverage.

2. **Cut or defer topics that aren't romantasy-load-bearing.** Detailed currency design, detailed map-making mechanics, detailed travel-time math — all of this is more "generic fantasy" than "romantasy-essential." If you need to lose 10 sections, lose these. They're available free elsewhere.

3. **Move some sections to an appendix.** Things like "100 romantasy subgenre examples," "quick reference: types of magic," or "courtship customs by real-world culture" can live as reference appendices at 0.5 pages each rather than full sections.

4. **Defer to a Volume 2.** If you're still over, announce you'll cover [cut topics] in a sequel workbook. Turns scope constraint into upsell strategy.

**If forced to choose 40 sections for a tight launch:**

Keep: geography + climate, cultures + peoples, class and hierarchy, religion (multiple sections — this is where romantasy lives), magic system + cost + limits, politics + government, court life, warfare at a high level, economy at a high level, language essentials, fashion as romantic signaling, food and hospitality, courtship customs, marriage customs, gender systems, queer acceptance, death and mourning, prophecy and fate, history as it bears on present romance, technology level, daily life. Plus character integration (protagonist-world, love interest-world), romance-worldbuilding intersection sections, and a revision/consistency appendix.

Cut for launch: extensive language invention, currency design specifics, detailed travel calculators, architectural detail pages, multiple subgenre-specific sections.

## D. Must-Have Features (present in every strong competitor)

The baseline — every competitor that works has these, so yours must too:

1. **Warm, permission-giving introduction.** Every strong workbook opens by lowering the stakes ("complete in any order," "skip what doesn't serve you," "first drafts are messy"). School of Plot and YDB both nail this. Do it.

2. **Teaching page before prompts in each section.** School of Plot and YDB both do this. Planner Palette skips it and suffers for it. Non-negotiable.

3. **Named comp titles as examples.** YDB does this throughout; Planner Palette does it glancingly; School of Plot doesn't at all. ACOTAR, Fourth Wing, From Blood and Ash, Folk of the Air, Shadow and Bone, Throne of Glass, From Blood and Ash — use them liberally.

4. **A romance arc timeline and plot-intersection framework.** Planner Palette's S7 and YDB's climax convergence both deliver this. Your version can sit as an integration spread connecting worldbuilding to the romance.

5. **A spice meter or intimacy level declaration.** Planner Palette has it; YDB has an intimacy scale (1–10). Low-effort for you; readers expect it.

6. **A revision/consistency check at the end.** Planner Palette's S10 is their strongest section. YDB has checkpoints throughout. Include a final consistency pass — magic rules, political structures, cultural elements, romance beats.

7. **Modular page design.** All three workbooks make each page self-contained. Readers can open to any page and fill it out. Preserve this.

## E. Signature Features (uniquely yours)

These are the 3–5 things no competitor has that should define your workbook:

1. **"Romance Lens" prompts inside every worldbuilding section.** Each section has a standard worldbuilding spread PLUS a dedicated "what does this mean for the romance" spread. Religion → romance lens asks how clergy view pre-marital sex, how marriage sacraments work, whether your couple can marry across faiths. Class → romance lens asks about acceptable cross-class romance, marriage as class mobility, how gifts signal courtship. This is the feature that positions your workbook. **This is the differentiator.**

2. **Illustrated comp-title case studies.** Every major section has a one-page visual case study of how a published romantasy book handled this element — a mini-diagram or annotated breakdown. "Religion in the Night Court." "The cost of bonding a dragon in Basgiath." "Class mobility through marriage in Atherton." Visual, not just prose. No competitor does this well.

3. **Prompt difficulty scaffolding.** Each section explicitly labels prompts by depth: "If you want the basics" (3–4 surface prompts) → "If you want to go deeper" (3–4 mid-level) → "If you want to really dig in" (2–3 advanced). Readers self-select their depth. Nobody else does this.

4. **The welding worksheets.** Dedicated cross-section spreads at key transition points: after worldbuilding-core, a page called "Welding worksheet: where does your magic system make your romance dangerous?" After religion: "Welding worksheet: what religious taboo does your couple break?" These are where your positioning becomes tangible.

5. **A "wearable worldbuilding" approach to fashion, food, and small daily culture.** Treat these not as lookup sections but as romantic signaling systems. What does it mean when the love interest gives your protagonist a cloak? A piece of bread? A piece of jewelry? This is a category underserved by every competitor and it's where romantasy actually lives on the page.
