# 72. Final Self-Edit Pass For Worldbuilding Consistency

You've drafted. You've revised. You've had beta readers. You're close.

Before you submit, query, or publish, run the worldbuilding consistency pass. Not a line edit; a world edit. Your job's to catch the places your world contradicts itself.

## Why Consistency Matters

Readers forgive a lot. Pacing bumps, a thin side character, a trope they don't personally love. They don't forgive inconsistency. A world that says one thing in chapter three and another in chapter twenty-one breaks the reader's trust.

Your magic rule, your species biology, your political structure, your naming convention: each needs to hold across the book. The pass is where you check.

## The Five Axes Of Consistency

- **Magic.** What magic does, what it costs, who can do it.
- **Species.** What fae, dragons, vampires, shifters, or whichever are physically capable of.
- **Politics.** Who rules, who's allied, who's rivalled, who's dead.
- **Language.** What words your culture uses, what honorifics, what names.
- **Geography and time.** How long it takes to cross a place, when festivals happen, what season the book's in.

A pass per axis is the cleanest method. One read-through per axis, tracking one thing.

## The Magic Pass

Read the whole manuscript looking only for magic scenes. Every instance.

- Does the magic do the same thing each time?
- Does the cost apply consistently?
- Are the limits honoured?
- Does a character suddenly do something the magic said she couldn't?

Keep a running list of "what magic can and can't do in this book." Check every magic scene against it. Where you find a contradiction, fix it. The fix's almost always either adding a cost, removing the scene's magic use, or explicitly writing the breaking of the rule as a plot event.

## The Species Pass

Read for species and creature appearances.

- Does your fae have the same physical traits in every scene? Pointed ears, inhuman eyes, specific beauty?
- Does your shifter's shift follow the same rule every time?
- Does your vampire's sun tolerance match across chapters?
- Do your dragons have consistent size, speech, breath-weapon, death-effect?

Species drift's common in first drafts. A dragon that's described as "enormous" in chapter two and "compact and agile" in chapter sixteen reads as two different dragons. Fix the drift.

## The Politics Pass

Read for court, faction, and political references.

- Is the ruler the same person at the same time?
- Are the alliances stable unless your plot explicitly shifts them?
- Do named characters stay alive until they die on the page?
- Does the succession rule apply consistently?

Politics drift's the second-most-common problem. A character mentioned in chapter three as "the regent's son" shouldn't be "the regent's nephew" in chapter eighteen unless the discrepancy's a plot point. Track named relationships.

## The Language Pass

Read for honorifics, titles, and specific words.

- Is your heroine called "the Maiden" consistently when she's being called that?
- Do the court's titles stay the same (High Lord, not alternating with High Lord of, or the Lord)?
- Are in-world names spelled the same way each time?
- Are greetings, oaths, and formal phrasings consistent?

Language drift's easy to catch with find-and-replace. Search for your key words; make sure the usage's consistent.

## The Geography And Time Pass

Read for setting and temporal references.

- Does it take three days to cross the mountain in chapter six and one day in chapter fifteen? Why?
- Is the festival in midwinter in chapter eight and autumn in chapter twenty-two?
- Does the heroine arrive at the capital at dusk, wake in the morning, and ride out... at dusk again?
- Are the seasons moving forward?

Time drift's the easiest to miss. A character who's been at the academy for "the past few weeks" in chapter eleven and "the past several months" in chapter fourteen has lived two different timelines. Check.

## The Named Characters Pass

A separate pass. Make a list of every named character.

- Does each one's name stay consistent?
- Does each one's relationship to the leads stay consistent?
- Is each one's physical description consistent (hair colour, eye colour, height)?
- Does each one's voice stay consistent?

A named character's continuity's part of the world's consistency. If you've got three side characters who blur together, your reader's not going to track them. Make each distinct.

## The Subplot Pass

Another read. Track each subplot.

- Does each one arc? Begin, middle, end?
- Does each one resolve or tee up the sequel?
- Does any subplot disappear mid-book without resolution?
- Does any subplot pay off in a way the reader's prepared for?

A subplot that starts and doesn't end is a loose thread. Tie it, cut it, or explicitly plant it for the next book.

## The Promise Pass

The hardest pass. Read for what your book promises the reader and whether it pays.

- Did you promise a trope? Does it land?
- Did you promise a scene (the ball, the trial, the climax battle)? Does it appear?
- Did you promise a character reveal? Does it come?
- Did you promise a bond, a mark, a claiming? Does it happen?

A book that promises an open-door scene and fades to black, or promises a mate bond and doesn't deliver, breaks its contract. Your reader's patient with craft but not with false promises.

## The Voice Pass

Your prose, consistent.

- Is the narrative voice the same in chapter one and chapter thirty?
- Do your heroine's speech patterns stay the same?
- Does your love interest's speech stay in register?
- Are there passages where your voice slips (usually chapters written on different days or moods)?

Voice drift's common in longer drafts. A read-aloud pass often catches it.

## The Cut Pass

After all the consistency passes, one more read.

- Is there a chapter that could be cut with nothing lost?
- Is there a scene that could be cut with nothing lost?
- Is there a character that could be cut or merged with another?
- Is there a subplot that could be cut with the book gaining, not losing?

The cut pass is painful. It's also the pass that usually makes the book better. A tighter book's a book with more impact. Be willing to lose scenes you love.

## The Final Read

One straight read, no notes. Just your book, one sitting. You're the reader now.

- Where did you stop believing?
- Where were you bored?
- Where did you cry or laugh?
- Where did you want more?
- Where did you want less?

Your own read's honest in a way no outside read is. You know what you meant; you can feel where it missed. Take notes afterward; don't edit mid-read.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Run the magic pass. List every magic scene; check against your rules. Note inconsistencies.

**[L1]** Run the species pass. Note any drifts.

**[L2]** Run the politics, language, and geography-and-time passes. One pass at a time, day apart, not all at once.

**[L2]** Run the subplot pass. Are all your threads tied or deliberately left for book two?

**[L3]** Run the promise pass. What did your book promise? Did it deliver? If not, what needs to change?

**[L3]** Run the cut pass. Identify one chapter, scene, or character you can remove. Cut it. Read the book without it. Is it better?

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Consistency's the invisible craft. Your reader doesn't notice when the world holds; she notices when it breaks. The passes above are your last chance to catch the break before she does. Do them slowly. Do them separately. The book you send out's the book she closes at the end. Let it hold all the way through.
