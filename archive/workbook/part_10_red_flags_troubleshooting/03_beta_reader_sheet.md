# 70. The Beta Reader Question Sheet

You've drafted. You're handing it to someone. She's going to read it and tell you what she thinks.

Except if you don't tell her what to look for, she'll tell you she liked it, or she didn't. That's not useful feedback.

This section's a sheet you give your beta reader before she starts, and another one she fills in after. Copy these. Use them. Your revision will be sharper.

## Who Your Beta Reader Should Be

- **Reads the subgenre.** She needs to know what the conventions are. A reader who's never read romantasy can't tell you if your book's hitting the genre.
- **Reads critically.** She notices things. She'll tell you when she's bored, when she's confused, when she's disbelieving.
- **Is honest.** Your friend who loves everything you write isn't your beta reader. She's your cheerleader, which is also useful, but different.
- **Has time.** Two or three hours to read, another hour to respond. Don't ambush a busy person.
- **Isn't your partner, usually.** The relationship interferes with the feedback.

Two or three beta readers is ideal. One's vulnerable to quirks. Five's administration you don't need.

## The Pre-Read Briefing

Send your reader this with the manuscript.

**Brief.**

- Title and one-line pitch.
- Subgenre and three comps.
- Heat level (closed door, fade to black, open door, explicit).
- Content warnings (be honest; your reader deserves to know).
- What you're stuck on (one line). "The middle drags." "The villain feels flat." "I can't tell if the romance lands."
- What you don't need feedback on (one line). "Typos I'll fix in proofreading." "The magic system's still in flux; don't worry about rules for now."
- Your timeline. "I'd love feedback within three weeks."

A beta reader with clear instructions gives better feedback. Brief her.

## The Post-Read Questions

Give her these after she's finished the manuscript. She answers in writing, or you schedule a conversation.

### On The Heroine

- What did you think of her? Three adjectives.
- Where did you connect with her? Name a scene or line.
- Where did she lose you, if at all? Name a scene or line.
- What did she want? What did she need? Could you name both?

### On The Love Interest

- Three adjectives.
- What's his Wound, as you read him?
- When did you start believing in the pairing?
- Was there a scene where he lost you? When?

### On The Romance

- Where did you feel the first charge between them?
- Where did you want more?
- Where did it feel rushed?
- Did the HEA land for you? If not, what was missing?

### On The World

- Name three worldbuilding details you remember clearly.
- Name something the world seemed to promise that didn't pay off.
- Was the magic system consistent?
- Were there rules you wanted explained that weren't?

### On The Plot

- What was the central obstacle, in one line?
- Where did the book drag?
- Where did the book surprise you?
- What was the midpoint turn, as you read it?
- Did the climax pay off the setup?

### On The Tone

- Three adjectives for the book's feel.
- Did the tone stay consistent?
- Were there scenes that felt like they belonged in a different book?

### On The Genre

- Did this book feel like the subgenre it's pitched as?
- What comps would you have put on it?
- Who would you recommend it to, if anyone?
- What conventions did it meet? Which did it subvert? Which did it miss?

### On The Tropes

- Which tropes did you spot?
- Which landed? Which felt tired?
- What would you have wanted more of?

### On The Specific Questions You Asked

- Go back to what you said you were stuck on. Does she have a take? Name the specific concern.

### The Last Three Questions

- What's the one change you'd make if it were your book?
- What's the one thing you'd protect?
- Would you read the sequel?

## The Conversation

After the questions, if you can, talk to her. Twenty or thirty minutes.

- Don't argue. Listen.
- Don't explain. If she's confused, the confusion's the data.
- Ask follow-ups. "You said the middle dragged; where specifically?"
- Write down every specific she names. Generalities are less useful; specifics are actionable.
- End by thanking her.

## What To Do With The Feedback

- **Pattern.** If one reader says something, it's data. If two readers say the same thing, it's pattern. If three do, it's truth about your book.
- **Ignore the ones you know are wrong.** Not every reader's right about every book. If one reader's reaction contradicts your clear vision and no one else shares it, set it aside.
- **Listen to the ones you know are right.** If you know in your gut the middle drags and every reader confirms, the middle drags. Fix it.
- **Don't revise from a single reading.** Give yourself a week. Your gut will sort signal from noise.

## The Reader Who Hurts You

Sometimes feedback stings. It doesn't mean the feedback's wrong. It also doesn't mean your book's broken.

- Read it once. Close it.
- Come back after a day.
- Read it again. Notice what still stings and what's useful.
- The sting's usually where the book's weakest. Use it.

## The Reader Who's Wrong

Sometimes feedback's just wrong. The reader wanted a different book than you wrote. You can tell because the feedback would make your book into something else.

- Thank her anyway.
- Set the feedback aside.
- Keep writing your book.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Draft your pre-read briefing. Title, pitch, subgenre, comps, heat, content warnings, stuck-on-one-line, don't-worry-about-one-line, timeline.

**[L1]** Copy the post-read questions into a document. Ready to send.

**[L2]** Pick your beta readers. Two or three. Write them a personal email (not a group blast) explaining what you're asking for.

**[L3]** After the feedback comes in, draft your revision plan. Three to five specific changes. Each tied to a reader's specific comment.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your beta reader's the test your book gets before the world sees it. She's the first voice back. Listen carefully; weigh honestly; revise deliberately. The questions above give you the shape of the feedback you actually need. Ask them. Take her seriously. Buy her coffee.
