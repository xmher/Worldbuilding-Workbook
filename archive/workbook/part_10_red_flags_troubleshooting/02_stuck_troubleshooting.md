# 70. Stuck? Troubleshooting

Every writer gets stuck. The prompt doesn't unlock. The scene won't come. The world feels flat.

This section is fifteen unsticking prompts. Pick the one closest to your block. Do it. Move.

## Stuck On The Heroine

**1. Write her morning.**

Not the book's morning. An ordinary morning, a year before the book begins. She wakes, she does something specific, she eats, she goes somewhere. One page. No plot pressure. You'll find her voice in the small choices.

**2. Write a scene she'd never tell anyone about.**

Something she did that she wouldn't share with her love interest, her sister, her closest friend. Not necessarily shameful; private. One paragraph. You'll learn what she keeps.

**3. Write her list.**

A list she keeps. Debts owed. Dead she counts. Books she's read. People she's lost. Places she'll never return. Fifteen items. Specific. You'll find her interior.

## Stuck On The Love Interest

**4. Write his grief.**

Not a flashback; a present-tense scene where grief's on his face. One page. Who died, who left, what he's carrying. You'll find his Wound.

**5. Write his small kindness.**

Not to the heroine. To someone else. A stranger, a child, an animal, a servant. What does he do that no one sees? One paragraph. You'll find the thing the heroine's going to love.

**6. Write the moment he decides she matters.**

Before the book starts, or early in it. The specific beat when he goes from "her, some mortal" to "her specifically." One paragraph. You'll find the romance's pivot.

## Stuck On The World

**7. Pick a single sensory image.**

Close your eyes. Pick the first strong image that comes: a colour, a sound, a smell. That's what your world looks like. A realm that smells like cold iron and pine smoke. A court that sounds like distant bells at every turn. A forest that tastes metallic when the magic moves. Write three lines from the image. The world builds from there.

**8. Write the recipe.**

A specific food in your world. Not generic "stew." The specific bread of the mountain villages, the spice-wine of the Autumn Court, the honey-flatbread the riders eat on patrol. Include one odd ingredient your reader hasn't heard of. One paragraph. The world becomes real around the recipe.

**9. Write the weather.**

Not "it was sunny." The specific weather pattern of your book's region. What do people dread? What do they celebrate? What turns the roads impassable, what brings the festivals out? One paragraph. You'll find the texture.

## Stuck On The Magic

**10. Ask what it costs.**

If the magic's not working, it's usually because it has no cost. Pick one cost: physical pain, aged days, severed memory, lost colour, borrowed breath. Apply it. Every time the magic's used, the cost applies. The stakes lift themselves.

**11. Ask who can't do it.**

Your magic's probably too available. Who specifically can't do this magic? Why? What does that group feel about the ones who can? One paragraph of the world's magical politics. The system sharpens.

**12. Ask what it broke.**

At some point, someone misused this magic. Something broke. Pick the event: a city fell, a god left, a species changed. Write one paragraph of the broken thing. That's your world's anchor.

## Stuck On The Romance

**13. Write the near-miss.**

A moment they almost kiss, almost touch, almost say the thing. They don't. Why not? What stops them? The near-miss does more for romance than the actual kiss most of the time. One page.

**14. Write the friend's observation.**

A side character watches your couple. She notices something. Writes it in her journal, tells a confidant, thinks it alone. Half a page from her perspective. You'll see the couple from outside, and you'll find their texture.

**15. Write the scene where one risks it.**

Not the grand gesture at the end. An earlier scene, where one of them risks something small and specific. A truth told. A vulnerability shown. A question asked she can't take back. Half a page. That's the romance's real engine.

## The Unsticking Rules

- **Pick one prompt.** Not three. One.
- **Write badly.** The prompt's job isn't a finished scene; it's unlocking the block. Bad writing's fine.
- **Ten minutes.** Set a timer. The block's usually shorter than you think.
- **Don't edit.** Write, save, close. Revision's later.
- **Use the output.** Whatever you wrote probably contains the scene or detail you needed. Bring it into your draft.

## If None Of These Work

Sometimes the block's structural. You've drafted something that doesn't fit, and your unconscious knows it. If the fifteen prompts don't move you, ask.

- **Is my heroine in the wrong scene?** Should she be somewhere else right now?
- **Is my plot actually about what I think it's about?** Sometimes what you're writing's telling you it's a different book.
- **Is my obstacle wrong?** Maybe the thing I've set up doesn't actually matter to my leads, and I'm pushing them toward a fight they don't care about.

Three questions. Answer them honestly. Sometimes the block's not a block; it's the book correcting you.

## The Block That's Just Life

Sometimes you're stuck because you're tired, stressed, sad, distracted. Writing's a cognitive load; you can't always carry it.

- **Take a walk.** Fifteen minutes.
- **Sleep.** One hour's nap or a full night.
- **Read something good.** Not your own work; someone else's, in the genre, that you love.
- **Eat something.** Low blood sugar's a surprisingly common block source.
- **Come back.** The book's still there.

Blocks are information, not failure. Listen to them. Then work again.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Pick the unsticking prompt most useful to you right now. Number it. Do it. Ten minutes.

**[L2]** Over the week, try three of the fifteen prompts. Track what unlocked.

**[L3]** Identify the type of block you face most often. Is it character, world, magic, romance? Design your own unsticking prompt for that specific block. One paragraph. Keep it for your toolkit.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your block isn't the book failing. It's the book asking a question you haven't answered. The unsticking prompts are the questions in form you can write. Answer one. The block's rarely the cliff it looks like.
