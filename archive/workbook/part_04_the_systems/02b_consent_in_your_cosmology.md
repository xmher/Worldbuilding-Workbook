# 19b. Consent In Your Cosmology

The consent scaffolding section (48) handles the craft layer: how characters and prose build consent across a whole book. This page handles the worldbuilding half. It asks whether your cosmology, your gods, or your institutions actively enforce consent, or whether they leave it entirely to your love interest's restraint.

Most romantasy worlds leave it to the characters. The ones the reader trusts build a layer into the world.

## Why Build It In

A world where consent is only as good as your love interest's manners is a world the reader has to police. A world where coercion has cosmological teeth is a world the reader settles into. The weight shifts from the heroine to the rules.

This matters most when your book has high consent pressure: bonds, glamour, heat cycles, captivity, large power gaps. The hotter the pressure, the more your world should be doing on this axis. If your book is sweet, post-bond, or low-pressure, you can skip this page without losing anything.

You don't need a goddess of consent on the cover. You need one thing in your worldbuilding that quietly backs the principle.

## Three Places To Put It

Pick one. Two if your stakes are high.

- **A deity.** A god or goddess whose domain covers vows, true names, or bodily sovereignty. Coerced oaths fail to bind because the goddess won't witness them. Forced bonds cost the user her favour. *Serpent And The Wings Of Night* uses Nyaxia as a witness-goddess of bonds.

- **An institution.** A church, a magisterium, a court of bargains, a council of mages. It arbitrates contracts and consent, has visible enforcement, and can be appealed to. Useful in court-heavy and academy worlds. Your heroine can run there. The reader feels the door exist.

- **A magic-system rule.** Coerced magic backfires. A bond formed under duress fails to seal, or seals wrong (bond-sickness, half-bond, loss of signet). The magic itself refuses the coerced yes. Useful in worlds with deep magic and a soft religion.

Each does the same job by different means. The world refuses to ratify the coerced yes.

## What This Buys You

- **Reader trust.** Your heroine isn't the only line of defence. The world stands behind her.
- **A villain consequence engine.** Your antagonist breaks the rule. The world punishes him. The reader feels the rule rather than hearing about it.
- **A real refusal route.** Your heroine can appeal to the goddess, the institution, or the magic. She often won't, because the cost of using it is too high. But the door exists, and the reader sees it.
- **A more interesting yes.** When the world also wants her free, her choosing him reads as choice, not capture.

## What This Doesn't Buy You

It doesn't replace consent scaffolding (section 48). The character and prose layers still have to work. A goddess of consent in your worldbuilding doesn't excuse a hero who doesn't ask. The world layer carries some weight. The rest still has to land on the page.

## A Few Working Patterns

Browse, then pick what fits your book.

- **The witness-goddess.** Marriages, bonds, and major bargains require her witness. Without her presence in the rite, the magic doesn't fire. Coercion is visible to her.
- **The wronged-oath curse.** Forcing or breaking an oath taken under duress curses the forcer. Bond-sickness, magic-loss, a slow corruption. The curse is known and feared.
- **The contract-court.** A magical court rules on bargains. Either party can call it. The other must answer. Fae-coded, useful for political romances.
- **The named-yes magic.** Major workings require the partner to name what she's consenting to, out loud. No naming, no working. The magic respects the verb.
- **The wards.** Magical wards prevent compulsion, glamour, or mind-influence in specific spaces: temples, weddings, healers' halls, the throne room. Wards have known limits. The limits are plot.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Does your world have a layer that protects consent independent of your characters? Name it: deity, institution, or magic-system rule. If no, decide whether you want one.

**[L2]** Write the rule in one sentence. "In my world, a [bond / oath / bargain / working] made under [coercion / glamour / heat / threat] [fails / curses / backfires / cannot bind]."

**[L2]** Who or what enforces it? Be specific. A named goddess. A named institution. A known principle of your magic.

**[L3]** Write one scene seed where this rule becomes visible on the page. A bond that won't seal. An oath that won't hold. A goddess who turns her face. Two or three sentences.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your heroine appeals to this rule once in the book. Where, when, and what does it cost her to use it? That scene is one of your strongest trust-builders with the reader.
