# 27. Etiquette, Protocol, Greetings

Etiquette is the everyday version of taboo. It's the small behaviours that mark someone as in or out of the culture, aware or unaware, deferential or rude.

Court fantasy lives on etiquette. Dark romance plays with it. Academy fiction bends it. Every romantasy has some, even if it's just a handshake and a nod.

## What Etiquette Does In Romantasy

Three things.

1. **It reveals status at a glance.** Who bows first, who sits where, who speaks first tells the reader the hierarchy without a word of explanation.
2. **It gives the heroine something to navigate.** The social minefield is half your court-scene material. She has to know the rules, or learn them, or break them deliberately.
3. **It gives the couple their private language.** The ways they ignore or bend etiquette between themselves is how the reader sees their intimacy.

An etiquette system that does all three is doing romance work. An etiquette system that's just decoration is a wasted page.

## The Greeting Layer

How do people in your world say hello?

Decide for each relationship.

- **Commoner to commoner.** A nod? A clasp? A specific phrase?
- **Commoner to noble.** A bow, a curtsy, a lowering of the eyes?
- **Noble to noble.** Depends on rank. Equal nobles: a nod and a phrase. Lower noble to higher: deference. Same-tier noble friends: a clasp or embrace.
- **Anyone to royalty.** The formal bow, the specific phrase, the proper kneel.
- **Bonded or mated pairs.** A private gesture, a forehead touch, a scent-mark.
- **Sworn brothers or shield-sisters.** A warrior's greeting, a blade-touch, a specific oath-phrase.
- **Stranger to stranger.** What's safe? What's the neutral address?

Write these down. Three or four lines is enough. Use them consistently.

## The Seating Rule

Where people sit in your world often tells the whole political story.

- **At the high table.** Who's at the centre? Who's on the ruler's right? Who's on the left? Who's pushed to the ends?
- **At the council.** Who gets a chair and who stands?
- **In the bedchamber.** Where does a lord's honoured guest sleep versus an ordinary visitor?
- **At the pack meeting.** Who sits by the alpha, who's kept at the edge?

A scene where someone's seated in the wrong place is a scene of quiet warfare. Use it once, your reader gets it. Use it twice, she'll track the politics of every seating chart for the rest of the book.

## The Address Layer

Go back to section 22 if you haven't done the honorifics work. Here's the etiquette overlay.

- **When is the honorific required?** Always in public, or only in formal settings?
- **When can a first name be used?** Close friends in private, lovers always, family sometimes, servants never?
- **What does a dropped honorific signal?** Intimacy? Insult? Challenge?

The moment your love interest drops your heroine's title and uses her first name is a scene. Plan where it happens.

## The Dining Etiquette

Most romantasy has at least one dinner scene. Know the rules.

- Who serves whom?
- Who speaks first?
- When can one begin eating?
- What's offered, what's refused, what's eaten in what order?
- What are the signals for "this meeting is political" versus "this meeting is familial"?
- How does your culture handle drinking? Toasts, rounds, specific cups?

A formal dinner scene with every rule followed is suspense. A formal dinner scene with rules broken is conflict. Your reader can feel the tension rising from which spoon someone picks up.

## The Touch Layer

Who can touch whom? In which contexts?

- Public touch. Hands? Forearms? Elbows? Small of the back? Any touch at all?
- Private touch. When alone, with permission, between specific categories of relationships.
- Bond or mate touch. What's it mean when a mated pair touches in public? What's it signal when they don't?
- Aggressive touch. A hand on a sword, a grip on an arm, a shove. When's it an insult, when's it a flirtation, when's it a prelude to violence?

Touch is the romance writer's most-used tool. Know your culture's rules well enough to break them deliberately.

## The Escort Question

Romantasy loves an escort moment. Someone offering an arm, someone leading someone through a room, someone claiming someone publicly.

Decide.

- Who's allowed to escort whom? (A betrothed, a family member, a sworn guardian, anyone invited?)
- What's the symbolic weight of accepting an offered arm? (Commitment? Approval? Shared dance?)
- What's the signal of a refused escort?
- How does your love interest claim your heroine in a crowded room through escort alone?

Design the escort scene. It's usually one of your best.

## The Bow And The Knee

Your world's deepest deference gestures.

- The full kneel: to whom, when, for what purpose?
- The half-kneel (knight to king): acceptance of service?
- The bow (varies by culture): how deep, how long, what it signals?
- The curtsy (or its equivalent): practised by whom, refused when?
- The kissing of hands: a courtier's gesture, a lover's, or both?

These are romantasy's small physical beats. Each one carries meaning. Use them sparingly and with intention.

## Greeting Hierarchy Reference

A quick reference for how greetings scale across rank. Pick the form for each pair your book uses.

| Who Greets Whom | Standard Form | Fantasy Variant | Romance Beat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commoner to commoner | Nod, short phrase | "Good morning", "Mother bless" | Everyday village scenes |
| Commoner to noble | Low bow, eyes down | Hand to heart, knee-dip | Outsider scenes; court entry |
| Commoner to royalty | Full kneel, specific phrase | Forehead to floor, "Long days" | Throne-room scene |
| Noble to noble (equal) | Nod and phrase | Clasped forearms; named bow | Court mingling scenes |
| Noble (lower) to noble (higher) | Shallow bow, title used | Hand to chest, named honorific | Political navigation |
| Noble to royalty | Bow or curtsy, deep | Named honorific; ceremonial phrase | Formal audience |
| Sworn brothers / shield-sisters | Forearm clasp, shared phrase | Blade-touch, oath-word | Warrior bond scenes |
| Bonded / mated pair (public) | Touch of forehead, hand | Scent-mark, mate-greeting | Intimacy signal |
| Bonded pair (private) | Any form | A private word only they use | The couple-specific gesture |
| Stranger to stranger | Neutral nod; "Safe travels" | Open palms, named neutrality | Road-meeting scenes |
| Enemy to enemy | Formal bow; no phrase | Curt nod; the blade not drawn | Tension; duel-precursor |
| Visiting royalty to host court | Elaborate protocol | Court-specific; gift-bearing | Alliance / arranged-match opening |

Two or three of these is enough for a book. Hold them consistently. The moment the rule breaks is the scene.

## Address And Honorifics Cheat Sheet

A quick reference for when which address form applies. Use alongside the Honorifics cheat sheet in section 22.

| Context | Form Used | What Its Drop Signals |
|---|---|---|
| Public formal | Full title and honorific | Deference lost; intimacy earned |
| Public informal | Title only, or shortened | Acquaintance is familiar |
| Private conversation | First name, or nickname | Close friend or lover |
| Intimate moments | Private name, endearment | Full intimacy |
| To an enemy | Title, slightly cold | Maintained hostility |
| To a superior you resent | Title, over-precise | Passive resistance |
| To a subordinate you respect | First name with warmth | Earned peer-ship |
| To a stranger | Neutral form; "Sir", "My Lady" | Politeness without commitment |
| Between rivals in public | Title with edge | Scene-ready hostility |
| Between bonded in public | Title or private name, depending on culture | Intimacy signal |

A good beat: your love interest uses her title in chapter one, her first name at the midpoint, her private name at the climax. The reader feels the shift without being told.

## Touch Layer Reference

A reference for designing your world's physical protocol. Pick the rules your book uses; make your couple's breach of them the scene.

| Touch | Standard Culture | Fantasy Variant | Romance Beat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Handshake / forearm clasp | Common among peers | Warrior's clasp, marked hand | First agreement; pact-sealing |
| Offered arm (escort) | Between kin or betrothed | Public claim | The escort scene; court entrance |
| Hand kiss | Courtly, reverent | Knight to lady; mage's blessing | The elegant romance beat |
| Hand to small of back | Proprietary, guiding | Mate-claim; protective | The proximity scene |
| Hair tucked behind ear | Intimate, allowed only by lovers | Private gesture | The love-scene precursor |
| Forehead touch | Bonded-pair signal | Mate mark, scent-share | The bond-reveal scene |
| Kiss on cheek | Greeting, kin or close | Regional custom | Minor intimacy; signal of trust |
| Kiss on forehead | Blessing, elder to younger | Healing gesture | The tender beat |
| Kiss on mouth | Lovers only | Claim-coded | The climactic kiss |
| Holding hands in public | Declaration in most cultures | Bonded-pair; claim | Public commitment scene |
| Wrist grip | Aggressive; restraint | Arrest, challenge | Enemies-to-lovers beat |
| Shoulder grip | Peer, masculine | Brother's comfort; warrior signal | Bromance-adjacent beats |
| Neck / throat touch | Dominant claim | Alpha-mate; monster-romance | Dark romance scene |
| Scent-mark (neck, wrist) | Shifter-specific | Rubbing scent | Mate-bond reveal |
| Weapon touched (blade drawn) | Declaration of violence | Hand on hilt | Pre-fight tension |

Touch protocol is romantasy's richest silent-language layer. The shift from "his hand grazed her elbow" to "he cupped her jaw" is the shift of the whole relationship.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Write your greeting layer for four pairings: commoner to commoner, noble to royalty, bonded pair in public, stranger to stranger.

**[L1]** Pick the one etiquette rule your heroine breaks early in the book. What's the rule? What's her mistake?

**[L1]** Pick the one etiquette rule your couple breaks between themselves as flirtation. Name it.

**[L2]** Write the seating chart for a court dinner your book includes. Who sits where? Who notices who's where they shouldn't be?

**[L2]** Describe your culture's touch rules. Public, private, bonded, aggressive. Four lines.

**[L3]** Draft the escort scene. Your love interest offers his arm to your heroine in a crowded court scene. Who sees? What does it signal? What does she do? 300 words.

**[L3]** Pick one etiquette that's falling out of fashion in your world (the older generation insists on it, the younger rolls their eyes). What is it, and who's on which side? That's a generational tension you can use in a side-character scene.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros runs military etiquette: salutes between ranks, bows to generals, addressing superiors by title, the right-hand-over-heart of the Riders' oath. Violet's failure to properly respect Xaden's rank (and her later refusal to) is a running etiquette tension. The academy's dining hall seating (squads together, ranked by year) does silent politics in every meal scene. Yarros uses institutional etiquette as her texture and doesn't need fae court flourishes.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's etiquette varies by court. The Night Court is informal among the inner circle (no honorifics between Cassian, Mor, Az, and Rhys), but rigid in public (Rhys is High Lord, and visitors must address him correctly). The Spring Court is more stratified. Calanmai and the formal balls carry their own etiquette (mask-wearing, dance order, the specific protocol of approaching the High Lord's consort). Feyre's learning of Night Court etiquette in book two (which chair, which form of address, when to speak at council) is a coming-of-age through manners.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The first time your love interest publicly breaks etiquette for your heroine is a scene. He escorts her when he shouldn't. He speaks her first name in front of the court. He sits her at his right hand. The court sees. The court talks. That's a midpoint declaration without your characters having to say a word.
