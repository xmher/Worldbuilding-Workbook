# 29. Sexuality Norms And Queer Acceptance

Every romantasy has a sexuality culture, whether the writer decides it or not. What's celebrated, what's shamed, what's private, what's public. Whether same-sex pairings are ordinary or forbidden. How your world treats desire.

The writers who decide this on purpose write the richer books.

## What This Section Does

Two jobs.

1. Help you think through your world's sexuality culture so your scenes land consistently.
2. Help you decide your world's position on queer pairings so your secondary characters (and potentially your leads) have a coherent world to live in.

The genre is rapidly expanding its inclusion of queer characters and pairings. Your book doesn't have to be queer to matter; it has to know what it's doing with its world's view of queerness.

## The Sexuality Culture Questions

Work through these for your main culture.

- **Public vs. private.** Is desire expressed openly, or kept strictly private? Does court culture include flirtation, or is it strictly decorous?
- **Pre-marital sex.** Tolerated, expected, forbidden? How does class affect the answer?
- **Extra-marital sex.** Tolerated, ignored, punished? Is the standard different for men and women?
- **Concubines, paramours, favoured companions.** Does your court have them (section 45)? How are they treated?
- **Sex work.** Legal, regulated, tolerated, shamed? Who does it, who's protected, who's not?
- **Courtly love.** Is there a tradition of idealised, often non-sexual devotion to someone out of reach? (ACOTAR gestures at this in places.)

Your answers shape the scenes your book can have. A closed-door culture can still have a love triangle; it can't have an open-door dinner-party flirtation.

## The Queer Question

Three broad stances.

- **Ordinary.** Same-sex pairings are unremarked-on. Characters might be queer; nobody comments. This is the current indie-romantasy default. Many readers expect it.
- **Accepted but distinct.** Same-sex pairings exist and are known. They may have their own traditions (specific rituals, specific spaces). The culture treats them as a normal variant.
- **Contested.** Queer pairings exist in your world but are politically, religiously, or socially contested. The book's queer characters navigate that pressure.
- **Rigidly forbidden.** Queer pairings are outlawed, hidden, or punished. A character's queerness is a risk in itself. (This is the rarest in modern romantasy and needs careful handling.)

Pick one. Hold it. A world that's "ordinary" (no one comments on same-sex pairings) shouldn't then have a homophobic rival character unless you've set up why that character is out of step with the culture.

## The Practical Question: Whose Romance Is This

If your leads are a man and a woman, the main plot runs on straight romance. Your secondary cast can still be queer. Most modern romantasy includes queer side characters; some include queer main pairings.

If your leads are a same-sex pair, the book is a queer romantasy. Same craft, same structure, same conventions, with the bond and obstacle stacks possibly emphasising different parts.

If your book is polyamorous or why-choose, your queer question overlaps with your poly question (section 44). A three-person bond can include same-sex pairings; the world has to make room.

## The Cultural Queer Tradition

If your world has accepted queer pairings, give it specific tradition.

- A same-sex bonding ceremony distinct from opposite-sex marriage, or the same ceremony with different pronouns.
- A specific title for a same-sex partner of a ruler (not just "consort" but a specific word).
- A sacred tradition (priests of a specific god are often queer; warriors of a specific order are often same-sex paired).
- A space or place associated with queer couples (a specific district, a specific temple, a specific kind of inn).

These details give your queer characters a world to live in, not just the default fantasy background with the homophobia removed.

## The Intersex, Non-Binary, And Gender-Fluid Question

Romantasy is starting to make space for characters outside the binary. Decide your world's position.

- Are there gods, spirits, or mythical beings who transcend or blur gender? (Most polytheistic mythologies have them.)
- Are there cultural traditions of non-binary identity? (Historical third-gender and two-spirit traditions are models.)
- Can a person in your world choose their gender, either magically or culturally?
- How does your language handle pronouns? Does your in-world culture have a non-binary pronoun equivalent?

If your book includes non-binary characters, decide the language and hold it consistently. If it doesn't, decide that the absence is a silence (your world might have more variety than the on-page cast shows) rather than a denial.

## The Consent Culture

Whatever your sexuality culture is, your world needs consent rules that make sense within it.

- Who can initiate a relationship? (See gender roles, section 28.)
- What makes a sexual encounter consensual in your world? (Spoken yes? Ritual context? Sobriety? Social class?)
- What happens when consent is violated? Is there a legal response, a magical one, a social one?
- How does the mate bond (section 31) interact with consent? (See section 48 for this specifically.)

A culture without clear consent rules is a culture where your book's consent politics will be murky by default. Build the rules; your reader will trust you.

## Sexuality Culture Comparison

A quick reference for the sexuality positions romantasy worlds take. Pick one dominant; secondary cultures may differ.

| Culture | Public vs Private | Pre-Marital Sex | Extra-Marital Sex | Concubines / Paramours | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Courtly decorous | Very private; flirtation veiled | Forbidden publicly, common privately | Scandalous if caught | None officially | Restraint as tension |
| Fae-open | Public flirtation; revel-sanctioned | Normal | Often tolerated | Common at court | Feast / rite scenes |
| Military / academy | Private; off-duty only | Normal | Against code | None | Behind-closed-doors beats |
| Religious-restrictive | Private; virgin ideal | Forbidden until bond | Heavily punished | Forbidden | Vow-breaking scenes |
| Merchant-pragmatic | Private | Permitted with protection | Grounds for contract renegotiation | Rare | Contract-coded romance |
| Shifter / pack | Private but scent-evident | Heat-cycle driven | Pack rules vary | Pack mating | Possessive scent romance |
| Noble-permissive | Public flirtation; private act | Normal for men; mixed for women | Quiet affairs tolerated | Court favourite tradition | Open-door balls; paramour politics |

Most romantasy blends: public-decorous court with private-passionate bond. Decide what your book's open-door scene needs the culture to support before you write it.

## Queer Tradition Menu

If your world treats queer pairings as ordinary or accepted-but-distinct, give the tradition specific texture. Pick one or two items.

| Tradition Element | What It Looks Like | Romance Angle |
|---|---|---|
| Same-sex bonding ceremony | Distinct or modified version of the opposite-sex rite | Wedding scene for queer side couple |
| Title for same-sex partner | A specific word (not just "consort") | Named; uses it at court |
| Sacred queer tradition | A god whose priests are queer; a warrior order same-sex paired | The temple scene; the order oath |
| Cultural space | A district, a temple, a specific kind of inn | The meeting place; the safe haven |
| Queer elder figure | A queer historical hero honoured | The reference in dialogue; the statue |
| Marriage variant | Handfasting with modified vows | Side-character subplot |
| Child-raising tradition | How queer couples build family | Adoption arc; magical-conception variant |
| Public celebration | A festival or day with queer visibility | A scene of warmth, not defiance |
| Everyday texture | Queer couples mentioned in passing as ordinary | Side-character normal-life moment |
| Resistance / contested | Queer pairings exist under pressure | The political arc; the hidden relationship |
| Historical shift | Once persecuted, now accepted (or vice versa) | Generational conversation |
| Magical equality | Bond, mate, signet works same-sex the same | The bond-reveal that's also queer |

Two specific details beat a vague "everyone's fine with it." A queer side couple who get married at a handfasting with specific vows is more memorable than a queer side couple who simply exist.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Where does your main culture sit on the public-private spectrum for sexuality? One sentence.

**[L1]** Which of the four queer stances describes your world? Commit.

**[L1]** Are any of your main or secondary characters queer? Name them.

**[L2]** Write two cultural details that make your world's queer tradition specific: a term, a ceremony, a place, a sacred role. One sentence each.

**[L2]** What's your consent rule, in one sentence? Who decides a sexual encounter is freely given?

**[L3]** Write a scene featuring a queer side character in your book. Not a dramatic scene. An ordinary one. What does it look like when queerness is treated as unremarkable in your world? What does it look like when it isn't?

**[L3]** Consider the version of your world where same-sex pairings are rigidly forbidden. What changes? What stories become possible? What stories become impossible? You're not committing to this version; you're testing what your current stance costs and gains.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's Navarro treats same-sex pairings as unremarkable. Violet's brother Brennan is queer; it's noted, accepted, and moves on. The culture's queer-ordinary stance is consistent; no one objects, no side character is quietly homophobic, no institution discriminates. This matches Yarros's blended-gender-roles culture: a world without rigid gender hierarchies also has room for unremarked-on queerness.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas handles queerness with growing visibility across her series. Mor is revealed as queer; Helion's bisexuality is established. Prythian's queer tradition exists but is underdeveloped in the early books. By book three and beyond, queer characters are more centred. A useful study for writers wanting to grow queer presence across a series: establishing it as part of the world early makes later expansion feel consistent rather than retrofitted.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your world's sexuality culture is one of the deepest layers of the consent scaffolding (section 48). If your book has open-door scenes, your culture has to support them. If your book handles dubcon, your culture has to have the tools to process it. Decide the culture before you write the bedroom scene, not after.
