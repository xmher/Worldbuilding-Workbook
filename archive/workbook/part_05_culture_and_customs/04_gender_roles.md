# 28. Gender Roles

Gender in romantasy is both inherited from the medieval-European fantasy default and actively remixed by the genre. Your reader expects a knight-in-armour silhouette and a challenge to it on the same page.

This section helps you design your world's gender landscape intentionally, not by accident.

## The Default You're Inheriting

Most fantasy worlds default to something close to medieval-European patriarchy. Male rulers, male warriors, female domesticity, female magic-wielders-with-a-footnote. Your reader arrives with this default already loaded.

That doesn't mean you have to use it. It means you have to decide what you're doing with it.

Three moves.

- **Inherit and interrogate.** Your world is patriarchal, and your heroine is pushing against it. The struggle is visible on the page. (Most mainstream romantasy.)
- **Invert.** Your world is matriarchal, and your love interest navigates the reverse pressure. (*The Bridge Kingdom* does this.)
- **Blend.** Your world has gender equality, and the plot obstacles come from elsewhere. (Some indie romantasy, most academy romantasy.)

The cost of inheriting unchanged is that your reader has seen the default a thousand times. A book that uses patriarchy without interrogation can read as lazy. A book that interrogates it gets something for the inheritance.

## The Axis Of Power

Ask.

- Who rules your kingdom? Which genders are in power, and how rigid is that?
- Who inherits? Primogeniture, equal inheritance, female-line descent, trial-by-worth?
- Who fights? Men only, women only, both, specific roles?
- Who rules the home? Fathers, mothers, grandmothers, eldest child?
- Who works magic? Gendered traditions, or free for all?
- Who's educated? Literacy, weapons training, court skills: who gets access?

Each answer shapes your characters' lives. Don't default; decide.

## The Romantic Dynamic

Romantasy cares particularly about how gender shapes the romance.

- **Who pursues whom?** Does the tradition say men pursue, women accept (patriarchal default)? Or women pursue, men accept (some matriarchal societies)? Or does your world have pursuit-free rituals (handfasting, mutual choice)?
- **What's the courtship expected of the man?** Gifts? Trials? Tournaments?
- **What's the courtship expected of the woman?** Chaperonage? Displays? Selection from suitors?
- **How is marriage arranged?** Family-arranged, self-chosen, bond-revealed, mating-rite driven?
- **What's expected in the bedroom?** Who initiates? Who's expected to be experienced? Who's expected to be inexperienced? (This is often a culture layer your book will bend or break.)

Romantasy often centres on a woman who breaks courtship expectations. A heroine who pursues, initiates, chooses, or refuses the traditional script is a genre favourite. Decide where she's breaking which rule.

## The Virgin Question

Romantasy has a complicated relationship with virginity. Some books use it (often without examining it), some interrogate it, some ignore it.

Decide.

- Does your culture value virginity in women? Men? Both? Neither?
- Is virginity connected to magic or bonds in your world? (Some worlds: a mate bond can only form with a virgin partner. Most worlds: no such rule.)
- Is your heroine a virgin? If yes, is it a plot point or a background fact?
- Is your love interest sexually experienced, and does your heroine care?

If virginity is a culture-layer in your book, handle it with examination. If it's not, name that so your reader knows you're not writing a book where it will ambush her.

## The Gender-Role Obstacle

Romance-specific application. Is gender itself one of your couple's obstacles?

- A warrior heroine in a culture that forbids women warriors.
- A man who wants domesticity in a culture that demands martial ambition.
- A heroine expected to be her father's political piece (married off for alliance), rebelling.
- A love interest raised to suppress emotion who has to learn to feel in public.

Gender obstacles stack with other obstacles (class, faction, species). When they do, they make for rich plots. When they stand alone, they can feel one-note. Stack deliberately.

## The Queer Question Sits Here Too

Your world's gender roles inform how queerness is treated. That's section 29. But a brief note here: if your world has gender roles that rigidly match gender to role, queerness is likely transgressive. If your world is fluid, queerness may be unremarkable.

Romantasy is increasingly including queer main characters and side characters. Decide your world's position before you populate your secondary cast. A book whose gender rules suggest rigid conservatism but whose queer side characters walk through unchallenged needs to justify the inconsistency.

## The Generational Shift

Gender roles shift over generations. Your older characters might hold rigid views; your younger ones, looser. This tension is rich material.

- The heroine's mother insists on a traditional marriage. The heroine doesn't.
- The love interest's father thinks men don't feel; the love interest, painfully, does.
- The old High Priestess forbids women at council; the new one convenes a mixed one.

A generational-shift subplot gives your world historical depth without a lot of exposition. Pick one.

## Gender System Comparison

A quick comparison of the three broad positions romantasy worlds take. Pick one as your world's dominant system; optionally a secondary for a contrasting culture.

| System | Who Rules | Who Fights | Who Inherits | Romance Obstacle | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Patriarchy (inherit) | Men primarily | Men primarily | Sons | Heroine pushes against ceiling | *ACOTAR*, most medieval-coded romantasy |
| Patriarchy (interrogate) | Men with visible cost to women | Varies | Primogeniture with resistance | The ceiling is the plot | *The Bridge Kingdom* (her side) |
| Matriarchy | Women primarily | Depends | Daughters | Love interest navigates reverse pressure | *The Bridge Kingdom* (his side) |
| Blended / equal | Both | Both | Either, equally | Obstacles come from elsewhere | *Fourth Wing* |
| Gendered roles (not ranked) | Both, different spheres | By domain | Depends on domain | Cross-domain romance | Some indie fantasy |
| Fluid / non-binary normal | Varies | Anyone | By merit or choice | Gender is not an obstacle | Some modern romantasy |
| Intersecting hierarchies | Complex; class and gender | Varies | Depends | Multi-layer obstacle | *The Cruel Prince* (fae) |

Most romantasy lands in row one or two (patriarchy, with resistance) or row four (blended, obstacles elsewhere). Pick deliberately. The row you pick sets the genre of obstacle you'll face.

## Courtship Script Reference

A reference for the courtship patterns romantasy uses. Pick yours; name the script your heroine is expected to follow before she breaks it.

| Script | Who Pursues | Who Accepts | Expected Acts | Comp Title |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Patriarchal courtship | Men pursue | Women accept or refuse | Gifts, letters, formal asking | Regency-coded romantasy |
| Matriarchal courtship | Women pursue | Men accept | Tests, gifts reversed, matriarch's approval | *The Bridge Kingdom* (partial) |
| Arranged / family-negotiated | Families arrange | Individuals (or not) | Contracts, dowries, vetting | *ACOTAR* (fae court marriages) |
| Bond-revealed | No one pursues; bond reveals | Both accept or resist | Mate recognition; the snap | *ACOTAR*, *Fourth Wing* (dragon bond) |
| Mating-rite driven | Festival selection | Either or both | Masks, dance, chosen at rite | *ACOTAR* (Calanmai) |
| Tournament / trial | Suitors compete | Chosen one selects | Trials, contests, public victory | *Throne Of Glass* |
| Handfasting / mutual | Both pursue | Both accept | A year-and-a-day trial marriage | Celtic-coded romantasy |
| Pack / alpha-claim | Alpha claims | Mate accepts or fights | Scent-mark, challenge, mating bite | Shifter romantasy |
| Merchant / arrangement | Contract-based | Signatories | Dowry, terms, witnesses | Trade-court romantasy |
| No formal script | Both, casually | Either | None required | Academy or modern romantasy |

Most romantasy heroines break the script they were raised to follow. Name the script; name which rule she breaks; name who objects.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Where does your world sit on the inherit/invert/blend spectrum? One sentence.

**[L1]** Who rules your kingdom, in gender terms? Who inherits? Who fights?

**[L1]** Is your heroine breaking a gender expectation? If yes, name it.

**[L2]** Write the courtship script in your world (who pursues, what's expected, what's the marriage). One paragraph.

**[L2]** Does virginity factor into your book's plot? If yes, how? If no, name that clearly to yourself.

**[L3]** Draft a scene where the gender rule of your world creates a specific obstacle for your couple. 300 words. What does the rule demand, and how does the couple resist, bend, or pay for resisting?

**[L3]** Pick one generational tension in your world. Write the two sides in dialogue: the older character holding the rule, the younger one questioning it. Half a page.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's Navarro is gender-blended: women fight, lead, rule. Violet's mother is a general; her sister is a cadet; Violet herself is a rider. There's no patriarchal ceiling to break. Her book's obstacles come from elsewhere (faction, political loyalty, the rebellion). This is a useful model: blended gender keeps the romance load elsewhere, which means the plot can carry different stakes.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas writes patriarchy with a visible ceiling and a heroine who breaks it. Feyre becoming High Lady of the Night Court is an explicit challenge to a system that had no High Ladies for centuries. The reaction from other courts (Tamlin's dismay, the other High Lords' disbelief, the political fallout) is the obstacle's payoff. Maas treats the patriarchy as a structure her characters have to renegotiate: she inherited it and made it a plot. Her subsequent books continue the renegotiation (Nesta's arc, Elain's, the women of the Illyrians).

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The rule your heroine breaks because she refuses to play a role her culture assigned her is a plot rail. Name it early. Let her break it in a specific scene. Let her pay a specific cost. Let her love interest choose to stand with her or against her. That's a chapter of romance in a single cultural rule.
