# 30. Death, Mourning, Afterlife In Practice

You designed your afterlife in section 19 as cosmology. This section is about the practice: what the living do when someone dies, and how your book uses it.

Romantasy has high stakes. People die. Your reader needs to feel those deaths, which means your world needs to have a ritual around death that the reader can recognise.

## Why Death Customs Matter

Three reasons.

1. **They make grief concrete.** A death scene without a ritual feels abstract. A death scene with a specific ritual (the washing, the coin in the mouth, the sung lament) lands.
2. **They give your characters something to do.** Mourning is action. What does a character do when her mother dies? If your world has a ritual, she does it.
3. **They carry political weight.** Who mourns publicly, who doesn't, whose funeral is attended, whose isn't: it's politics in the shape of grief.

A romantasy without death customs has a limp when its stakes arrive. Build them.

## The Death Ritual

What happens when someone dies in your world?

- **Preparation of the body.** Washed, anointed, dressed, arranged. By whom (women of the family, priests, ritual specialists)?
- **Wake or vigil.** Is there a night of watching the body? Who's present? What's said or sung?
- **Funeral.** Buried, burned, exposed, sea-given, given to the creatures? Is it private or public?
- **Post-funeral meal or gathering.** A shared meal, a drinking of a specific wine, a breaking of a specific bread?
- **Mourning period.** How long? What's forbidden during it? What marks the end?
- **Annual remembrance.** Is there a yearly day, a shrine visit, an anniversary rite?

Pick two or three of these to build deeply. Sketch the rest.

## The Mourning Marker

What do mourners look like in your world?

- Black clothing? White? A specific colour that's otherwise uncommon?
- A specific garment (a veil, an armband, a cloak)?
- A shaved head, cut hair, unbound hair?
- An ash mark, a painted cheek, a token on a chain?
- No outward sign at all, which is its own culture choice?

A character wearing the mourning marker of your culture tells the reader without a word that she's grieving. Design one specific marker and use it.

## The Death Of A Bonded Partner

Most romantasy has a mate bond (section 31). Death of a bonded partner is always a high-stakes scene.

- Does the surviving partner die too? (Often.)
- Does the surviving partner choose to die? (Ritual suicide, willed release.)
- Does the surviving partner diminish but live? (Colours fade, magic dims, joy leaves.)
- Does the bond transfer, release, or persist?

Your answer to the bond-death question (section 31, axis 8) sets the highest emotional stakes of your book. Revisit it now: does the answer match the grief your book is going to try to earn?

## The Death Of A Beloved Side Character

Romantasy often kills a beloved side character. An inner-circle member, a dear friend, a sibling. The reader has to feel it.

- Plan the death. Who dies, when, why?
- Plan the grief. How do your leads respond? Does the ritual of mourning give them structure?
- Plan the consequence. How does the death change the book? A death that doesn't change anything is a beat that didn't earn its page.

If your book has no side-character death, that's fine; not every book needs one. If it does, give the character a ritual the reader has seen before (even briefly), so the grief lands in a shape the reader recognises.

## The Enemy Death

What does your world do when an enemy dies?

- Display? Is the body shown, paraded, or hung?
- Burial? Denied, dumped, given to creatures, buried without rites?
- Political weight. Does the enemy's death matter to successors, allies, watchers?
- Your leads' response. Relief? Grief? (Sometimes romantasy kills an enemy who was also a complicated figure. The lead feels complicated things.)

Your enemy's death is a scene. Don't let it drop flat because you didn't design the world's response to it.

## The Long Death Of A Long-Lived Character

If your world has immortal or long-lived beings, some of them have lived long enough that everyone they loved is gone. That's a grief the genre explores well.

- A fae who remembers his mortal lover from five centuries ago.
- A vampire who's outlived seven dynasties of the family he guarded.
- An immortal priest who's seen every child he blessed return as a ghost.

If this applies to your love interest (common), his grief history is part of his Wound (section 7). Design it specifically. One named person he loved and lost does more than a vague sense of centuries-long sorrow.

## The Afterlife On The Page

If your world has a visitable afterlife (ghosts, ancestor communication, the dead speaking in dreams), design its rules.

- Who can communicate with the dead? Always, or only at specific times?
- What do the dead say? (Warnings, information, grief, love?)
- What's the cost of the communication? (Every magic has one.)
- Can a living character visit the dead? At what price?

Your afterlife on the page is a tool. Use it sparingly. A dead mother who speaks once, at the right moment, is a scene. A dead mother who narrates chapters from beyond is another book.

## The Death That The Book Doesn't Earn

A warning. Romantasy sometimes kills characters for shock without doing the grief work. The reader notices.

Before you kill anyone, ask.

- Has this character earned enough page-time for the death to land?
- Is the death specifically meaningful, or could any character have died here?
- Does the grief scene exist, or does the book move on too fast?
- Is the death reversible? (If it is, the grief will feel thin.)

Be willing to kill a character, or be willing not to. Don't kill and flinch.

## Mourning Marker Menu

| Marker | What It Looks Like | Culture It Fits |
|---|---|---|

| Armband | Black band on arm; sometimes embroidered | Military-inflected |

| No outward sign | Privacy required; grief stays hidden | Stoic warrior culture |
| Jewellery broken / removed | A specific piece removed or shattered | Loss of a spouse; bond-mourning |

A heroine in a black armband, or with a single cut strand of hair, or with an ash stripe renewed each morning: these are character-marking details that do grief work on every page she's on.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's Navarrian death customs are military-inflected: the bodies of fallen riders and their dragons are burned with specific ritual, and the rider's name is carved in the academy's memorial. The institutional grief is present without heavy ceremony. When a rider dies, their dragon often dies with them, which Yarros uses for narrative weight. The shared death of rider-and-dragon is the highest grief beat in her world, and she plans it carefully when it happens.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas writes death rituals on a court-by-court basis. The Night Court has its own mourning traditions; the Illyrians have a warrior-burning ceremony. Rhysand's grief for his mother and sister is a continuous thread. Feyre's grief for the human she was (the "death" of her mortal self after the transformation) is part of the second book's emotional weight. Maas lets her characters mourn specifically and for long enough that readers feel the cost. Grief doesn't move fast in her books, which is part of why the stakes land.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If your couple's happy ending involves the death of someone they both loved, design the funeral scene before the epilogue. The book that lets its couple be happy without acknowledging the grief earned along the way reads as thin. Give them a moment of shared mourning. Let the ritual do its work. Then let them be happy.
