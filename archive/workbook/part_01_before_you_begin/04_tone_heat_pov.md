# 4. Tone, Heat, Point Of View

Three decisions that shape every scene you'll write. Make them now, in pencil if you like, but make them. A book that drifts across tone registers, heat levels, or POV choices reads as confused, and confused is the fastest way to lose a romantasy reader who knows exactly what she wanted.

## Tone

Tone is the overall emotional colour of the book. It's the answer to: how does your reader feel while reading?

Romantasy tones cluster into five main registers.

- **Sweet.** Warm, hopeful, playful. Low stakes at the emotional level even when plot stakes are high. Banter-heavy. *A Deal With the Elf King*, *The Very Secret Society of Irregular Witches*.
- **Epic.** Sweeping, mythic, high stakes throughout. Romance and plot equally heavy. *Throne of Glass*, *From Blood and Ash*.
- **Courtly.** Glittering, arch, political. Wit as a weapon. *The Cruel Prince*, *Crescent City*.
- **Gritty.** Military, bloody, earned. Violence on the page. *Fourth Wing*, *The Poppy War* (though the latter isn't romantasy proper).
- **Dark.** Morally complicated, transgressive, often dub/non-con themes handled with consent scaffolding. *Haunting Adeline*, *Plated Prisoner*.

A book can blend two. A gritty-courtly book is a working stack. A sweet-dark book is usually a mess. You can check compatibility by asking: would a reader who bought the book for tone A be happy with a chapter written in tone B? If yes, they can live together.

Pick your dominant tone and your secondary. Most scenes hit the dominant; a handful sit in the secondary.

## Heat Level

Four standard categories. Your heat level is a promise to your reader. It belongs on your blurb, on your back-cover copy, and in your trope list.

- **Closed door.** No on-page sex. Scenes fade to black at the bedroom door. Think Sarah Addison Allen, Kerri Maniscalco's YA.
- **Fade to black.** Scenes approach intimacy, then cut. Implication is clear; description isn't. Often used in YA-adjacent romantasy.
- **Open door.** On-page sex, described with tenderness, detail, and craft. Varies from moderate to explicit. Mainstream romantasy default.
- **Explicit / dark.** On-page sex, sometimes extended, sometimes rough, often tied to bond or power dynamic. *Haunting Adeline*, *Plated Prisoner*, much of omegaverse.

Every heat level is valid. What isn't valid is drifting between them without signalling. A book that's closed door for seven chapters and then delivers an extended explicit scene in chapter eight will surprise readers on both ends of the spectrum.

### A Note On The Specific Heat Tags

BookTok uses tag vocabulary that your reader will recognise. Some of the common ones.

- **Spicy.** Generic term for open door. Useless on its own; needs a number.
- **Spice level.** 1 pepper (barely open door) to 5 peppers (explicit throughout). Self-assigned, loose.
- **Smut.** Extended explicit content. Not a pejorative in the genre; a subgenre identifier.
- **Dark.** Heat plus morally complicated. Expect consent scaffolding.
- **Dubcon / non-con.** Explicit tag for books that handle consent complications. Required labelling if present.
- **Slow burn.** Heat arrives late. Can coexist with any heat level above.
- **Insta-heat.** Heat arrives early, sometimes in chapter one. Common in indie romantasy.

You don't need to use the tag vocabulary. You do need to know what your reader expects when she sees it.

## Point Of View

Three questions here, each separable.

### Whose POV

- **Heroine only.** Reader sees everything through her eyes. The love interest stays partly mysterious. Most classic romance POV.
- **Dual POV.** Heroine and love interest both get chapters. Reader sees both interiorities. Current romantasy default (Maas, Yarros).
- **Multi-POV.** Three or more viewpoint characters. Useful in ensemble romantasy, why-choose structures, or political epics.

Dual is the genre's safe default. Single is more intimate but withholds the love interest's mind. Multi is ambitious and can dilute the romance unless carefully handled.

### What Tense

- **Past tense.** Traditional, flexible, invisible. Most published romantasy.
- **Present tense.** Urgent, cinematic, specific. *The Hunger Games* set the precedent; some modern romantasy uses it (*Fourth Wing* does). Not a default choice; a deliberate one.

### What Person

- **First person.** Immediate, intimate, limited. *Fourth Wing* uses first person present. Common in YA-adjacent and indie romantasy.
- **Third person limited.** Slightly more distance, more flexibility, access to more of the world. *A Court of Thorns and Roses* uses this (first person for Feyre actually; third for others). Most mainstream romantasy.
- **Omniscient.** Rare in the genre. Reads as old-fashioned for contemporary readers.

Pick the combination that suits your book. First person present is the most cinematic; third person past is the most flexible. Either is fine.

## The Compatibility Check

Some combinations read more naturally than others.

- Sweet tone + closed door + first person past = YA-adjacent, warm, safe read.
- Gritty tone + open door + first person present = *Fourth Wing*-adjacent, urgent, visceral.
- Courtly tone + open door + third limited past = *ACOTAR*-adjacent, lush, layered.
- Dark tone + explicit + dual POV = indie dark romantasy, current market sweet spot.

If your combination is unusual, name why. A sweet tone with explicit heat needs a reason on the page. A multi-POV dark romantasy needs careful craft to keep the darkness consistent.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your dominant tone, your heat level, and your POV choices (who, what tense, what person). Five decisions on one line.

**[L1]** Is this combination recognisable from your Comp A? If not, which comp is it closer to?

**[L2]** What's one scene you're afraid to write because of your heat level choice? Too hot or too cold? That's the scene the book probably needs.

**[L2]** If your POV is dual, which character gets the first chapter, and why her (or him)? The choice is a promise.

**[L3]** Write the opening paragraph of your book twice: once in your chosen POV/tense/person, once in a different one. Which version is yours? Which version feels easier, and which feels more like the book you want to write? They're often not the same.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros chose first person present past the point of most romantasy's comfort. It reads as cinematic and urgent; it also locks readers tightly to Violet's interiority, which is what makes the bond-magic work. If *Fourth Wing* were in third person past, the relic scars and the power bleed would feel less visceral. Her tense choice serves her magic.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas chose first person for Feyre in book one and expanded to multi-POV across the series. The opening commitment to Feyre's voice is what builds the reader's attachment; the later expansion is what lets her tell Nesta's and Cassian's story without losing the core pair. Her POV scaled with her ambition. Yours can too.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Your heat level and your bond mechanic should match. A closed-door book with a consummation-required bond has a problem. An explicit book with no physical stakes for the couple has a different problem. Make these two decisions in the same week.
