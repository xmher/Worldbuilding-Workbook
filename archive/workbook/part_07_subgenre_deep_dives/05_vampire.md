# 53. Vampire

Vampire romance has its own long shelf. *Interview With The Vampire* built the modern register; *Twilight* made the YA shelf; J. R. Ward's *Black Dagger Brotherhood* anchored the adult paranormal one; *A Discovery Of Witches* brought academic gothic; *Crescent City* fused vampires into romantasy proper.

Your reader arrives with a deep catalogue. Your job is knowing which vampire conventions you're using, which you're bending, and which you're burning.

## The Vampire Question

What does a vampire mean in your world? Pick your version.

- **Undead.** Dead and reanimated. A corpse that walks. The older, gothic tradition.
- **Changed.** Was human, was turned, is now something else. Alive, but different.
- **Born.** Vampires are their own species; they have parents and children. (Black Dagger Brotherhood.)
- **Cursed.** Afflicted by a condition; still essentially themselves but bound by rules.
- **Hybrid.** A variant of fae, demon, witch blood; your vampire's a subclass of something larger.

Each version carries its own feel. Undead vampires are gothic horror wearing a romance coat. Born vampires are essentially another species. Pick the version your book needs; don't mix registers by accident.

## The Feeding

The most customisable axis. Decide.

- **Blood only, human.** Classic. Morally loaded. Your vampire's feeding is an act against another person.
- **Blood only, animal.** The restrained version. Your vampire can survive without human feeding but isn't at full strength.
- **Blood plus other.** Sex, emotion, life-force, magic. Broader, more flexible.
- **Mostly dietary.** Your vampire eats, drinks, and occasionally needs blood as a supplement.
- **Specific people only.** Your vampire feeds from one person (a bonded mate) or one bloodline.

Your choice decides what the bite means. A bite in a blood-only-human world is a crime or an intimate trust. A bite in a mostly-dietary world is a ritual. Know the weight.

## The Bite

Design its experience.

- **Painful.** The old register. Bites hurt; humans resist them.
- **Pleasurable.** The modern register. Bites are often sexualised.
- **Neutral.** A puncture, a feeding, nothing more.
- **Bonding.** Each bite deepens a bond between vampire and source.
- **Marking.** A bite leaves a visible mark; the fed-from is claimed.

The bite is one of your book's signature scenes. Design its sensation, its duration, its aftermath. Does it heal? Leave a scar? Show when the bite happened (colour, warmth, a persistent mark)?

## The Turning

Can your vampires turn humans?

- **Yes, freely.** Any vampire can turn anyone. Dangerous; usually forbidden.
- **Yes, ritually.** Turning requires specific conditions, intent, a ceremony.
- **Yes, only by sire.** The vampire who bites the human must also turn him; others can't complete it.
- **No, never.** Vampires are born; humans can't become them. (Black Dagger Brotherhood.)
- **Only by accident or catastrophe.** Rare, unpredictable, often tragic.

If turning's possible, the question of whether your heroine will accept it becomes the book's emotional climax. If it isn't, the mortality-gap conflict is different: she ages, he doesn't, and that's the grief.

## The Sun

How does daylight work in your world?

- **Fatal.** Classic vampires burn to ash.
- **Painful and weakening.** They can exist in sun but with difficulty, and briefly.
- **Merely uncomfortable.** Sensitive, preferring night, but functional.
- **Fine.** Your vampires walk in daylight without consequence. Twilight and modern variants.
- **Conditional.** Sunlight affects them only in specific contexts (recently-fed versus not, inside versus outside, witnessed versus alone).

The sun rule decides your book's rhythm. Nocturnal vampires give you night scenes, candlelight, carriage rides before dawn. Sun-walking vampires free you from that frame but cost you the gothic texture.

## The Old Rules

The traditional vampire conventions. Which are you keeping?

- **No reflection.** Mirrors don't show them.
- **No entry without invitation.** A threshold they can't cross uninvited.
- **Running water.** Can't cross it, or can't cross it without effort.
- **Holy ground.** Churches, consecrated land, can't enter or hurts to enter.
- **Silver, stake, beheading.** Specific vulnerabilities.
- **Garlic, crosses, holy water.** The folklore kit.

Pick three or four. Use them specifically. A vampire who can't cross running water is a vampire whose love interest lives on the far side of a river. A vampire who can't enter without invitation gets a set-piece when he finally is invited. Use the rule as a tool.

## The Age Question

How old are your vampires, and what does age mean?

- **Young.** Turned recently, still close to human. Bridging worlds.
- **Middle.** A century or two. Memory of older times, but recognisably modern.
- **Old.** Several centuries. History textured through their bones. The love interest often sits here.
- **Ancient.** Millennia. A different kind of creature. Morality may be different; attachment may be different.
- **Mixed.** Your book has vampires across the age range, and the politics of age is part of your plot.

An ancient vampire love interest carries centuries of grief, centuries of wrongs, centuries of people he's loved and lost. A young vampire is a different story: still learning what he is. Pick the age your romance needs.

## The Vampire's Nature

What moral weight does vampirism carry in your book?

- **Cursed.** Vampires are damned, tragic, and their love is always complicated by what they are.
- **Natural.** Vampires are just another creature; nothing moral attaches to being one.
- **Elite.** Vampires are aristocratic, powerful, admired.
- **Despised.** The world hates and hunts them.
- **Complicated.** They're respected or feared depending on where and when.

Your moral weight changes the book. A cursed vampire's love story is a redemption arc. An elite vampire's is a court intrigue. A despised one's is a survival story.

## The Vampire Society

Do your vampires have their own world, or live among humans?

- **Hidden society.** Secret, parallel, with its own politics, often unknown to humans. (Most modern vampire paranormal.)
- **Open society.** Known to humans, politically present. A vampire king, a vampire court.
- **Isolated.** Vampires live apart (a castle, a citadel, a city of their own).
- **Integrated.** Vampires and humans coexist openly, sometimes uneasily.

A hidden society gives you masquerade-style plots. An open one gives you direct political romance. Pick.

## The Daywalker, The Halfling, The Dhampir

Your variants. Decide if they exist.

- **Daywalker.** A vampire who can walk in sun. Often through blood-magic, rare bloodline, or a specific bond.
- **Dhampir.** Half-vampire, often born of a vampire-human pairing. Walks in sun; has some powers; often caught between worlds.
- **Thrall.** A human bound to a vampire, not quite turned, not quite free.
- **Feeder.** A regular blood source, willing, sometimes bonded.

These liminal figures are often romantasy's best characters. A dhampir love interest carries the between-worlds weight without the full vampire cost. A thrall raises the consent question. Pick the liminal roles your cast needs.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Pick your vampire version. Undead, changed, born, cursed, hybrid?

**[L1]** Design your feeding rule. What do they need, from whom, how often?

**[L1]** Pick your sun rule. One line.

**[L2]** Write your first bite scene. Pain, pleasure, neutral? Set it and stage it. 400 words.

**[L2]** Decide if your heroine will be turned by book's end. If yes, design the ceremony. If no, design the mortality conversation.

**[L3]** Design your vampire society. Hidden, open, isolated, integrated? Two factions within it; one crisis currently shaping it. Half a page.

**[L3]** Write the daylight scene. Your vampire and heroine caught out as the sun rises. What happens, what does it cost, what does it reveal? 500 words.

---

*Case study: A Discovery Of Witches.* Deborah Harkness writes vampires as an ancient biological lineage, integrated with witches and daemons into a hidden paranormal society. Matthew Clairmont, her love interest, is over fifteen hundred years old and carries the grief of every person he's loved and lost. Harkness uses the age-gap frame honestly: the centuries of history he carries change how he loves, how he decides, how he protects. Her feeding rule is restrained (he doesn't feed from Diana for most of the first book; when he does, it's a crisis); her sun rule is mild (vampires walk in daylight but prefer shade); her threshold rule's dropped. The book pushes scholarship and science as its register, which makes the old conventions land differently.

*Case study: Black Dagger Brotherhood.* J. R. Ward's vampires are born, not made. They're their own species, with their own language (Old Language), their own society (the Brotherhood is an elite warrior order), and their own romantic conventions (true mate bonds, feeding between bonded pairs, blood-scent as desire). Ward's sun rule is classic (deadly), her feeding rule is paired-only for bonded vampires, and her politics (the war with the lessers, the King's court) is the world her romances sit in. Each book centres a different Brother. The pattern's given indie vampire romance a durable template: a pack or brotherhood of paired-off hero candidates, one romance per book, world-level arc running underneath.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The vampire's bite is your book's most loaded scene. Don't waste it on page ten. Build to it. Let the reader feel the hunger, the restraint, the trust. Let the first bite be the scene it should be. Then the second bite becomes something else, and the third something else again. A well-managed bite arc is a romance arc.
