# 58. Mythology-Based Romantasy

Mythology-based romantasy is a growing shelf: *A Touch Of Darkness*, *Dark Olympus*, *Neon Gods*, *Lore Olympus*, *Lovely War*. The reader wants gods on the page, myth bent into romance, and her favourite pantheon refreshed.

Your job is to know the myth well enough to honour it, then bend it with purpose.

## The Pantheon

Pick your gods.

- **Greek.** The most common modern choice. Hades and Persephone is the flagship pairing.
- **Norse.** Dark, weathered, with a built-in doom. Loki pairings are the modern favourite.
- **Egyptian.** Rich cosmology; less saturated in the current market.
- **Celtic or Irish.** Fae-adjacent; gives you the old stories underneath the modern court.
- **Slavic, Japanese, Hindu, West African, Mesoamerican.** Less-used pantheons with deep traditions. Requires careful research and respect.
- **Invented pantheon drawn from multiple sources.** Your own gods, inspired by several traditions. See section 20 for custom pantheon design.
- **Historical-specific.** A particular era's gods, in a particular region's version.

The Greek shelf is crowded; you'll need a specific angle to stand out. A less-saturated pantheon gives you more space but demands more research. Pick one, read deeply, write specifically.

## The Faithfulness Question

How closely do you follow the source myth?

- **Strict.** The story's Persephone descending to the Underworld; you tell the classical version, closer to translation than adaptation.
- **Loose.** You keep the core figures and premise; you reshape the details to suit your romance.
- **Remix.** The figures are there, but their relationships, timelines, and outcomes are yours.
- **Inspired-by.** The pantheon's a backdrop; your characters are your own, walking in the myth's landscape.
- **Modern retelling.** The gods in the present day, running clubs, companies, courts.

Pick your faithfulness register and hold it. A book that oscillates between strict and remix confuses its reader.

## The Setting

Mythology-based romantasy can live anywhere. Pick.

- **Ancient world.** Classical Greece, Viking-era Scandinavia, pharaonic Egypt. Period-appropriate.
- **Modern world.** The gods are here now, running things. (Dark Olympus, Neon Gods.)
- **Mythic realm.** Olympus, Asgard, the Duat, a place out of time.
- **Alternate world.** A secondary world where the pantheon lives, recognisable but shifted.
- **Between worlds.** A mortal heroine crosses into the mythic realm and back.

Modern retellings are currently strong on the shelf. The ancient-world setting's rarer but has its audience. Pick yours; set early.

## The Gods' Nature

What are your gods, exactly?

- **Eternal and all-powerful.** Classical register; they're gods, they can do anything.
- **Eternal but limited.** They have domains; outside them, their power's reduced.
- **Mortal gods.** They can die; they have felt grief, seen deaths in their own families.
- **Once-mortal ascended.** They were human and became gods; their origin's part of their character.
- **Metaphorical.** Your "gods" are magical beings whose divinity is a cultural assignment rather than absolute.

The nature of divinity shapes the stakes. A god who can't die is a different love interest than one who can. A god of love is a different character than a god of the dead.

## The Pairing

Mythology-based romantasy often runs on a classic pairing. Decide whether you're using one or inventing yours.

- **Hades and Persephone.** The dominant modern pairing. Underworld romance, seasonal return, the question of consent in the original myth reworked for a modern reader.
- **Eros and Psyche.** The older romance. Hidden identity, trust, trial.
- **Achilles and Patroclus.** The queer classical pairing. Recently re-centred.
- **Loki pairings.** Norse trickster with many options.
- **Orpheus and Eurydice.** The descent, the look-back, the loss. Usually rewritten for an HEA.
- **Invented pairings.** Your own gods or your own re-pairings.

The dominant pairings are crowded shelves. If you're entering one, your angle has to be clear. If you're pairing gods in a non-classical way, your readers will come for the novelty; make it work.

## The Core Myth As Plot

Usually your chosen myth is the plot's spine. Plan how.

- **Follow the myth.** The book walks through the source. The romance is layered onto known beats.
- **Invert the myth.** The source goes one way; your version goes the other. (Persephone isn't abducted; she walks down herself and stays because she wants to.)
- **Continue the myth.** Your book starts after the known story ends. What happens in year two of Persephone's return?
- **Precede the myth.** Your book is what led to the famous story. The lovers' earlier courtship.
- **Sideline the myth.** Your book's plot is its own; the myth's a frame the reader recognises.

Pick one. Your reader's carrying the source. What you're doing with it is part of her pleasure.

## The Consent Question In Myth

Many of the classical myths have consent problems by modern standards. Persephone's abduction, Io's transformations, Leda's swan. Your romantasy has to answer them.

- **Recast.** In your version, Persephone goes willingly. The abduction's a cover story.
- **Invert the power.** In your version, Persephone is the threat; Hades is the one overwhelmed.
- **Honest reckoning.** In your version, the original wrong happened and the characters deal with it. Harder work.
- **Skip the problematic myths.** Choose the ones that don't carry the baggage.

The modern romantasy reader won't accept an uninterrogated version of the problem myths. Your craft has to show you've thought about the source. Read section 48 on consent scaffolding if your book's handling this directly.

## The Underworld, The Mountain, The Tree

Mythology-based romantasy loves specific mythic settings. Pick yours.

- **The Underworld.** Hades, the Greek dead. River, gates, the three-headed dog, the throne room.
- **Olympus.** The mountain, the high hall, the council, the thrones.
- **Asgard or Valhalla.** The Norse hall. Warriors, mead, the coming end.
- **The World Tree.** Yggdrasil or its analog. Roots, branches, worlds strung between.
- **The Duat.** The Egyptian underworld. Weighing of hearts, the journey.
- **The Otherworld, Tir na nOg.** Celtic. Timeless, deep-fae-adjacent.

Pick one and build it with specificity. A vague Underworld is a mistake. The reader wants the specific river, the specific judge, the specific pomegranate.

## The Mortal In The Myth

Your heroine may be mortal, may be a lesser divinity, may be a god. Decide.

- **Mortal, lifted up.** She's human; she catches a god's attention; her mortality becomes part of the story.
- **A nymph or lesser divinity.** She's of the mythic world but not at its top tier. Room to grow.
- **A goddess in her own right.** Your book's a peer romance between two powers.
- **A re-incarnated mortal.** She's human now but was someone before; the myth has its grip on her.

Pick the power level of your heroine against your god. A mortal-god romance has the mortality gap; a peer romance has the political one; a re-incarnated plot has the long memory.

## The Pantheon's Politics

Gods have politics. Decide yours.

- **A council.** The gods rule by committee; alliances shift; factions form.
- **A hierarchy.** One god at the top; others in descending authority. Your book might be a succession.
- **An enmity.** Two gods or factions at war. Your lovers cross the line.
- **A family.** The pantheon's a dysfunctional extended family. Your romance is in the family drama.
- **A diaspora.** The gods are scattered, hiding, or dwindling. Your book's about what's left.

The politics layer is what saves mythology-based romantasy from being just a retelling. Your reader's already seen the myth; she's here for what you do with the court around it.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Pick your pantheon and your setting. Two lines.

**[L1]** Name your pairing. Classical, queer, invented, bent?

**[L1]** Decide your faithfulness register. Strict, loose, remix, inspired-by, modern retelling?

**[L2]** Write your version of one consent-problem moment from the source myth. How does your book handle it? One paragraph.

**[L2]** Describe your mythic setting in specific sensory detail. What's the river, the hall, the tree, the gate? 300 words.

**[L3]** Design your pantheon's politics. Council, hierarchy, enmity, family, diaspora? Who's your lovers' biggest rival in the pantheon, and why? Half a page.

**[L3]** Draft the scene where your heroine first sees your god-love-interest in his full power. Not just domain-coded; fully divine. 500 words. How does she look? How does he respond to being seen?

*Case study: A Touch Of Darkness.* Scarlett St. Clair's Hades and Persephone series sets the myth in a modern Greek-god-populated world. Hades runs a nightclub empire; Persephone is a mortal journalism student. St. Clair uses the abduction-into-contract beat as a writer's contract signed under pressure (Persephone commits to an impossible bargain she thinks she's going to win), inverts the consent question (Persephone's drawn to Hades from the start; the bargain's a frame for time with him), and lets the Underworld be both a literal location and the texture of Hades's business. The series has grown across multiple books, each centring different couples from the pantheon, building a shared modern-god world. A useful model for modern-retelling romantasy with a single author's evolving worldbuilding.

*Case study: Dark Olympus.* Katee Robert's pantheon is a small city-state where the thirteen Olympians are offices (anyone can be Apollo; the current Apollo's the one who holds the title). Her pairings draw from the myths but often bend them (Electric Idol pairs Eros and Psyche as the son of Aphrodite and a political rival's daughter; Neon Gods pairs Hades and Persephone with an adult consent frame built in). Robert's explicit scenes are the series' signature; her modern-political register lets her play the pantheon as a high-glamour power system. A useful study in taking the classical cast and placing them in a tightly-designed city where office-holding is the story and the romance is the fight.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The god's power is a romance tool, not a plot trick. Design what he's god of, then stage the scene where he uses that specific power for her. A god of death who stops death for her. A god of winter who lets spring in for her. A god of silence who speaks, for her. That's what mythology-based romantasy is: the myth bent, once, for love.
