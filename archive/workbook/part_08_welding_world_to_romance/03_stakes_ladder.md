# 61. The Stakes Ladder

Your book needs stakes on four rungs. Personal, relational, political, cosmic. The good romantasies climb the ladder as the book progresses, so the final chapters have everything riding on the outcome.

This section is the audit. Do your stakes climb, or do they sit on one rung?

## The Four Rungs

Each stake is at a different scale.

- **Personal.** What does your heroine stand to lose about herself? Her identity, her vow, her safety, her peace.
- **Relational.** What does she stand to lose in her closest bonds? Her love interest, her sister, her dragon, her mentor.
- **Political.** What does her choice cost the wider world she's in? A court falls. A faction turns. A treaty breaks.
- **Cosmic.** What's at stake at the scale of the gods, the world, the magic itself? An age ends. A god dies. A realm is sealed.

A book on only one rung is thin. A book across all four rungs is full. Most romantasies use three.

## The Ladder Climbs

The ladder's not a static list. It's a progression. Stakes start personal and grow.

- **Act one.** Personal. Your heroine wants something for herself, fears something for herself.
- **Act two, rising.** Relational. Her love interest is in it with her; her bond's at stake. Her sister, her inner circle.
- **Act two, midpoint.** Political. What she does starts to affect the wider world. A faction moves because of her choice.
- **Act three.** Cosmic, or political at its widest. The book ends on a scale larger than it started on.

A book where the stakes stay personal through to the end feels small. A book where the stakes start cosmic leaves nowhere to go.

## The Personal Stake

Build this first. It's what your reader hooks into.

- **What does she want?** (Section 6.)
- **What does she need?** (Section 6.)
- **What's her Wound?** (Section 6.)
- **What does she lose if she doesn't get what she needs?** Name the specific loss.

A heroine whose personal stake is "being happy" is a heroine without a personal stake. A heroine whose personal stake is "avoiding the marriage her father's arranged because the memory of her mother's forced marriage is what the Wound's made of" has a stake you can write with.

## The Relational Stake

Who else gets hurt if your heroine fails? Or if she succeeds?

- **Her love interest.** The direct relational stake. The romance's survival.
- **Her inner circle.** Her siblings, her best friend, her mentor, her bonded animal.
- **Her family.** If her choice costs them, name the cost.
- **Her enemies, sometimes.** A complex relational stake: even the loss of an enemy can matter if the enemy's complicated.

The relational stake is where romantasy earns its tears. If the reader's crying, it's because she's afraid for someone she's come to love beyond the couple.

## The Political Stake

Your world's politics give you this rung.

- **The court or faction your leads belong to.** What happens to it if they fail or succeed?
- **The treaty, alliance, or balance.** What breaks?
- **The succession or throne.** Who takes power, or loses it?
- **The people.** Romantasy often has a population whose fate is at stake. Name who.

A political stake without named people is abstract. A political stake with named people (the children of her village; the soldiers of his command) lands. Specify.

## The Cosmic Stake

The largest scale. Not every book needs this, but most romantasies of trilogy length reach it.

- **A god.** Awakening, returning, dying, being replaced.
- **A realm.** Sealing, opening, collapsing, being remade.
- **A magic.** A law of your system about to change, end, or invert.
- **An age.** The ending of one era and the start of another.

Cosmic stakes can inflate. Watch the ceiling. If you go cosmic in book one, book two needs somewhere to go. Many writers hold cosmic stakes for later books and let early books be personal-relational-political.

## The Ladder And Your Couple

Your couple lives on all four rungs. Design their stakes per rung.

- **Personal.** What each of them risks as an individual.
- **Relational.** What their bond specifically risks.
- **Political.** What their union costs or wins for the world.
- **Cosmic.** What their love means at the scale of the gods.

An ACOTAR-scale romance has all four: Feyre's personal freedom, her bond with Rhys, the Night Court's political role, Prythian's cosmic balance. A *Bridge Kingdom*-scale romance holds three: Lara's personal vow, her bond with Aren, Ithicana and Maridrina's political shape, with less cosmic weight. Both work.

## The Stake That Escalates

One of romantasy's sharpest craft moves: an early-book stake that grows.

- The personal fear she has in chapter one becomes, by the climax, the thing that could unmake a realm.
- The relational loss she's avoiding becomes, by the midpoint, the fate of a court.
- The political crisis of act one becomes, by act three, a cosmic reckoning.

Design one escalation. Pick a stake that starts on one rung and climbs. Your plot has its arc built in.

## The Stake That Shrinks

Less common, but powerful when done right. Sometimes a cosmic stake shrinks back to the personal by the end.

- The goddess-level crisis resolves to her choosing to stay alive for him.
- The fall of a kingdom resolves to her choosing her own life over her birth's expectation.
- The sealing of a realm resolves to their kiss.

The point of the shrinking stake: even with the world at stake, the book's centre is the couple. That's romantasy's thesis. Be willing to let the cosmic dissolve back into the personal at the right moment.

## The False Stake

A warning. Some books promise stakes they don't deliver.

- **The death threat that won't happen.** The reader knows the leads survive.
- **The political crisis that resolves off-page.** The treaty signs without her presence.
- **The cosmic threat that only exists in dialogue.** Characters say a god is coming; the god never arrives.

False stakes are stakes your plot doesn't earn. The reader feels them as manipulation. Your ladder's only as strong as its weakest rung.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your personal stake in one line. What does she lose if she fails?

**[L1]** Name your relational stake in one line. Who else bears the cost?

**[L1]** Name your political stake in one line. What world-element shifts?

**[L2]** Map the ladder across your plot. What's the personal stake in act one? What's the political stake at the midpoint? What's the cosmic stake in act three? One sentence each.

**[L2]** Design one escalating stake. An early-book fear that grows into a late-book world-shape. One paragraph.

**[L3]** Audit your stakes honestly. Do any of them resolve off-page, in dialogue, or without the couple's involvement? Name the false stakes. How will you make them true?

**[L3]** Draft the scene where the cosmic stake shrinks back to the personal. Your heroine, at the moment of world-shaping choice, chooses him. What does that look like on the page? 500 words.

---

*Case study: ACOTAR series.* Maas climbs all four rungs across her books. Book one is personal and relational (Feyre's family, her capture, her bond with Tamlin under the curse). Book two adds political (the Night Court's role in Prythian, the brewing war). Book three goes cosmic (the King of Hybern, the Cauldron, the end of the war). Each book escalates; the stakes ladder keeps climbing. By the end of book three, all four rungs are active simultaneously: Feyre's personal choice, her bond with Rhys, the fate of the Night Court, the fate of Prythian. A clean model of the escalating ladder.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros starts on personal and relational (Violet's survival, her bond with her sister, her bond with Xaden). By the end of book one, political stakes are active (the rebellion, the war with Poromiel). Book two and beyond push toward cosmic (the venin, the wards, the wider magical reality). Yarros holds cosmic until book two; her first book is sharper for it. A useful model: you don't need all four rungs in book one; you need the first three, with room to grow.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The romance is on every rung. The couple's love is personal (his presence in her life), relational (their bond), political (their union shifts courts), and sometimes cosmic (their mating changes the magic). A romance that only exists on the personal rung leaves stakes on the table. Let their love be the thing that costs the kingdom. That's what the subgenre's built for.
