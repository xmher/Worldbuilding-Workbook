# 59. The Obstacle Audit

You've built a world. Parts 3 through 7 gave you geography, magic, religion, culture, subgenre. Now you audit.

The audit asks one question of every element: does this thing make your couple's romance harder?

If yes, keep it. If no, cut it or bend it until it does.

## Why The Audit Matters

Worldbuilding that doesn't push against the romance is decoration. Pretty, sometimes. But it isn't doing the book's work.

Romantasy runs on obstacles. The wall between courts, the curse that separates the lovers, the treaty that binds one to another, the mate bond she can't accept, the magic that costs him every time he uses it. A world without obstacles is a world where the couple just gets together. A world with too many obstacles is a book that never lets them breathe.

The audit finds the right mix. It identifies which worldbuilding elements are earning their space, and which aren't.

## The Five Obstacle Categories

Every obstacle in your book falls into one of five categories. Most romantasies use three or four.

- **Physical.** A wall, a sea, a veil, a distance. Your couple is literally not in the same place.
- **Political.** A treaty, a faction, a family, a court. Your couple's being together costs something to someone with power.
- **Magical.** A bond they can't form, a curse they can't break, a rule of your magic system that forbids them.
- **Cultural.** A class line, a species line, a religious line, a gender role. Your world's norms don't want them together.
- **Personal.** A Wound, a Want, a past that's in the way. Not worldbuilding; but worldbuilding shapes what wounds are possible.

A romance that only runs on personal obstacles is a contemporary romance with a fantasy coat. A romantasy uses at least two worldbuilding categories as well.

## The Audit Itself

Work through every worldbuilding element you built. For each, ask three questions.

- **Does this element create an obstacle for my couple?** If yes, name it in one line.
- **What category?** Physical, political, magical, cultural, personal.
- **Is the obstacle active in my plot?** Does it appear as a scene, a problem, a force? Or is it just on the page as exposition?

Score each element honestly. The ones that score three yeses are your load-bearing walls. The ones that score zero need a second look.

## Example: Auditing A Fae Court World

Imagine you've built a standard fae court setting. Let's audit.

- **The geography of the two courts.** Physical obstacle? Yes, they're a journey apart. Political? Yes, the courts are rival. Active? Depends on your plot. If your couple crosses the border twice, it's active. If they meet at a neutral event and the border never appears, it's decoration. Score: possibly three.
- **The mate bond rule.** Magical obstacle? Yes, once identified the bond forces choices. Active? Yes, every scene where one or both resists or accepts the bond. Score: three.
- **The naming tradition where nobles have three names and commoners have one.** Active? Maybe, if a commoner love interest's single name is a scene. Often it's colour. If you don't use it, cut it or use it.
- **The seasonal calendar with eight sacred days.** Active? One or two of them might be scenes. The others are colour. Use the ones you can use; don't sweat the rest.

Most of your worldbuilding is colour. That's fine. The audit finds the four or five elements that are doing real work.

## The Obstacle Stack

Your couple's central obstacle (section 10) is probably made of more than one element. Stack them.

- **Primary obstacle.** The one thing that keeps them apart at the start.
- **Secondary obstacle.** The complication that emerges mid-book.
- **Hidden obstacle.** The thing the reader doesn't know yet; it breaks in act three.
- **Generated obstacle.** The one your leads create themselves by trying to solve the earlier ones.

A stack of three or four obstacles gives your book its layers. One obstacle on its own resolves too fast. Five or more and the reader gets tired.

## The Obstacle That's Also The Bond

Romantasy's sharpest design is the obstacle that's also the thing that pulls them together.

- The bond that binds them against their will.
- The prophecy that puts them in the same room and on opposite sides.
- The magic that makes them allies only if they share the cost.
- The curse that requires them to stay near each other, whether they want to or not.

An obstacle-bond does double duty. It's the reason they're together and the reason they can't be together. These are the scenes readers underline. Design for at least one.

## The Flat Obstacle

A warning. Some obstacles feel thick but are flat.

- **The misunderstanding.** A single conversation would clear it up. Unless your world has a reason that conversation can't happen, this is a flat obstacle.
- **The ex who shows up and then leaves.** A scene of jealousy without real threat.
- **The rival with no power.** A character who wants to break the couple up but can't actually do it.
- **The secret that doesn't matter.** A kept secret whose revelation changes nothing.

If your obstacle can be cleared in one scene with no cost, it's flat. Thicken it or cut it.

## The Too-Thick Obstacle

The reverse problem. An obstacle so large the reader stops believing the couple can cross it.

- **The species barrier with no precedent.** If no one in your world has ever successfully loved across this line, your couple's success needs to be designed.
- **The curse with no known cure.** Your reader needs at least a hope, a clue, a partial solve.
- **The enemy with godlike power.** If he can kill them at will, they need a reason he doesn't.

A too-thick obstacle needs the world to give the couple a tool. Design the tool before you design the obstacle.

## Prompts

**[L1]** List five worldbuilding elements you've built. For each, write the obstacle it creates for your couple in one line. If an element creates no obstacle, mark it.

**[L1]** Identify your couple's primary obstacle. Which category? One sentence.

**[L1]** Identify your couple's secondary obstacle. Different category, ideally. One sentence.

**[L2]** Design your obstacle stack. Primary, secondary, hidden, generated. One line each.

**[L2]** Find the obstacle that's also the bond. If you don't have one, design one. What pulls them together that also keeps them apart?

**[L3]** Audit every major worldbuilding element (eight to twelve items). Score each for obstacle creation, category, and active use. Half a page. The elements that score zero go on a cut-or-bend list.

**[L3]** Take one element that scored zero. Rewrite it so it creates an obstacle. Half a page. You're not adding a new element; you're making an existing one work.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's worldbuilding is heavily audited for obstacle use. The riders' academy is both setting and obstacle (Violet has to survive it; the survival pressure's real). The war is both backdrop and obstacle (Xaden's rebellion ties stand against Navarro, which is Violet's home). The dragon bond is both magic and obstacle (Sgaeyl and Tairn are mated dragons, which forces Xaden and Violet into proximity). Almost every element earns its page by creating a pressure on the couple. The elements that don't (some of the worldbuilding around gryphons, for instance, in book one) either get more page in later books or stay in the background. Yarros's discipline is visible: if something's in the world, it's probably doing work.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas's first book runs on a stacked obstacle: Feyre is mortal, Tamlin is fae, the curse binds Tamlin, the wall separates the worlds, Amarantha rules Under the Mountain. Every worldbuilding element pushes the couple toward separation or toward cost. When Maas introduces an element that doesn't serve the obstacle (some of the Spring Court's inner politics early on), she tends to reposition it as obstacle-relevant in the next book (Tamlin's Spring Court failure becomes the political obstacle of book two). A useful pattern: even worldbuilding that feels like decoration in book one can be re-deployed as obstacle in book two if the writer's tracking.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The audit's pass-fail question isn't "is this worldbuilding good?" It's "is this worldbuilding making my couple's life harder?" An element that doesn't make their life harder is an element your reader will forget. One that does is an element she'll remember. Audit without mercy. Cut or bend what's not earning its page. The book's sharper for it.
