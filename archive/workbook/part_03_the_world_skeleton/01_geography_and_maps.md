# 12. Geography And Maps

You don't need a map. You do need a geography.

A map is a visual aid. Some writers find drawing one clarifying; others find it a distraction. What every romantasy writer needs is a clear sense of the physical shape of her world: what's where, what's between, and what keeps her couple apart or forces them together.

This section is about geography as obstacle engine. Pretty landscapes are decoration. Landscapes that separate, gather, or threaten your couple are plot.

## The Three Geographic Obstacles

Romantasy geography usually does one of three jobs.

1. **Separates the couple.** A wall, an ocean, a veil, a mountain range, a court boundary, a cursed forest they can't cross.
2. **Forces them together.** A confined space, an academy with no exits, a carriage journey, a siege, a shared trail.
3. **Threatens them.** A landscape that wants them dead. A storm coast, a tithe-taking wood, a plain with things that hunt.

Name at least one feature of your map that does each job. If you can't, your geography isn't working yet.

## The Features That Earn Their Keep

Not every landscape element pulls weight. Some do.

- **Walls and borders.** The simplest separator. ACOTAR's wall between mortal and fae lands; *The Bridge Kingdom*'s titular bridge; any academy's perimeter.
- **Seas and oceans.** Large-scale separation, slower than walls to cross. Often culturally meaningful (the other side is foreign, strange, untrustworthy).
- **Mountain ranges.** Defensive, culturally isolating, slow to cross. A natural faction boundary.
- **Forests.** Romantasy loves a dangerous forest. Fae live in one; monsters hunt in one; the heroine gets lost in one. Forests can hold magic that roads can't.
- **Deserts and wastelands.** Harsh passage. Usually a place where characters are tested, or where exiled people live.
- **Rivers.** Natural travel routes, natural borders, natural pause-points for scenes.
- **Cities.** The populated heart of your world. Where the court lives, where the academy sits, where the politics happen.
- **Cursed or forbidden places.** A valley no one enters. A mountain no one climbs. The site of an old war or an old god. Useful for Act Three climaxes.

You don't need all of these. You need enough to give your couple somewhere to be separated, thrown together, and tested.

## The Starting Points Question

In most romantasy, the couple start in different places. Name each starting point.

- Where does your heroine live when the book opens?
- Where does your love interest live when the book opens?
- What's between them? A border, a sea, a kingdom, a wall, a species line?
- How long would it take to cross that distance at the fastest possible speed (horse, ship, dragon, magic portal)?

The distance between them is the distance the book has to close. If it's too small, the obstacle feels thin. If it's too large, the pacing gets heavy. The right distance is the one that makes getting together feel earned but not impossible.

## The Scale Problem

Most romantasy writers over-scope their world. They try to build three continents, twelve kingdoms, forty cities.

Your reader doesn't need all that. She needs the places your couple goes, plus a sense of the world around them.

A scale test: how many named places does your book visit in Act One?

- One or two: you've scoped for a single-kingdom story. Fine.
- Three to five: you've scoped for a journey story. Fine.
- Six or more: you've scoped for an epic. Make sure your pace supports it.

You can have more places existing in your world than your couple ever visits. You probably should. But the ones on the page need to pull weight.

## The Map-Drawing Question

Draw a map if it helps you. Don't if it doesn't.

Things a map can do for you.

- Force you to commit to distances (is the journey three days or thirty?).
- Clarify who's adjacent to whom (two kingdoms that share a border have a different relationship than two separated by a third).
- Expose geography you haven't really built (the blank space on the page is the question you haven't asked).
- Give your reader (in the eventual published book) a visual anchor.

Things a map can't do for you.

- Make your geography load-bearing. A pretty map of a useless world is still a useless world.
- Substitute for deciding what your features do.
- Replace the actual geographic answer to the obstacle question.

If you draw one, keep it rough. The designed version happens later.

## The Scene-Level Geography

At the scene level, geography is the room, the garden, the corridor, the clearing. Small-scale geography does small-scale work.

- A balcony separates two characters by a few feet and a social code.
- A locked door separates them by two inches of oak and a law.
- A shared carriage presses them together for a hundred miles.
- A bedroom with a fireplace does a completely different job than a bedroom without one.

Your scene geography should answer the same question your world geography does: what does this space do to the couple? If the room doesn't matter, pick a room that does.

## Map Feature Menu

Here's a browsable menu to steal from when you're sketching your map. You don't need half of these. Pick the ones your plot actually uses.

**Natural features**

- Rivers, streams, fords, waterfalls, rapids
- Mountains, ridges, passes, high plateaus
- Forests: deep wood, old-growth, cursed grove, fae-held
- Plains, steppes, heaths, moors
- Coast, cliffs, beaches, tidal flats
- Marsh, fen, bog, swamp
- Desert, dunes, dry basin, cracked flats
- Volcanoes, geysers, lava fields
- Caves, caverns, sinkholes, gorges
- Lakes, ponds, inland seas
- Islands, archipelagos, isolated atolls

**Built features**

- Capital city, secondary cities, market towns, villages
- Castle, keep, fortress, watchtower, beacon chain
- Temples, cathedrals, wayside shrines, abandoned sanctuaries
- Academy, tower of learning, mages' college, scriptorium
- Harbours, docks, lighthouses, shipyards
- Roads, bridges, causeways, aqueducts
- Walls (city, kingdom, mortal-fae border), gates
- Tombs, barrows, crypts, royal burial grounds
- Ruins: temple, palace, battlefield, drowned city
- Inns, hunting lodges, manor houses, crofts

**Romantasy-specific features**

- Mortal-fae wall or veil (*ACOTAR*)
- Bond-stones or soul-tree groves
- Dragon aeries, riders' parapet, hatching grounds (*Fourth Wing*)
- Court seats (Spring, Night, Day) with climate signatures
- Wild zones: the unseelie wood, the tithe grove, the cursed valley
- Sacred rite sites: equinox circles, Calanmai-coded standing stones
- Prophecy wells, oracle sanctums, reading pools
- Black market or smuggler's cove (for bargains, geas trades)
- Bonding cabin, mating-flight range, exile island
- A ley-line crossing (for magical transport or power surge)

**Obstacle features (the ones with teeth)**

- A wall your couple's on opposite sides of
- A sea she has to cross for him
- A cursed wood no one returns from
- A mountain pass that closes in winter
- A border guarded by a rival court
- A capital she's exiled from
- A village her family has to leave

Pick two features from the obstacle list. Those are your plot. The rest is texture.

## Your Starting Points Worksheet

Fill this in before you draft Chapter One. Every answer is a plot decision.

| Question | Your Answer |
|---|---|
| Where does your heroine live when the book opens? | |
| Where does your love interest live when the book opens? | |
| What lies between them (wall, sea, forest, court line)? | |
| How long does crossing take at the fastest speed? | |
| What does that distance cost to cross (money, magic, permission)? | |
| Who or what prevents easy crossing? | |
| What's the first crossing that happens in your book? | |
| Who crosses first, toward whom? | |

If the crossing is instant and free, your geography isn't separating. If the crossing is impossible, your book can't happen. The right answer's a costly-but-possible crossing the plot earns.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name the single geographic feature that most separates your couple. One sentence.

**[L1]** Name the single geographic feature that most forces them together. One sentence.

**[L1]** Where does each of them live at the book's opening? Two sentences.

**[L2]** List the five named places your book visits in Act One. For each, name one sensory image that makes it not interchangeable with any other.

**[L2]** Pick the most important city, court, or settlement in your book. What's the geography of its layout? Where does your couple meet within it?

**[L3]** Draft a rough map. Paper, digital, pencil on a napkin, whatever. Mark the starting point of each lover and the path between them. Don't make it pretty. Make it informative.

**[L3]** Pick one feature of your map and ask: if this weren't here, what would change about the book? If the answer is "nothing much," cut it. If the answer is "the whole plot," build it carefully.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros's world is small on purpose. The Riders Quadrant at Basgiath is the containing geography; the outpost missions of book two expand it slightly; the war beyond those walls is gestured at more than shown. The confined academy is the geography that forces Violet and Xaden together, and the political map of the provinces is the geography that keeps her mother's regime and his rebellion in tension. Tight scope, heavy lift.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses a classic court-map: seven courts divided by cardinal directions and seasonal identities, with a wall between fae and mortal lands and a mountain (Under the Mountain) where power is centralised. Each court has a distinct sensory identity (Spring is warm petal-drift, Night is glittering dark, Autumn is ember-light). The geography does double duty: it's the separator, the threat, and the cultural flavour. Every book visits new courts and the reader learns the world by traversing it.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** Every feature of your map is a potential obstacle or reunion engine. The sea is where she's exiled. The mountain is where he hides. The wall is the thing one of them crosses for the other. Pick your features for what they can do to your couple. Decorate later.
