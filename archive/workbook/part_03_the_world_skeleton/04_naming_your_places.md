# 15. Naming Your Places

A place name is a sound. Your reader hears it before she parses it. If the sound is generic, the place feels generic. If the sound is specific, the place feels like somewhere.

This section is a naming cheat sheet. Not rules, patterns. Steal the ones that work for your world; leave the rest.

## The Phoneme Test

Before you name anything, decide the sonic palette of your world.

- **Soft sounds.** Velara, Aelwen, Silmaris, Linnion. Flowing, elven, courtly. Reads as high fantasy with elegance.
- **Hard sounds.** Drak, Korn, Vrath, Nizgar. Martial, orcish, grim. Reads as warrior-coded or monstrous.
- **Wet sounds.** Moradon, Aelweth, Beuliach, Taliesin. Celtic-adjacent. Reads as mystical, old, layered.
- **Dry sounds.** Achik, Qar, Thel, Mek. Desert, ancient, stripped. Reads as dry-place coded.
- **Cold sounds.** Skarn, Isval, Krynn, Vornir. Northern, sharp-edged, often Slavic-adjacent. Reads as cold-place coded.

Pick one palette per culture. If your fae speak in soft sounds and your dragon riders speak in hard ones, the reader feels the cultural difference in the names before she's told anything.

## The Prefix And Suffix Library

Most fantasy place names are a root plus a prefix or suffix. Build your own small library.

- **Place-suffixes.** -hold, -reach, -gate, -march, -fall, -cross, -haven, -thorn, -spire, -mire, -crest, -vale.
- **Place-prefixes.** Black-, White-, Iron-, Salt-, High-, Low-, North-, Grey-, Wild-, Old-.
- **Kingdom-suffixes.** -ia, -an, -land, -realm, -moor, -mark, -reach, -gardh.
- **City-suffixes.** -burg, -port, -gate, -mere, -haven, -holm, -keep.
- **Court or noble-seat suffixes.** -hall, -throne, -court, -spire, -seat.

Pick four or five from the library. Use them consistently. An academy called Basgiath and a city called Calldyr tells you Yarros's world uses Celtic-adjacent roots. Stick to your palette.

## The Two-Part Pattern

Most memorable place names have two elements.

- Descriptor + geography. *Blackthorn*, *Saltmarsh*, *Ironhold*, *Whitecliff*.
- Owner + feature. *Velaris* (the city of stars in ACOTAR, root-derived), *Rivenhold*.
- Character + site. *Tamir's Keep*, *Kallen's Spire*.
- Event + place. *Wolfsbane Ridge* (where a wolf was slain), *Traitor's Gate*.

Two-element names carry meaning without overexplaining. A reader who sees Ashmoor understands the place was burned once.

## The Cultural Marker

Different cultures in your world should sound different.

- Your fae court might use flowing, multi-syllable names (Aelwynne, Carrathiel).
- Your mortal kingdom might use blunt, single-syllable or compound names (Ashford, Holt).
- Your northern mountain people might use consonant-heavy, cold-sounding names (Skarnhold, Krynnfell).
- Your southern trading cities might use longer, trade-language names (Aldaressa, Calimshan).

The reader should be able to guess where a character is from by the sound of her home's name. That's a cultural marker doing work for free.

## The Name For Your Central City

Your heroine's home (or her love interest's, or the place the book spends most time) needs a name that lands.

Three tests.

- **Said aloud, does it sound good?** Some names read fine and speak badly. Yarros's Basgiath speaks well. Tolkien's Gondor is a masterclass in saying-aloud.
- **Is it short enough for the reader to hold?** More than three syllables and readers start skipping. (Exceptions exist, but they earn it.)
- **Does it distinguish from other fantasy places?** A name that's already well-known (Aragorn's city, Rhysand's city) muddles your book.

Don't overcook your central name. Short, strong, memorable.

## The Faction Or Court Names

If your book has named factions, courts, or houses, pick a naming pattern and hold it.

- Cardinal directions. (North, South, East, West; Dawn, Dusk; Sun, Moon; the courts of Night, Day, Spring, Autumn.)
- Element or material. (House Iron, House Silver, the Court of Ash.)
- Ancestor or founder. (House Velarion, the Line of Aeron.)
- Symbol or beast. (House of the Stag, the Ravenhold, the Order of the Wolf.)

Pick one pattern. Don't mix (the House of the Stag and the Night Court in the same book reads as borrowed).

## Names To Avoid

- Names that look like other fantasy names. If your queen is Daenerys, you have a problem.
- Names that require footnotes. (If the reader can't pronounce it, she'll skip it.)
- Names that sound like English words with accents (Lünd, Cîty). Diacritics are decorative; don't rely on them to make a name feel fantasy.
- Names borrowed without context from real-world cultures. Romantasy is starting to reckon with this. Do the research.

## Phoneme Palette Comparison

A quick comparison table to help you pick your sonic palette.

| Palette | Example Sounds | Subgenre Fit | Comp Titles |
|---|---|---|---|
| Soft / flowing | Velara, Aelwen, Silmaris | High fae, court romantasy | *ACOTAR*, *From Blood and Ash* |
| Hard / martial | Drak, Korn, Vrath, Nizgar | Dragon rider, warrior-coded | *Fourth Wing*, *The Jasad Heir* |
| Wet / Celtic | Moradon, Aelweth, Taliesin, Beuliach | Mystical, bond-heavy, old-magic | *Fourth Wing* (Basgiath, Calldyr), *Priory of the Orange Tree* |
| Dry / ancient | Achik, Qar, Thel, Mek | Desert, god-blood, mythic | *The Jasmine Throne*, *A Tempest of Tea* |
| Cold / sharp | Skarn, Isval, Krynn, Vornir | Northern, shifter, vampire | *From Blood and Ash* (some), *Frostblood* |

Pick one primary palette for your dominant culture. Pair a secondary palette with your opposing faction or visiting court. The contrast is free worldbuilding.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Name your continent (or the main region your book lives in). One word or two.

**[L1]** Name your couple's home places. One for her, one for him.

**[L1]** Pick your sonic palette. Soft, hard, wet, dry, cold, or a stated other. Commit.

**[L2]** Write the names of five places your book visits. For each, give a one-line sensory association.

**[L2]** Write the names of your factions, courts, or houses. Pick a pattern and hold it.

**[L3]** Read ten place names from your book aloud. Which ones flow? Which ones stumble? Rewrite the stumblers. A name you can't say is a name the reader will ghost-read.

**[L3]** Rename the single most important place in your book. Just try a different name. Live with it for a day. Does the original still feel right, or does the alternative reveal something? Sometimes the second try is the real one.

---

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros uses Celtic-adjacent roots consistently: Basgiath, Calldyr, Tyrrendor, Aretia. The phoneme palette is soft-wet, which gives the world a mythic feel that grounds the military grit. Her names for dragons (Tairn, Sgaeyl, Andarna) share the same phonetic family, which makes the world's languages feel connected.

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses a cardinal-direction plus seasonal pattern for her courts (Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter, Dawn, Day, Night). It's legible, scalable, and tells readers the structure of Prythian at a glance. Her city names (Velaris, the House of Wind) are soft-sound, lightly Latinate, and memorable. The naming convention is part of the reason the world feels mapped.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** The place your couple first kisses, first sleeps together, first fights: those names will end up in the book's most-quoted lines. Pick them carefully. A bad name in a love scene is a tonal break the reader notices even if she can't say why.
