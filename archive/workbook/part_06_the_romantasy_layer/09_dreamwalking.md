# 38. Dreamwalking And Shared Visions

Optional section. If your book doesn't use dream magic, skip to 39. If you're considering it, this section is short and useful.

A dream in romantasy is the private room of a bond. Unlike most magic, which is public and visible, a dream is a place only two people can be. That's why the genre keeps using them, and why some of the most intimate scenes in romantasy happen there.

## What A Shared Dream Gives You

A dreamwalking mechanic lets the story do three things it can't easily do anywhere else.

- **First meetings before meeting.** Your couple can know each other before they physically meet. Armentrout's heartmate dreams use this.
- **Emotional intimacy without physical proximity.** Two characters can talk, touch, and trust in a space where the world can't overhear. Useful when the plot forces them apart.
- **Information transfer.** A message, a warning, a confession can pass between them even when no letter could.

Dreams are also useful for villains. A dreamwalker in your antagonist column is a character who can reach your heroine even when she's locked in the safest room in the kingdom.

## Types Of Dream Magic

Pick one. Stacking types is usually worse than picking one and using it three times.

- **Fated dreams.** The pair is drawn to meet in dreams by the bond itself. Neither chooses. Often begins before they meet in waking life. Armentrout's heartmate is the genre anchor for this.
- **Deliberate entry.** One character, usually magically gifted, can choose to enter another's dreams. Consent becomes the key question. Is it visiting or invading?
- **Shared inheritance.** A family, lineage, or species shares dreams with each other. Ancestors visit, siblings meet in sleep, the whole line of descent is linked.
- **Seer dreams.** One or both partners receives prophecy in dreams, which may or may not be accurate. Used for plot tension and tragic irony.
- **Telepathic reach.** The bond's mental bridge extends into sleep, which means the pair can talk at night as easily as they do by day.
- **Dream-world as physical space.** The dream is a real place that magically manifests, often landscaped from both partners' memories. Can be revisited, built in, and lost.

## The Consent Question In Dreams

Dreams are one of the genre's sharpest consent scenes. A character entering another's dream without permission is trespass, whether or not anything sexual happens.

Three ways the genre handles it.

- **Explicit permission.** The dreamer consents before the walker enters. Cleanest, most consent-positive. Works for most modern romances.
- **Permission implicit in the bond.** The bond is read as consent to be reached. Trickier, because "the bond said yes" is the beginning of every consent conversation, not the end.
- **No permission, acknowledged cost.** The walker enters uninvited. The book treats this as a transgression the plot will pay for later. Valid for dark romance, never for sweet.

Consent inside the dream matters as much as consent to enter it. Anything that happens in a shared dream is happening to both people. Decide what counts, and be consistent.

## Dream Beats Worth Using

Recognisable scenes the genre has made great use of.

- **The dream where they first talk.** Before they meet in waking life. Safer, because dreams can be denied later.
- **The dream where he shows her his past.** A wound, a childhood room, a lost sibling. Accelerates intimacy without the physical risk.
- **The dream where she's in danger.** He wakes her. His waking body is already moving toward her before his eyes open.
- **The dream where one of them loses control.** Rage, lust, grief spills into a shared space that was meant to be safe. Worth payoff.
- **The dream they can't remember.** They spoke; both of them know they did; neither can bring the words back. Set up for mid-book revelation.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Does your book have shared dreams? If yes, who can enter whose?

**[L1]** How do shared dreams work in your world: fated, chosen, inherited, or something else?

**[L2]** What can happen in a dream that can't happen elsewhere in your world? Name three things.

**[L2]** What's the consent rule for entering another person's dream? Who decided it?

**[L3]** Write the first dream your couple shares. Where are they? Who arrives first? Does either of them know it's a dream?

**[L3]** Who in your world is famous for having died in a shared dream, or having come back changed? Your world has a story about this; write the one-line version.

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas uses dream magic selectively. Rhys first reaches Feyre through dream-connection while she is Under the Mountain, which establishes the mental bridge that becomes central to their bond. The dream-visits are always framed as acts of care and mercy; Maas never lets Rhys enter without narrative invitation. Worth studying for how carefully the consent is handled even before the bond is named.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros doesn't use shared dreams as a mechanic. Violet and Xaden communicate through the bond awake, not asleep. A reminder that dream magic is optional. If your bond does its work in waking scenes, you don't need a second venue for it.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** What does your couple know about each other that they only learned in a dream? That's the thing they'll be accused of lying about when the world finds out.
