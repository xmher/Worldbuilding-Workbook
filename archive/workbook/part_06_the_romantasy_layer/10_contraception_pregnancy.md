# 39. Contraception And Pregnancy In Magical Worlds

Romantasy handles contraception badly on average. The easy fix is to never mention it. The better fix is to decide what's available in your world, who can access it, and what that answer does to your plot.

This matters for three reasons. Readers are tracking it now. Pregnancy plots are rising in popularity. And if the genre keeps doing mating-heat and bonded-sex as central engines, it needs to answer the question of what happens afterward.

## What To Decide

At minimum, your world should have clear answers to these.

- **What contraception exists.** Herbal, magical, charmed object, ritual, technological, bond-regulated, or nothing at all.
- **Who has access.** Commoners? Nobles? Only the initiated? Only women? Only men?
- **How reliable it is.** Near-perfect, somewhat reliable, notoriously unreliable, or seasonal?
- **Who knows.** Is contraception common knowledge, a guild secret, a mother-daughter transmission, or forbidden?
- **What pregnancy costs.** Socially, magically, physically, politically.

You don't have to put these on the page. You do have to know them. A writer who knows these answers can write a sex scene that lands.

## Contraception Options By Flavour

Pick what matches your world.

- **Herbal teas, tonics, or charms.** Grounded and folkloric. Works in most fantasy worlds. Can be unreliable in ways the plot uses.
- **Pregnancy-blocking magic.** A minor spell, a charmed pendant, a ritual bath. Works in high-magic worlds.
- **Bond-regulated fertility.** Only bonded pairs can conceive. Unbonded sex is recreational. Works hard in fated-mate worlds where the bond is sacred.
- **Seasonal infertility.** Characters are only fertile in specific windows, tied to cycles in section 36. Works for worlds with heavy biology.
- **Nothing at all.** Every act of intercourse is potentially procreative, with predictable consequences. Useful for grounded, high-stakes, or historically-coded worlds.
- **Selective conception.** Partners choose together, magically, when to conceive. Very utopian, which is why almost no one writes it.

Your choice changes the weight of every sex scene in the book. A casual fling in a world without contraception isn't casual.

## Pregnancy As A Plot Engine

Pregnancy has always been a romance engine. In romantasy it's often also a political one.

- **Mate-pregnancy.** The bond deepens during pregnancy; the world treats the pregnant bonded mate differently.
- **Half-breed children.** The child's species matters. Succession, politics, and prejudice all run through the child's body.
- **Succession pressure.** A royal couple without an heir is a world-sized problem. A royal couple with a disputed heir is a civil war.
- **Secret pregnancy.** Classic. She's carrying his child; he doesn't know; the plot does.
- **Pregnancy as prophecy.** The child is foretold. Their mother is now important in ways she wasn't before.
- **Pregnancy as target.** Enemies will act to end it. Your couple has to protect more than themselves.

Pregnancy is a high-voltage trope. Use it because the book needs it, not because a subplot needed filling.

## Recovery, Birth, And After

Pregnancy ends, one way or another. What does the rest of the book look like then?

- **Recovery.** Weeks, months, or magical acceleration?
- **Bond effects.** Does the bond shift during and after pregnancy? Do both partners change?
- **Childcare.** Who raises the child? Wet-nurses, pack, the court, the parents?
- **Succession rights.** When does a child become heir, citizen, or pack member?

Don't write a pregnancy arc without knowing how the first month of the child's life goes. The reader will ask.

## Adoption, Surrogacy, Non-Biological Family

Not every couple in your world will have biological children. Not every couple will want to.

Decide:

- Is adoption socially recognised? Magically?
- Can bonded pairs share parenting without biological parenthood?
- What does your world do with children no one claims?
- Can same-sex couples have biological children through magic, ritual, or adoption, and how is each flavoured?

These questions matter for reader representation, and they matter for worldbuilding depth.

## Prompts

**[L1]** What contraception is available in your world? Write one sentence naming it.

**[L1]** Do your main couple use it? If yes, what method. If no, why not.

**[L2]** If your heroine becomes pregnant at any point in the book, does she tell him, and when? Does she tell anyone else first?

**[L2]** What does a noble pregnancy look like in your world, and how does it differ from a commoner's?

**[L3]** Who in your world runs the contraception supply chain? Is it a guild, a temple, a folk tradition, a black market? Who benefits, and who gets left out?

**[L3]** Write the scene where a healer tells your heroine what she needs to know about her own fertility, bond-linked or otherwise. Who the healer is, and how kind they are, tells you a lot about your world.

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* High Fae contraception is mentioned in passing (a tea that stops conception); the Fae have long pregnancies (centuries of slow life). Mating bonds deepen fertility during frenzy, but Maas keeps the mechanics vague. Nesta's pregnancy with Cassian in *A Court of Silver Flames* becomes a late-series plot because the child's magical inheritance puts her body at risk, and the book treats the question of bodily autonomy and non-disclosure with considerable care. Worth studying for how a late-series pregnancy can change the whole tone of a book.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Yarros doesn't make pregnancy a central plot through *Fourth Wing* or the early parts of the series. The Empyrean books are wartime-coded, and the soldiers aren't having children on the page. A reminder that you can run a multi-book romantasy without taking on pregnancy as a plot engine, if your setting gives you other engines.

---

> **Romance Leverage:** If your heroine became pregnant mid-book, how many people would want to control what happens next? That's the size of your Act Three.
