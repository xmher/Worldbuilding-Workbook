# 45. Consorts, Concubines, Courtly Structures

Some worlds have one slot for a romantic partner. Some worlds have several, with different names, different rights, and different social weight.

If your book has a queen-and-her-consorts, a king-and-his-paramour, or a High Lord with an official and an unofficial love, your world has a structure for that. Readers expect the structure to be named, rule-bound, and consequential.

## Why These Structures Exist

Formalised non-marriage romantic arrangements aren't a quirk of fantasy. They're a real response to real power imbalances, and they serve the genre for the same reasons they served history.

- **They separate love from dynasty.** A king marries for alliance; his heart may belong elsewhere. A consort structure lets the court accommodate both without pretending the marriage is more than it is.
- **They create visible gaps between rank and affection.** A formal spouse the ruler doesn't love, and a paramour they do, is a structural engine for tragedy and political intrigue.
- **They give the genre a rank the heroine can occupy that isn't queen.** Favourite, paramour, mistress, consort-of-the-left-hand. Each comes with its own powers and its own risks.
- **They complicate exit.** A paramour can't simply leave. The court and the relationship are tangled.

A world with a consort structure is a world where the heroine's question isn't only "does he love me?" It's "what does he officially owe me, what can he do for me, and what does the court think I am?"

## The Common Structures

Pick the ones your world uses. Stack carefully.

### Consort

A formal, often publicly recognised partner of a ruler, ranking below a spouse but above a casual liaison. May have titles, lands, a place at court, the right to bear legitimate children in some settings. *The Priory of the Orange Tree* and many fae-court romances draw on this.

### Concubine

A kept partner, often living within the ruler's household, with no independent rank but usually with legal protections. Historically present in Ottoman, Chinese, Mughal settings. In romantasy, often reworked into something closer to a consort with different aesthetics.

### Paramour

An acknowledged lover, outside the official marriage, without formal rank. A ruler's paramour may have enormous influence and no legal standing at all. Edward IV and Jane Shore. *Crescent City*'s court politics run on this kind of ambiguity.

### Courtesan

A professional of the court, typically educated and independently positioned, whose romantic and sexual life is part of their role. Venice, Heian Japan, and many fantasy reworkings. Different from concubine because the courtesan's power isn't granted by one patron.

### Royal Favourite

A person the ruler elevates, sexually and socially, without a formal title. Can be male or female. Often the target of court intrigue because the favourite's position depends entirely on the ruler's attention.

### Kept Lover

A partner supported financially and sexually, usually discreetly, often by a married noble. Below royal-favourite in rank but above casual. Common in fae court romances with Regency flavour.

### Left-Hand Marriage

A morganatic marriage: a ceremony that legitimises the union but not the inheritance. Children can't inherit the titled parent's rank. European courts used this for aristocrats marrying below them. Useful when your book wants the marriage scene without the dynastic consequence.

## What Each Structure Controls

Every structure answers the same questions, differently. List your answer for each:

- **Living arrangement.** Own household, ruler's household, separate wing of the palace, a gift estate?
- **Public visibility.** Acknowledged at court, seen on state occasions, never mentioned in writing?
- **Children.** Legitimate, legitimised, bastard-but-recognised, bastard-unrecognised?
- **Duration.** For life, until the ruler's marriage, at the ruler's pleasure, until dismissed?
- **Income.** Titled, pensioned, supported from personal funds, unpaid?
- **Protection on the ruler's death.** Safe, exiled, executed, ignored?
- **Right to refuse.** Can the consort say no, leave, demand release? Or is the position compulsory once accepted?

Your heroine's position in this structure is your plot. If she's the wife, the paramour's continued presence is a wound. If she's the paramour, the wife's existence is her ceiling. If she's the favourite, the next favourite is her death.

## Courtly Love As Its Own Thing

Courtly love is medieval Europe's contribution to the genre: a coded, often unconsummated relationship between a knight and a married noblewoman, conducted in public through gestures, tokens, and poetry.

Romantasy borrows the aesthetic more than the structure. If you're using courtly love:

- **It's visible.** The whole court knows.
- **It's ritualised.** Specific gestures, specific language, specific expected restraint.
- **It's often unconsummated.** The frustration is the point.
- **It coexists with the lady's marriage.** The husband is expected to tolerate, within limits.

Courtly love structures are useful when you want a long, public, publicly-watched romance before the couple's private break-through.

## The Consent Question, Tangled

Consort and concubine structures come with consent issues that the genre has to address head-on.

A person whose livelihood depends on staying in a ruler's bed can't refuse that bed on an ordinary day. Every "yes" in that structure is shadowed by "if I say no, what happens."

Three moves the better books make:

- **The heroine has outside leverage.** Money, family, magic, reputation. Her "yes" isn't a last-resort yes; it's a chosen one.
- **The ruler removes the pressure.** He frees her, settles her independently, makes clear she can leave. Only then does the relationship continue on grounds the reader can trust.
- **The book acknowledges the imbalance.** Characters name it. The structure isn't treated as neutral. The romance works *against* the structure, not with it.

A book that uses consort structures without examining the consent question is a book the genre's thoughtful readers will put down. Section 48 closes this.

## Prompts

**[L1]** Does your world have formal non-marriage romantic roles? List them.

**[L1]** What does each role control: residence, income, children, protection? Fill in one line per role.

**[L2]** Is your heroine in one of these roles? Or is she the spouse while someone else occupies one? Which role has more power in your world's culture?

**[L2]** How does a person leave their role? By death, by the ruler's pleasure, by negotiated release, by their own choice? The exit tells the reader how real the consent was.

**[L3]** Write the document that formalises your heroine's role: the court record, the settlement, the letter. What does it promise her? What does it carefully not promise?

**[L3]** Who in your world rose from one role to another? (Favourite to consort, concubine to queen, paramour to left-hand wife.) What did that climb cost them, and what do people still say about them?

---

*Case study: ACOTAR.* Maas doesn't formalise consort structures inside the mated-pair romance; Feyre and Rhys are the unambiguous pair. But the Night Court's earlier history, the mortal queens of the continent, and the politics of the Court of Nightmares all draw on courtly structures in flashes. Worth reading for the atmospheric work: the presence of a formal court around the central pair makes the central pair matter more.

*Case study: Fourth Wing.* Navarre is wartime. Yarros keeps her romantic structures lean. There are no consorts, no court paramours, no formal second-rank relationships. Everyone who matters in Violet's bed is either her partner or absent. Efficient for a military book. Would not be efficient for a court book.
