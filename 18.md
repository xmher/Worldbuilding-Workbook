# SECTION 18: The Agency Check

The most powerful romantasy stories are those where characters choose each other despite everything — not because magic forces them, not because prophecy demands it, not because they have no other option. The worldbuilding creates pressure, obstacles, and opportunities, but at the end of the day, the characters must have agency.

Agency means the ability to make meaningful choices. In romance, this is crucial: readers need to believe that these two people genuinely want each other, not that they're biologically compelled, magically bound without consent, or politically trapped with no alternatives.

But here's the challenge: romantasy often includes elements that seem to override choice. Fated mates. Prophecies. Magical bonds. Arranged marriages. These are popular tropes because they create instant stakes and force proximity — but they can accidentally strip characters of agency if you're not careful.

This section helps you test whether your worldbuilding supports character agency or undermines it. You've already explored bonds in Section 11 (Magic & Intimacy), prophecy in Section 6 (Religion, Myth & Prophecy), and forced proximity in Section 13 (Pressure Cooker). This is where you pull all of those threads together and stress-test them. Those sections helped you design your systems. This one helps you check whether they're working.

---

## A Note Before You Begin

**Agency doesn't mean easy choices** — characters can face impossible situations and still have agency, as long as they're genuinely choosing rather than being compelled. **Tropes aren't inherently agency-violating** — fated mates, magical bonds, and arranged marriages can all support agency if handled carefully. **The question isn't "are they free?" but "do they choose?"** Characters can be constrained by circumstances and still actively choose each other within those constraints.

---

## Understanding the Agency Paradox

In romantasy, you often want two contradictory things:

**You want:** The relationship to feel destined, inevitable, cosmically significant

**You also want:** The characters to freely choose each other

The best romantasy makes the answer: **"Both."**

The magic/fate/world pushes them together, but they still have to choose to stay. The bond is real, but they have to decide whether to accept it. The prophecy says they're meant to be, but they could still walk away — and what makes the story powerful is that they don't.

This is what separates the romances that stick with readers from the ones that feel hollow. In *A Court of Mist and Fury*, Rhysand knows about the mating bond long before Feyre does, but he never uses it to force her hand. He waits. He lets her come to him on her own terms, even when it costs him. The bond exists, but what makes readers swoon is his choice to respect her agency within it. In *Fourth Wing*, the dragon bond between Violet and Xaden creates a telepathic link neither of them chose, but their relationship isn't the bond. It's what they decide to do with it, how they navigate trust and secrets and vulnerability despite the magical connection that already exists.

**The key question:** If you removed all external pressure, would they still choose each other?

If the answer is no, you have an agency problem.

### Writing Choice on the Page: Craft Techniques

Knowing your characters have agency isn't enough. You have to make the reader see it in the prose itself. A choice moment that works has three stages: the catalyst that forces a decision, the deliberation where the character weighs alternatives, and the commitment where they act.

**The deliberation is where most writers stumble.** If a character goes straight from "the bond pulled her toward him" to "she was in his arms," there's no evidence of choice. The reader needs to hear the character think, even briefly. What are they giving up? What's the easier path? Why are they choosing this one anyway?

**Watch your sentence construction.** Agency lives in who holds the verb. Compare:

*The bond pulled her toward him. She was overwhelmed by the intensity of it. Before she knew it, she was pressed against his chest.*

In those sentences, the bond is the agent. The character is an object being moved around by magic. Now compare:

*The bond flared, a hot tether in her chest. She gripped the back of the chair and breathed through it, waiting until the instinctual fog cleared. He was still the man who had lied to her. She weighed the lie against the roar in her blood, and chose to stay exactly where she was. "You're going to have to do better than a magical pull," she said.*

Same bond. Same pull. But the character is the one holding the verbs: she gripped, she breathed, she weighed, she chose, she said. The bond is a sensation she evaluates, not a force that moves her.

**Three common prose-level mistakes that strip agency:**

**Filter words that create distance.** "She felt drawn to him" places a buffer between the reader and the action. "The pull was there, insistent and warm, but she didn't move" puts the character in direct contact with the sensation and shows her choosing her response.

**Skipping the deliberation entirely.** If the character never considers alternatives, their actions look like plot points rather than choices. Even one sentence of internal weighing changes everything: *She could leave. Vivi was waiting at the border. But leaving meant abandoning the artifacts, and she wasn't done yet.*

**Making the bond the subject of every sentence.** When the prose describes the bond's effects for paragraph after paragraph without the character responding, evaluating, or pushing back, the bond becomes the protagonist of the scene. Alternate between what the bond does and what the character does about it. The character's response is where agency lives.

**A quick diagnostic:** If you replaced your protagonist with a lamp in a key romantic scene, would the scene unfold the same way? If the love interest could have the same interaction with an inanimate object, your character isn't driving the moment.

### The Consent Spectrum

**Highest agency:** Bond can be rejected with no consequences. They choose to accept it.

**High agency:** Bond can be rejected at moderate cost. They weigh options and choose.

**Moderate agency:** Bond can be rejected at significant cost. Choice is hard but real.

**Low agency:** Bond cannot be rejected without death or destruction. No choice about the bond, but choice remains in how they live within it.

**No agency:** Bond controls feelings and actions. They cannot choose differently. (This is non-consensual and problematic unless the story is explicitly about breaking free.)

## Agency in Cultural Context

What agency looks like depends on the world you've built.

(Reference: Section 3 — Culture; Section 5 — Government)

Not every world offers the same freedoms, and that's not a flaw in your worldbuilding. A character in a rigidly hierarchical society who whispers their love interest's first name instead of their title is making an act of defiance as powerful as any dramatic declaration in a freer world. The key is calibrating agency to your setting.

## The Compulsion Spectrum: Deciding What Your Bond Does

Before you repair anything, you need to understand what you're aiming for. Not every romantasy romance needs the same level of agency, and not every reader wants the same thing. Fated mates readers pick up these books *because* they want the pull, the inevitability, the cosmic confirmation that these two belong together. The question isn't whether to include compulsion. It's how much, and how your characters respond to it.

The bond in your story will fall somewhere on this spectrum:

**Full compulsion:** The bond is irresistible. The characters can't stay away from each other regardless of logic. This creates a primal, visceral romance with high physical intensity. The reader satisfaction comes from watching characters fight a losing battle against something bigger than them. The agency risk is high, so the characters need strong identities and goals outside the bond to avoid feeling like puppets. This works best in dark romantasy or enemies-to-lovers where the tension comes from *not wanting* to want someone.

**Choice within fate:** The bond identifies a compatible match, but the characters still have to build the relationship. Fate brought them together; they choose to stay. This is the most common approach in popular romantasy because it delivers both the swoony inevitability and the earned connection. In *ACOMAF*, the mating bond is real, but Rhysand waits months to reveal it so Feyre can choose him for who he is, not what the Cauldron decided. The bond confirms what's already growing. This works best when the heroine's autonomy is a central theme of the story.

**Fate as conflict:** The bond is a burden, a complication, or even a curse. The characters actively fight against the predestination. This creates maximum angst and provides a platform for characters to prioritise their own judgement over magical decree. In *Zodiac Academy*, Tory Vega rejects her Elysian Mate bond with Darius Acrux during the "Divine Moment" beneath the stars, knowing it will curse her to never find love again. She says no because he bullied her, because she refuses to let magic erase what he did. The stars confirm they're mates, but she chooses her own dignity over the cosmic reward. That rejection is one of the most discussed agency moments in the genre. This approach works best in star-crossed arcs or when destiny itself is the antagonist.

**Where does your bond sit on this spectrum?**

*Knowing your position helps you calibrate how much agency work your story needs. Full compulsion requires the most; choice within fate requires moderate work; fate as conflict builds agency into the premise.*

_______________________________________________________________

**Which does your target reader want?**

Some readers crave the "safe harbour" of compulsion: the certainty that this person is *theirs*, permanently and irrevocably. Others want the "defiant stand" of agency: the heroine who earns her love through choice, not fate. Most readers want both, which is why the middle of the spectrum is so popular. Know your audience and tune your worldbuilding accordingly.

### The Acceptance Ritual

One of the most effective techniques for preserving agency within a fated bond is to build a specific, voluntary action into your worldbuilding that "seals" the bond. This transforms the bond from something that happens to the characters into something they choose to do.

In *ACOMAF*, Maas uses a cultural tradition: a female offering food to her mate. In the famous soup scene, Feyre heats a bowl of soup while Rhysand tells her his side of the story. She explicitly makes the offering conditional: she'll decide whether to give him the bowl after he finishes talking. The acceptance of the bond becomes a conscious, informed decision performed through a mundane domestic act. The swoony satisfaction comes from Rhysand's years of devotion; the agency comes from Feyre's refusal to accept it until she understands the full context.

In *Zodiac Academy*, the "Divine Moment" requires active acceptance under the stars. In *From Blood and Ash*, the "Joining" is a ritual that requires the active consent of all participants, expanding the concept of bonds beyond a simple pair.

**Does your world have a ritual, action, or declaration that "seals" the bond?**

*If yes, describe it. If no, consider creating one. An acceptance ritual is one of the simplest, most effective ways to add a visible moment of choice to a fated bond.*

## The Agency Repair Kit

If your removal tests or checklist revealed problems, here's where to fix them. Each repair preserves the trope while restoring character choice.

### If the magical bond is doing too much:

- **Add friction:** Even with a bond, they don't automatically like each other. They have to choose to work with it. The bond in *ACOTAR* creates pull, but Feyre and Rhysand still have to navigate trust, communication, and the messy reality of two complicated people learning to be together.
- **Delay the reveal:** Have one character know about the bond first and choose to hide it so the other can develop genuine feelings without magical pressure. This is the Rhysand model: he waits because he values Feyre's autonomy more than his own desire to claim the bond.
- **Add cost to acceptance:** Accepting the bond means giving something up. The choice has weight.
- **Build an acceptance ritual:** Create a specific, voluntary action that seals the bond (a vow, an offering, a ritual) so the bond isn't complete until both characters choose it.
- **Show choosing within the bond:** They control whether to act on it, how they treat each other, what kind of relationship they build.

### If prophecy is removing agency:

- **Make it vague:** Prophecy says they're connected but not how or what it means.
- **Make it resistible:** They can fight it. Maybe it's even wrong. Prophecy is suggestion, not command.
- **Make it a late reveal:** They fall in love first, then discover prophecy validates what already happened.

### If political pressure is removing agency:

- **Give them the option to escape — and show them choosing to stay** for their own reasons. The exit door needs to be visible so that staying is clearly a choice.
- **Show them making the arrangement meaningful:** It started political; they chose to make it real.
- **Add genuine choice within the forced situation:** How they treat each other is still their choice.

### If power imbalance is removing agency:

- **Give the less powerful character leverage** — something the powerful one needs. Not charity from above, but genuine power the protagonist earns or possesses independently.
- **Show the powerful character restraining their power** — choosing not to use it. In *ACOMAF*, Rhysand has the power to compel Feyre through their bargain, but he consistently chooses not to. His restraint is a form of respect that readers recognise.
- **Address the imbalance explicitly:** Characters acknowledge it and actively work to create equality.

### If the dangerous situation is the only glue:

- **Build emotional intimacy between action beats.** Don't let every conversation be about strategy or survival. Give them quiet moments where they connect as people, not just allies.
- **Show them choosing connection when survival doesn't require it.** If they only interact when danger forces it, the relationship will feel transactional.
- **Plant a peacetime moment.** Even a brief scene where they imagine or experience life without danger, and still want to be together, proves the connection runs deeper than adrenaline.

## The Agency Checklist

For strong character agency, you should be able to check most of these:

☐ If I removed all magical bonds/prophecy, they'd still be attracted to each other

☐ Both characters have goals and identity outside the romance

☐ There's a specific moment where each actively chooses the other

☐ This choice costs them something significant

☐ Both characters can (and do) say "no" to each other and have it respected

☐ The less powerful character has meaningful agency within the power dynamic

☐ They have reasons to choose each other beyond "fate says so"

☐ Both resist the relationship at some point before choosing it

☐ Alternatives exist that they consciously reject

☐ Readers can see the choice on the page (not just author intention)

☐ The ending is shaped by their choices, not delivered by plot convenience

5-7 checked: Moderate — some elements need strengthening
0-4 checked: Agency problems — worldbuilding may be overriding genuine choice

## Final Thought: Choice Is What Makes It Love

Magic can bring them together. Prophecy can suggest they're meant to be. Political pressure can force proximity. Danger can create intimacy.

But at the end of the story, they need to choose each other freely — despite the cost, despite the alternatives, despite the easier paths available, because of who they are and what they've discovered in each other.

That choice is what makes the romance feel earned.

That choice is what separates "they ended up together" from "they chose each other."

That choice is what makes it love.
