# How to Use This Workbook

This is a worldbuilding workbook, so most of what's inside is about building a world: its lands, its peoples, its magic, its history, the way power and faith and daily life all fit together. That comes first, because a romantasy still needs a world that holds up on its own. Readers can tell when a setting is just a backdrop for the love story, and it pulls them out of it.

But you're writing romantasy, not straight fantasy, so as you build we'll also look at how the world you're making can put pressure on your romance. A border that keeps your leads apart, a law that forbids them, a magic that forces them together. These prompts are marked so you can spot them, and you can skip them whenever they don't fit your story. They're there when the world naturally creates something for your romance to push against, not as a box to tick on every page.

One quick way to know if you're in romantasy territory: if you could take out either the romance or the fantasy and still have a whole story, you're probably not there yet. In romantasy both carry weight, and each should be harder to imagine without the other.

A word on how much to do. You don't need to fill in every page. Readers want a world they can sink into for a few hundred pages, not an encyclopaedia, and you only need to build enough to make your scenes feel real. Some sections will matter enormously to your story and some won't matter at all, and the ones that don't, you can skip without a second thought. Work in whatever order suits you, and change any answer later. Nothing here is fixed.

# Showing, Telling & Choosing What Matters

Here's a thing worth knowing before you start, because it changes how you'll use everything that follows.

This workbook is where you explain your world to yourself. You'll write down that magic costs a memory, that a certain love is forbidden, that one people looks down on another, that a kingdom fears the sea. Those are facts, and facts are useful. They help you understand the world before you ask a reader to believe in it.

But your reader doesn't need all your facts. They need the right details at the right time.

Sometimes you'll **show** a fact, because the reader needs to feel it. If magic costs memory, they might see a woman return from a spell and fail to recognise her own sister.

Sometimes you'll **tell** it, because the reader simply needs to understand it and move on.

Sometimes you'll **summarize** it, because weeks of travel, court lessons, or magical training do not need to happen moment by moment.

Sometimes you'll **imply** it, because a room going silent can teach a taboo better than a paragraph of explanation.

Sometimes you'll **delay** it, because the reader does not need the full answer yet.

And sometimes you'll **cut** it, because you built it for yourself, not for the page.

So when this workbook asks you to "show" something, it does not mean you need to dramatize everything. It means: choose one detail that matters. One image. One reaction. One gesture. One cost. One moment where the world presses on a person.

The goal is not to show everything. The goal is to know what deserves the spotlight.

### The Six-Tool Compass: What Should I Do With This Detail?

You don't have to memorise this. It's here as permission, not homework. Whenever you've decided a piece of your world and you're not sure what to do with it, you have six choices, not one:

| Tool | Use it when… |
|------|--------------|
| **Show** | The reader needs to *feel* the moment. |
| **Tell** | The reader needs clear information quickly. |
| **Summarize** | Time passes, routines repeat, or logistics need compressing. |
| **Imply** | The reader can understand through context, reaction, silence, or behaviour. |
| **Delay** | The reader does not need the full answer yet. |
| **Cut** | The detail does not affect character, romance, plot, tension, atmosphere, stakes, or theme. |

Here's how that works in practice. Take one fact — *the royal court forbids magic in public* — and watch how each tool turns it into something different:

> **The fact:** The royal court forbids magic in public.
>
> - **Show** — A servant drops a tray when a candle lights by itself.
> - **Tell** — Public magic had been illegal in the capital for fifty years.
> - **Summarize** — For three weeks, she kept her magic hidden through dinners, audiences, and court ceremonies.
> - **Imply** — Every chandelier in the palace was lit by hand. No one mentioned why.
> - **Delay** — Let the reader watch people panic at a small spark of magic, and learn the law only later.
> - **Cut** — The exact legal history of the ban stays in your notes, unless it ever affects the story.

Same fact, six different jobs. None of them is more "correct" than the others; it depends entirely on what your story needs at that moment.

Showing is wonderful, but it's only one of the six, and it's not always the right one. A great deal of beautiful writing is clean telling, brisk summary, and quiet implication. The skill isn't showing more, it's choosing well.

### The prompts in this workbook

Every so often you'll meet a prompt that asks you to practise turning one fact into the right kind of story moment, so the habit starts now rather than on a blank first chapter. They're marked so you'll know them, and they're all optional, every time:

**Show it** asks you to turn one detail of your world into a small concrete image. Not a scene, not your characters, just proof the thing is real. If you've decided magic is feared, showing it might be as small as a mother pulling her child back from a robed stranger in the market. One image that means more than the word "feared" ever could.

**Try a scene** is rarer, and it shows up only on the romantic moments that genuinely want it. Here you sketch a few lines between two people. To show you what that looks like without inventing your couple for you, the examples use a sample pair: a smuggler who runs cargo into a failing realm of the dead, and the buyer she's traded with for years through go-betweens, who turns out to be the one bound to her when an old law catches them both. You'll see them turn up now and then. Borrow the shape, not the couple.

**Tell it cleanly**, **Summarize it**, **Imply it**, and **Cut or keep?** are the other four tools, and you'll meet them where the world risks getting over-built. They're just as valuable as showing. A clean sentence of telling, three sentences of summary, an implied rule, or a fact moved to your private notes can do more for your story than a scene it didn't need.

**Choose the tool** turns the whole thing over to you. It hands you one fact and asks which of the six tools it deserves. By the end of the workbook that question should feel natural, because it's the real skill underneath all the others.

All of them are optional. Skip any of them whenever you'd rather just get the fact down and move on. They're here to build a muscle, not to slow you down.

# The Big Picture

This first section is quick. It sketches out the basics of your world so the rest of the workbook has something to build on. You'll figure out your magic, your map, and your politics in proper detail later, but a few core decisions now will make all of those easier.

You can change any of your answers later. Nothing here is locked in. This is just a starting point.

### Your World in a Sentence

Most worlds you remember can be summed up in a single line. A war college where students ride dragons and the wash-outs don't survive. A faerie court where every favour is a debt and every debt has teeth. You don't need your plot or your characters yet, just the core idea everything else hangs off.

### Does Magic Exist Here?

You'll build your full magic system in its own section, so don't worry about rules or limits yet. For now there's just one thing to settle, because the answer shapes almost everything else: is there magic in this world, and what does it look like in a sentence?

It might be common enough that everyone uses a little, or so rare that the few who have it are feared. It might come from bloodlines, or the land, or deals with something better left alone. The reason this matters now is that if magic exists, your peoples, your history, and your rulers all have to account for it, so it's better to know it's there before you build them.

_______________________________________________________________

### What Keeps Your Leads Apart

It's early for romance questions, but this is the one worth asking now, while the world is still taking shape. Most romantasy runs on a single source of friction between the leads: a war, a law, a difference in rank or power, a promise one of them already made to someone else. Often that friction is also the most interesting thing about the world itself.

You don't need your couple yet, or even their names. If you already know what stands between them, write it down. If you don't, leave this blank. It tends to show up on its own as the world fills in, and there's a whole section at the end to dig it out properly.

_______________________________________________________________

### What You're Not Building

Not every story needs deep politics, a complicated magic system, a dozen feuding kingdoms, or a thousand years of backstory. Plenty of brilliant romantasy takes place in one isolated castle with two people and a secret. Deciding what your story doesn't need is just as useful as deciding what it does, and it'll stop you filling in pages of this workbook out of obligation rather than because your story actually calls for them.

So before you go further: what can your story happily do without?

# Lands & Geography

Geography is the ground everything else sits on, which makes it a good place to start. Where a land is hot or frozen, cut off by mountains or open to the sea, rich in one thing and short of another, all of that shapes who lives there, how they live, and who they've been fighting for the last three hundred years. You don't need to map every river or name every hill. You need enough of a sense of the place that the rest of your world has somewhere to happen.

A note before you start: you won't need every question here, and you shouldn't force answers you don't have yet. Work through the parts that matter to your story and leave the rest. You can always come back.

### Climate and Terrain

The land people live on shapes nearly everything about them: what they eat, what they wear, what they build, what they fear, even what they pray for. Getting a feel for it now keeps your world consistent later, and gives you a well of specific details to reach for when you're writing scenes. You don't need to answer all of these for every region. Do it properly for the one or two places your story actually spends time in.

### Naming Places

A place name does a lot of quiet work. Before a reader knows anything else about a city, its name has already told them something about who lives there. Harsh, consonant-heavy names feel different from soft, flowing ones, and a reader picks all of that up without thinking about it. So the first thing to decide isn't what your names mean, it's how they should sound.

What feeling do you want your world's names to give? Pick a few words that describe the sound you're after (jagged, lilting, ancient, plain, grand) before you invent a single name.

### Resources and Trade

What a land has, and what it can't get for itself, drives a surprising amount of a story. A region rich in iron makes weapons and wins wars. One that depends on a single spice or dye gets wealthy and gets invaded. Somewhere with no fresh water lives and dies by who controls the wells. Trade follows need, and conflict tends to follow trade.

### Borders and Barriers

The things that divide your world are often more interesting than the things in it. A mountain range, a haunted forest, a sea no ship comes back from, a wall built to keep something out. Barriers decide who can reach whom, and that decides who trades, who marries, who goes to war, and who has never so much as met.

Sketch your version: someone near the barrier, telling you with their body what it means. Keep this small: one image, one reaction, one gesture, or one line of dialogue. The reverse works just as well: somewhere they can't leave, a pass closed by snow, a ship mid-voyage, a city under siege, forces two people together whether they want it or not. Sometimes it's the same place doing both at once, cutting them off from everyone else while trapping them with each other.

### Places That Force Them Together

Geography keeps people apart, but it can also lock them in. The trap might be a ship's hold for the length of a long voyage, a mountain pass closed by the season's first snow, a siege that won't break for weeks, a court where two people are seated next to each other across months of formal dinners, or a bargain that ties both of them to the same isolated keep until it's paid. Whatever its shape, the world refuses to let them get away from each other, and that's where some of the genre's most charged scenes live.

A pressure-cooker setting does two things at once. It strips away the rest of the world, so your leads have only each other to talk to, and it makes leaving look harder than staying, which buys you the slow burn the genre runs on.

Is there a place or situation in your world that could lock your leads together for a stretch of time, with no easy way out?
_______________________________________________________________

What forces the trap, and what keeps it shut? It might be weather, war, magic, law, a bargain neither can break, an oath, or a duty. The reader needs to feel that walking away isn't an option, not just that the characters are choosing not to take it.
_______________________________________________________________

How long does it hold them, and what changes between them in that time?
_______________________________________________________________

`[R]` Every pressure-cooker setting has a moment where one of them quietly hopes the trap holds a little longer. What does each of your leads secretly want this confinement to give them, even while pretending they want out?

### Do You Need a Map?

No. Plenty of good books were written without one, and a map can quietly become a way of avoiding the actual writing. That said, some people think better with a picture in front of them, and a rough sketch helps you keep travel times honest so nobody crosses a continent in a weekend.

If you want one, draw it by hand or use a tool like Inkarnate or Azgaar's Fantasy Map Generator. Either way, start with the places that matter to your story rather than the coastline. Mark where your characters live, where they're forbidden to go, where the border between two homelands falls, and where the scenes you're most excited to write take place. # Magic

Magic is often the heart of a romantasy world, and it's where a lot of your romance can live too. But here's the thing most new writers get backwards: magic gets more interesting the more limits you put on it, not less. If your characters can solve any problem with a wave of the hand, there's no tension, and tension is the whole game. A magic system doesn't need to be complicated. It needs to have a cost.

It's easy to fall down a hole here, inventing a hundred spells and elaborate rules nobody will ever read. You don't need that. You need a handful of rules that matter, that you understand well enough to play fair with. A good test: the more your magic solves your characters' problems, the more your reader needs to understand how it works. If magic is just atmosphere, you can leave it mysterious.

### Where Magic Comes From

Start with the source, because it shapes everything else: who has magic, who doesn't, and why.

### Magic That Pulls People Together or Drives Them Apart

`[R]` This is where magic becomes romance. The same system you built above can do enormous work on your love story, if you let it. A few of the levers that work especially well:

- Magic that responds to emotion, so a character's feelings give them away or spill over when they least want them to.
- Magic that's shared or passed between two people, so using it means trusting each other, touching, being vulnerable.
- Magic that's forbidden, so every use is a risk taken together and a secret kept.
- Magic that needs physical contact to work, so every spell becomes a reason to be close.

Looking at the magic you've built, which of these could it do? And how might it force your leads together, or split them apart?
_______________________________________________________________

A bond is one of the most powerful tools romantasy has, so it's worth its own thought. A bond is a magical link between two people: a tie that lets them feel each other, share dreams, sense danger, sometimes against their will. It creates instant, undeniable intimacy and raises the question the whole genre loves: is this real, or is it just the magic?

If there's a bond between your leads, what does it actually do, and can they resist it or shut it out?
_______________________________________________________________

Did your leads choose this bond, or was it forced on them by blood, fate, or someone else's spell? A bond two people walk into willingly carries a different ache from one neither of them asked for.
_______________________________________________________________

# History

Your world didn't start the day your story does. It has a past, and that past is what makes it feel real instead of like a stage set. The good news: you don't need centuries of kings and dates. You need the handful of things from the past that still press on the present, the old wounds people haven't forgiven and the events they can't stop arguing about.

Think of history as the reason things are the way they are now. Every grudge between your peoples, every law that seems strange, every ruin your characters walk past, all of it came from somewhere.

### The Shape of the Past

Don't build a timeline. Build a short list of the events that still matter. A war, a betrayal, a golden age that ended, a disaster, the day the magic changed. A few big ones are plenty.

List the handful of past events that still shape your world today, roughly when each happened, and why it still matters.

# Religion & Belief

What people believe shapes how they live, marry, mourn, and judge each other, even the ones who don't believe at all. You don't need to invent a full theology with scripture and saints. # Finding the Friction

You've built a world. Now comes the part that makes this a romantasy workbook and not just a fantasy one: looking back at everything you've made and finding the pressure already sitting inside it.

# Turning Your World Into Story

You've built a world and found the friction inside it. This last section is about the hand-off: how all those facts in all those boxes become a story a reader actually feels. Remember the very first thing in this workbook — the six tools, and the rule underneath them. *The goal is not to show everything. It's to know what deserves the spotlight.* Here's how to make that call once you're actually writing.

### The Worldbuilding Page-Space Test

Worldbuilding earns space on the page when it changes something for the reader. So for any detail you're tempted to put in, ask one question:

**Does this detail change emotion, conflict, choice, romance, or consequence?**

- If **yes**, it may deserve space. Slow down and let the reader feel it.
- If **no**, you can still tell it quickly, summarize it, imply it, delay it, or cut it.

Inventing a detail doesn't entitle it to page space. A useful tell: if deleting a piece of worldbuilding costs the story nothing, cutting it doesn't lose you anything — it gives you momentum. You built more world than you'll use, and that's exactly right. The rest lives in your notes.

### The Narrative Camera

Think of your attention as a camera the reader looks through. Most of writing is just deciding where to point it and when to move:

- **Zoom in** for the moments that matter — the first touch, the broken vow, the spell that costs something. These you show.
- **Zoom out** for the connective tissue — travel, weeks passing, routine, court logistics. These you tell or summarize.
- **Move on** when a detail has done its job. Don't linger because it was hard to invent.
- **Cut** what only exists because you invented it, not because the story needs it.

A lush world isn't a slow one. Immersive doesn't mean exhaustive. The camera that lingers on everything ends up emphasising nothing.

This is where it all gets concrete. Take one paragraph from your draft, or write a short sample paragraph about your world right now — three or four sentences will do.
