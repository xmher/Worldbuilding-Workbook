# The state of romantasy in 2026: a genre at war with itself

Romantasy has become publishing's most lucrative and most scrutinized genre, and the cracks in its worldbuilding are now louder than its bestseller numbers. **The subgenre now outsells traditional fantasy in adult fiction**, yet its flagship titles — *Fourth Wing*, *A Court of Thorns and Roses*, *From Blood and Ash*, *Haunting Adeline*, *The Plated Prisoner* — face increasingly pointed critiques about logic holes, thin worlds, consent, and representation. The contradiction is the story: readers who once forgave the flimsy geography and infodumps in exchange for dragons and mate bonds are now, in 2026, sharper consumers who can love a book and still gleefully dissect it on TikTok. BookTok created this market; BookTok is also auditing it. What emerges is a genre that earns hundreds of millions of dollars while quietly teaching a generation of readers to read more critically — and asking whether romantasy can grow up without losing the feverish emotional appeal that made it huge.

## The worldbuilding feels like Wikipedia with a map

The most consistent complaint across reader communities is that flagship romantasies **borrow geography and culture rather than invent them**. Sarah J. Maas's Prythian is a near-identical tracing of Britain, with Hybern standing in for Ireland (from "Hibernia") and the Night Court functioning as a moody nighttime Switzerland. Critics note that Maas lifts Welsh, Latin, and Celtic references as a sheen of "Authentic Historical Legitimacy" without the follow-through; one widely shared essay argues Hybern's "pale, ruddy, soulless-eyed" fae echo 17th- and 18th-century anti-Irish propaganda, likely by accident rather than intent, but echo it nonetheless. The pattern is what critic Ellen Jacob calls **"a larger pattern of uncritical appropriation in fantasy"** — cultures mined as aesthetic banks rather than imagined communities.

*From Blood and Ash* takes the same shortcut more openly: place names like Masadonia, Atlantia, and Pompay read as vowel-swapped Mediterranean real estate, and character names (Penellaphe, Hawke, Vikter) come across to many readers as random-generator outputs. The result is a world that feels decorative rather than load-bearing. When pressed on what the Kingdom of Solis actually looks like beyond "a big wall called the Rise" and a castle, readers struggle to answer — one audiobook reviewer noted she could describe almost no geography after hours of listening.

*Fourth Wing*'s Navarre has the opposite problem: **its internal logic collapses under gentle pressure.** Why would a military dictatorship force the orphaned children of executed rebels (the "marked ones") into elite dragon-riding positions at the heart of its army? Screen Rant and forum critics call it "an obvious recipe for disaster" that exists solely to set up the rebellion subplot. Readers on fantasy forums also find the pacing disingenuous: tension is repeatedly defused by Violet reciting exposition, and the 500-plus-page runtime balloons further in *Iron Flame* and *Onyx Storm* without the world getting denser.

## The infodump has become a defining stylistic tic

Across the most commercially dominant titles, the opening chapters operate as lore dumps rather than scenes. Reviewers of *From Blood and Ash* describe paragraphs "long and winding, peppered with confusing details of myths and lore" that make readers question what Ascension is, why eyes turn black, and why the villain is called "the Dark One" (a name that, in Spanish translation, literally matches the Darkling from the Grishaverse). Jennifer L. Armentrout's prose is also criticized for **repetition** — one reader counted the phrase "my heart skipped a beat" roughly 41 times in a single book, and the character Poppy's constant rambling questions become a running joke ("no one is surprised she has a question").

Sarah A. Parker's *When the Moon Hatched*, the 2024 breakout that sold into 2026 with a delayed sequel (*The Ballad of Falling Dragons*, February 2026), faces the inverse criticism: **prose so ornate it stops meaning things.** Viral Goodreads takedowns skewer lines like "a smoky musk pinched with the smell of freshly split stone, softened with notes of something buttery" and "her eyes hardening like molten ore dropped in a bed of snow," alongside jarring tonal shifts between fantasy register ("discreet swell of her babe-laden abdomen") and Twitter-speak ("I see you, asshole"). The cleverness is doing work the worldbuilding hasn't earned.

## Consent is the discourse that won't die

The genre's most heated ongoing debate is **how romantasy depicts consent**, and the frontier is dark romantasy and dark romance. H.D. Carlton's *Haunting Adeline* is the case study. The author's trigger warnings originally labeled graphic sexual assault scenes as "consensual non-consent" before public pressure forced amendment; the book remains massively popular on BookTok while drawing essays from library blogs, morbidly-beautiful.com, and WordPress critics arguing it "romanticizes the justification of sexual assault" via a stalker antihero who dismantles trafficking rings by day and forces his love interest by night. A 2014 *Journal of Women's Health* study on *Fifty Shades* readers — cited often in the current discourse — found correlations between exposure and acceptance of stalking and coercion in real relationships, giving the argument empirical teeth beyond aesthetic objection.

Raven Kennedy's *Plated Prisoner* series triggers a softer version of the same fight. Book one, *Gild*, contains graphic sexual assault and an extended grooming/Stockholm dynamic between Auren and King Midas that readers either frame as **an intentional critique the protagonist grows out of** or as exploitation dressed as empowerment. The series' own trajectory — with fans reporting the quality declined after Midas exits as the villain in later books — suggests even sympathetic readers find the ethical architecture fragile without its bad object.

The defense is coherent: dark romance offers a contained space to process trauma or explore kinks, and trigger warnings are meant to sort readers by consent. The criticism is equally coherent: when the fandom ritually denies that on-page assault occurred (claiming it was "really" CNC), **the genre is teaching readers to misread coercion in their own lives.** This is the most substantive cultural debate romantasy has produced, and it has no resolution in sight.

## Representation remains the genre's structural weakness

The Ripped Bodice's annual *State of Racial Diversity in Romance Publishing* report shows romance authors of color **decreased by roughly 2% from 2022 to 2023**, and romantasy specifically — as fantasy's offshoot — is even whiter than contemporary romance. A TikTok search for "romantasy" in 2025–2026 surfaces the same rotation of white authors (Yarros, Maas, Armentrout, Broadbent, Kennedy, Cole, Parker). BIPOC authors like Tasha Suri, S.A. Chakraborty, Chloe Gong, Roshani Chokshi, Nisha J. Tuli, and Isabel Cañas exist and sell, but co-owner Leah Koch of The Ripped Bodice argues readers should stop focusing on diverse *characters* and start tracking diverse *authors*, since "fictional characters can't be discriminated against."

Queer representation has become its own flashpoint. ACOTAR's Morrigan is the most-debated queer character in the genre: her late-series coming-out reads to many as **a rushed retrofit** — written as a love interest for Azriel for three books, then revealed in *ACOWAR* as primarily attracted to women while still having sex with men for "protection." Lesbian readers find it erases sapphic experience; bisexual readers find it one of the only nuanced bi depictions in mainstream romantasy; everyone finds the foreshadowing almost nonexistent. High Lord Helion fares worse, functioning as the "slutty bisexual" stereotype who proposes orgies on first appearance. The critique isn't that Maas shouldn't write queer characters — it's that she appears to do so **under fan pressure rather than conviction**, producing the kind of representation that lands on its knees rather than its feet.

## Fourth Wing is the genre's disability-rep exception

The bright spot in representation critique is Rebecca Yarros's depiction of **Violet Sorrengail's invisible disability**, coded as hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (the author and her four sons have hEDS). The condition is never named in-world, which fits the setting and which EDS patients have praised for its subtlety: joint instability, subluxations, easy bruising, heart palpitations, premature graying, and the mental overhead of constant forward-planning are woven into Violet's character without becoming her entire identity. The Ehlers-Danlos Society and individual readers with chronic illness have called it the **first mainstream fantasy protagonist with realistic EDS**, and the forthcoming Amazon adaptation will mark the first major TV representation of the condition. It's a genuine achievement inside a book that also contains logic holes about military governance — proof that the two things can coexist.

## The ACOTAR contraception problem is actually the whole problem

One running joke on BookTok — why do Maas's fae have reliable contraception but no condoms, no visible economy, and no clear legal systems? — points at something real. **Romantasy worldbuilds what the romance needs and skips what it doesn't.** "Fantasy contraception" is a recognized trope (TV Tropes has a page), and the genre uses it to preserve "eternal sexual freedom" without biological consequences. The problem is that the same selective worldbuilding extends to governance (Prythian has High Lords but no High King, and no explanation for how seven autonomous regions avoid civil war), economy (nobody pays for anything in Velaris), and demographics (where are the children, the farmers, the disabled elderly fae?).

The defense is that **this isn't epic fantasy**, and readers shouldn't expect Sanderson-grade magic systems in a book whose engine is emotional catharsis. Fair. But as romantasy matures into a genre with 600+ page installments competing with *Game of Thrones* for cultural space, readers are increasingly unwilling to accept the pass. The sequel to *When the Moon Hatched* was delayed from October 2025 to February 2026 with Parker explicitly citing the need to "get it right" — a small signal that even breakout authors now feel the pressure to deliver more than vibes.

## Where the genre is heading in 2026

Three trends define the current year. **First, the cozy romantasy pivot**: industry analysts describe "cozy fantasy" and "cozy romantasy" as rising subcategories, trading dragon academies and blood wars for bookshop-owning dragons and bakery witches. The genre is branching into lower-stakes territory to catch readers burned out on war-college trauma. **Second, BookTok micro-niching**: dark romantasy, historical romantasy, monster romance, and sapphic romantasy now have their own influencer networks, and publishers maintain dedicated TikTok teams. Romantasy titles regularly see 500–800% sales spikes within days of going viral. **Third, page-count inflation**: nearly every breakout holiday 2025 release exceeded 600 pages, partly driven by Kindle Unlimited's pages-read payment model and partly by reader appetite for extended escapism in a bleak political moment.

Upcoming 2026 releases show the genre consolidating around known brands rather than discovering new ones: *The Ballad of Falling Dragons* (Parker, February), *Brimstone* (Callie Hart, late 2025), the next *Kindred's Curse* installment from Penn Cole, and the continuing Empyrean and Blood and Ash series. Indie stars like Amber V. Nicole (*Blood of Hercules*) and Penn Cole prove Kindle Unlimited remains a viable launchpad — but the path to mainstream visibility still runs through the same narrow BookTok algorithm.

## Conclusion: the genre is better than its critics and worse than its fans

Romantasy deserves neither the dismissal it gets from literary gatekeepers nor the uncritical adoration from its biggest fandoms. **The best of it — Violet's disability arc, the sapphic indie scene out of Neon Hemlock and BIPOC-led presses, the emotional intelligence of writers like Tasha Suri — proves the genre can do serious work.** The worst of it rewards stalker protagonists, sells Celtic aesthetics as research, and teaches young readers that coercion and love can wear the same face.

The genre's next decade turns on whether it can hold those contradictions honestly. The signs are mixed: readers are becoming more critical, publishers are still chasing the safe white-author formula, BookTok is fragmenting into micro-communities that could either deepen or dilute critical discourse. What's certain is that romantasy has already done something unusual — it has **made a generation of casual readers into genre critics**, people who will argue about fae biology and consent mechanics with the intensity their parents reserved for *Lord of the Rings*. That's a culture, not a trend. The question isn't whether romantasy survives 2026. It's what it decides to be.
