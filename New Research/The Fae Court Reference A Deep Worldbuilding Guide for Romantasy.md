# The Fae Court Reference: A Deep Worldbuilding Guide for Romantasy

This reference document is structured for a 20-page workbook deep dive. It maps the full landscape of fae worldbuilding in modern romantasy — from Maas's seasonal courts to Slavic leshy, from the glamour-consent debate to 60+ generative prompts. Cite specific books, folklore sources, and craft problems. Flag contested interpretations. Treat this as a working reference, not a finished essay.

---

## Section 1: The full range of fae cosmologies in modern romantasy

Fae cosmology is the skeleton beneath every court. The structure an author picks dictates **which conflicts are possible, which characters matter, and which aesthetics the reader gets**. Below is the full taxonomy used across the subgenre, with mechanics and structural functions.

### Seasonal courts (ACOTAR model)

Sarah J. Maas's *A Court of Thorns and Roses* established the dominant template: **seven courts of Prythian, split into Solar and Seasonal**. The Seasonal Courts — **Spring, Summer, Autumn, Winter** — are organised around the literal seasons and climates they embody. The Solar Courts — **Dawn, Day, Night** — are organised around hours of the sun's movement. This gives Maas a seven-node political map that doubles as an aesthetic palette.

- **Spring Court (Tamlin).** Eternal spring, rural estate, rose-and-bramble aesthetic. Represents stagnation disguised as paradise. Structurally it is the origin trap: Feyre's first court, the one she must leave to grow. In *ACOTAR*, Spring is also the most traditionally patriarchal, which lets Maas critique "beauty and the beast" while inhabiting it.
- **Summer Court (Tarquin).** Coastal, pearl-and-turquoise palace of Adriata, laid-back and communal. Represents youth, alliance-building, and loss (the Book of Breathings theft in *A Court of Mist and Fury* makes Tarquin's later enmity emotionally earned).
- **Autumn Court (Beron, then Eris).** Forest fires, blood feuds, paternal cruelty. Structurally the "bad father" court — Beron is one of the few unredeemed High Lords, and the court houses Lucien's trauma arc.
- **Winter Court (Kallias and Viviane).** Snow-bound, wolves, child tragedy (the massacre of Winter's children by Amarantha). Represents recovery and love-as-survival; Kallias/Viviane is one of the series' few equal, mated High Lord couples.
- **Dawn Court (Thesan).** Healers, mountain peaks, sunrise palette. Represents neutrality; used as a plot lever for healing and diplomacy rather than as a dramatic engine.
- **Day Court (Helion).** Libraries, scholarship, golden light, sun-magic. Represents knowledge as power; Helion is a sexually-progressive counterweight to Spring's repression, and his Lucien-paternity arc gives the court emotional stakes.
- **Night Court (Rhysand).** Three tiers: the Court of Dreams (Velaris, progressive, artistic, loving), the Court of Nightmares (Hewn City, brutal, feudal, fearful), and the Illyrian war-camps. Structurally, Night is the *romance destination* — a court big enough to contain multitudes, which lets Maas justify Feyre's ideological conversion without flattening her.

**Political mechanics.** High Lords inherit power; they cannot be democratically deposed. The **Cauldron** selects the mate bond and distributes power during creation myths. **The Tithe** (ACOTAR ch. 18) is a twice-yearly payment of goods and, crucially, *blood-magic-enforced debts* from lesser fae to High Lords — the scene where Feyre forgives a water-wraith's debt is the first crack in her relationship with Tamlin.

**What seasonal courts do for story.** They give you (1) an instant aesthetic shorthand, (2) a rotating cast of rival leaders, (3) a year-wheel that maps political arcs onto rituals (Calanmai, Starfall, Nynsar, Winter Solstice), and (4) automatic metaphoric resonance — stagnation for Spring, grief for Winter, renewal for Dawn.

### Seelie / Unseelie splits

The **Seelie/Unseelie** binary is the second dominant structure. Etymologically, "seelie" derives from Scots/Old English *sælig* ("blessed, happy"); "unseelie" is simply the negation. In Scottish folklore (particularly as collected by Sir Walter Scott and later Katharine Briggs) the Seelie Court is trooping fairies who may harm mortals but often help them; the Unseelie Court is malevolent, often appearing as the Sluagh (the restless dead).

- **Holly Black (*The Folk of the Air*).** Black does not use a pure Seelie/Unseelie split — Elfhame is a **High Court** with subordinate lesser courts: the **Court of Teeth** (ice, cruelty, the villains Lady Nore and Lord Jarel), **Court of Termites** (assassins, the Ghost, the Roach, the Bomb), **Court of Shadows** (Dain's, then Cardan's spy network), **Court of Moths**, **Court of Larks**, **Court of Cups**. Holly's innovation: courts organised by **function** (war, espionage, revelry) rather than aesthetic alone. *The Stolen Heir* duology adds the **Court of Teeth** and exiled Unseelie as protagonists.
- **Karen Marie Moning (*Fever* series).** Moning uses the truer Celtic split: **Seelie (light) vs Unseelie (dark)**, each with **royal castes**. The Seelie Queen has four Royal Hunters; the Unseelie King has four Royal Princes (Cruce, War, Famine, Pestilence, Death — though Cruce is separate). The **Unseelie Sifting** is Moning's version of glamour-shifting. The *Hallows* (Spear of Luisne, Sword of Light, Cauldron of Forgetting, Stone) are sacred objects — a direct borrow from the Irish Four Treasures of the Tuatha Dé Danann. Moning's setting is urban Dublin, which sets her apart: the fae **invade our world**, they don't rule a separate one.
- **Traditional Celtic origins.** In Irish tradition proper, there is no Seelie/Unseelie divide — this is Scottish. In Ireland you have the **Aos Sí** (singular *duine sídhe*, "person of the mound") with *Trooping* vs *Solitary* fae as the older division. The Irish *Daoine Maithe* ("good people") was a propitiatory name — you called them good so they wouldn't be otherwise. Writers who import Seelie/Unseelie into "Irish" settings are committing a common anachronism.

### Elemental courts (Throne of Glass)

In Maas's *Throne of Glass*, fae are organised by **elemental gift** rather than court — fire, water, wind, earth, ice, plus rarer gifts like healing or mind-reading. **Queen Maeve** of **Doranelle** rules the Fae; her court is a single autocracy of elemental lords rather than a pluralistic map. The elemental structure matches characters (Aelin = fire, Rowan = ice/wind, Lorcan = death-magic) and lets Maas build magical compatibility into romantic compatibility. The "carranam" bond — two fae who can share and amplify each other's magic — is its own form of mating bond.

**Structurally, elemental courts replace geography with power-type**. This is good for magic-system clarity and bad for political texture — there is less "home court" identity than in seasonal systems.

### Sin/virtue courts

The **Seven Deadly Sins** court model is largely **demon territory, not fae**. Kerri Maniscalco's *Kingdom of the Wicked* uses the Seven Princes of Hell (Wrath, Pride, Envy, Greed, Lust, Gluttony, Sloth) and its **Prince of Sin** spin-off (starting with *Throne of the Fallen*, 2023) deepens each court into standalone romances. **Crucially: these are demon courts, and Maniscalco's fae are a separate species** living on the island of Faerie, divided into Seelie (sun/summer-worshipping) and Unseelie/Wild Court (moon/winter-worshipping), the latter ruled by **Prim Róis and Lennox** with four blood-heirs.

Workbook note: if you use sin/virtue courts for fae, you are blending folk-Catholic demonology with Celtic fae traditions — a hybrid that **should be flagged in your worldbuilding**, not hidden. Holly Black's Court of Cups flirts with this (decadence as identity) without going full Deadly Sins.

### Day/Night courts (distinct from Seelie/Unseelie)

A Day/Night binary maps onto *time* rather than *morality*. Maas's Solar Courts are one instance but split into three, which dilutes the binary. **Melissa Marr's *Wicked Lovely*** uses a sharper version: the **Summer Court, Winter Court, Dark Court, High Court**, with the Summer King and Winter Queen locked in a seasonal balance (more than two, but the Dark vs Light axis is real). **Holly Black's Tithe trilogy** uses Seelie/Unseelie but the Unseelie King Nicnevin's court is night-coded, not evil-coded.

The structural advantage of a Day/Night split over Seelie/Unseelie: **no moral valence baked in**. Night fae can be as kind as Day fae; the difference is circadian, magical, aesthetic. Rhysand's Night Court works precisely because Maas untethers "night" from "evil."

### Regional fae traditions

Before inventing a court, know what you're adapting. Rough map:

- **Irish** — *Aos sí* (pronounced "ees shee"), *Tuatha Dé Danann* ("people of the goddess Danu"), *Daoine Sídhe*, *banshee* (*bean sí*, "woman of the mound"), *leprechaun* (solitary shoemaker), *púca* (shapeshifter, often malevolent-trickster), *dullahan* (headless rider), *fear gorta* (famine figure), *cluricaune*. The Tuatha Dé are the gods-turned-fae after the Milesian invasion.
- **Scottish** — the source of **Seelie/Unseelie**, plus *sluagh* (host of the unforgiven dead), *cait sìth* (fairy cat), *each-uisge* (water horse, kills riders), *kelpie*, *selkie* (seal-skin wife), *baobhan sìth* (vampire-dancer), *ghillie dhu* (tree-guardian), *brownie* (house spirit), *redcap* (murderous border-fae).
- **Welsh** — *Tylwyth Teg* ("Fair Family"), *Gwragedd Annwn* (lake maidens — a different Arthurian register), *Plant Annwn* (children of Annwn, the Welsh otherworld), *Bendith y Mamau* ("the Mothers' Blessing" — euphemism), **Arawn** ruling Annwn. Welsh fae are often more queenly and less trickster-y than Irish.
- **Cornish** — *piskies* (distinct from English "pixies"), *spriggans* (ugly guardians of treasure), *knockers* (mine spirits), *buccas*.
- **Breton** — *korrigan* (dwarfish), *Ankou* (death-figure).
- **Slavic** — these are **spirits more than fae**, with different cosmology. **Domovoi** (house-spirit, protective, lives behind the stove), **rusalka** (water-spirit of drowned girls — note: the romanticised Disney reading contrasts with the older vengeful one), **leshy** (forest guardian, shapeshifter, trickster but just), **vodyanoy** (male water-spirit, drags swimmers), **Baba Yaga** (witch-goddess in chicken-legged hut — do not conflate with fae), **vila/wili** (storm-maidens), **poludnitsa** (noon-witch, heat-stroke personified), **kikimora** (malevolent house spirit, counterpart to domovoi), **polevik** (field spirit). Slavic spirits are *territorial and contractual*, not court-organised — a key distinction.
- **Germanic/Norse** — *álfar* split into **Ljósálfar** (light elves) and **Dökkálfar** (dark elves), sometimes a third **Svartálfar** (black elves, possibly dwarves — contested). *Huldra* (hollow-backed forest woman), *nix/nixie* (water spirit), *kobold* (mine/house), *alp* (nightmare spirit). The **Wild Hunt** (Odin's or Woden's ride, also Herne the Hunter in England, Gwyn ap Nudd in Wales) is pan-Germanic and pan-Celtic.

### Non-Western fae-equivalents

**These are not fae** and should be adapted with care. They get used in romantasy but each carries living cultural weight.

- **Japanese *yōkai*** — the umbrella category. **Kitsune** (fox, gains tails with age, the nine-tailed *kyūbi* being archetypal; distinct traditions: *zenko* benevolent, *yako* mischievous), **tengu** (mountain spirits, often warrior-priests), **kappa** (water imp with water-bowl on head), **tanuki** (raccoon-dog, trickster), **yuki-onna** (snow woman), **bakeneko** (cat spirit). *Oni* are demons, not *yōkai*, despite Western conflation. Sensitivity: Shinto is a living religion.
- **Arabic/Islamic *jinn*** — named in the Qur'an, created from smokeless fire, possessing free will (can be Muslim or not). Classes include **marid** (powerful, watery), **ifrit** (fiery, warlike), **ghul**, **sila**. Wish-granting is a post-*Thousand and One Nights* Western inflection. **Extreme sensitivity**: jinn belief is part of living Islamic theology, not folklore-for-aesthetic.
- **Filipino *diwata*/*engkanto***, **Korean *dokkaebi***, **Chinese *huli jing*** (fox spirit, parallel to kitsune).
- **Indigenous/First Nations spirits** — flag the hardest do-not-appropriate territory. Wendigo, skinwalkers, and many other figures are part of specific living tribal traditions with explicit author guidance (often: don't).

## Section 2: Core fae mechanics across the subgenre

Mechanics are the *rules* that make a fae feel fae rather than an elf, demon, or vampire. Below, each mechanic is mapped folkloric origin → modern romantasy use → craft implication.

### Glamour, illusion, and appearance rules

**Folklore.** "Glamour" is a Scots word for magical concealment or beautification, popularised in English by Walter Scott. Ointment made from four-leaf clover, self-bored stones (hag-stones), or specific herbs breaks it in traditional lore. A mortal who inadvertently anoints an eye with fairy ointment sees through glamour forever — and is often blinded in that eye when caught.

**Modern uses.**
- **Holly Black** uses explicit, rule-bound glamour. In *The Cruel Prince*, glamour can compel humans to obey; Jude's iron-wrought protection and sheer willpower resist it. Black treats glamour as a *consent violation* — characters discuss this on page.
- **Maas (ACOTAR)** has High Fae able to change appearance (Rhys's shift to his war-form, Mor's golden beauty) but the *political* glamour of Amarantha Under the Mountain is the central act of the first book.
- **Moning (Fever)** has *sidhe-seers* — mortal women who see through fae glamour — as the entire heroine class. The "seeing" is the plot engine.

**Craft rules to establish in your world:** (1) Can glamour be voluntary or involuntary? (2) What breaks it? (3) Does everyone who wears glamour know they're wearing it? (4) Can fae glamour other fae? (5) Is glamour an illusion (affects others' perception) or a transformation (changes the fae)?

### Iron, salt, cold iron, rowan, running water

**Folklore.** Iron aversion appears across Indo-European fae lore, probably reflecting the cultural memory of Bronze Age peoples being displaced by Iron Age peoples. **"Cold iron"** in folklore simply means wrought iron (as opposed to heated, forge-worked metal in active use) — not a special metal. Salt's power is broader and cross-cultural (apotropaic in many traditions). **Rowan** (mountain ash) is the premier Celtic anti-fae wood; hawthorn and elder are ambivalent (hawthorn trees are fairy trees — don't fell them). **Running water** traps and repels — fae cannot easily cross it, which is why bridges and fords are liminal.

**Authors.** Holly Black uses iron most consistently and painfully — Cardan's court is partly defined by iron intolerance, and Jude's brief iron-tipped sword is a statement. Maas uses **ash wood** (the faebane arrows that wounded Rhys) and **Faebane** (a drug that suppresses magic) rather than iron. Moning uses iron. Seanan McGuire's October Daye uses iron as existential threat to changelings. Patricia Briggs uses iron in the Mercy Thompson series for the Gray Lords. Melissa Marr uses iron in *Wicked Lovely*.

**Craft rule.** If your fae have no vulnerabilities, they have no tension. Pick one — **iron, ash, salt, running water, true name, a specific ritual word, cold, silver** — and enforce it.

### True names

**Folklore.** Name-magic is near-universal — Rumpelstiltskin, the Egyptian Ra-Isis myth, the biblical angel who would not give Jacob his name, Ursula K. Le Guin's *Earthsea* codifying the principle. A true name grants **power to compel**.

**Modern uses.** Holly Black: fae have true names, often secret, sometimes tattooed in hidden places. **Seanan McGuire's October Daye**: name-magic is central, with hidden true names and the "changeling" class. **Melissa Marr**: names power the courts. **Elise Kova's *A Deal with the Elf King*** builds the elves' magic on true-naming — reviewers have called the true-name system underdeveloped, which is instructive. **Patricia Briggs** uses true names for the Gray Lords.

**Rules to set.** Who knows their true name? Can it be spoken aloud without giving it up (some traditions require *intentional* gift)? Can it be stolen? Can it be changed (name-changing as coming-of-age)? Does speaking it compel or merely summon?

### Fae bargains, oaths, debts, geas

**Folklore.** The Irish **geas/geasa** (plural) is a sacred prohibition or obligation — **Cú Chulainn's geasa** (never eat dog, never refuse hospitality) kill him when enemies arrange for him to break both simultaneously. Geasa are *imposed*, often at birth or initiation, not bargained. Separately, fae bargaining appears across Celtic ballad tradition — **Tam Lin** is saved through Janet's willingness to hold him through transformations after striking what is effectively a bargain.

**Modern structure.** A fae bargain needs (1) offer, (2) acceptance, (3) consideration (what each party gives), (4) magical enforcement. Maas's signature is the **tattoo** — Rhys marks Feyre's arm in *ACOTAR*, binding her to a week a month in the Night Court. Laura Thalassa's **Bargainer series** structures the entire romance around accumulated bargains. Elise Kova's *Married to Magic* novels use the marriage-as-bargain. Raven Kennedy's *Gild* opens with Auren bound by gold to King Midas.

**Bargain craft.** A good bargain scene does three things: (1) establishes stakes, (2) reveals character through what each party asks for, (3) leaves room for unintended consequences. The best bargains become romantic currency — the debt is transmuted into trust, then desire.

### Fae cannot lie (and how they lie anyway)

**Folklore.** The "fae cannot lie" rule is often credited to modern fantasy (Holly Black popularised it in romantasy), but precedents exist in the Irish tradition of *fír flathemon* (the ruler's truth) and scattered "fairy truthfulness" motifs. Diane Purkiss notes it is more modern convention than ancient rule.

**How fae lie anyway.**
- **Technical truths.** "I did not kill your brother" — true, because you had him killed.
- **Implication.** "Would I ever hurt you?" (Answer the fae wants in the listener's head; nothing committed.)
- **Omission.** Stating what is true and leaving out what matters.
- **Redirection.** Answering a different question.
- **Ambiguous referent.** "She is well" — which "she"? When?
- **Conditional framing.** "If I were you, I would trust him" — the fae is not you.

Cardan in *The Cruel Prince* is the genre's masterclass in weaponised half-truth; Jude, as a mortal, lies freely, which is both her advantage and her moral complication.

### Fae food and mortal addiction

**Folklore.** Persephone and the pomegranate is the Indo-European type. Christina Rossetti's **"Goblin Market"** (1862) is the definitive Victorian fae-food poem — fruit that addicts and withers mortal girls. In Celtic tradition, **eating in the Otherworld traps you there**; Oisín returns from Tír na nÓg and crumbles to dust when he touches Irish soil.

**Modern.** Holly Black's Jude eats faerie fruit once and is hooked — Madoc's rule that the mortal children avoid fae food is central to the books' early rhythm. Maas's faerie wine at **Fire Night / Calanmai** (*ACOTAR* ch. 15–16) lowers Feyre's inhibitions, which becomes a consent flashpoint (see section below). Melissa Marr, Seanan McGuire, Laini Taylor's work all honour the rule.

### Time slippage between Faerie and mortal realms

**Folklore.** Oisín spends what feels like three years in Tír na nÓg and returns to find three hundred years gone. Welsh and Scottish tales mirror this. Washington Irving's **Rip Van Winkle** is an American inflection.

**Types.**
1. **Faerie fast, mortal slow** (most traditional — a night there, a century here).
2. **Faerie slow, mortal fast** (rarer — useful for "I am old but the world left me behind" arcs).
3. **Fixed but unpredictable** (time moves normally but you can't tell which direction).
4. **Variable by court** (different courts have different rates — underused and highly generative).

**Modern.** Maas's Prythian runs roughly parallel to the mortal lands, so time-slippage isn't a mechanic there; Holly Black's Elfhame runs parallel too. This is *low-hanging fruit* for writers who want folkloric grounding — most romantasy ignores traditional time-slippage entirely.

### Changelings and half-fae

**Folklore.** Fae steal a human infant and leave a *changeling* (a sickly fae child, or an enchanted log). Historians including **Diane Purkiss** and **Joyce Underwood Munro** have documented that changeling beliefs were used to explain and sometimes to justify the **killing of disabled or ill children**. This is not a romantic trope at its root — writers should know the history.

**Modern.** Seanan McGuire's *October Daye* builds a whole social class around changelings — half-fae children who belong nowhere. Holly Black's Jude Duarte is not a changeling (she's fully human in Faerie) but occupies the same liminal space. Maas's half-fae characters (Lucien's origins, Nesta's transformation) exist but the trope is underused compared to possibility.

### Wild Hunt, tithes, sacrifices

**Folklore.** The Wild Hunt is pan-Germanic/Celtic: Odin's or Woden's hunt, Herne the Hunter, Gwyn ap Nudd, the Sluagh in Scotland, Arawn in Wales. It is the dead-and-fae riding the sky, and **seeing it is often fatal**. The **Teind to Hell** (Tam Lin: "ilka seven years we pay a teind to Hell, and I fear 'twill be mysel'") is the Scottish tithe — a fae sacrifice to an older power.

**Modern.** Holly Black's *The Stolen Heir* puts the Wild Hunt at centre stage with Lady Nore's reanimated army. Maas's Tithe (ACOTAR ch. 18) is a debt-collection ritual, not a blood sacrifice. Seanan McGuire, Jennifer Roberson, and indie urban-fae writers use the Hunt more literally.

### Glamour, consent, and the craft problem — dedicated discussion

This deserves its own sub-section because **it is the single most contested mechanic in modern romantasy**, and a workbook author who ducks it is not serving readers.

**The problem.** Glamour can affect *perception, attraction, and memory*. This is structurally identical to the real-world consent violations modern readers are taught to recognise — intoxication, coercion, manipulation. When a fae love interest glamours a human partner (even "mildly"), the encounter is, strictly, non-consensual. When fae wine lowers inhibitions in a scene that ends sexually, that is (structurally) intoxication-based consent violation.

**How the genre has handled it, on a spectrum.**

1. **Ignore the problem.** Older urban fantasy and some contemporary romantasy simply don't raise it. Glamour is wielded freely; the narrative treats it as neutral. **This reads badly to contemporary audiences** and increasingly draws reviewer criticism.

2. **Handwave with "fated."** If a bond is destined, the story implies consent is pre-given. This is the mating-bond cheat, and it has hard limits — readers notice.

3. **The *ACOTAR* **Fire Night / Calanmai** approach (debated).** In *ACOTAR*, the Spring Court's Fire Night (ch. 15–16) involves magic that drives participants into sexual frenzy; Feyre is nearly caught up. Rhys intervenes. The scene is defended (Rhys removes her from the situation) and critiqued (Feyre's later aggressive encounter with Tamlin is pressed by magic neither partner fully controls). Reader discourse on Reddit, Goodreads, and BookTok has been extensive and split; workbook authors should read the scene and form their own view. Maas's *A Court of Silver Flames* further complicates this with Nesta's intoxication-heavy scenes.

4. **Engage directly (Holly Black).** Black makes glamour a tool of political violence. Cardan cannot glamour Jude (she's protected and willful), and when glamour is used on mortals, it is shown as the violation it is. Jude's iron-willed resistance is part of her character.

5. **Heroines who see through (Moning's Fever).** Mac's sidhe-seer abilities mean glamour simply *doesn't work* on her. This sidesteps the consent problem by removing its efficacy.

6. **Explicit consent mechanics (the modern best practice).** Some newer authors build glamour-consent directly into the magic: a fae must ask permission to glamour a lover, or glamour leaves traces, or breaking consent breaks the mate bond. Elise Kova's *Married to Magic* books flirt with this through their marriage-vow-as-magical-contract framing.

**Workbook craft guidance.**

- **Decide your rule and keep it.** Is glamour opt-in, opt-out, involuntary, resistible, permanent, traceable?
- **Show the moral cost.** If your fae *can* glamour and do, show the consequences — for the person glamoured and for the fae's soul.
- **Give your protagonist agency tools.** Mortal resistance (iron, willpower, sidhe-sight, a teacher) is the craft answer to glamour coercion.
- **Do not use mating bonds to paper over consent.** The most common complaint in modern reviews is "the bond decided for them." Make sure the characters decide.
- **Sex scenes with magic present need extra care.** Fae wine, bond-heat, rut, aphrodisiac magic — every one is a consent question in disguise. Either address it on page or don't deploy it.

## Section 3: High Fae vs Lesser Fae hierarchy

The **High Fae / Lesser Fae** split is romantasy's class system. It is where the genre does its most direct work on power, passing, and love-across-status.

**How authors structure it.**

- **Maas (ACOTAR).** High Fae are humanoid, beautiful, immortal-ish, magically gifted. Lesser fae range across a bestiary — winged Illyrians (who are lesser fae but ascendant), water-wraiths, suriel, Attor, Peregryns, nymphs. High Fae rule; lesser fae serve, fight, or live in pockets of autonomy. The Illyrian storyline (Rhys, Cassian, Azriel — part-Illyrian, culturally denigrated) is the series' sharpest class commentary.
- **Black (Folk of the Air).** "Gentry" vs "common fae" vs "solitary fae." The Court of Termites (assassins) is explicitly working-class fae — the Ghost, the Roach, the Bomb are lesser fae with more moral clarity than most High Court nobles. Black's class work is the genre's best.
- **Moning (Fever).** Royal caste vs lower caste; the Unseelie King's four Princes are Royal, but countless lesser Unseelie populate the Dublin invasion. The Rhino-Boys and Shades are lesser Unseelie; the Lord Master sits ambiguously between.
- **Maniscalco.** Seelie vs Unseelie with blood-heirs at the top.
- **Thalassa, Kova, Kennedy.** All use some version of "the fae nobility vs everyone else," often flattened.

**What this structure does for plot.**

- **Power imbalance and rebellion.** A High Fae / Lesser Fae structure pre-loads class conflict. Uprisings, revolutionary subplots, and generational resentment become available without exposition. Black's Court of Teeth revolt, Maas's Illyrian tensions, the Valkyries' push for rights in *Throne of Glass* — all leverage this.
- **Mobility and passing.** Can a lesser fae disguise as High? Can a half-blood pass? This becomes the "racial passing" plot in magical form and must be handled with craft — it is not neutral.
- **Labour and economy.** Who makes the food, cleans the palace, fights the wars? If only High Fae appear on page, your world is hollow.

**What it does for romance.**

- **Forbidden-love across hierarchy.** The classic beat: High Lord loves lesser fae seamstress/healer/warrior. Done well, this interrogates power (she keeps her agency; he gives up something real). Done badly, it becomes noblesse-oblige fantasy.
- **Passing as plot engine.** A lesser fae infiltrating the court (Jude-like dynamics, even though Jude is mortal, not lesser fae).
- **"Beauty and the Beast" inverted.** High Fae love interest is beautiful; lesser fae protagonist may be scaled, clawed, winged — *and the beauty has to learn to see*. Grace Draven's *Radiance* does this outside fae but the dynamic transfers.
- **Status reversal.** The mortal/lesser-fae heroine becomes more powerful than the High Lord she loves (Feyre by the end of *ACOMAF*, Aelin by late *ToG*).

## Section 4: Political structures

Fae courts need government. Most romantasy uses a small menu — workbook authors should expand it.

**High Lords, Queens, Kings.** Absolute monarchs, often magically bound to the land itself (Maas: High Lord magic derives from the Cauldron and is shared with the court's territory; Kova: the Elf King is magically required to channel the Fade). This tethers the ruler to the land and makes abdication lethal — good dramatic pressure.

**Councils and advisory structures.** ACOTAR has the Inner Circle — not a formal council but a trust-group. Folk of the Air has the Living Council. Moning has Mac's Keltar druid circle (allies, not advisors). Real craft opportunity: **formal advisory structures underused**. Consider a Council of Seasons, a Conclave of Elders, a Seat of the Hallows, a Mage-Council.

**Trials as plot devices.** The fae trial is genre catnip — a public performance of power and truth-telling where the rules work against the protagonist. Feyre's three trials Under the Mountain (ACOTAR); Jude's trial scenes in the High Court; Aelin's King's Flame trial (*Throne of Glass*). Trial scenes work because they compress theme, stakes, and character into a rule-bound set-piece.

**Successions.** How does power transfer?
- **Cauldron-bestowed (Maas ACOTAR).** High Lord power passes on death, magically, usually to eldest or most worthy son. Beron's Autumn Court succession — Eris is eldest but the question of inheritance is contested.
- **Trial by combat** (Folk of the Air tournaments, Dain's assassinated coronation).
- **Chosen by a sacred object** (the Hallows, the Glaive, the Bloodthirsty Sword).
- **Democratic among peers** (rare in fae fantasy — use it).
- **By mate** (the partner of the fallen ruler inherits — underused).

**Tithes.** Ritual tribute. Maas's Tithe is biannual and enforced by magical debt. The Scottish **Teind to Hell** is a blood-tithe paid every seven years. Tithes are *character engines*: who pays, who resents, who hides, who collects?

**Conscription.** Forced military service. Illyrians are conscripted into Rhys's army; Moning's Hunters serve the Queen; Black's Court of Teeth was a conscription nightmare under Lady Nore. Conscription scenes compress class, race, and personal stakes.

**Assassinations.** The currency of fae courts. Black's Court of Shadows is an institutional assassination corps. Maas's Amarantha (ruler Under the Mountain) is overthrown through distributed murder. The **rules of political murder** matter: is it legal? Does it trigger blood-feud? Can a High Lord be assassinated?

**Diplomacy between courts.** The council of High Lords in ACOMAF (chs. 42–44) is the subgenre's set-piece diplomatic scene. Treaties, trade pacts, hostage exchanges, state marriages — all deserve more page-time than most books give them.

## Section 5: Romantic dynamics specific to fae courts

The fae love interest is the most-read character type in modern romantasy. Below are the dynamics that show up across the genre, with notes on what each accomplishes.

### Fae-lord / human-woman power imbalance

The default romantasy dynamic: dangerous immortal male, breakable mortal woman. **It works** because the magical imbalance externalises the emotional imbalance of early love — we all feel small in front of the person we want. **It fails** when the imbalance is never corrected.

**How modern authors rewrite it.**
- **Give her power.** Feyre becomes High Fae; Aelin is already queen; Jude outplays them all; Mac becomes the MacKayla-prime.
- **Give her refusal.** A mortal who says no and is respected is rarer than she should be. Kova's Luella negotiates terms.
- **Show him bending.** The scene where the powerful fae concedes, apologises, or renounces power for her is the genre's oldest pleasure. Rhys giving Feyre the Night Court by co-rule. Cardan's crown moment.
- **Deconstruct it on page.** Black in *The Cruel Prince* makes the power imbalance the text, not subtext — Jude discusses it, names it, and leverages it.

**What not to do.** Use the imbalance as an excuse for behaviours you would not accept from a human lover — surveillance, confinement, "protective" lying, suppressed agency. Readers notice.

### Court-crossing as forbidden romance

Lovers from rival courts — Night/Spring (Feyre/Rhys begin Night/Spring-adjacent), Seelie/Unseelie (Moning's Mac and Barrons operates on a related axis). **Craft use:** court-crossing gives you *automatic political stakes* and a ready-made forbidden structure without inventing a Montague/Capulet from scratch. The payoff: an alliance forged through the romance that reshapes the map.

### Arranged bonds vs chosen bonds

**Arranged.** Political marriage (Kova's Luella and the Elf King; Raven Kennedy's Auren originally gifted to Midas). **Chosen.** The bond itself is the choice (Rhys refusing to force Feyre's acceptance in ACOMAF). The genre's sweet spot: an *arranged* bond that becomes *chosen* — the structure forces proximity, then love arrives honestly.

### Bargain-as-courtship

Using fae bargains to escalate romantic tension is now a recognised sub-trope. **Laura Thalassa's Bargainer** makes it the spine — each bargain deepens the relationship. Maas's Rhys/Feyre tattoo bargain is the archetype. **Craft pattern:** the initial bargain is transactional; later bargains become intimate (ask me anything, tell me your dreams, give me a night); the final "bargain" is commitment.

### Mating bonds in fae contexts

This is the **most imitated and most misunderstood** mechanic in the genre. Key distinctions from werewolf mating bonds:

| Feature | Werewolf mating | Fae mating (romantasy) |
|---|---|---|
| Recognition | Usually on sight/scent | Often gradual, or sudden-but-delayed awareness |
| Reciprocity | Often one-way initially | Usually two-way by nature |
| Rejection | Catastrophic or impossible | Rejectable (ACOTAR rules) |
| Source | Biological/instinctive | "Cauldron-sent" / cosmic |
| Compulsion | Often overwhelming | Variable — Maas has "mate-rage," Kova has softer pull |

**Maas (ACOTAR).** The bond is Cauldron-sent, two-way, can be *felt* before acknowledged, can be rejected by either party (Nesta initially rejects Cassian's; the rejection costs both). Acceptance is ritualised — cooking a meal and offering it is the Night Court ceremony. The bond **snapping into place** (Rhys's realisation before Feyre's) is the genre's most-imitated beat.

**Throne of Glass.** Rowan and Aelin are **carranam** — magic-sharing partners — *before* they are mates. The mating is reciprocal and less cosmic than in ACOTAR.

**Holly Black.** No mating bond mechanic — Cardan and Jude's commitment is ritual and political, made through crownings and vows. **This is a craft choice worth copying.** A love story without a mating bond forces the relationship to earn every beat.

**Craft rules for designing a mate bond:**
- Reciprocity (one-way? two-way?)
- Reveal timing (instant? years?)
- Refusal cost (none? social? magical? fatal?)
- Physical effects (heat, pull, telepathy, shared sensation?)
- Origin (cosmic, genetic, elective, ritual?)
- Consummation requirement (do they have to sleep together? Live together? Marry?)

### Fae courting rituals

- **Gift-giving.** Rhys's gifts to Feyre across ACOMAF are carefully escalated — a room, a paint set, a title. In fae tradition, gifts create obligation; a refused gift is an insult.
- **Naming.** Exchanging true names, or offering a pet-name, or giving a new name.
- **Seasonal courtship.** Courting tied to sabbats — Beltane, Samhain, midsummer, midwinter.
- **Feats/trials.** Suitor performs a task (Celtic ballad tradition; Kova's marriage setups).
- **Cooking and feeding.** Night Court mating ceremony. In folklore, feeding is binding.
- **Hair.** Braiding, cutting, binding lovers' hair is a cross-cultural ritual; underused in romantasy.

### Fae weddings, handfastings, bondings

**Handfasting** is Celtic — tying hands with cord during vows, historically a "year and a day" trial marriage. Maas uses handfasting-like imagery; neo-pagan communities still practise it. **Bondings** (magical soul-linking) are more severe than marriage — often permanent, sometimes telepathic. **Weddings** can be political (Feyre/Tamlin), mating-ceremony (Feyre/Rhys), or handfasting (shorter, simpler).

**Craft note.** Give your world multiple tiers: a handfast (trial), a marriage (legal), a mating ceremony (cosmic), and a bonding (soul). Let characters choose which level to enter.

### Fae fertility and half-bloods

**Low fertility** is a common romantasy trope — fae children are rare, pregnancies are magical events. **Why the trope works:**
- It makes succession urgent.
- It makes love interests vulnerable (pregnancy as high-stakes).
- It makes half-bloods politically loaded (proof of crossing-over).
- It slows population growth so your immortals haven't overrun the world.

**Half-bloods in practice.** Lucien's paternity (ACOTAR), Jude's sister Vivienne's fae child, Nesta's pregnancy (*ACOSF*), half-Illyrian Rhys. Politically, half-bloods are usually **discriminated against** by both parent cultures. Craft use: a half-blood protagonist has automatic dual identity and dual stakes.

## Section 6: Author comparison — what to steal, what to avoid

### Sarah J. Maas — *ACOTAR* + *Throne of Glass* + *Crescent City*

**Strengths.** Emotionally legible courts; strong aesthetic differentiation; big ensemble that gives her dozens of viable POV-candidates; the mate-bond mechanic; distinct High Lord voices. The Velaris sequence in *ACOMAF* chs. 21–30 is the genre's set-piece for "romance-destination worldbuilding."

**What to steal.** Aesthetic shorthand per court; the gift-escalation courtship; the Inner Circle as *found-family-in-court*; the ceremonial cooked-meal mating.

**What to avoid.** Magic system inconsistency across books (powers grow to meet plot need); thin political scaffolding for a continent-scale empire; repeated "male-protects-female-against-her-will" beats that read worse on re-read; the habit of resolving political conflict with personal violence.

### Holly Black — *Folk of the Air* + *Modern Faerie Tales* + *Stolen Heir* duology

**Strengths.** The genre's best *political* fae — every scene is a power exchange. Explicit engagement with glamour-consent. No mate-bond cheat; relationships earn their beats. Court names (Teeth, Termites, Moths, Shadows) organised by function. Mortal protagonist (Jude) whose *lack* of magic is her edge.

**What to steal.** Function-based court naming; the "fae can't lie" rule made generative rather than decorative; political trials as scenes of character; the ruler-who-loves-his-assassin dynamic.

**What to avoid.** Very little. The most commonly cited "weakness" is that Black's books are shorter and colder than Maas's, which is a feature not a bug. If you copy Black, do not soften her cruelty — her books work because nothing is free.

### Karen Marie Moning — *Fever* series + *Highlander* series

**Strengths.** Urban fantasy fae (Dublin), the sidhe-seer mechanic, the Hallows as magical objects with real rules, sexual tension sustained over books. The *Highlander* series adds time-travel and Celtic druid lore with fae subplots.

**What to steal.** Bringing fae into the contemporary world; the heroine-who-sees-through-glamour as consent solution; the Hallows/sacred-objects approach as an alternative to court-map.

**What to avoid.** Plotting that depends on heroine amnesia/ignorance; consent grey zones in the Mac/Barrons relationship that many modern readers critique; dense worldbuilding that front-loads without pay-off.

### Kerri Maniscalco — *Kingdom of the Wicked* + *Prince of Sin* spin-off

**Clarification first.** The Seven Princes are **demons**, not fae. Maniscalco's *fae* are a separate species on the island of Faerie, organised Seelie (sun, summer) vs Unseelie/Wild Court (moon, winter, ruled by Prim Róis and Lennox with four blood-heir princes/princesses). The sin-court model is demonic Italian-Catholic worldbuilding, not Celtic.

**Strengths.** Sicilian setting; sin-as-aesthetic for each prince's court (House Wrath, House Pride, etc.); marriage-bargain Mark mechanic that binds Emilia and Wrath magically; blending witch-lore with demon courts.

**What to steal.** The *cuisine* (Maniscalco's Sicilian food detail models how to give a court material culture); the **magical Mark as binding tattoo**; the court-as-extension-of-ruler's-personality trope.

**What to avoid.** If you're writing fae, don't confuse readers by labelling sin-courts as fae. Keep your taxonomy clean.

### Scottish fae romances — Moning *Highlander*, Kresley Cole, Donna Grant

**Kresley Cole's *Immortals After Dark*** treats fae as one of many **Lore** factions (Valkyrie, Lykae, Vampires, Fae). The Queen of Plenty (Morgana) and the Rí Ùr lead a Sylvan fae court in books like *MacRieve*. Cole's mating bonds are Lykae-centred but fae appear throughout.

**Donna Grant's *Dark Kings*** is Highlander + dragons + druids + fae; Grant's *Reaper* and *Dark Fae* series move closer to pure fae romance, with Seelie/Unseelie structure and the Fae King Usaeil as a recurring villain.

**What to steal from Scottish-set fae.** The *Celtic grounding* — druids, seasonal festivals, Highland-coded honour culture — gives a fae world more weight than generic-European-fantasy.

**What to avoid.** The "sexy Highlander ancestor as fae cursed" plot is a small sub-market; if you're not already in it, think carefully before entering.

### Indie bestsellers

**Scarlett St. Clair.** *Hades & Persephone* (Hades, A Touch of Darkness) is **Greek-pantheon, not fae** — gods ruling divine courts (Olympus, Underworld). Relevant because St. Clair's court mechanics (divine hierarchy, bargain-style contracts in the Underworld, court-crossing) *feel* fae and influence the subgenre. The **Adrian X Isolde / When Stars Come Out** series leans into vampires, not fae. Worth reading as a **romance escalation craft reference**, not as fae worldbuilding.

**Raven Kennedy — *The Plated Prisoner*** (Gild, Glint, Gleam, Glow, Gold). Six kingdoms, King Midas with magical gold touch, Slade Ravinger as the Red King (and the fated love interest). The fae here are more Norse-coded than Celtic, with Orea as a broken land. **What to steal:** the gilded-prisoner metaphor as trauma externalisation; multiple POV across kings; dark-romance pacing. **What to avoid:** consent in early books is contested; Kennedy addresses this as the series progresses, but the craft lesson is "sign-post the dynamic early or you lose readers."

**Jennifer L. Armentrout — *Blood and Ash* + *Flesh and Fire*.** Atlantians are **part-god, descended from Primals** — not technically fae, though often shelved with fae romantasy. Mating is "heart-mates," not mating bonds. Armentrout's court mechanics are Kingdom-of-Atlantia vs Solis, with the gods' courts above. What to steal: her propulsive pacing; her bargain-revelation structure (Hawke/Casteel's identity reveal as bargain payoff). What to avoid: pacing habit of cliffhanger-per-chapter.

**Carissa Broadbent — *Crowns of Nyaxia*** (*The Serpent and the Wings of Night*). Vampire courts, not fae: House Night, House Shadow, House Blood. But the court-tournament structure and goddess-bond mechanic are directly imitable for fae writers. **Daughter of No Worlds** (Ara) involves fae-adjacent magic.

**Elise Kova — *Married to Magic*.** Standalones — *A Deal with the Elf King* (Luella & Eldas, Hades/Persephone + Beauty and the Beast), *A Dance with the Fae Prince* (Katria & Lord Fenwood, Psyche/Eros + Cinderella), plus vampire, siren, and wolf installments. The Fade separates human and Midscape realms. **What to steal:** fairytale-retelling structure under a unified world; standalone pacing with continuity; marriage-as-magical-contract. **What to avoid:** reviewers consistently note the true-name system is underdeveloped — if you use naming magic, commit.

**Jennifer Estep — *Crown of Shards* / *Gargoyle Queen*** — not pure fae but court intrigue at a high level. Worth reading for political craft.

**K.F. Breene.** *Demigods of San Francisco* (urban fae-adjacent), *Deliciously Dark Fairytales* (more overt). Prolific indie; imitate her publishing cadence, not necessarily her worldbuilding depth.

**Grace Draven — *Wraith Kings*** (starting *Radiance*). The Kai are not fae but function as fae-adjacent (magical, non-human, beautiful by different standards). **What to steal:** the "she is not beautiful by his standards, and neither is he by hers, and they see each other anyway" dynamic. Craft masterclass.

**Thea Harrison — *Elder Races*.** Wyr, Fae, Demonkind, Elves, Vampyres as distinct races; Seven courts; the Djinn King Khalil; Oracle. Harrison's world is **the most structurally complete** fae/urban-fantasy hybrid. What to steal: the multi-race court system; the Oracle mechanic (neutral magical authority); the treaty-map.

**Seanan McGuire — *October Daye*.** Urban fantasy fae, changeling protagonist. Long-running (20+ books). Masterclass in changeling politics, name-magic, and regional fae (Bay Area). Every romantasy author should read at least book one (*Rosemary and Rue*).

**Patricia Briggs — *Mercy Thompson***. Werewolves-primary but Gray Lords and fae courts are integrated. Strong iron-and-oath mechanics.

**Melissa Marr — *Wicked Lovely***. YA-adjacent, four-court structure (Summer, Winter, Dark, High), explicit engagement with mortal-fae consent. A good bridge between Celtic grounding and modern sensibility.

**Laura Thalassa — *The Bargainer***. Indie bestseller. Bargains-as-courtship in its purest form. Des (the Bargainer) and Callie's relationship is bargained into existence.

**C.N. Crawford — *Frost and Nectar*, *Fae of the North Court*, etc.** Indie prolific; seasonal-courts variation.

**Helen Scheuerer — *Legends of Thezmarr*.** Non-fae-primary but courtly, worth noting.

**Danielle L. Jensen — *The Bridge Kingdom*, *Dark Shores*.** Not fae-primary; *Dark Shores* has fae-adjacent magic.

**Rebecca Yarros — *Fourth Wing***. Dragon-rider, not fae. Often shelved together due to marketing; **do not treat as fae worldbuilding**.

**Stephanie Garber — *Caraval*, *Once Upon a Broken Heart***. Fae-adjacent (the Fates in *Once Upon a Broken Heart* operate like fae with bargains). Good bargain-mechanic reference.

**Penn Cole — *Spark of the Everflame***. Indie bestseller, fae-coded.

**Robin Hobb — *Farseer* / *Tawny Man***. Elderlings, not fae; craft-level prose above most of the subgenre, worth reading for character depth.

## Section 7: The "generic fae court" problem

**What readers roll their eyes at** (synthesised from Goodreads, r/romantasy, BookTok critique):

1. **Every court = one season / one mood / one aesthetic.** If the "Spring Court" is just pastels and roses and nothing else, it's a Pinterest board.
2. **The High Lord with the exact same tortured backstory.** Dead mother, cruel father, protective of a sister, powerful, moody, beautiful.
3. **Mate bond as instant-love shortcut.** The bond decides; the characters don't.
4. **No material culture.** What do these fae *eat* when not feasting? What do they *wear* when not in ballgowns? What do they *do* for work?
5. **No economy.** How do fae pay for things? Are there markets? Crafters? Banks?
6. **Palace-only worldbuilding.** We never see a farm, a shop, a school.
7. **All fae are the same height, build, and skin-tone except for hair colour.** Real folklore populations are weirder.
8. **"Fae can't lie" declared, never used generatively.** If the rule doesn't produce at least three plot twists, it's wasted.
9. **Iron mentioned once, never again.** Vulnerability needs to be a live threat, not a footnote.
10. **The Feyre-clone protagonist.** Mortal/half-fae woman, no family, enters fae world, becomes powerful, captures the High Lord's heart. Done a hundred times; only memorable when the character's particular interiority is specific.
11. **Villain court with no internal logic.** "They're evil because they're Unseelie" is not worldbuilding.
12. **No religion, no philosophy, no art, no history older than three generations.**
13. **Geography as vibe, not constraint.** Characters walk from Spring to Night in a day when it should take weeks.
14. **Immortal characters who think and act like 22-year-olds.** If your character is 500, give them 500 years of habits.
15. **Disposable lesser fae.** Named in crowds, killed in battles, never characters.

**What distinguishes memorable courts.** Specificity. A memorable court has (1) an economy (who pays whom for what), (2) a cuisine (one named dish, one taboo food), (3) a ritual calendar (at least three annual events), (4) an internal conflict (not just external), (5) a vocabulary (three to five words unique to this court), (6) a geography that constrains behaviour, (7) a class structure with more than two tiers, (8) a religion or lack thereof (atheism is worldbuilding too), (9) an art form the characters actually practise, (10) a relationship to death.

**Common pitfalls.** Aesthetic-over-structure (pretty but empty). Inconsistent magic rules (powers scale to plot). Forgotten minor fae (who never reappear). Static courts (nothing ever changes). One-dimensional rulers (evil or good). Unearned worldbuilding (exposition-dumped, not shown).

## Section 8: Cultural and folkloric sourcing

**Celtic (Irish, Scottish, Welsh, Cornish, Breton)** — the dominant romantasy source. Read **Katharine Briggs's *Dictionary of Fairies*** (1976) as baseline. **W.Y. Evans-Wentz's *The Fairy-Faith in Celtic Countries*** (1911) is foundational but dated; use with a critical eye. **Thomas Keightley's *The Fairy Mythology*** (1828/1850) collects a huge cross-European range. **Yeats's *Fairy and Folk Tales of the Irish Peasantry*** (1888) for primary texture. **John and Caitlín Matthews** for contemporary Celtic scholarship.

**Norse.** The **Prose Edda** (Snorri Sturluson) and **Poetic Edda** are primary. **Kevin Crossley-Holland's *The Norse Myths*** is a readable retelling. **H.R. Ellis Davidson** for scholarship.

**Slavic.** **Linda Ivanits's *Russian Folk Belief*** (1989) is the standard academic text. **Alexander Afanasyev's** tales for primary. Sensitivity note: Slavic folklore is less mined by romantasy, which makes it attractive — but Russian/Ukrainian/Polish/Balkan traditions are distinct and shouldn't be homogenised. **Katherine Arden's *Winternight Trilogy*** is the best modern romantasy adaptation of Russian folk belief.

**Germanic.** The Grimm brothers' ***Deutsche Mythologie*** (Jacob Grimm, 1835) is foundational. **Claude Lecouteux** for medieval Germanic belief. **Carlo Ginzburg's *Ecstasies: Deciphering the Witches' Sabbath*** for the Wild Hunt complex.

**Other European.** Basque (lamiak), Iberian (xana, mouras), Italian (fate, janara), Greek (nereids — note: classical, not fae, but adjacent).

**How to adapt with respect rather than appropriation.**

- **Living vs historical traditions.** Shinto (kitsune, tengu) is a living religion. Islam (djinn) is a living religion. Celtic folk traditions are less "practised religion" and more "folklore still loved" — but Irish and Scottish communities have ownership questions too.
- **Rule of thumb.** The more a tradition is currently practised as belief (not story), the more care you owe it.
- **Invent wholesale when:** the real tradition would require more research than you can do, the tradition's context is too specific to transplant, or your use would simplify something sacred.
- **Root in real tradition when:** you can do primary reading, you will name your sources in acknowledgements, and your use deepens rather than flattens the original.
- **Name-changes.** If you invent fae that are 80% sidhe and 20% original, don't call them sidhe. Call them something else and acknowledge the influence.
- **Sensitivity readers.** For non-Western or First Nations traditions, hire one.
- **Do not use sacred names lightly.** Tuatha Dé Danann, specific goddess names, specific heroes — using them for a hot faerie prince is a choice with consequences.

**Scholarly/research resources for authors.**

- Katharine Briggs, *A Dictionary of Fairies* (1976) and *The Fairies in English Tradition and Literature* (1967)
- Diane Purkiss, *Troublesome Things: A History of Fairies and Fairy Stories* (2000; US title: *At the Bottom of the Garden*)
- W.Y. Evans-Wentz, *The Fairy-Faith in Celtic Countries* (1911)
- Lewis Spence, *British Fairy Origins* (1946)
- Carole G. Silver, *Strange and Secret Peoples: Fairies and Victorian Consciousness* (1999)
- Nicola Bown, *Fairies in Nineteenth-Century Art and Literature* (2001)
- Claude Lecouteux, *Phantom Armies of the Night* and *Demons and Spirits of the Land*
- Terri Windling's **Endicott Studio** archives (online)
- **Sarah Clegg's *Woman's Lore***
- **Simon Young's Fairy Investigation Society** journal for contemporary folklore
- Folklore Society journal **Folklore** (UK)

## Section 9: Fresh and underused fae angles

The genre's frontier is everywhere the "generic fae court" isn't. Ten underused directions:

**Lesser-fae POV.** A protagonist who is not the High Lord's love interest and may never meet the High Lord. Holly Black's *Stolen Heir* comes close (Suren is royal but exiled). Imagine: a water-wraith cartographer, a nymph midwife, a brownie investigating a murder in the palace kitchens.

**Non-seasonal courts.** Alternatives include: **courts of sense** (Sight, Sound, Touch, Taste, Smell, Sixth); **courts of hour** (Dawn through Night in twelve); **courts of craft** (Weavers, Smiths, Singers, Healers, Scholars); **courts of musical mode** (Dorian, Phrygian, Lydian); **courts of philosophical virtue** (Courage, Justice, Temperance, Prudence); **courts of weather** (Storm, Mist, Drought, Bloom); **courts of metal** (Gold, Silver, Iron-Court-of-the-Exiled, Quicksilver); **courts of celestial body** (Sun, Moon, each planet, each major star); **courts of colour**; **courts of language** or **script**.

**Fae cities.** Most romantasy fae live in forests or palaces. A **fae city** — dense, loud, poor, rich, unequal — is massively underexplored. Imagine fae tenements, fae subways, fae markets with night and day schedules, fae neighbourhoods defined by which species can pass through which wards.

**Fae industries and economies.** What do fae **make and trade**? Options: **dreams** (harvested, bottled, sold), **memories** (as currency and as commodity), **glamour** (commissioned like portraits), **time** (the wealthy live longer because they buy time from the poor), **weather** (farmers purchase specific seasons), **emotion** (nobles commission sculpted emotional atmospheres for parties), **names** (a registry and black market), **song**, **silence** (sold to those who cannot sleep). Holly Black touches some of this in the Court of Teeth's frozen economies.

**Fae daily life.** What do fae do when not plotting? They garden. They argue about art. They play board games (give them one). They bake. They care for sick parents. They fight with their siblings. They get bored. **Show this.** The most vivid romantasy worlds are specific here — Velaris's bookstores, restaurants, and artists come closer than most, but even Maas rarely shows a fae doing laundry.

**Fae religion and theology.** Do fae worship? Maas's "Cauldron" and "Mother" are invoked but rarely theologised. Possibilities: **ancestor worship** (each family tends its dead); **place-worship** (each tree/spring has rites); **no religion** (fae are atheist, which is itself worldbuilding); **heretical sects**; **fae saints**; **pilgrimages**; **apostates**; **inquisitions**. A religious fae world is a different world.

**Fae class systems beyond High/Lesser.** Medieval guild structures (Smiths' guild, Weavers' guild, with masters and apprentices); ranked nobility (dukes, counts, barons, knights — each with magical weight); caste by birth-season (summer-born vs winter-born as social markers); merit-based ranking (tested every decade); bloodline-by-ancestor (descendants of specific ancient fae have status).

**Fae technology or lack thereof.** Magitech is underused. Clockwork fae cities. Industrial Fae ("the Iron Revolution" — what happens when fae figure out how to survive and manipulate iron?). Steampunk fae. Information-age fae. Or the **opposite**: deliberate technological regression as religious practice. Silicon-allergic fae fleeing human modernity.

**Fae aging, childhood, and education.** Most romantasy fae are adult when we meet them and have no on-page childhood. **Write fae children.** What is a fae kindergarten? What do fae teach their young? How do immortals raise someone for a century before they're considered adult? What is fae adolescence? Cardan's childhood flashbacks in *Folk of the Air* are one of Black's subtlest craft choices.

**Fae death rituals and afterlife beliefs.** When an immortal dies, what happens? Does everyone who has ever known them attend? Do fae believe in an afterlife, reincarnation, dissolution, merging-with-the-land, return-to-the-Cauldron? Are there fae *ghosts*? Is death *voluntary* at some age? Is there a fae hospice? Victorian mourning practice offers enormous material to adapt.

## Section 10: Sixty-plus workbook prompts

Each prompt is designed to force a specific choice, not a vague musing. Use them in any order.

### Court design (12)

1. Name three courts that are **not** organised by season. Pick one. Now name the court's governing principle in one sentence and list three things this principle forbids.
2. Draw your court's map. Mark the single place where commoners are not allowed, and the single place where nobles are not allowed. Why each?
3. Your court's ruler has one habit that contradicts the court's official aesthetic. Name it. (Spring Court's High Lord who only eats meat; the War Court's king who gardens.)
4. Write a 50-word tourism advertisement for your court — and then write a 50-word warning notice posted at its border.
5. Decide the one physical law of your court that differs from the human world — and one secondary consequence of that law the characters have to work around.
6. Your court has a colour that is illegal to wear. Which, and why?
7. Name three rival courts. For each, write one line of why your court hates them — and one line of what your court secretly envies.
8. Your court's magic weakens under one condition. Name it, and name the single most feared weather event.
9. Describe the throne room in six sentences — but three of the sentences must describe something that has *nothing* to do with power (an old drawing, a scratch, a stain).
10. Name the court's national animal, national plant, and national dish. Now explain how each got that status in under 100 words of history.
11. Write the court's founding myth as it is told to children. Then write it as dissident historians tell it.
12. Your court has a second capital — a backup city nobody speaks of. Where is it, and what's there now?

### Bargain-writing (6)

13. Draft a three-clause fae bargain between your protagonist and your love interest using a currency that is **not** gold, sex, or a favour. (Time? Memories? A name? A sense? A child-that-might-be?)
14. Write the exact wording of a bargain that appears to favour one party and actually favours the other. The trick must be in a preposition or a modifier.
15. Design a bargain that escalates over three interactions: first transactional, then intimate, then existential. Write the opening sentence of each.
16. Draft a fae bargain your protagonist refuses. What does refusal cost?
17. Write a scene where a character breaks a bargain — whether they can, how the magic catches them, what happens to their body.
18. Invent a bargain-adjacent institution in your world (a debt-market, a bargain-broker's office, a public registry). Write the sign on the door and the first thing a client hears inside.

### Mating bond design (6)

19. List six attributes of your mating bond: reciprocity, timing of reveal, rejection cost, physical effects, cosmic source, consummation requirement. Write one answer per.
20. Write the scene where your protagonist first *feels* the bond without knowing what it is. They should interpret it as something else (indigestion, anger, deja vu).
21. Your mating bond has one rule that works against romantic convention (can be forcibly severed; appears to the wrong party first; never appears if both parties are happy). Name it.
22. Write the rejection of a mating bond. How does the rejected party survive?
23. Invent a courtship ritual unique to your world that must precede the bond's acknowledgement. Include one physical object, one spoken formula, and one taboo.
24. Your protagonist's bond partner is someone politically impossible. Write the scene where they acknowledge it anyway. Set it somewhere public.

### Court-crossing politics (6)

25. Two courts at war. Your protagonist is a child of one and lover of the other. Write the inciting incident in 300 words.
26. Name three treaties between your courts. For each, one clause nobody remembers but which is about to matter.
27. Design a diplomatic ritual (an exchange, a shared meal, a contest) that occurs when two courts meet. Include what can never be done.
28. Your protagonist attends a cross-court wedding. Describe the seating plan and the three hidden feuds at the head table.
29. A court-crossing elopement. Write the letter left behind by the eloping party, and the response written on its back by their parent.
30. Two courts share a border. Describe the border market: what is traded, what is forbidden, who polices it.

### Daily life detail (12)

31. What does a lesser fae eat for breakfast on an ordinary morning? Include where the food came from.
32. Write a day in the life of a fae servant in the palace — from waking to sleeping. The High Lord must not appear.
33. Your fae have a game. Describe its rules, when it's played, what winning means socially.
34. Name three hobbies specific to your fae aristocracy — and three specific to commoners. What crosses between?
35. Write a shopping list a fae household keeps. Include something mundane, something magical, and something illegal.
36. Describe a fae child's toy in three sentences. Include who made it.
37. What does fae laundry involve? Answer in under 100 words.
38. Describe a fae tavern. Who drinks there? What's the cheapest drink? What's the most dangerous?
39. Write the small talk between two fae strangers waiting for the same thing. What do they complain about?
40. Your fae have a swear word. Where does it come from? What happens if you use it in front of a child?
41. Describe a fae wedding that is *not* your protagonist's — a side character's. Include the meal and one guest who ruins something.
42. Write a fae funeral. Show the ritual, not the grief.

### Magic system (6)

43. Name the one rule of your magic that never bends. Then write the scene where a character nearly breaks it — and what stops them.
44. What cannot be done with magic in your world? List three absolute impossibilities. Commit.
45. Your magic has a cost. Is it time, health, memory, pieces of self, another's permission, a material? Pick one, and name what happens when someone overdraws.
46. A magical discipline exists that nobody has practised in three generations. What is it? Why not? Who's about to revive it?
47. Design an everyday use of magic that makes domestic life different from ours (cleaning, cooking, travel, birth control, contraception, contraception failure). Include one cultural consequence.
48. Write the spell or ritual at the centre of your book. Include the single word that must be spoken and the single thing that must be sacrificed.

### Hierarchy and class (6)

49. Your court has more than two tiers. Name five, with the visible marker of each (a colour, a material, a gesture, a word, a body modification).
50. Write a scene of a lesser fae being dismissed by a High Fae. The lesser fae must retain dignity without breaking the rule.
51. Invent a social mobility mechanism — how can a lesser fae rise? List the three paths, and the one the establishment denies exists.
52. Your protagonist is a half-blood. Name the insult each side uses for them.
53. Describe the court's servant quarters. Include the single object that signals the class system is breaking down.
54. Write the oath of service a lesser fae swears when entering court employment. Include the one clause readers should find chilling.

### Romance-specific (12)

55. Your love interest's first gift to your protagonist is not flowers or jewellery. What is it, and what does it oblige?
56. Write the scene in which your love interest first uses their power *for* your protagonist rather than *over* them.
57. Write a dialogue in which your love interest lies without lying. They must answer three questions, all truthfully, and reveal nothing.
58. Describe the first time your protagonist sees your love interest's unglamoured form. Begin in the middle of the revelation.
59. Your couple must complete a courtship ritual with three stages. Write the first sentence of each stage.
60. Write the argument that almost ends your couple — and make it about something that is not the plot.
61. Your love interest has a former lover in court. Write their first meeting with your protagonist. No violence. No confession.
62. A political marriage is offered to your love interest. Write the conversation where they tell your protagonist. Include what they do not say.
63. Write your couple's handfasting, marriage, mating ceremony, or bonding. Choose the tier, and write the vow that is spoken only between them.
64. Write the first time your protagonist asks a fae favour of their lover — and the first time your lover asks one back. Make the second one worse.
65. Describe the first night your couple shares in which magic is not present. What do they do?
66. Your couple will be separated for a long time. Write the object each keeps of the other.

### Conflict and plot (6)

67. Name the single event that must happen for your plot to begin. Now move it to the wrong court and write what changes.
68. Your antagonist believes they are right. Write their manifesto in 200 words. No villain monologue — a reasoned brief.
69. A trial scene. Your protagonist is accused. They are guilty. Write the opening statement of their defence.
70. The Wild Hunt arrives. Who does it take, and who did your protagonist expect it would?
71. Write the scene of betrayal — but make the betrayer the character readers most trust.
72. Your world ends one way — war, divine intervention, a revealed truth, a refused bargain. Pick. Then write the small moment, three chapters earlier, when it became inevitable.

### Cultural sourcing (6)

73. Identify the real-world folk tradition your court is most inspired by. Write one sentence naming your debt. Now list three things you will **not** import from that tradition, and why.
74. Read one scholarly essay on your source tradition. Write a paragraph of how your fae differ from the folkloric original — and commit to one specific divergence you will defend on page.
75. Name the real-world festival closest to your court's signature ritual. Now name the three details you are changing.
76. Your court contains a linguistic borrowing from a real culture. Consult a native speaker or a scholar. Write the correct pronunciation and the cultural weight of the word. Decide whether to keep or replace it.
77. Identify the living tradition most adjacent to your fae. (Shinto for kitsune-inflected fae; Islam for djinn-adjacent figures; Celtic neopaganism for sidhe-adjacent courts.) Write your personal statement of how you will handle it responsibly.
78. Write your author's note. It should credit three scholarly sources, name three things you invented wholesale, and acknowledge one thing you know you simplified.

---

## Closing note for the workbook author

The most memorable fae courts are not the ones with the best aesthetics — they are the ones where **every structure produces pressure**. A court's political system should generate conflict; its economy should generate class tension; its religion should generate doubt; its mating bonds should generate loss as well as love; its glamour should raise questions the characters have to answer on page. Every rule you set is a scene you are promising to write.

The Celtic folk traditions that ground this subgenre are rich enough to sustain a thousand more romantasies — and strange enough to save you from the generic pitfalls of the Pinterest court. Read Briggs, read Purkiss, read Yeats, read the ballads. Then invent. The genre needs more specificity, more material culture, more economy, more religion, more weirdness, and more characters who are not High Lords or the women High Lords love. The frontier is everywhere the Feyre-clone isn't.

Use the prompts above in any order. Keep the mechanics consistent. Interrogate the glamour. Write the servants.
