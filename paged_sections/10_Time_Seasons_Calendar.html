Section 10

Building Your Magic System — The Foundation

Magic is often what makes a fantasy world feel like fantasy. But magic that can do anything is magic that means nothing. The most memorable magic systems have rules, costs, and limits—and those constraints are what make them interesting. When your character reaches for their power, we need to know what they're risking, what they're capable of, and what's completely beyond them.

This section builds your magic from the ground up: where it comes from, who can use it, how it works, and—most importantly—what it can't do. Get this foundation right, and your magic will feel real. Skip it, and you'll find yourself writing your way into corners or solving problems too easily.

One more thing before we dive in: magic in romantasy isn't just about battles and plot problems. It's also about intimacy—how your characters connect, what they hide from each other, what it means when they finally let someone see their power (or their vulnerability). As you build your system, keep one eye on how magic might bring your leads together or push them apart.

The Source

Every magic system has an origin—where the power actually comes from. This shapes everything: who has access, how it's viewed by society, and what it costs to use.

Where does magic come from in your world?

Options: the gods or divine forces, the natural world (ley lines, elemental forces), an internal source (bloodlines, souls, emotions), external objects, knowledge and study, a bargain or bond, something unknown or mysterious.

Is there a single source of magic, or multiple types with different origins?

If multiple, how do they interact—or conflict?

Can the source be depleted, corrupted, or cut off? What happens if it is?

How do people in your world explain where magic comes from?

Is the truth different from what most believe?

Romantasy Check: Could the source of magic connect your leads—or divide them?

Sometimes magic creates multiple layers of division. In ACOTAR: Feyre is human; Tamlin is High Fae. That's the obvious gap—she's mortal and powerless in his world. But there's also something he's not telling her. The reader experiences the divide as a mystery: why won't he tell me the truth?

Or the division might be more straightforward: Magic runs through noble bloodlines, and she's aristocracy while he's common-born—except he's developing abilities that shouldn't exist in someone like him.

How might the source of magic affect your leads' relationship?

Who Can Use Magic

Not everyone has to be magical—and who can wield magic shapes everything from social structure to your protagonist's place in the world.

How common is magic in your world?

Can nearly everyone use some form? Limited to certain bloodlines, species, or chosen ones? Can anyone learn it with training? Vanishingly rare?

What determines whether someone can use magic?

Birth (bloodlines, species, being "chosen"), training and study, a triggering event (trauma, ritual), possession of an object or bond, random chance, a sacrifice or bargain.

Can magical ability be gained if you weren't born with it? Can it be lost or taken away?

If magic is inherited, how does that affect families, dynasties, and marriages?

If it's learned, who controls access to that knowledge—and who's excluded?

Romantasy Check: How does who can use magic affect the dynamic between your leads?

In Serpent & Dove: Lou is a witch hiding her magic; Reid is a witch hunter sworn to kill her kind. It's not just that one has magic and one doesn't—it's that his entire identity is built on destroying what she is. The question isn't just "who has power"—it's "what happens when he finds out?"

How Magic Works

This is the mechanical heart of your system—the "physics" of magic. You don't need to explain everything to readers, but you should understand how it operates so your story stays consistent.

How does someone actually do magic? What does the process look like?

Spoken words, physical gestures, intense concentration, drawing symbols, channeling through objects, emotional states, rituals, physical contact, visualization, music or art.

What does magic look like when it happens?

What do bystanders see, hear, or feel?

How long does it take to use magic?

Can it be done instantly, or does it require preparation?

Can magic be interrupted? What happens if it's disrupted mid-use?

Is magic precise or unpredictable?

Does the same action always produce the same result, or is there variation based on skill, emotion, or circumstance?

Romantasy Check: Is there anything about how magic is performed that creates vulnerability or intimacy?

In Fourth Wing: Dragon riders share a mental bond with their dragons—and sometimes with each other. Violet and Xaden can speak mind-to-mind, which sounds romantic until you realize it means he knows when she's scared, when she's lying, when she's thinking about him. There's nowhere to hide. The bond doesn't just connect them; it strips away the walls they've both built.

How might the process of using magic create vulnerable or intimate moments between your leads?

The Cost

This is where your magic system gets genuinely interesting. Unlimited power is boring; power with a price creates drama. What does magic take from the user—and is it worth paying?

What does using magic cost?

Physical energy/exhaustion, pain/damage, shortened lifespan, emotional toll, rare materials, time, moral cost (corruption, debt), something from the world around them.

Does the cost scale with the power used?

Is a small spell a small price, while world-shaking magic demands everything?

What happens when someone overextends—uses more magic than they can safely handle?

Is the cost immediate, or does it accumulate over time?

Can someone use magic freely for years and then pay all at once?

Are there situational or plot-driven costs—times when magic can't be used safely?

In ACOTAR: Rhysand limits his magic use because the enemy can track it. Think about: Can magic be traced? Would using it alert enemies or violate a treaty? Does magic leave evidence?

Does your protagonist know the full cost of their power, or will they find out the hard way?

Romantasy Check: How might the cost of magic create romantic tension or sacrifice?

In From Blood and Ash: Poppy's gift lets her sense others' emotions and take away their pain—but it means absorbing that pain into herself. Every time she helps someone, she suffers for it. When she uses her power on Hawke, she's not just healing him; she's choosing to hurt for him.

Or: His power is fueled by strong emotion, which means falling in love makes him more powerful but also more dangerous. Or: Magic costs memories, and she's starting to forget why she's fighting—including starting to forget him.

How might the cost of magic create stakes in the relationship?

The Limits

What magic cannot do is more important than what it can. Limits create stakes, force creativity, and prevent your characters from solving every problem with a wave of their hand.

Hard Magic vs. Soft Magic: Where Does Your System Land?

Hard Magic has clearly defined rules, costs, and limitations. Readers understand what magic can and can't do, so magical solutions feel earned.

Soft Magic is mysterious and not fully explained. It creates wonder and danger. The risk: solving major problems with undefined magic can feel like cheating.

Most romantasy falls somewhere in between—defined enough for stakes, mysterious enough for wonder.

How to write it: For hard magic, show the rules through consequence. Don't explain that healing magic requires equivalent pain — show the healer gritting their teeth, show the price paid. For soft magic, maintain consistency in mood and limitation: if magic feels dangerous and unpredictable, keep that feeling; don't let it become convenient when needed. In both cases, write magic through the body: the physical sensation of drawing power, the exhaustion after use, the way it feels to be on the receiving end. When your character tests their limits, show the attempt, the strain, and the moment they hit the boundary — whether that's a rule they can't break or a cost they can't pay. Magic becomes real when readers feel its weight.

Final Thought: Magic Is About Choices

The best magic systems aren't about power—they're about cost, consequence, and choice. Every time your character reaches for their magic, they should be weighing what they gain against what they risk.

Magic in romantasy should create both obstacles and opportunities for connection. It can divide your leads (forbidden powers, opposite sides, dangerous secrets) or unite them (shared abilities, complementary gifts, the vulnerability of showing someone your true power).

Build magic that forces your characters to make hard choices—and makes readers feel the weight of those choices.
